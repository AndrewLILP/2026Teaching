Phase 1 — Identifying & Defining

Before you touch Minecraft, define the problem. Everything connects back to this.

Problem & Audience

Problem Statement Required

Write a clear, concise statement of the design problem your game solves. Avoid vague phrases like "entertain people" — be specific about who, what, and why.

Guiding questions
  • Who is your player? (age, context, experience level)
  • What need, challenge, or opportunity does your game address?
  • Why does Minecraft make sense as the platform for this?
Player Profile Required

Describe your target player. This is not just "people who like Minecraft" — give a specific, realistic picture of the person you are designing for.

Cover these points
  • Age range and prior gaming experience
  • How they will access the game (solo, classroom, LAN, etc.)
  • Any accessibility needs to consider

Requirements

Functional Requirements Required

List what your game must do. These are testable — you'll use them in Phase 4 to judge your success. Aim for 5–8 specific requirements.

Examples of functional requirements
  • The game must have a clear win condition communicated to the player
  • The custom mechanic must trigger when the player enters a specific zone
  • Players must be able to restart without leaving the world
Non-Functional Requirements Required

These describe how your game should feel or perform — qualities that are harder to test but still matter.

Consider
  • Playability — how long should a session take? (e.g. completable in 15 min)
  • Tone — serious challenge, casual fun, educational?
  • Performance — should it run without lag on school computers?
  • Clarity — can a new player understand the rules without reading a manual?
Legal & Ethical Considerations Required

Address the real-world responsibilities you need to think about before and during development.

Things to consider
  • Resource pack and add-on licences — have you checked the creator's terms?
  • Appropriate content — is everything in your game suitable for your audience?
  • If online multiplayer is involved — what data might be collected?
  • Copyright of any music, images, or textures you use

Phase 2 — Researching & Planning

Analyse what already exists, then design your own solution. Pseudocode before code — always.

⚠️ Pseudocode rule You must write pseudocode for your custom mechanic BEFORE you open MakeCode or the Scripting API. This is not optional. Evidence of this step is required for a C grade or above on the algorithms descriptor.

Research

Existing Game Analysis #1 Evidence

Choose an existing game (Minecraft or otherwise) and analyse its design decisions. Don't just describe it — explain why the developers made those choices.

Analyse these elements
  • What is the core mechanic? How is it taught to the player?
  • What inputs, processes and outputs does the game use?
  • What makes the UX clear (or not) for a new player?
  • What would you borrow or change for your own game?
Existing Game Analysis #2 Evidence

Repeat the analysis for a second game. Comparing two games gives you more design vocabulary to draw on.

Design

System Diagram Required

Draw your game as a system. Show the relationship between inputs, processes, outputs, and player feedback. This can be hand-drawn and photographed — it must be included in your portfolio.

Your diagram must show
  • Inputs (player actions, world events, time, etc.)
  • Processes (what your code does with those inputs)
  • Outputs (changes to the game world, player feedback, score, etc.)
  • At least one feedback loop (how output influences future input)
Pseudocode — Custom Mechanic(s) Code

Write pseudocode for each custom mechanic using NSW pseudocode conventions (BEGIN/END, IF/THEN/ELSE, FOR/WHILE). Do NOT copy JavaScript here — this must be language-independent.

Your pseudocode must include at least
  • One variable that stores changing data
  • One selection structure (IF / ELSE IF / ELSE)
  • One iteration structure (FOR or WHILE loop)
Design Sketches / UI Wireframes Evidence

Sketch the key spaces and UI elements of your game before building. Annotate your sketches to explain design decisions — don't just draw, explain why.

Sketch and annotate at least
  • The player's starting experience / tutorial moment
  • The main play space or arena
  • Any on-screen UI elements (signs, scoreboards, HUD)
  • The win / end state the player sees
Project Plan with Milestones Required

Plan out your build schedule. Be realistic — leave time for testing, fixing bugs, and getting feedback. A plan you stick to is evidence of project management skills.

Phase 3 — Producing & Implementing

Build your game. Document every meaningful iteration — the log IS the evidence.

⚠️ Language requirement Your custom mechanic must be implemented in JavaScript (MakeCode) or TypeScript (Scripting API). Command blocks alone do not satisfy the CT5 algorithms outcome above a D grade.

Code

Annotated Source Code Code

Your final code file(s) must include comments that explain what each section does AND why you made that choice. Uncommented code gets no credit for design thinking.

Required in your code
  • At least one variable with a meaningful name (not x, a, temp)
  • At least one IF / ELSE IF / ELSE selection structure
  • At least one FOR or WHILE loop
  • Comment above each function explaining its purpose
  • Comment where you made a deliberate design choice

Iteration Cycle 1 Evidence
Record for each cycle
  • What did you build or change?
  • What problem or decision prompted this change?
  • Did it work as expected? Evidence? (screenshot, video, test result)
  • What will you do next as a result?

Phase 4 — Testing & Evaluating

Structured testing and honest evaluation. Grade A students identify things they'd do differently — that shows mastery, not failure.

Testing

Structured Test Table Required

For each mechanic or feature, document what you expected, what actually happened, whether it passed or failed, and what you did as a result. This is NOT "I played my game and it worked."

Test table columns
  • Feature / mechanic being tested
  • What I expected to happen
  • What actually happened (include screenshots)
  • Pass / Fail
  • Action taken (fix, accept, defer)
Peer Playtest — Tester 1 Evidence

A real person plays your game and gives written feedback. You cannot test your own game fairly — you already know how it works.

Tester must answer
  • Did you understand what to do without being told? (Y/N + comment)
  • Did you encounter any bugs or unexpected behaviour?
  • What was the most enjoyable part?
  • What was confusing or frustrating?
  • What would you change if you could?
Peer Playtest — Tester 2 Evidence

Evaluation

Final Evaluation Against Requirements Required

Return to your functional and non-functional requirements from Phase 1. For each one, evaluate whether your game meets it, partially meets it, or does not meet it — and explain why.

Structure your evaluation
  • State the requirement
  • Met / Partially met / Not met
  • Evidence (what in your game shows this?)
  • If not fully met — what would be needed?
Reflection Required

This is your most personal piece of writing. A strong reflection shows honest self-assessment — it's not a summary of what you built, it's an analysis of how you built it and what you learned.

Reflect on these
  • What was the most significant technical challenge you overcame? How?
  • What design decision are you most proud of, and why?
  • What would you do differently if you started again?
  • How did your project change from your original plan?
  • What skills did you develop that you didn't have at the start?
