Identifying & Defining Lesson 01 of 10

What Makes a
Board Game Work?

Analyse Monopoly as a system, deconstruct it into its parts, and define what your version needs to be.

Learning Intentions
  • Understand how Monopoly works as an interacting system of rules and components
  • Identify the game primitives (board, token, dice, property, money, turn) and explain their purpose
  • Define a set of functional and non-functional requirements for your group's game
  • Produce a hand-drawn board layout that reflects your chosen theme
Success Criteria — I can…
  • Describe at least 4 game primitives and explain how they interact
  • Write at least 6 functional requirements and 2 non-functional requirements
  • Produce a labelled board sketch with named space types (Property, Tax, Go, Chance)
  • Justify my group's chosen theme with reference to at least one design decision

Part 1 · Whole Class (15 min)

Play it. Then pull it apart.

Your teacher will run a 10-minute hot-seat demo of Monopoly — either on screen or with a physical board. As you watch, your job is not to play: it is to observe. What is happening at each step? What information is being tracked? What decisions are players making?

Use the table below to annotate what you notice.

Game Event What information is involved? What rule applies? Who does something?
Roll dice Two numbers → sum determines move distance Player must move exactly that many spaces Current player
Land on unowned property Property name, price, rent value, owner = none Player may choose to buy or leave it Current player decides
Land on owned property Rent amount, owner's name, buyer's money Player must pay rent to owner Both players — money transfers
Pass Go Player's position, salary amount ($200) Collect salary when passing or landing on Go Current player receives money
Bankruptcy Player's total money, debt owed If money < 0 after paying, player is eliminated Eliminated player exits; others continue
Your example…

Copy this table into your notebook and add at least two more rows from your own observations.

Part 2 · Concept (10 min)

The Game Primitives

Every board game is built from a small set of primitives — the fundamental building blocks that everything else is made of. Monopoly uses seven core primitives. In Unity, each primitive will become a C# class or component.

🎲
Dice
Generates a random number to drive movement. Provides unpredictability.
🗺
Board
A loop of spaces with a fixed order. Players navigate it repeatedly.
⬡
Space
Each position has a type and triggers a specific action when landed on.
♟
Token
Represents a player's position on the board. Visual identity.
💰
Money
A shared resource that flows between players. Tracks progress toward winning or losing.
🏘
Property
A space that can be owned. Generates income for the owner when others land on it.
🔄
Turn
Structures who acts when. Ensures fairness and progression.

Discussion question

Which primitive is most important — if you removed it, the game would completely fall apart? Discuss with your group and record your answer in your notebook with a justification.

Part 3 · Group Work (15 min)

Design Your Version

Your group will build a Monopoly-inspired game, not a Monopoly clone. You need to choose a theme that gives your board its identity — the theme affects space names, property categories, and the visual design of your UI.

Theme ideas to get you started

You are not limited to these — they are starting points.

Theme Property categories Go space idea Tax space idea
🏙 Metro City Suburbs, CBD, Industrial, Waterfront Collect your salary Council rates due
🚀 Space Station Docking bays, Habitats, Labs, Command Refuel at base Oxygen tax
🏰 Fantasy Kingdom Villages, Towns, Castles, Dragon Lairs Collect tribute Kingdom tithe

Part 4 · Group Work (10 min)

Define Your Requirements

Before you write a single line of code, you need to know exactly what your game must do. Requirements come in two types:

Functional — what the system does (actions, behaviours, logic). Non-functional — how the system behaves (performance, usability, aesthetics).

Requirements Builder Functional: 0 / 6  ·  Non-functional: 0 / 2
F Functional — what it does
NF Non-functional — how it behaves

This tool helps you draft requirements on screen — copy your final list into your notebook. You need at least 6 functional and 2 non-functional to meet the success criteria.

Worked examples

Type Requirement Why it matters
F The game must allow 2–4 players to take turns rolling a dice and moving their token Without this, no game loop exists
F When a player lands on an unowned property, they must be offered the option to buy it Core Monopoly mechanic
F The game must detect when a player's money drops below zero and eliminate them Defines how the game ends
NF The current player's name and money must always be visible on screen Usability — players need to see their status at all times
NF The board theme must be visually consistent — all spaces share the same colour palette Aesthetic coherence

Part 5 · Group Work (10 min)

Sketch Your Board

Using the planning sheet your teacher provides (or a blank page), sketch your board layout. Your board must be a loop of spaces — just like Monopoly. You do not need to draw perfectly; this is a planning document, not artwork.

Your board sketch must include:

Requirement Minimum Notes
Total spaces 20 spaces More is fine — 24–32 is typical for a good game loop
Space types represented All 4 types Property, Tax, Go, Chance — every type must appear at least once
Property groups 2 colour groups Each group has 2–3 properties with similar rent values
Go space Exactly 1 Starting position — all tokens begin here; players collect salary when passing
Space labels Every space named Even placeholder names like "Property 1" are fine at this stage

Space type quick reference

🏘
Property
Can be bought. Has a purchase price and a rent value. Belongs to a colour group.
💸
Tax
Landing here deducts a fixed amount from the player. No owner — money disappears.
🃏
Chance
Triggers a random event — could be good or bad. Adds surprise to the game.
▶
Go
Starting space. Players collect salary each time they pass or land on it.

Tiered Tasks

Extension Work

If your group finishes the main tasks before the end of the lesson, choose from the tier that matches your confidence level.

Basic
  • Write a plain-English description of what happens during one complete turn in your game
  • List three things that could go wrong in your game (edge cases) — e.g. what if two players land on the same space?
Intermediate
  • For each property group, decide a price and rent value — explain how you chose them (balance, fairness)
  • Write your turn sequence as numbered steps — this is the beginning of your algorithm
  • Sketch a rough UI layout showing where player info and the dice button will appear on screen
Advanced
  • Research one published indie board game on itch.io — annotate how it handles turn structure, win condition, and player feedback
  • Write pseudocode for the full turn sequence using NSW pseudocode conventions
  • Propose a unique mechanic not in standard Monopoly — describe how it would be implemented in code

End of Lesson

Notebook Checkpoint

Before you pack up, make sure your notebook has evidence of today's thinking. This is your primary assessment artefact — rough is fine; empty is not.

  • Completed analysis table — at least 5 game events annotated, including your own additions
  • A short paragraph explaining which game primitive your group considers most important, and why
  • Your group's chosen theme — with one sentence justifying the design decision
  • Requirements list — at least 6 functional and 2 non-functional, in your own words
  • A labelled board sketch — minimum 20 spaces, all 4 space types represented, every space named
  • Reflection (2–3 sentences): What surprised you about how Monopoly works as a system? What do you think will be the hardest part to build in Unity?
