Part 1 · Requirements Evaluation (20 min)

Did We Build What We Said We Would?

In Lesson 1, your group wrote a requirements list — at least 6 functional and 2 non-functional requirements. Now it's time to return to that list and honestly assess whether each one was met. This is not about being harsh — it is about being precise. "Partially met" with a clear explanation is more valuable than "Met" with no evidence.

What counts as evidence? A requirement is Met when you can point to something in the game and say "here is where this works." A requirement is Partially Met when it works in some cases but not others, or when it works differently from what was originally specified. A requirement is Not Met when it was planned but not implemented — and that is fine to acknowledge honestly.

Part 2 · Written Evaluation (20 min)

Four Questions, Four Paragraphs

A strong evaluation is structured. Work through these four prompts in order — each targets a different aspect of the project. Write directly into the text areas below, then copy into your notebook.

Q1 How effectively does your game meet its requirements?
Reference specific requirements by name. Use evidence from your requirements evaluation table above. Avoid vague phrases like "mostly works" — be specific about what works, what doesn't, and why.
0 words
Q2 Describe one design decision that worked well and justify it.
A design decision is a choice you made during planning or building — not just a feature that works. Explain what you chose, why you chose it, and what evidence shows it was a good decision. Think about: UI layout, board size, Go salary amount, property pricing, turn structure.
0 words
Q3 Describe one thing you would change if you had more time, and explain technically how you would implement it.
This question has two parts — the "what" and the "how". The "how" is what makes an A-range answer. Don't just say you'd add houses; explain which class would need a new field, what method would calculate rent differently, and how the UI would communicate the change to players.
0 words
Q4 How did the peer testing in Lesson 9 improve your game?
Be specific — name the bugs found, explain what caused them, and describe what you changed. If a tester found something you hadn't noticed, reflect on why you missed it and what that tells you about the value of external testing.
0 words

What distinguishes A-range evaluation

Specificity: Names requirements, methods, and evidence — not "the game works well".

Technical depth: The "what would you add" answer explains HOW, not just WHAT.

Honesty: Acknowledges requirements not met without making excuses.

Process thinking: Retrospective notes show understanding of why decisions were made, not just what was done.
