Testing & Evaluating Lesson 09 of 10

Structured
Peer Testing

Play another group's game with fresh eyes, complete the observation checklist, log every bug you find — then receive their feedback on yours and start fixing the highest-priority issues.

Learning Intentions
  • Understand the difference between a bug, a design flaw, and a missing feature
  • Apply a structured observation checklist as a tester for another group's game
  • Record findings in a bug log with severity ratings and reproduction steps
  • Prioritise received feedback and begin fixing the highest-severity issues
Success Criteria — I can…
  • Complete the observation checklist for the game I tested with ✓/✗ and notes
  • Write at least 3 bug log entries with severity, description, cause, and fix attempted
  • Produce a priority fix list from feedback received, ordered by severity
  • Demonstrate at least one fix applied in Unity before the end of the lesson

Part 1 · Testing as a Discipline (5 min)

The Tester's Mindset

A tester's job is not to enjoy the game — it is to break it. Good testing means deliberately trying things the developer didn't expect: clicking the Roll button twice fast, buying the most expensive property immediately, trying to pay rent when you have $0. The more creative the tester, the more valuable the feedback.

Finding type Definition Example Severity
Bug — Critical Crashes the game or makes it unplayable Clicking Roll Dice causes a NullReferenceException and freezes 🔴 Critical
Bug — Major Breaks a core mechanic without crashing Go salary fires every turn, not just when passing 🟠 Major
Bug — Minor Something works incorrectly but is playable Message text sometimes doesn't update after a purchase 🔵 Minor
Design flaw Works as coded but feels wrong or unfair Go salary of $1000 makes the game trivially easy ⚪ Design
Cosmetic issue Visual or text problem that doesn't affect play Player name is cut off in the HUD when it's longer than 8 characters ⬜ Cosmetic
Missing feature Something expected isn't there No indication of whose turn it is after a player is eliminated 🟣 Missing
Testing etiquette: Write findings as observations, not criticisms. "Money doesn't update after rent payment" is a useful bug report. "The UI is confusing" is not. Specific, reproducible descriptions are what developers need. If you can reproduce a bug consistently, note the exact steps — this is the most valuable thing you can give.

Part 4 · Receiving Feedback & Fixing (25 min)

From Feedback to Fixes

When you receive the checklist and bug log from the group who tested your game, resist the urge to be defensive. Every bug found now is one less bug in your final submission. Read all feedback, then use the priority fix list below to decide what to tackle in the remaining time.

How to prioritise

Fix first Fix if time allows Defer to after submission
🔴 Critical bugs — game crashes or is unplayable 🟠 Major bugs — core mechanic broken ⬜ Cosmetic issues — text cut off, colours wrong
Any bug that will prevent the peer test in L9 from running 🔵 Minor bugs — something works wrongly but game continues 🟣 Missing features not in original requirements
Anything the tester couldn't get past at all ⚪ Design flaws — balance issues, confusing UX Ideas from feedback that would be "nice to have"
🔧 Priority Fix List — drag to reorder
Debugging process — what to write in your notebook: For each fix you attempt, record: (1) what the bug was, (2) what you thought was causing it, (3) what you changed in the code, (4) whether it worked. If it didn't work, record what you tried next. This chain of reasoning — hypothesis → test → result — is exactly what an A-range notebook demonstrates.

Part 5 · Test Log Quality

Two Bug Reports — Spot the Difference

Both of these describe the same bug. One is useful; one is not. The quality of your bug log is assessed — not just the quantity of entries.

✗ Weak bug report

Bug: The money is wrong

Severity: Major

Steps: Play the game

Notes: It just doesn't work

✓ Strong bug report

Bug: Player 1 money doesn't decrease when paying rent

Severity: Major

Steps: 1. Player 2 owns City Rd ($30 rent). 2. Player 1 (start $1500) lands on City Rd. 3. Message says "paid $30 rent" but Player 1 still shows $1500.

Likely cause: ChargeMoney() may not be updating the HUD, or Player 2 is receiving the payment but Player 1 isn't being charged

The strong report tells the developer exactly what to look at — ChargeMoney() and the HUD update in ChargeRent(). The weak report gives nothing to work from.

End of Lesson

Notebook Checkpoint

  • Completed observation checklist — copied into notebook with the game name of the group you tested, all items marked ✓ / ✗ / N/A with notes on each failure
  • Bug log — at least 3 entries as a tester, each with: description, severity, reproduction steps, likely cause
  • Priority fix list — received feedback ranked by severity, with an estimate of fix time for each item
  • At least one fix applied — describe what the bug was, what you changed in the code, and the result
  • If a fix didn't work — record your debugging process: what you tried, what happened, what you'll try next
  • Reflection (2–3 sentences): What was the most surprising bug found in your game — something you hadn't noticed yourself? What does that tell you about the importance of testing by someone who wasn't involved in building it?

Peer test logistics

Swap computers with another group. Tester logs bugs on this page — the bug log and checklist live here. After 20 minutes, swap back. Each group then reads the feedback received and builds their priority list.

Minimum: 2 complete turns as tester. Aim for: at least one property buy, one rent payment, one player elimination if time allows.

Grade indicators — this lesson
A 5+ strong bug reports with reproduction steps and likely causes; priority list ordered with reasoning; at least 2 fixes applied with debugging process documented
B Checklist complete; 3–4 specific bug reports; priority fix list present; at least 1 fix applied and documented
C Checklist mostly complete; 3 bug log entries; priority list attempted; fix attempted with some documentation
D Checklist partially completed; some bug entries present; evidence of engaging with received feedback
E Tested another group's game; at least 1 bug recorded; received feedback noted in notebook
🔒 Teacher Note

Pre-assign testing pairs before class — avoid having best friends test each other's games (feedback tends to be too gentle) and avoid sworn rivals (feedback tends to be too harsh). The checklist export feature generates a plain-text summary students can photograph. During the fix phase (last 20 min), circulate: students who are stuck on Critical bugs need teacher support; students with only Cosmetic issues can use the time for notebook reflection. Save the scene before fixing anything.
