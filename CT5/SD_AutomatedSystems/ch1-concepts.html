What is automation?

From supermarket doors to self-driving cars — automated systems are everywhere. In this chapter you'll learn what makes a system automated, how to tell human-controlled from autonomous, and how Unity lets you simulate both.

1.1 — What makes a system automated?

Machines that make decisions

Every time you walk through a sliding door at a shopping centre, an automated system has already made a decision without you lifting a finger. It detected you, decided you were close enough, and activated the motor — all in a fraction of a second.

That's the essence of automation: a system that senses its environment, processes that information, and acts on it — with little or no human input at the moment of action.

Definition — Mechatronic system

A mechatronic system is one that combines mechanical parts, electronic components, sensors, and software to sense its environment and respond to it. The software (program) controls the electrical system, which in turn controls the mechanical parts. Automated systems are a subset of mechatronics — they follow a programmed sequence of steps to complete an activity, often with little or no human involvement during operation.

Everyday automated systems

You interact with automated systems dozens of times a day without thinking about it. Here are some familiar examples and what each one is actually doing behind the scenes:

Automatic sliding doors PIR sensor detects motion → motor drives door open → timer closes it
Security floodlight Motion triggers light → relay holds it on → countdown resets to off
Robotic vacuum Lidar maps the room → pathfinding algorithm navigates → returns to dock
Traffic lights Timer cycles states → inductive loop sensors adjust timing → pedestrian button interrupts
Dishwasher Temperature + timer → sequential wash/rinse/dry phases → beep when done
Speed camera Radar measures speed → compare to limit → trigger camera + log data if over
1.2 — Human vs autonomous control

The control spectrum

Automated systems don't all work the same way. Some need a human to make every decision — others make all decisions themselves. Most real-world systems sit somewhere in between.

Human-controlled Every action requires a direct human input. The machine only moves when told to. Input.GetAxis()
Semi-autonomous Human sets a goal; the system handles the details. Cruise control, autopilot assist. Input + NavMeshAgent
Fully autonomous No human input during operation. The system senses, decides, and acts on its own. NavMeshAgent only
UNITY NOTE

In Unity you can build both types in the same scene. A player character driven by Input.GetAxis() is fully human-controlled — every keypress causes movement. A NavMeshAgent drone that follows waypoints and uses Physics.Raycast() to detect obstacles is fully autonomous — it needs no player input to function. Placing both in the same scene lets you directly compare the two approaches, which is exactly what the syllabus asks you to do.

1.4 — Social and ethical impacts

Who benefits — and who doesn't?

Every automated system has consequences beyond just the task it performs. When we design or evaluate an automated system, we need to think carefully about who is affected and how.

Positive impacts

  • Increases safety in dangerous environments (mining, fire suppression)
  • Improves accessibility — automatic doors assist wheelchair users
  • Reduces repetitive or physically demanding work
  • Enables 24/7 operation without fatigue

Negative impacts

  • Displaces workers in manufacturing, retail, transport
  • Systems can fail or be hacked, causing harm at scale
  • Automated decisions can embed existing biases
  • High upfront cost limits access for smaller organisations

Ethical questions

  • Who is responsible when an autonomous system makes a mistake?
  • Should a system prioritise speed or safety when they conflict?
  • What data does a system collect — and who owns that data?
  • How do we ensure automated systems are fair to all groups?

Diverse perspectives

  • Older adults may struggle with touchscreen-only interfaces
  • Facial recognition systems have shown racial bias in testing
  • Automated systems on Country may conflict with land rights
  • People with disability may benefit most — or be excluded most
SYLLABUS LINK
