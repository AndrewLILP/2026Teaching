6.5 — Social, ethical & cybersecurity considerations

Who is affected — and how?

The NSW syllabus requires you to evaluate the social, ethical, and cybersecurity dimensions of automated systems — not as a separate topic but as an integral part of system design. The three case studies below connect directly to systems in this unit. Expand each one and discuss the questions in your notebook.

Speed camera data logging Privacy · Data ownership · Consent
▼
Data privacy
Your Grade A speed camera simulation logs every detection event — vehicle position, speed, and timestamp — to a List<string> and displays it on a UI panel in real time. In a real deployment, this data would be stored on government servers, potentially indefinitely. Drivers are rarely informed their speed is being monitored unless they pass a sign. The data could be used for purposes beyond its original intent — traffic analysis, insurance pricing, or law enforcement cross-referencing.
  • Who owns the data collected by a speed camera — the government, the road operator, or the driver whose vehicle is recorded?
  • Should drivers be notified in real time that their data is being collected? What form would that notification take?
  • What data minimisation principle could be applied — i.e. what is the minimum data the system needs to function, and what should be discarded?
  • In your simulation, your detection log is visible on screen to anyone watching. In a real system, who should have access to this data, and what controls would you implement?
Autonomous patrol drone in public space Autonomy · Surveillance · Accountability
▼
Autonomous systems
Your patrol drone detects the player using a cone-of-vision check and logs the detection. In a real deployment — a shopping centre, university campus, or public park — an autonomous drone with camera and detection capability raises significant questions. Its decisions are made by an algorithm written by a developer who may never visit the space it patrols. If it makes an incorrect detection and a person is wrongly flagged, there may be no human in the loop to catch the error.
  • Should members of the public be informed that an autonomous surveillance drone is operating in a space? How and where?
  • Your drone's FOV algorithm makes a yes/no detection decision. What factors could cause false positives — incorrectly flagging an innocent person — and what harm could that cause?
  • If the drone makes a mistake that results in harm, who is accountable — the developer, the company that deployed it, or the operator who approved it?
  • Consider the perspective of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities whose Country a drone might patrol. What specific concerns might arise, and how should they be addressed before deployment?
Conveyor automation displacing workers Economic impact · Accessibility · Transition support
▼
Societal impact
Your conveyor belt quality control system automatically sorts items that were previously inspected by human workers. In a real manufacturing plant, installing such a system might eliminate dozens of inspection roles. These roles are often filled by workers with fewer formal qualifications, including people from culturally and linguistically diverse backgrounds, older workers, and people with disability who found the repetitive work accessible. The business saves money — but the transition impact falls unevenly.
  • Identify at least two groups of workers who might be disproportionately affected by automating the quality control role. Explain why each group is more vulnerable.
  • What responsibilities does a company have to workers whose roles are eliminated by a system it installs? What does "responsible automation" look like?
  • Are there tasks within a modern factory that should deliberately remain human-performed, even if automation is technically possible? Justify your answer.
  • Consider a person with a physical disability who found the inspection role accessible. Could your automated system be designed to create a new accessible role instead of eliminating one? What would that look like?
Cybersecurity in automated systems Attack surface · Data integrity · System safety
▼
Cybersecurity
Your Unity simulation runs on a local machine with no network connection — it has essentially no attack surface. But consider the same system deployed as embedded software in a hospital's automated medication dispensing unit, a water treatment plant's pump controller, or an airport's automated luggage sorting system. These systems are increasingly networked for remote monitoring. A successful cyberattack on an industrial control system can cause physical harm — not just data loss.
  • For your specific simulated system, identify what data it collects or processes. If this were a real networked system, which parts of that data would be most sensitive to unauthorised access or modification?
  • What is the difference between a cyberattack on a personal computer and a cyberattack on an industrial control system? Why is the latter potentially more dangerous?
  • What security principles — data minimisation, access control, encryption at rest, audit logging — would you apply to a real deployment of your system?
  • Your system has a "fail state" — what happens if the software crashes mid-operation? Describe a safe failure mode for your system where the physical outputs default to the safest possible state.
