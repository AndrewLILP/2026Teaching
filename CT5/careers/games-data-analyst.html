Games / Data Analyst

Games generate enormous amounts of data — every click, decision, win and loss is a data point. A games analyst turns that raw information into insights that make games better, fairer and more engaging.

Games industry Enterprise Computing 11–12 Software Engineering 11–12 CT5-DAT-01 · CT5-DAT-02

Game contexts

Where does a games analyst work?

Data analysis appears across every genre. Each game type generates different data — and asks different questions of the analyst.

🎲
Board game
Digital Board Games

Turn-based strategy and property games track every move, landing and transaction. Analysts look for imbalanced rules, dominant strategies and where players lose interest.

e.g. property landing frequency, average game length, turn-by-turn cash flow
🃏
Collectible game
Games with Collectibles

Card and loot-based games generate data on collection rates, card usage, trade volumes and win rates by deck type. Analysts balance the economy and identify unfair combinations.

e.g. card win-rate by rarity, collection completion rate, trade frequency
🚗
Driving simulation
Racing & Driving Sims

Racing games produce spatial and time-series data — lap times, crash locations, brake points and player route choices. Analysts use heatmaps to identify dangerous track sections and tuning the AI difficulty curve.

e.g. lap time distribution, crash heatmap coordinates, AI difficulty win rate
🏢
Business simulation
Business Management Sims

Management sims model complex economic systems — supply chains, staffing, budgets and market forces. Analysts verify that simulated economies stay balanced and that AI competitors behave realistically.

e.g. revenue trend over quarters, staff turnover rate, market share fluctuation

Role overview

What does a games analyst actually do?

The role combines data skills with game knowledge. No two days are the same — but these four activities cover the core of the work.

🔍

Collect & clean data

Raw game logs contain errors, duplicates and gaps. Before any analysis can happen, data must be validated and structured. This might mean writing scripts that parse log files or querying a game database.

📊

Visualise trends & outliers

Charts, heatmaps and dashboards turn millions of rows into a single insight. An analyst might produce a bar chart showing which board game properties are landed on most — revealing a game-balance problem invisible to playtesters.

💬

Communicate findings to designers

Data alone does not change a game — insights need to be communicated clearly. Analysts write reports, present dashboards and translate numbers into plain-language recommendations for designers and producers.

⚖️

Monitor ethics & data ownership

Player data is sensitive. Analysts must understand what data can be collected, how it is stored securely, and what players have consented to. This is especially important for games played by minors.

CT5-DAT-01 · CT5-DAT-02

Data types in games

Different questions require different types of data. Understanding the distinction between quantitative, categorical and structured data is fundamental to any analysis role.

Quantitative

Measured numbers

Data that can be counted or measured. Supports calculations like averages, totals and distributions.

Board Number of turns to win
Driving Lap time in seconds
Collect Cards in a player's deck
Biz sim Quarterly revenue ($)
Categorical

Named groups

Data that places items into distinct categories. Cannot be meaningfully averaged — but can be counted and compared.

Board Property colour group landed on
Driving Track section (straight, hairpin, chicane)
Collect Card rarity (common, rare, legendary)
Biz sim Department type (sales, R&D, ops)
Structured

Organised records

Data stored in a consistent format — rows, columns, fields. Structured data can be queried, filtered and joined across multiple tables.

Board Game log: player ID, turn number, tile landed, timestamp
Driving Telemetry: lap, checkpoint, speed, X/Y position
Collect Trade record: card ID, from player, to player, date
Biz sim Finance table: quarter, income, expenses, net

CT5-DAT-02

Choosing a visualisation

The right chart depends on the question being asked — not just the data available. Here are six common visualisation types used in games analysis.

Bar Chart
Comparing categories
"Which property is landed on most often?"
Best for Comparing a value across named categories — property tiles, card types, game modes.
Line Chart
Trends over time
"How does average session length change over the first 30 days?"
Best for Showing how a value changes over time — session length trends, revenue over quarters, player retention over days.
Heatmap
Spatial density
"Where on the track do most crashes occur?"
Best for Spatial game data — crash locations, player movement paths, areas of a map visited most frequently.
Scatter Plot
Relationships between variables
"Does a larger deck size lead to a higher win rate?"
Best for Exploring whether two variables are related — deck size vs win rate, playtime vs in-game revenue spent.
Donut Chart
Part-to-whole proportions
"What proportion of players own each rarity tier?"
Best for Showing how a whole is divided — rarity tier ownership, market share in a business sim, player type breakdown.
Histogram
Distribution of values
"How are game completion times distributed across all players?"
Best for Understanding the shape of a dataset — are most players slow or fast? Is the difficulty curve creating a spike of very short completions?

CT5-SAF-01 · Ethics & data ownership

Data ethics in games analytics

Collecting player data comes with serious responsibilities. These four principles sit at the core of ethical games analysis — and are increasingly enforced by law.

🔒 Privacy

Players have a right to know what data is collected about them. Analysts work within privacy policies and legal frameworks — including Australia's Privacy Act — that restrict what can be recorded, stored and shared.

A driving sim that records exact route choices must inform players this data is logged and explain why.

✅ Consent

Data collection requires informed consent. Players must actively agree to tracking, and consent must be meaningful — not buried in terms-of-service fine print. Games aimed at children require parental consent.

A collectibles game collecting trading behaviour from under-13 players must obtain verifiable parental consent.

⚠️ Bias in data

Datasets reflect the players who generated them. If early access players are mostly experienced gamers, difficulty data may not reflect the experience of casual players. Analysts must consider who is represented in the data — and who is not.

A board game balanced using data from competitive players may be frustrating for families playing casually.

🛡 Security & data ownership

Player data stored on servers is a target for breaches. Analysts must understand how data is encrypted in transit and at rest, who has access to it, and how long it is retained. Players should be able to request deletion of their data.

A business simulation storing player financial decisions must encrypt that data and not sell it to third parties without consent.

Course performance descriptors

These descriptors describe typical achievement at each grade level as it relates to data analysis in a games context.

Extensive understanding
Selects and applies safe, secure and ethical practices in the use of data. Skilfully selects relevant data, media and processes to effectively communicate information.
"I selected a histogram to show lap time distribution and explained why it revealed a difficulty spike invisible in an average — then discussed how the telemetry data was anonymised before analysis."
B
Thorough understanding
Selects and applies safe, secure and ethical practices in the use of data. Selects relevant data, media and processes to communicate appropriate information.
"I used a bar chart to compare landing frequencies across board game tiles and explained that the data was collected from 50 playtests without storing player names."
C
Sound understanding
Applies safe, secure and ethical practices in the use of data. Selects appropriate data, media and processes to communicate information.
"I made a bar chart showing which tiles were landed on most. The data was collected from our class playtests and did not include personal information."
D
Basic understanding
Uses data safely and responsibly. Uses data to communicate basic information.
"I counted how many times each tile was landed on and put it in a chart. I did not use anyone's personal information."
E
Identifies
Works safely with data. Uses data to communicate information in a very limited way.
"I counted the landings on a table. I kept the data safe."

Your notebook captures thinking in progress — not polished final answers. Honest reflection and genuine curiosity count for more than correct-sounding summaries. Pick one prompt per tier or work through all three.

Foundation Getting started
Name two types of data that could be collected from a board game you have played. What would each data point tell you about how the game is going?

Why might a company want to know how long players take to finish a level? Who benefits from that information?
Core Going deeper
Imagine you collected lap time data from a driving simulation played by your class. Which visualisation type would best show the spread of times, and why? Sketch it in your notebook.
A business management simulation tracks every financial decision a player makes. What data security practices should the developer put in place? Refer to at least two principles from the ethics section.
Explain how bias could enter a dataset collected from a collectibles game where most early testers were experienced card game players. How might this affect decisions made from that data?
Extension Thinking critically
Design a simple data collection plan for your own game project. Include: what data you would collect, how it would be structured, where it would be stored, and what question each data point would help answer.
Compare the data needs of a board game analyst and a driving simulation analyst. How do the questions they ask differ, and what does this tell us about the relationship between game genre and data strategy?
A games company wants to use player behaviour data to automatically adjust difficulty in real time. Evaluate this proposal — consider the technical requirements (CT5-DAT-01/02), the ethical implications (CT5-SAF-01), and whether you think it is a good idea.
