Your Project
Notebook

This notebook documents how you investigated, modelled, and evaluated a real-world network — whether that's a social network mapped with graph theory, or an infrastructure network designed for security and access control. Connections are everywhere; this project makes them visible and analysable.

⬤ Nodes / People ⬤ Edges / Connections ⬤ Paths / Flow ⬤ Security / Vulnerabilities
Two pathways Social network OR infrastructure network — your choice

Pathway A — Social Network Modelling: Map and analyse a real social or community network using graph theory. A sociogram of your school year group, a kinship network, an online influencer network, or a collaboration network. Apply nodes, edges, degree, paths, and centrality to reveal patterns invisible to the naked eye.

Pathway B — Infrastructure & Security Network: Design, evaluate, and secure a network for a real or scenario-based organisation. Specify access control, identify vulnerabilities, develop cybersecurity responses, and test your design against realistic threat scenarios.

The prompts in this notebook work for both pathways. Where they differ significantly, each card is labelled. Both pathways require graph theory concepts, both require an ethical lens, and both require you to connect people to systems.

Identifying & Defining
Graph Theory Concepts
Graph theory gives you a precise mathematical language for describing networks. Whether your network is social or technical, these concepts apply — and using them correctly is evidence of computational thinking.
Node / Vertex Edge / Connection Path / Route Degree Directed vs undirected Centrality Vulnerability / Single point of failure
📐 Applying graph theory to your network
All
  • 01What do nodes represent in your network? (People, devices, servers, locations, organisations?) What does it mean for a node to have a high degree in your specific context?
  • 02What do edges represent? (Friendships, messages, data flows, physical cables, access permissions?) Are your edges directed (one-way) or undirected (mutual)? Why?
  • 03Calculate or estimate the degree of at least three nodes in your network. What does each degree value tell you about that node's role?
  • 04Identify at least one path between two non-adjacent nodes. How many edges does it traverse? Is there a shorter path? What does this path represent in the real world?
  • 05Is your network connected? Can you reach every node from every other node? If not, what does the disconnected portion represent?
Graph theory concepts — reference
⬆ To reach Grade B–A: don't just define the terms — apply them to your actual network. "Node D has degree 3 because it connects to B, E, and F, making it the hub of the right-hand cluster" is application. "Degree means the number of connections" is just definition.
write your graph theory analysis in your notebook document — include annotated diagrams
📊 Centrality & influence
All
  • 01Which nodes have the highest degree in your network? In a social network these are likely connectors or influencers. In an infrastructure network these may be central switches or servers. What does their high degree mean for the network's function?
  • 02What happens if the highest-degree node is removed? Does the network stay connected? Does it split into isolated clusters? This is the concept of a single point of failure — describe what that would mean in your real-world context.
  • 03Are there any bridge edges? An edge whose removal would disconnect the network entirely? Identify at least one and explain its real-world significance.
⬆ To reach Grade A: the single-point-of-failure analysis connects graph theory directly to real-world consequences. "If node D (the school's main switch) fails, nodes E and F (student classrooms) lose all connectivity to the internet" is systems thinking applied to graph theory.

Ethics, Privacy & Diverse Perspectives
Networks connect people — and that means they carry social and ethical weight. This section is not separate from your technical work; it shapes every decision about what data you collect, whose connections you map, and how you present what you find.
👥 Whose network is it?
All
  • 01Who are the people in your network? If you are mapping a social network, consider the diversity of the group — how are people of different cultural backgrounds, genders, ages, and abilities represented? Does your model reflect that diversity, or does it flatten it?
  • 02The syllabus specifically asks you to consider Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples' perspectives on networks. Kinship networks are among the oldest and most sophisticated social network structures in the world. How does this context inform how you think about connection, community, and belonging in your own network?
  • 03Do the people in your network know they are being modelled? If you collected connection data from real people, did you have their informed consent? What are your obligations to the people your nodes represent?
⬆ To reach Grade A: engage with the ATSI perspective genuinely — not as a checkbox. "Kinship networks in Aboriginal communities encode obligations, responsibilities, and relationships across generations — a structure that modern social network theory can describe but not fully capture" is a meaningful connection.
write your response in your notebook document
🔐 Privacy, data ownership & cybersecurity
All
  • 01What data does your project collect about people or systems? Who owns this data? How will it be stored, protected, and eventually deleted?
  • 02What are the risks of your network model or security plan being misused? A sociogram that identifies social isolates could be used harmfully. A security audit that identifies vulnerabilities could be exploited if it fell into the wrong hands. How will you protect your work?
  • 03What cybersecurity principles apply to your network? Consider confidentiality (who can see what), integrity (can data be tampered with), and availability (what happens if the network goes down). How do these apply to your specific network context?
⬆ To reach Grade B–A: security and privacy are not afterthoughts in this focus area — they are core content. Show that your ethical thinking is built into your design from the start, not added at the end.

Researching & Planning
Security & Access Control
Every network — social or technical — has security considerations. This section goes deeper for infrastructure network projects, but social network students should not skip it: the data about connections is itself sensitive.
🛡️ Threat identification
All
  • 01What are the threats to your network? For infrastructure networks: unauthorised access, data interception, denial of service, malware. For social networks: data harvesting, impersonation, manipulation of influence, exposure of private connections.
  • 02Which nodes or edges are most vulnerable? Use your graph theory analysis — high-degree nodes and bridge edges are often the highest-value targets for both attackers and malicious actors.
  • 03For each threat, rate its likelihood and impact. Which threats are your design must address, and which are acceptable risks given your scope?
⬆ To reach Grade B–A: connect your threat analysis directly to your graph theory findings. "Node D has degree 3, making it the hub of the network — a DDoS attack on this node would isolate clusters E and F entirely" shows integrated systems thinking.
write your threat analysis in your notebook document
🔑 Access control design
All
  • 01Who needs access to what in your network? Define at least three user roles and describe what each role can see, do, and modify. (e.g. Student, Teacher, Administrator for a school network; Follower, Collaborator, Owner for a social network model.)
  • 02Apply the principle of least privilege. Each user should only have access to what they actually need — no more. Review your role list and check whether any role has broader access than necessary.
  • 03How is identity verified in your network? What authentication method is appropriate for each user role? (Password, multi-factor, certificate-based, biometric?)
  • 04What audit trail does your network keep? How would you know if unauthorised access occurred?
Example — access control matrix
// Role        | Student data | Network config | Audit logs
Student      | Own only      | None           | None
Teacher      | Class only    | None           | None
IT Admin     | None          | Full           | Full
Principal    | Aggregate     | None           | Read only

// Each role has the minimum access needed

Final Evaluation
