// EIS: Designing for User Experience · Stage 5

Your Project
Notebook

This notebook documents how you designed a UI, interactive media product, or UX improvement for a real audience. Every decision you make — about layout, colour, wording, interactions, and accessibility — should be justified against the four design qualities that run through this entire project.

Functionality
Does it do what it needs to do?
The product performs all required tasks reliably. Features work as expected, inputs produce correct outputs, and nothing breaks during normal use.
Accessibility
Can everyone use it?
The product can be used by people with a range of abilities, devices, and contexts. It meets accessibility standards and doesn't exclude users by design.
Usability
Is it easy to use well?
Users can achieve their goals efficiently and without frustration. Navigation is intuitive, feedback is clear, and errors are recoverable.
Aesthetics
Does it look and feel right?
The visual design is appropriate for the audience and purpose. Colour, typography, spacing, and motion work together to create a coherent, engaging experience.
Two pathways Design something new OR redesign something existing — your choice

Pathway A — New product: Design and build a UI or interactive media product from scratch for a real audience. This could be a web interface, a mobile app screen set, an interactive video, an information dashboard, or any other interactive product.

Pathway B — UX redesign: Research and document the UX failures of an existing product, then design, prototype, and test improvements. You are not building a full working version — you are producing a researched design proposal with prototypes and usability evidence.

Both pathways require the same notebook evidence: user research, design decisions justified against all four qualities, prototype iterations, and usability testing. The prompts work for both. Where they differ, they are labelled.

You are proposing a UI or UX project you could build. Your notebook covers all four D&P phases. In Producing & Implementing, you complete design and tool tutorials on SEQTA, create low-fidelity prototypes, and develop a full project management plan.

Identifying & Defining
Problem & Audience
Good UX design starts with a clear problem and a specific person to solve it for. Vague problems produce vague designs. The more precisely you define your audience and their need, the more every design decision has somewhere to anchor.
🎯 The problem or need
All
  • 01State your pathway. Are you designing something new or redesigning an existing product? Name the product or the problem space precisely.
  • 02Describe the problem or need your product addresses. Not "I want to make an app" — but "people who [do X] currently experience [frustration Y] because existing products [fail at Z]."
  • 03Why is a UX or UI solution the right response? What does good design make possible here that couldn't be achieved another way?
  • 04How has this type of interface or interactive media evolved over time? Identify at least two key shifts — from command-line to graphical interfaces, from desktop to mobile, from static to interactive media. How do these shifts inform your own design context?
⬆ To reach Grade B–A: your problem statement should be specific enough that someone could test whether your design solves it. "Better UX" is not a problem. "New users cannot find the save function without help in under 2 minutes" is.
write your response in your notebook document
👤 Audience & diverse users
All
  • 01Describe your primary user. Age, context of use, technical experience, goals, and frustrations. The more specific, the more useful — a design for "everyone" serves no one well.
  • 02Describe at least one secondary user — someone who will use the product but with different needs, abilities, or context. (e.g. an older user, someone using a screen reader, someone with low bandwidth, someone using a keyboard instead of a mouse.)
  • 03What assumptions have you made about your users that you still need to verify? List them — these become your user research priorities.
  • 04What would make your product genuinely inaccessible to some users in your audience if you don't design for it? (Colour contrast, font size, keyboard navigation, screen reader support, language complexity.)
⬆ To reach Grade A: Grade A design considers users beyond the default. Designing for a keyboard-only user, a colourblind user, or a first-language speaker of another language requires explicit design decisions — and those decisions often make the product better for everyone.

Identifying & Defining
Requirements & Success Criteria
Requirements translate your audience's needs into testable specifications. Every requirement connects to one or more of the four design qualities — and your success criteria are how you'll know whether you've met them.
Functionality requirements
What must the product do? List specific features, interactions, and behaviours.
Accessibility requirements
What standards must it meet? Who must be able to use it, and how?
Usability requirements
How easy must it be to use? Define task completion targets and error tolerance.
Aesthetic requirements
What should it look and feel like? Describe the visual register, tone, and emotional response it should evoke.
📋 Requirements across all four qualities
All
  • 01Write at least two requirements for each of the four design qualities. Use the framework above as your structure. For accessibility, reference WCAG 2.1 AA as a standard where relevant.
  • 02Write 4–6 success criteria — specific, testable statements a user could verify. (e.g. "A new user can complete the main task in under 3 steps without instructions." "All text has a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 against its background.")
  • 03Which requirements are must-haves for your MVP, and which are stretch goals? Mark them clearly.
⬆ To reach Grade C and above: every requirement must be testable. "The design looks good" is not a requirement. "The colour palette uses a minimum 3:1 contrast ratio for all UI elements" is. Vague requirements produce untestable evaluations.
write your requirements table in your notebook document — organised by the four design qualities

  • 03What tools will you use to build or prototype your product? Justify your choice — are they appropriate for the platform and your skill level?

Identifying & Defining
Legal, Ethical & Social Considerations
UI and interactive media products have significant legal and ethical responsibilities — especially around copyright, privacy, and the influence designs can have on people's behaviour and wellbeing.
⚖️ Copyright & intellectual property
All
  • 01What assets will your product use — images, fonts, icons, audio, video, code libraries? For each, note the source and the licence that allows you to use it.
  • 02What is the difference between fair use, Creative Commons, and commercial licences in the context of your product? Which applies to the assets you've chosen?
  • 03If you are redesigning an existing product (Pathway B), what are the intellectual property implications of reproducing or adapting elements of its interface?
write your response in your notebook document
🔐 Privacy & data responsibilities
All
  • 01Does your product collect any data from users? (Form inputs, interaction tracking, stored preferences, location, analytics.) For each type, explain what it's used for and how it's protected.
  • 02What would users expect to happen to their data? Is that expectation clearly communicated in your UI? Where?
  • 03What privacy laws or guidelines apply to your product in an Australian context? (Australian Privacy Act, Children's Online Privacy, school data governance.)
write your response in your notebook document
🌍 Social impact & diverse representation
All
  • 01Who is represented in your product's imagery, language, and examples? Consider gender, cultural background, age, ability, and socioeconomic context. Does the design include or exclude any group by default?
  • 02Could your design nudge users towards harmful behaviour? (Dark patterns, addictive scroll mechanics, misleading calls to action, hidden unsubscribe options.) How have you designed against this?
  • 03How does your product affect users' digital wellbeing? Does the experience leave them feeling empowered or anxious? Does it respect their time and attention?
⬆ To reach Grade A: ethical design is not just compliance — it's a set of positive choices. "We chose not to use autoplay because it removes user agency" and "we use plain language throughout because our users include EAL/D students" are the kind of deliberate choices that constitute Grade A design thinking.

Researching & Planning
Existing Product Analysis
Every design decision has been made before. Analysing existing products reveals what works, what fails, and what your users already know how to do — all of which shapes what you should build.
🔍 Evaluate an existing product against all four qualities
All
  • 01Choose an existing product — for Pathway A, find something in your space. For Pathway B, this is the product you're redesigning. Include screenshots.
  • 02Evaluate it against each of the four design qualities:
    • Functionality: Does every feature work as expected?
    • Accessibility: Who is excluded? What standard does it fail?
    • Usability: How many steps to complete the core task? Where do users get stuck?
    • Aesthetics: Is the visual language consistent, appropriate, and clear?
  • 03What will you borrow, improve, or avoid? For each decision, name the quality it connects to.
⬆ To reach Grade B–A: your evaluation should connect to your own design. "This product fails WCAG AA contrast on its primary button — my design will use a 5:1 ratio minimum to ensure visibility for low-vision users" is analysis. "The colours are hard to see" is observation.
write your evaluation with annotated screenshots in your notebook document
🧩 Design pattern library
All

UI design patterns are reusable solutions to common interaction problems — navigation menus, form validation, modal dialogs, progress indicators, card layouts. Collecting them before you design prevents you from reinventing what already works.

  • 01Identify 4–6 design patterns your product will use. For each: name it, show an example from an existing product, and explain why it suits your context.
  • 02Note any patterns you considered but rejected. What made them unsuitable for your audience or platform?
include screenshots and annotations in your notebook document
Researching & Planning
User Research
You are not the user. User research replaces your assumptions with evidence. Even a small amount of real user data — three people thinking aloud while using a product — is more valuable than a designer's best guess.
🗣️ User interviews or observations
All
  • 01Talk to or observe at least two potential users. Ask them about their current experience with the product or problem space — not about your proposed solution. Summarise what you heard.
  • 02What did your users say that surprised you? What assumptions did you have that turned out to be wrong?
  • 03What are the top 3 user needs your research revealed? State each one as a need, not a feature. (e.g. "Users need to find their most recent entry without scrolling" — not "add a search bar.")
⬆ To reach Grade B–A: user research that changes your design is stronger evidence than research that confirms what you already planned. If your research didn't change anything, it either wasn't deep enough or you're not being honest about what it found.
write your research findings in your notebook document — include quotes or observation notes
🗺️ User journey map
All
  • 01Map the user journey for your product's core task — from first arrival to task completion. Show every step, decision point, and what the user feels at each stage.
  • 02Mark the pain points — moments where users are most likely to feel confused, frustrated, or lost. These are your highest-priority design problems.
  • 03Include error paths. What happens when the user does something unexpected? Does the journey still recover gracefully?
include your user journey map as an image in your notebook document
👤 Persona
All
  • 01Create a user persona based on your research — not a fictional character, but a composite of real patterns you observed. Include: role/context, goals, frustrations, technical confidence, and accessibility needs.
  • 02When you face a design decision, ask: "What would this persona do here?" Use this persona as a design test throughout your project — reference it in your design rationale.
⬆ To reach Grade B–A: a persona is a tool, not a decoration. If it never appears again after you create it, it wasn't useful. Show that your persona shaped specific design decisions — "I chose a large tap target here because my persona uses a phone one-handed on the bus" is how a persona earns its place.
include your persona as a styled document or image in your notebook
Researching & Planning
Design & Prototyping
Designing is iterative. Your first sketch is a hypothesis. Your prototype tests it. Every iteration should be justified against the four design qualities — and the reason you moved from one version to the next should be explicit.
✏️ Wireframes — low fidelity
All
  • 01Draw low-fidelity wireframes for every screen or view in your product. These are boxes and labels — no colour, no final fonts. Include them as images in your notebook document.
  • 02Create at least two alternative layouts for your most important screen. Annotate the trade-offs: which arrangement better supports usability? Which better supports your aesthetic goals?
  • 03Annotate every element — what it does, what the user does with it, and which design quality it primarily serves.
include annotated wireframes as images in your notebook document
🎨 Visual design system
All
  • 01Define your colour palette — name each colour, give its hex value, describe its role (background, primary action, error state, text). Check every text/background combination for WCAG AA compliance (4.5:1 for body text, 3:1 for large text).
  • 02Choose your typography. Name your typeface(s), define size and weight for each text role (heading, body, label, caption), and justify why these choices suit your audience and platform.
  • 03Define your spacing and layout rules. What is your base spacing unit? How does the layout adapt to different screen sizes?
  • 04Create a style tile or design reference sheet showing your colour, type, and key UI element styles together. Include it in your notebook document.
⬆ To reach Grade A: a visual design system that demonstrates creativity and innovation doesn't mean unusual for its own sake. It means every choice — colour, type, spacing — is deliberate, consistent, and justified in terms of your audience and purpose. Randomness is not creativity.
include your style tile in your notebook document
🖥️ High-fidelity prototype
All
  • 01Produce a high-fidelity prototype — either a clickable mockup (using a design tool) or a working implementation of your core screens. Include screenshots or a link in your notebook document.
  • 02For each screen, write a design rationale note — 2–4 sentences explaining the key choices and which design quality each decision serves. This is the evidence your evaluation will reference.
  • 03Compare your prototype to your wireframes. What changed and why? Were changes driven by user feedback, technical constraints, or a better idea?
📚Check SEQTA for prototyping tool guides and design system resources.
include screenshots and design rationale notes in your notebook document
♿ Accessibility audit
All
  • 01Run an accessibility audit on your prototype. Check: colour contrast ratios for all text, keyboard navigability (can you tab through the interface?), meaningful alt text for all images, appropriate heading hierarchy, sufficient touch target sizes.
  • 02What did you find? List every accessibility issue discovered, note its severity (critical / major / minor), and describe the fix you applied.
  • 03What accessibility issues remain after your fixes? Are they acceptable given your scope, or do they need to be addressed before the product is considered complete?
⬆ To reach Grade B–A: an accessibility audit that finds nothing is suspicious. Real products almost always have accessibility issues at first. Finding them and fixing them — with documented evidence — is stronger than claiming your design is already accessible.
write your accessibility audit findings and fixes in your notebook document
Researching & Planning
Project Plan
UX projects are non-linear — you iterate between design and testing, and findings from one stage often send you back to revise an earlier one. Your plan should reflect this, not pretend the process is a straight line.
📅 Sprint breakdown
All
  • 01Break your project into 3–5 sprints. A typical UX sequence: (1) User research + problem definition, (2) Wireframes + visual design system, (3) High-fidelity prototype, (4) Usability testing + iteration, (5) Final product + evaluation.
  • 02What is the exit condition for each sprint? Name something concrete — a completed wireframe set, a style tile, a prototype with 3 tested screens.
  • 03Where have you built in iteration time? Design without testing is guessing. Show where in your plan you have space to revise based on what testing reveals.
include your sprint plan as a table in your notebook document
🛠️ Tools & assets
All
  • 01Name your design and build tools. (Wireframing, prototyping, image editing, coding, video editing.) For each, note why it suits your platform and your skill level.
  • 02List the assets you need to source. For each: the type (image, icon, font, sound), the source, and the licence that allows you to use it.

Product Documentation
Product documentation is a record of your design decisions — the choices you made, the alternatives you considered, and the reasoning behind each one. It is your strongest evidence of design thinking.
Year 10 only — build this section throughout production, not all at once at the end.
📄 Design rationale — screen by screen
Y10
  • 01For each screen or major component, include a screenshot and write a design rationale: what key decisions were made, which quality each decision addresses, and what alternatives you rejected.
  • 02Use the four pillars as headers within each rationale: Functionality / Accessibility / Usability / Aesthetics. For each, write 1–2 sentences connecting the screen's design to the relevant requirement.

Testing & Evaluating
User Testing
User testing means watching real people use your product — not asking them if they like it. The goal is to observe what they do, not hear what they say they would do.
🧪 Usability test sessions
All
  • 01Run at least two usability test sessions with real users — ideally people who match your primary persona. Give each tester a specific task to complete without your guidance.
  • 02Observe and record. Note exactly what each tester does — where they click, where they hesitate, where they express confusion. Do not explain or help during the test.
  • 03After each session, identify: what worked well (the tester completed the task easily), what failed (the tester couldn't complete the task or needed help), and what surprised you.
⬆ To reach Grade B–A: the most valuable user testing observation is a user doing something you didn't anticipate. "The tester clicked the logo to go home, but my logo wasn't linked — they had no way back" is stronger evidence than "the tester said they liked the colours."
write your usability test observations in your notebook document — one entry per session

Testing & Evaluating
Final Evaluation
A final evaluation of a UX project is a structured account of how your product performs against all four design qualities — with evidence from user testing, accessibility audits, and your own design decisions.
📊 Against all four design qualities
All
  • 01Evaluate your product against each of the four design qualities in turn. For each, state your judgement and cite specific evidence — a test result, an audit finding, a user quote, or a screenshot.
  • 02Which quality was hardest to achieve? What made it difficult — was it a technical constraint, a skill gap, or a genuine tension between qualities (e.g. aesthetics choices that reduced accessibility)?
  • 03Were legal and ethical responsibilities met? Are all assets properly licensed? Is user data handled appropriately? Does the design avoid exclusion and dark patterns?
⬆ To reach Grade A: evaluating all four qualities with specific evidence — not just "the design looks good" but "the contrast audit showed all text met WCAG AA, confirmed by the test table in section T&E" — is what the "selects relevant data, media and processes to effectively communicate" CPD descriptor looks like in a UX project.

  • 03What did you learn about the design process — not just about the tools, but about how to think about a user's experience from the outside?

How to use this page

Click a grade card to select your target. For this focus area, your strongest evidence lives in your design rationale, user research, accessibility audit, and usability test results. All four design qualities — functionality, accessibility, usability, and aesthetics — must be evidenced for Grade B and above. A beautiful product with no usability evidence is not a Grade A submission.

Functionality evidence
Requirements test table, working prototype, feature checklist. Can you point to each functional requirement being met?
Accessibility evidence
Accessibility audit with contrast ratios, keyboard test results, alt text review. Does your WCAG compliance have documented proof?
Usability evidence
Usability test observations, task completion data, iteration log. Did real users test it, and did you change things as a result?
Aesthetics evidence
Style tile, design rationale notes, before/after screenshots. Are your visual choices justified for your audience and purpose?
