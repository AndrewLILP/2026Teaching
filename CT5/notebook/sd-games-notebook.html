Pseudocode & Algorithms
Write the logic before you write the code. Pseudocode lets you think through your algorithms in plain language — before you get tangled in syntax.
📝 Core game loop pseudocode
All
  • 01Write pseudocode for your game loop — the code that runs every frame or on every player action. Use NSW pseudocode conventions.
  • 02Trace through your pseudocode with a specific example. What happens when a player presses jump? When they collect an item? When health reaches zero?
NSW Pseudocode — example structure
// Game loop — runs every frame
BEGIN GameLoop
  IF playerInput = "jump" THEN
    applyForce(player, upward)
  END IF
  checkCollisions(player, platforms)
  updateScore()
  IF player.health ≤ 0 THEN
    triggerGameOver()
  END IF
END GameLoop
⬆ To reach Grade B–A: your pseudocode should be technically precise enough that someone else could implement it. Include variables, conditions, and loops — not just vague descriptions.

Producing & Implementing
Project Management
Planning a project well is a skill in itself. This section asks you to use project management tools to show that your proposal is realistic, sequenced, and ready to build.
Year 9 — plan your project as if you are about to build it next term.
Year 10 — your project plan is in Researching & Planning. Use sprint logs to track progress.
🗺️ User story map
Y9

A user story map organises what your game needs to do from the player's point of view. It helps you see the whole project before you start, and identify what must be built first.

  • 01Write user stories for your game using the format: As a [player], I want to [do something] so that [I get some outcome]. Aim for 8–12 stories covering the core experience.
  • 02Arrange your stories into a map — group related stories into activities (e.g. "moving around", "collecting items", "winning and losing"), then order activities left-to-right by when a player encounters them in the game.
  • 03Draw a horizontal line across your map to separate the MVP (minimum viable product — the smallest version that works) from stretch goals. What must be in the first release?
  • 04Include your user story map as an image in your notebook document — drawn by hand, on a sticky note tool, or in a digital format.
⬆ To reach Grade B–A: your user stories should be written from the player's perspective, not the developer's. "As a player, I want clear feedback when I take damage" is stronger than "add a health bar."
include your user story map as an image in your notebook document
📌 Kanban board
Y9

A Kanban board visualises your work as cards moving through columns — typically To Do → In Progress → Done. It helps you see your workload and avoid starting too many things at once.

  • 01Create a Kanban board for your proposed project. Use the columns: Backlog / To Do / In Progress / Done. Each card should be one specific task (not "make the game").
  • 02Populate the Backlog with all the tasks from your user story map. Break large stories into smaller, buildable tasks.
  • 03Move the first sprint's tasks to To Do. What would you work on in your first week of building?
  • 04Include a screenshot of your board in your notebook document. Note what tool you used and why.
Tools you could use

Trello, GitHub Projects, Notion, a physical board with sticky notes, or a table in your notebook document. The tool doesn't matter — the thinking does.

include a screenshot of your Kanban board in your notebook document
🏃 Scrum board & sprint planning
Y9

Scrum is an iterative framework for managing project work in short cycles called sprints. A Scrum board is like a Kanban board, but the columns reset with each sprint and you commit to a fixed amount of work per cycle.

  • 01Plan three sprints for your proposed project. For each sprint, define: the sprint goal (what the player should be able to do at the end), and the specific tasks you would commit to completing.
  • 02How long is each sprint? Justify the length — a sprint that's too short produces nothing useful; a sprint that's too long loses focus.
  • 03What is the definition of "done" for each sprint? Write a clear, testable statement for when the sprint is complete.
  • 04What would you do in a sprint review? Describe what you would demonstrate and who you would show it to.
⬆ To reach Grade B–A: each sprint should end with something a player can actually interact with — not just code that compiles. The iterative process means testing and refining, not building everything then testing at the end.
write your sprint plan as a table in your notebook document
📊 Gantt chart
Y9

A Gantt chart maps tasks against time, showing when each task starts, how long it takes, and whether tasks depend on each other. It gives you (and your teacher) a clear picture of whether your project is realistic.

  • 01Create a Gantt chart for your proposed project spanning the full production term. Show each major task as a bar across the weeks it would be worked on.
  • 02Show dependencies. Which tasks must be finished before others can start? (e.g. player movement must exist before collision detection can be tested)
  • 03Mark your milestones — the end of each sprint, prototype reviews, and the final submission deadline.
  • 04Include a buffer week. Where in your plan have you left time to catch up if something takes longer than expected?
  • 05Reflect on your chart: Does it look realistic? What is the most time-pressured part of your plan?
Tools you could use

A table in your notebook document, a spreadsheet with colour-coded cells, an online Gantt tool, or draw it by hand. Label weeks clearly and keep it legible.

⬆ To reach Grade B–A: your Gantt chart should show realistic estimates — not every task taking the same amount of time, and not everything finishing a week early. A plan that acknowledges difficulty is more credible than one that pretends everything is easy.
include your Gantt chart as an image or table in your notebook document
