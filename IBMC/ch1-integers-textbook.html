The Dead Sea surface sits 430 m below sea level. Mount Everest's summit is 8,849 m above. A deep-sea anglerfish lives 1,200 m below the surface. Using integers to represent these heights — which is furthest from sea level? What is the difference between the highest and lowest points?

1.1

Comparing and Ordering Integers

Direction, magnitude, number line, < and > symbols, ordering sets

📘 Key Concepts

What is an integer?

Integer: Any whole number, its negative, or zero. The set of integers is written ℤ = {… , −3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3, …}

Integers have two properties that together determine their position on the number line:

Magnitude: The distance of a number from zero. Written as |n|. For example, |−5| = 5 and |3| = 3.
Direction: Whether the integer is positive (to the right of zero) or negative (to the left of zero). Zero has no direction.

The number line

Integers are ordered on a number line. Moving right means increasing in value; moving left means decreasing in value.

Key rule: For any two integers, the one further to the right on the number line is the greater value. This means −1 > −10, even though |−10| > |−1|.
Worked Example 1.1.1 Ordering a set of integers

Order the following integers from least to greatest:   3, −8, 0, −2, 5, −1

1
Identify negatives and positives
Negatives: −8, −2, −1  |  Zero: 0  |  Positives: 3, 5
Negatives are always less than zero; positives are always greater than zero.
2
Order the negatives (greatest magnitude = least value)
−8 < −2 < −1
−8 is furthest left on the number line, so it is the smallest.
3
Order the positives
3 < 5
On the number line, 3 is to the left of 5.
4
Combine: negatives → zero → positives
−8, −2, −1, 0, 3, 5
This is the complete ordering from least to greatest.

🏛 History Lens — Brahmagupta and the Birth of Negative Numbers
🧮

Brahmagupta (598–668 CE)

Brahmasphutasiddhanta, India, 628 CE

For most of human history, mathematicians across the world — from ancient Egypt to Greece and Rome — refused to accept negative numbers. A result below zero was considered "absurd" or "meaningless." You cannot own −3 sheep.

The Indian mathematician and astronomer Brahmagupta was the first to write formal arithmetic rules for negative quantities, framing them as debts (ṛṇa) and positive quantities as fortunes (dhana). His key rules, written in 628 CE: "A debt minus zero is a debt. A fortune minus zero is a fortune. The sum of a debt and a fortune is their difference."

His insight was placing debts and fortunes on the same scale — exactly what the number line does. Even so, European mathematicians resisted for a thousand years: as late as the 17th century, René Descartes called negative solutions "false roots."

🔗 Mathematical Connection

When you place −5 and +3 on a number line and compare them, you are using the same conceptual framework Brahmagupta formalised in 628 CE: both values exist on a single directed scale, with zero as the reference point. Every integer has both a magnitude (distance from zero) and a direction — the two properties Brahmagupta's debt/fortune model embodies.

Adding and Subtracting Integers

Number line movement, directed number sentences, subtracting as adding the opposite

📘 Key Concepts

Adding integers — the number line model

Addition means movement in the direction of the sign. Starting at the first number, move right for a positive addend and left for a negative addend.

Same signs → add magnitudes, keep the sign.
−3 + (−4) = −7    (both negative: add 3 and 4, answer is negative)
Different signs → subtract magnitudes, keep the sign of the larger magnitude.
−7 + 3 = −4    (magnitudes: 7 − 3 = 4; the larger magnitude is 7, which is negative)

Subtracting integers

Subtracting is the same as adding the opposite.
a − b = a + (−b)
For example: 5 − (−3) = 5 + 3 = 8
Two signs together: + (−) → subtract  |  − (−) → add. Think of "opposite of opposite = same direction."

1.3

Multiplying and Dividing Integers

Sign rules derived from patterns, all four operations combined

📘 Key Concepts

Discovering sign rules through patterns

Rather than memorising rules, we can discover them by extending a pattern:

4 × 3 = 12
4 × 2 = 8  (decreasing by 4 each time)
4 × 1 = 4
4 × 0 = 0
4 × (−1) = −4  (the pattern continues)
4 × (−2) = −8
∴  positive × negative = negative

Extending further, (−4) × (−1): if (−4) × 1 = −4, and we decrease the multiplier by 1 each time, the product must increase by 4:

(−4) × 1 = −4  →  (−4) × 0 = 0  →  (−4) × (−1) = +4  ∴ negative × negative = positive

The sign rules

First factorSecond factorResultExample
+++ (positive)3 × 4 = 12
+−− (negative)3 × (−4) = −12
−+− (negative)(−3) × 4 = −12
−−+ (positive)(−3) × (−4) = +12
Division follows the same sign rules as multiplication. Same signs → positive result; different signs → negative result.

Calculate:   (−6) × (−4)   and   (−5) × 3

(−5) × 3:   negative × positive = negative

1.4

Order of Operations with Integers

BODMAS, nested brackets, fraction bars as grouping symbols

📘 Key Concepts

BODMAS — the agreed order

When an expression contains more than one operation, we must follow an agreed order to get a consistent result.

B
Brackets
O
Orders
D
Division
M
Multiplication
A
Addition
S
Subtraction
Division and Multiplication have equal priority — work left to right. Same rule applies for Addition and Subtraction.
Fraction bars act as brackets. Evaluate the numerator and denominator separately before dividing.

Example:   (−6 + 2)/2 = −4/2 = −2
Nested brackets: Evaluate the innermost brackets first, then work outward. Round ( ) → Square [ ] → Curly { }

Same signs: add magnitudes and keep negative.
4

−11 − 1 = −12

Chapter 1 Summary

Key facts and vocabulary

Key Facts

1.1 — Comparing & Ordering

  • Integers: ℤ = {… −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, …}
  • Greater value = further right on number line
  • Magnitude |n| = distance from zero
  • Higher magnitude ≠ higher value (for negatives)

1.2 — Adding & Subtracting

  • Same signs: add magnitudes, keep sign
  • Different signs: subtract magnitudes, take sign of larger
  • a − b = a + (−b): subtracting = adding the opposite
  • −(−b) = +b: opposite of negative is positive

1.3 — Multiplying & Dividing

  • + × + = +,   − × − = +  (same signs → positive)
  • + × − = −,   − × + = −  (different signs → negative)
  • Division follows the same sign rules
  • Even power of negative = positive; odd = negative

1.4 — Order of Operations

  • BODMAS: Brackets → Orders → ÷/× → +/−
  • Division and × have equal priority: left to right
  • Fraction bars act as brackets (group separately)
  • Nested brackets: innermost first

Key Vocabulary

Integer
Any whole number, its negative, or zero (ℤ)
Magnitude
Distance from zero; written |n|
Directed number
A number with both size and direction
Absolute value
The magnitude of a number: |−5| = 5
Additive inverse
The opposite of a number: additive inverse of 3 is −3
BODMAS
Order of operations: Brackets, Orders, ÷, ×, +, −
