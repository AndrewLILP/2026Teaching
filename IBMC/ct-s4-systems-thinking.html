ST1.1What is a System? CT5-EVL-01

A system is a group of interrelated components that work together to achieve a purpose. Understanding something as a system — rather than as isolated parts — is called systems thinking. It is one of the most powerful frameworks in computing, engineering, science, and business.

Inquiry Question

When the internet goes down, your phone, laptop, and smart TV all stop working — even though they're different devices from different manufacturers. What does this tell us about systems and interdependence?

The IPO Model: Inputs, Processes, Outputs

The simplest model of any system describes what goes in, what happens inside, and what comes out.

A feedback loop occurs when a system's output becomes part of its next input — allowing the system to adjust itself. This is what makes digital systems dynamic and responsive rather than static.

Enterprise Information Systems

An enterprise information system (EIS) is a large-scale digital system used by organisations — businesses, schools, hospitals, governments — to manage information and support their operations. Enterprise systems are characterised by:

  • Scale: many users, large volumes of data, multiple interconnected components
  • Integration: different parts of the system share data and processes
  • Reliability: downtime has real consequences — think of a hospital's patient record system
  • Security: sensitive data must be protected from unauthorised access

ST1.2Networks — Structure and Components CT5-DAT-01

A computer network is a system of interconnected devices that communicate and share resources. Networks are the physical and logical infrastructure that makes enterprise information systems possible — without them, all computing would be isolated and local.

Inquiry Question

The internet was designed in the 1960s partly to survive a nuclear attack — by having no single central point that could be destroyed. How does this design principle (decentralisation) still shape the internet today?

Network Topologies

A network topology is the arrangement — physical or logical — of how devices are connected. The choice of topology affects reliability, cost, and performance.

Comparing Topologies

Protocols — The Language of Networks

A protocol is a set of rules that governs how data is transmitted between devices. Without agreed protocols, devices from different manufacturers could not communicate. Protocols define: the format of data packets, how errors are detected, how connections are established and terminated, and how data is routed.

Essential Protocols
ProtocolFull NameWhat it does
TCP/IPTransmission Control Protocol / Internet ProtocolThe foundational protocol suite of the internet. IP handles addressing (where data goes); TCP ensures reliable, ordered delivery by acknowledging received packets.
HTTPHypertext Transfer ProtocolRequests and delivers web pages; defines how browsers and web servers communicate. The "language" of the World Wide Web.
HTTPSHTTP SecureHTTP with encryption (TLS/SSL). The padlock icon in your browser indicates HTTPS — data is encrypted in transit so it can't be intercepted.
DNSDomain Name SystemTranslates human-readable domain names (e.g. google.com) into IP addresses that routers can use. It's the "phone book" of the internet.

SMTP/IMAPSimple Mail Transfer / Internet Message AccessSMTP sends email; IMAP retrieves and manages email stored on a server. Together, they enable email systems.
Wi-Fi (IEEE 802.11)Wireless networking standardDefines wireless LAN communication. Different versions (802.11n, ac, ax/Wi-Fi 6) offer increasing speeds and reliability.

Mathematics Connection — Ratios, Rates, and Network Performance

Network performance is measured using rates (from Chapter 1, Section 1.3). Bandwidth is the maximum data transfer rate (e.g. 100 Mbps). Latency is the time delay for data to travel from source to destination (e.g. 12 ms). Throughput is the actual rate achieved under real conditions — always less than or equal to bandwidth. If a file is 500 MB and your download speed is 25 Mbps, how long does the download take? \(500 \text{ MB} = 4000 \text{ Mb};\; \text{time} = 4000 \div 25 = 160 \text{ seconds}\). This is a direct application of the rate formula \(\text{time} = \text{distance (data)} \div \text{rate (speed)}\) from Maths Ch. 1.

ST1.3Data Storage and Transmission CT5-DAT-01

All digital data — text, images, video, audio — ultimately exists as binary: sequences of 0s and 1s. Understanding how data is stored and transmitted is fundamental to understanding what it means to "send" information across a network, and why security and privacy matter.

Inquiry Question

When you send a message on Instagram, where does the message "go"? Who can see it, and for how long? What would it take for you to truly delete it?

How Data is Represented

Computers store all information in binary — base 2, using only two values: 0 (off) and 1 (on). Each binary digit is called a bit. Eight bits form a byte. All other data types are encoded using agreed standards built on bytes.

Data Storage Units
UnitAbbreviationSizeTypical use
Bitb1 binary digit (0 or 1)Individual on/off signal
ByteB8 bitsOne character of text (ASCII)
KilobyteKB1,024 bytesA short text document
MegabyteMB1,024 KB ≈ 10⁶ bytesA high-quality photo; a short MP3
GigabyteGB1,024 MB ≈ 10⁹ bytesA feature film; phone storage
TerabyteTB1,024 GB ≈ 10¹² bytesEnterprise database; home NAS
PetabytePB1,024 TB ≈ 10¹⁵ bytesFacebook stores ~4 PB/day of new data
Mathematics Connection — Indices and Scientific Notation

Storage units grow by powers of 2: 1 KB = 2¹⁰ bytes = 1,024. 1 MB = 2²⁰. 1 GB = 2³⁰. This is directly the index laws from Maths Ch. 1, Section 1.5. Converting units uses the quotient law: 500 MB ÷ 1,024 = 0.488 GB. Large storage figures are naturally expressed in scientific notation: Facebook's 4 PB/day ≈ 4 × 10¹⁵ bytes. Whenever you see terabytes or petabytes, scientific notation (Maths Ch. 1, Section 1.1) is the most efficient way to express and reason about these quantities.

How Data Travels — Packet Switching

Data sent across a network is not transmitted as one continuous stream. Instead, it is broken into small units called packets. Each packet contains:

  • Header: source IP address, destination IP address, packet sequence number, protocol
  • Payload: a fragment of the actual data being sent
  • Trailer: error-checking information (checksum)

Packets travel independently across the network — different packets from the same message may take different routes, then be reassembled at the destination. This is called packet switching and is the foundation of how the internet works.

Activity 3 — Packet Switching Simulation

As a class, simulate packet switching using index cards:

  1. Write a short message (e.g. "Hello, this is a packet switching demo") and number each word as a packet (P1, P2, P3…).
  2. Three "routers" (students) each receive a subset of packets in random order, via different paths.
  3. The receiver reassembles the packets in order using the sequence numbers.

Encryption — Protecting Data in Transit

Encryption is the process of converting data into an unreadable format (ciphertext) using an algorithm and a key, so that only authorised parties with the correct key can decrypt and read it. It is the primary defence against data interception during transmission.

ST1.4Modelling Networks and Social Connections CT5-DAT-01 · CT5-DAT-02

Social networks — the connections between people, organisations, and ideas — can be modelled mathematically as graphs. A graph consists of nodes (or vertices) representing entities and edges (or links) representing connections. Graph theory gives us precise tools to analyse these structures.

Inquiry Question

The "six degrees of separation" idea suggests any two people in the world are connected by at most six social connections. Is this mathematically plausible? What properties of a social graph would make it true?

Network diagrams are formal tools used by IT professionals to document and communicate the structure of a system. ST1.5Network Security and Responsible Use CT5-SAF-01

Every system connected to a network is a potential target. Security is not a feature that gets added at the end — it must be designed in from the beginning. Understanding threats, defences, and your own ethical responsibilities as a technology user is central to the Computing Technology syllabus.

Inquiry Question

If a company is hacked and your data is stolen, who is responsible — the company, the hackers, the government for not regulating strongly enough, or you for using the service? Can responsibility be distributed across a system?

Defences and Security Measures

Layered Security (Defence in Depth)

No single security measure is sufficient. Effective security uses multiple layers — so if one is breached, others still protect the system.

LayerMeasureWhat it does
PhysicalLocked server rooms, badge access, CCTVPrevents physical access to hardware
NetworkFirewall, VLAN segmentation, intrusion detectionControls and monitors traffic entering and leaving the network

Privacy, Ethics, and the Law

In Australia, the handling of personal data is governed by the Privacy Act 1988 and the Australian Privacy Principles (APPs). These establish rules about: what data can be collected, how it must be stored, who it can be shared with, and individuals' rights to access and correct their own data.

Personally Identifiable Information (PII)

PII is any data that can be used, alone or in combination with other data, to identify a specific individual. Examples:

  • Direct PII: Full name, date of birth, Medicare number, passport number, email address, home address
  • Indirect PII: IP address, device ID, location data, browsing history — alone these may not identify you, but combined with other data often can

Computing Technology students must not collect PII in their projects without considering privacy principles. When designing a system that collects user data, ask: Do we actually need this? How long do we keep it? Who can access it? How is it secured?

Ethical and Legal Responsibilities

Access to computing skills comes with responsibilities. The following are illegal under Australian law (Criminal Code Act 1995 and state equivalents) and are never acceptable in Computing Technology class or otherwise:

  • Accessing a computer system without authorisation ("hacking"), even to "test" security
  • Disrupting a computer service (e.g. DDoS attacks)
  • Creating or distributing malware
  • Collecting or sharing others' personal data without consent
  • Using others' intellectual property without permission

Automation and Its Impacts

Automation is the use of technology to perform tasks previously done by humans, without direct human control for each individual action. In enterprise contexts, automation ranges from simple (a scheduled backup script) to sophisticated (an AI system that approves loan applications).

Systems Thinking is the thread connecting all six sections. Every topic — from network topologies to social graphs to data encryption to automation — is understood as a system: components, connections, behaviours, and consequences. This lens carries forward into Computational Thinking (Chapter CT2) and Design Thinking (Chapter CT3).

Where This Leads — Connections Across the Course

Systems Thinking in this chapter provides the foundation for everything that follows. In Computational Thinking (CT2), you will apply algorithmic logic to automate tasks within the enterprise systems you've studied here. In Designing for User Experience (CT3), you'll use the network infrastructure from this chapter as the delivery mechanism for the interfaces you design. In Analysing Data (CT4), the data flowing through these networks is the raw material for statistical analysis — directly connecting to the Statistics and Probability chapter in Mathematics.
