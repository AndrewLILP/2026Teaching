2.1Length MA4-LEN-C-01

Length is the most fundamental measurement. In Stage 4 we extend from straight-sided shapes to curves, introducing the relationship between a circle's diameter and its circumference — one of the most beautiful constants in mathematics.

Inquiry Question

How do you measure a curve? Why does every circle have the same ratio of circumference to diameter, regardless of its size?

Perimeter of Composite Shapes

A composite shape is formed by combining or cutting two or more basic shapes. To find the perimeter, add only the outer boundary — interior edges that have been joined are not part of the perimeter.

Worked Example 2.1a — Perimeter of a Composite Shape

An L-shaped veranda has the following dimensions (all in metres). Find the perimeter.

1
Find the missing side. The full top edge is 160 m; the short top-right portion is \(160 - 90 = 70\) m.
2
Add all outer edges: \(160 + 70 + 70 + 70 + 90 + 140 = 600\) m

Perimeter = 600 m

Circumference of a Circle

The ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter is always the same, regardless of the circle's size. That constant ratio is pi, written \(\pi \approx 3.14159...\)

$$C = \pi d = 2\pi r$$

where \(d\) is the diameter and \(r\) is the radius (\(r = d/2\)).

About Pi (π)
  • \(\pi\) is an irrational number — its decimal expansion never repeats or terminates.
  • For calculations, use the \(\pi\) button on your calculator unless told to use \(\pi \approx 3.14\).
  • Answers involving \(\pi\) may be left in exact form (e.g. \(12\pi\) cm) or rounded to a specified number of decimal places.
Worked Example 2.1b — Circumference

A circular water tank has a diameter of 3.6 m. Find the circumference, correct to 2 decimal places.

1
Write the formula: \(C = \pi d\)
2
Substitute: \(C = \pi \times 3.6 = 3.6\pi\)
3
Evaluate: \(C \approx 11.31\) m

Circumference ≈ 11.31 m

Worked Example 2.1c — Finding Radius from Circumference

The circumference of a circular running track is 400 m. Find the radius of the track, correct to 1 decimal place.

1
\(C = 2\pi r\), so \(400 = 2\pi r\)
2
\(r = \dfrac{400}{2\pi} = \dfrac{200}{\pi} \approx 63.7\) m

Radius ≈ 63.7 m

Arc Length

An arc is a portion of a circle's circumference. Its length depends on the central angle \(\theta\) (in degrees).

$$\text{Arc length} = \frac{\theta}{360} \times 2\pi r$$
Worked Example 2.1d — Arc Length

A sector has radius 8 cm and central angle 135°. Find the arc length, correct to 2 decimal places.

1
\(\text{Arc length} = \dfrac{135}{360} \times 2\pi \times 8\)
2
\(= \dfrac{3}{8} \times 16\pi = 6\pi \approx 18.85\) cm

Arc length ≈ 18.85 cm

Computing Connection — GPS, Maps, and Great Circle Distance

GPS systems calculate positions on the Earth's surface using arc length on a sphere. The shortest path between two points on Earth follows a great circle — the equivalent of a straight line on a flat surface. Mapping software like Google Maps converts latitude and longitude coordinates into distances using circular arc formulas. The Haversine formula — used in navigation apps — is a direct application of circumference and arc length on a sphere. Every time you get a travel time estimate, arc-length mathematics is running behind the scenes.

2.2Area MA4-ARE-C-01

Area measures the amount of flat surface enclosed by a boundary. All area formulas can be derived from the basic rectangle formula \(A = lb\). Understanding why each formula works — not just memorising it — deepens your ability to apply them in unfamiliar situations.

Inquiry Question

Can every area formula be derived from the rectangle? How are triangles, parallelograms, and circles all connected to the same fundamental idea?

Area Formula Reference
ShapeFormulaVariables
Rectangle / Square\(A = lb\)\(l\) = length, \(b\) = breadth
Triangle\(A = \dfrac{1}{2}bh\)\(b\) = base, \(h\) = perpendicular height
Parallelogram\(A = bh\)\(b\) = base, \(h\) = perpendicular height
Trapezium\(A = \dfrac{1}{2}(a+b)h\)\(a, b\) = parallel sides, \(h\) = height
Rhombus / Kite\(A = \dfrac{1}{2}xy\)\(x, y\) = diagonals
Circle\(A = \pi r^2\)\(r\) = radius
Sector\(A = \dfrac{\theta}{360} \times \pi r^2\)\(\theta\) = central angle in degrees
Common Error — Perpendicular Height

For triangles, parallelograms, and trapeziums, the height must be perpendicular (at 90°) to the base. The slant side length is not the height. Always check that \(h\) is measured at a right angle.

Worked Example 2.2a — Area of a Triangle

A triangular sail has a base of 4.5 m and a perpendicular height of 8 m. Find its area.

1
\(A = \dfrac{1}{2}bh = \dfrac{1}{2} \times 4.5 \times 8\)
2
\(A = \dfrac{1}{2} \times 36 = 18 \text{ m}^2\)

Area = 18 m²

Worked Example 2.2b — Area of a Trapezium

A cross-section of a dam wall is a trapezium with parallel sides of 12 m and 20 m, and a perpendicular height of 9 m. Find its area.

1
\(A = \dfrac{1}{2}(a + b)h = \dfrac{1}{2}(12 + 20) \times 9\)
2
\(A = \dfrac{1}{2} \times 32 \times 9 = 16 \times 9 = 144 \text{ m}^2\)

Area = 144 m²

Worked Example 2.2c — Area of a Circle

A circular pizza has a diameter of 30 cm. Find its area, correct to the nearest cm².

1
Radius: \(r = 30 \div 2 = 15\) cm
2
\(A = \pi r^2 = \pi \times 15^2 = 225\pi\)
3
\(A \approx 706.86 \approx 707 \text{ cm}^2\)

Area ≈ 707 cm²

Area of Composite Shapes

To find the area of a composite shape, either add simpler areas together, or subtract a region that has been removed from a larger shape.

Worked Example 2.2d — Composite Area (Addition and Subtraction)

A rectangular room 9 m × 6 m has a circular skylight of diameter 2 m cut into the ceiling. A painter needs to paint the ceiling. Find the area to be painted.

1
Rectangle area: \(A = 9 \times 6 = 54 \text{ m}^2\)
2
Circle area: \(r = 1\) m, \(A = \pi \times 1^2 = \pi \approx 3.14 \text{ m}^2\)
3
Area to paint: \(54 - \pi \approx 54 - 3.14 = 50.86 \text{ m}^2\)

Area ≈ 50.86 m²

Computing Connection — Pixels, Rasters, and Image Resolution

In digital imaging, a pixel (picture element) is the basic unit of area. A screen with resolution 1920 × 1080 contains 1920 × 1080 = 2,073,600 pixels — that's the screen's total area in pixels. When you resize an image, software uses area scaling: doubling width and height multiplies pixel count by 4 (area scales as the square of the linear dimension). In Computing Technology, when you analyse image data in enterprise systems or write code to process images, you apply area calculations to understand file sizes, scaling, and compression ratios.

2.3Volume and Capacity MA4-VOL-C-01

Volume measures the amount of 3-dimensional space inside a solid object. Capacity refers to the amount of fluid a container can hold — the two concepts are linked through unit conversion.

Inquiry Question

All prism volumes use the same underlying formula. What does this suggest about the relationship between 2D area and 3D volume?

Volume of a Right Prism

A right prism has a uniform cross-section along its entire length. Its volume is always:

$$V = A_{\text{cross-section}} \times h$$

where \(h\) is the length (or height) of the prism measured perpendicular to the cross-section. This works for any prism — rectangular, triangular, trapezoidal, hexagonal, and so on.

Volume Formula Reference
SolidFormula
Rectangular prism (cuboid)\(V = lwh\)
Triangular prism\(V = \dfrac{1}{2}bh_{\triangle} \times l\)
Cylinder\(V = \pi r^2 h\)
Capacity Conversions
  • \(1 \text{ mL} = 1 \text{ cm}^3\)
  • \(1 \text{ L} = 1{,}000 \text{ mL} = 1{,}000 \text{ cm}^3\)
  • \(1 \text{ kL} = 1{,}000 \text{ L} = 1 \text{ m}^3\)
Worked Example 2.3a — Volume of a Rectangular Prism

A fish tank measures 80 cm long, 40 cm wide, and 45 cm high. Find its volume in cm³ and its capacity in litres.

1
\(V = lwh = 80 \times 40 \times 45 = 144{,}000 \text{ cm}^3\)
2
Capacity: \(144{,}000 \text{ cm}^3 \div 1{,}000 = 144 \text{ L}\)

Volume = 144,000 cm³ = 144 L

Worked Example 2.3b — Volume of a Triangular Prism

A triangular prism-shaped tent has a triangular cross-section with base 3 m and height 2.4 m. The tent is 4.5 m long. Find its volume.

1
Cross-section area: \(A = \dfrac{1}{2} \times 3 \times 2.4 = 3.6 \text{ m}^2\)
2
\(V = A \times l = 3.6 \times 4.5 = 16.2 \text{ m}^3\)

Volume = 16.2 m³

Worked Example 2.3c — Volume of a Cylinder

A cylindrical rainwater tank has an internal diameter of 1.8 m and a height of 2.5 m. How many litres of water can it hold? (1 m³ = 1 kL)

1
Radius: \(r = 1.8 \div 2 = 0.9\) m
2
\(V = \pi r^2 h = \pi \times 0.9^2 \times 2.5 = \pi \times 0.81 \times 2.5 = 2.025\pi \approx 6.362 \text{ m}^3\)
3
Capacity: \(6.362 \text{ m}^3 = 6.362 \text{ kL} = 6{,}362 \text{ L}\)

The tank holds approximately 6,362 litres

Worked Example 2.3d — Finding a Dimension from Volume

A rectangular swimming pool holds 225 kL of water. The pool is 15 m long and 6 m wide. Find its depth.

1
Convert capacity: \(225 \text{ kL} = 225 \text{ m}^3\)
2
\(V = lwh \Rightarrow 225 = 15 \times 6 \times h = 90h\)
3
\(h = \dfrac{225}{90} = 2.5\) m

Depth = 2.5 m

Computing Connection — Voxels, 3D Modelling, and Rendering

In computing, voxels (volumetric pixels) are the 3D equivalent of pixels — tiny cubes used to represent 3D objects in games, medical imaging, and simulation software. A 3D model on a screen is built from thousands or millions of prisms and cylinders. Volume calculations are used in game physics engines to determine collision detection (does Object A overlap Volume B?), in 3D printing slicers to calculate material usage, and in medical CT scans where each scan slice is a cross-sectional area multiplied by slice thickness to give volume — exactly \(V = A \times h\).

2.4Angle Relationships MA4-ANG-C-01

Angles are everywhere — in the design of buildings, in navigation, in the articulation of robot arms, and in the field of view of a camera lens. Understanding how angles relate to each other is essential for both geometry and practical problem-solving.

Inquiry Question

Why do the angle relationships of parallel lines hold true regardless of how far apart the lines are or how steeply the transversal crosses them?

Angle Types and Basic Relationships

Angle Relationships — Definitions and Rules
RelationshipRuleNotes
Complementary anglesSum to 90°"C" for Corner (right angle)
Supplementary anglesSum to 180°"S" for Straight line
Angles at a pointSum to 360°Full revolution
Vertically oppositeEqualFormed by two intersecting lines
Adjacent angles on a straight lineSum to 180°Also called supplementary
Worked Example 2.4a — Finding Unknown Angles

Two lines intersect. One of the four angles formed is 67°. Find the other three angles, giving reasons.

1
The angle vertically opposite to 67° is also 67° (vertically opposite angles are equal).
2
Each angle adjacent to 67° is on a straight line with it: \(180° - 67° = 113°\) (angles on a straight line are supplementary).
3
The two 113° angles are also vertically opposite to each other ✓

The four angles are: 67°, 113°, 67°, 113°

Parallel Lines and Transversals

When a transversal crosses two parallel lines, it creates 8 angles. These angles have specific relationships that can be used to find unknown angles and to prove that lines are parallel.

Angle Pairs — Parallel Lines and Transversal
NamePositionRelationshipMemory Hint
CorrespondingSame position at each parallel line (F-shape)EqualF angles
AlternateOn opposite sides of the transversal, between the parallels (Z-shape)EqualZ angles
Co-interior (same-side interior)On same side of transversal, between the parallels (C-shape)Supplementary (sum to 180°)C angles
Always State Your Reason

In geometry, every statement must be justified. Writing "corresponding angles, parallel lines" is not optional — it is what distinguishes a mathematical argument from a guess. Examiners specifically look for geometric reasons at Stage 4 and beyond.

Worked Example 2.4b — Parallel Lines with a Transversal

Lines \(AB\) and \(CD\) are parallel. A transversal crosses \(AB\) at \(P\) and \(CD\) at \(Q\). The angle \(APQ = 124°\). Find all other angles at \(P\) and \(Q\), giving reasons.

1
\(\angle BPQ = 180° - 124° = 56°\) (angles on a straight line, supplementary)
2
\(\angle APR = 56°\) where \(R\) is vertically opposite to \(\angle BPQ\) (vertically opposite angles)
3
\(\angle PQD = 124°\) (corresponding angles, \(AB \parallel CD\))
4
\(\angle PQC = 56°\) (alternate angles, \(AB \parallel CD\)) — or supplementary to 124°
5
Co-interior check: \(\angle BPQ + \angle PQD = 56° + 124° = 180°\) ✓ (co-interior angles, parallel lines)
Worked Example 2.4c — Multi-Step Angle Problem

In the diagram, \(PQ \parallel RS\). Find the value of \(x\).

The angle at PQ is \((3x + 10)°\) and the angle at RS on the same side of the transversal is \((5x - 30)°\). These are co-interior angles.

1
Co-interior angles sum to 180° (parallel lines): \((3x + 10) + (5x - 30) = 180\)
2
\(8x - 20 = 180\)
3
\(8x = 200\), so \(x = 25\)

Check: \(3(25)+10 = 85°\) and \(5(25)-30 = 95°\). Sum = 180° ✓

Computing Connection — Rotation, Graphics, and Robotics

Angles are the foundation of computer graphics transformations. Every time an object rotates on screen, the CPU calculates the new position of each vertex using rotation matrices — which are built from trigonometric functions of angles. In robotics programming, joint angles determine the position of a robot arm — this is called forward kinematics. Parallel line relationships appear in image recognition algorithms that detect vanishing points to understand perspective in photographs. The alternate and corresponding angle rules you learn here are used to calibrate camera geometry in machine vision systems.

2.5Properties of Geometrical Figures MA4-GEO-C-01

Geometry is the study of shape, size, and the properties of figures. In Stage 4 we classify and analyse triangles and quadrilaterals by their properties — and use those properties to find unknown angles and lengths.

Inquiry Question

Why is the angle sum of every triangle always 180°? Is there a simple, convincing argument — not just a formula to remember?

Triangles

Types of Triangles
ClassificationBy sidesBy angles
EquilateralAll sides equalAll angles 60°
IsoscelesTwo sides equalBase angles equal (angles opposite equal sides)
ScaleneNo sides equalNo angles equal
Right-angledAny side lengthsOne angle exactly 90°
ObtuseAny side lengthsOne angle > 90°
AcuteAny side lengthsAll angles < 90°
Triangle Angle Properties
  • Angle sum of a triangle: The three interior angles always sum to 180°.
  • Exterior angle theorem: An exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two non-adjacent (remote) interior angles.
  • Isosceles triangle: Base angles (angles opposite the equal sides) are equal.
  • Equilateral triangle: Each interior angle is 60°.
Worked Example 2.5a — Angle Sum and Isosceles Triangle

In triangle \(ABC\), the triangle is isosceles with \(AB = AC\). Angle \(A = 48°\). Find angles \(B\) and \(C\).

1
Since \(AB = AC\), angles opposite these equal sides are equal: \(\angle B = \angle C\) (base angles of isosceles triangle).
2
\(\angle A + \angle B + \angle C = 180°\) (angle sum of triangle)
3
\(48° + 2\angle B = 180°\), so \(2\angle B = 132°\), giving \(\angle B = \angle C = 66°\)

∠B = ∠C = 66°

Worked Example 2.5b — Exterior Angle Theorem

In triangle \(PQR\), \(\angle P = 53°\) and \(\angle Q = 68°\). The exterior angle at \(R\) is formed by extending side \(QR\). Find the exterior angle at \(R\).

1
Method 1 — Exterior angle theorem: Exterior angle = sum of non-adjacent interior angles = \(53° + 68° = 121°\)
2
Method 2 — verify: Interior angle at \(R = 180° - 53° - 68° = 59°\). Exterior angle = \(180° - 59° = 121°\) ✓

Exterior angle at R = 121°

Worked Example 2.5c — Properties of a Parallelogram

In parallelogram \(ABCD\), angle \(A = 112°\). Find angles \(B\), \(C\), and \(D\).

1
\(\angle C = \angle A = 112°\) (opposite angles of a parallelogram are equal)
2
\(\angle B + \angle A = 180°\) (co-interior angles, parallel sides \(AB \parallel DC\)): \(\angle B = 180° - 112° = 68°\)
3
\(\angle D = \angle B = 68°\) (opposite angles equal)

Check: 112° + 68° + 112° + 68° = 360° ✓

Worked Example 2.5d — Multi-Step: Triangle and Parallel Lines

In the diagram, \(XY \parallel PQ\). At point \(A\) on \(XY\), angle \(XAB = 55°\). At point \(B\) (between the parallel lines), angle \(ABP = 40°\). Find angle \(ABX\) and angle \(XAB\) + angle \(ABP\), and hence find angle \(AXB\) if triangle \(ABX\) is formed.

1
Draw a line through \(B\) parallel to \(XY\) and \(PQ\). Call the angle on the \(XY\) side \(\alpha\) and on the \(PQ\) side \(\beta\).
2
\(\alpha = 55°\) (alternate angles, \(XY \parallel\) new line through \(B\))
3
\(\beta = 40°\) (alternate angles, \(PQ \parallel\) new line through \(B\))
4
Angle \(ABX = \alpha + \beta = 55° + 40° = 95°\) — this is the classic "Z-angle split" technique.
Computing Connection — Computational Geometry and Collision Detection

Video games rely on computational geometry — the mathematical study of geometric objects and their relationships. Detecting whether two game characters collide requires checking if geometric shapes overlap. The simplest approach uses bounding boxes (rectangles) or bounding triangles, exploiting the properties of these shapes you have learned here. More complex collision detection uses convex polygon decomposition: breaking any shape into triangles (using the properties of diagonals of quadrilaterals) to make the maths tractable at game-speed. Properties of parallelograms also appear in vector graphics, where paths are defined by control points that form implicit parallelogram structures.

2.6Pythagoras' Theorem MA4-PYT-C-01

Pythagoras' Theorem is one of the oldest and most widely used results in all of mathematics. It connects the three sides of any right-angled triangle in a relationship that has been independently discovered by civilisations across the world — Babylonian, Egyptian, Chinese, and Greek.

Inquiry Question

Pythagoras' Theorem has hundreds of known proofs. What does this say about mathematical truth? Can a result be "true" in more than one way?

Pythagoras' Theorem

In any right-angled triangle, the square of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle) equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides.

$$c^2 = a^2 + b^2$$

where \(c\) is the hypotenuse and \(a\), \(b\) are the two shorter sides (legs).

  • The hypotenuse is always the longest side and is always opposite the right angle.
  • When finding a shorter side: rearrange to \(a^2 = c^2 - b^2\).
  • Answers are often irrational — leave as a surd (e.g. \(\sqrt{50}\)) or round as instructed.
Worked Example 2.6a — Finding the Hypotenuse

Find the hypotenuse of a right-angled triangle with legs of 9 cm and 12 cm.

1
\(c^2 = a^2 + b^2 = 9^2 + 12^2 = 81 + 144 = 225\)
2
\(c = \sqrt{225} = 15\) cm

Hypotenuse = 15 cm — this is a Pythagorean triple: 9, 12, 15 (a multiple of 3, 4, 5)

Worked Example 2.6b — Finding a Shorter Side

A right-angled triangle has hypotenuse 17 m and one leg 8 m. Find the other leg, correct to 2 decimal places.

1
\(c^2 = a^2 + b^2 \Rightarrow 17^2 = 8^2 + b^2\)
2
\(289 = 64 + b^2 \Rightarrow b^2 = 225\)
3
\(b = \sqrt{225} = 15\) m

The other leg = 15 m — this is the 8, 15, 17 triple.

Worked Example 2.6c — Real-World Application

A 6.5 m ladder leans against a vertical wall. The foot of the ladder is 2.5 m from the base of the wall. How high up the wall does the ladder reach? Give your answer correct to 2 decimal places.

1
The ladder, wall, and ground form a right-angled triangle. The ladder is the hypotenuse (\(c = 6.5\) m). One leg is the distance from the wall (\(a = 2.5\) m). Find the height \(b\).
2
\(b^2 = c^2 - a^2 = 6.5^2 - 2.5^2 = 42.25 - 6.25 = 36\)
3
\(b = \sqrt{36} = 6\) m

The ladder reaches 6 m up the wall.

Worked Example 2.6d — Finding the Diagonal of a Rectangle

A television screen is described as 65 inches diagonal. If the screen is 56.7 inches wide, find its height to 1 decimal place. (This is how TV screen heights are actually calculated by manufacturers.)

1
The diagonal is the hypotenuse: \(c = 65\), \(a = 56.7\)
2
\(b^2 = 65^2 - 56.7^2 = 4225 - 3214.89 = 1010.11\)
3
\(b = \sqrt{1010.11} \approx 31.8\) inches

Screen height ≈ 31.8 inches

Converse of Pythagoras' Theorem

The converse of Pythagoras' Theorem allows us to test whether a triangle is right-angled: if \(c^2 = a^2 + b^2\), then the triangle must be right-angled.

Worked Example 2.6e — Is This a Right-Angled Triangle?

A triangle has sides 7 cm, 24 cm, and 25 cm. Is it right-angled?

1
Check if the largest side squared equals the sum of squares of the other two. Largest side: 25 cm.
2
\(25^2 = 625\) and \(7^2 + 24^2 = 49 + 576 = 625\) ✓

Computing Connection — Distance Formula, Game Physics, and GPS

Pythagoras' Theorem is the foundation of the distance formula used in every 2D and 3D computing application. The distance between two points \((x_1, y_1)\) and \((x_2, y_2)\) in a coordinate system is \(d = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2}\) — a direct application of Pythagoras. In game development, this formula runs millions of times per second to calculate enemy proximity, bullet trajectories, and audio attenuation (sound gets quieter with distance). In GPS systems, 3D Pythagoras (\(d = \sqrt{\Delta x^2 + \Delta y^2 + \Delta z^2}\)) is used to determine distances to satellites, and the intersection of multiple such distances gives your position. In machine learning, the Euclidean distance (Pythagorean distance) between data points is used in clustering and classification algorithms like k-nearest neighbours.

Cross-Strand Connection — Algebra and Measurement

Every measurement formula in this chapter is an algebraic relationship. When you rearrange \(C = 2\pi r\) to find \(r\), or solve \(V = \pi r^2 h\) for \(h\), you are applying the equation-solving skills from Chapter 1 (Section 1.6). The two strands are deeply connected: algebra gives you the tools to work with the formulas that measurement provides. In Stage 5, these connections deepen as you apply surds (from index work) and trigonometry (from right-angled triangles) to increasingly complex measurement contexts.
