1.1Representing Numbers MA4-RN-C-01

Every number can be expressed in multiple forms. Understanding how to move fluently between these forms — and to operate with numbers expressed in different ways — is a core mathematical skill that underpins everything in this chapter.

Inquiry Question

Why does the same quantity need different representations? When is a fraction more useful than a decimal? When is scientific notation more useful than a standard number?

Integers and Directed Numbers

An integer is a whole number — positive, negative, or zero. The set of integers is written as \(\{..., -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, ...\}\).

Directed numbers (or signed numbers) include positive and negative values. We use them whenever quantities can be above or below a reference point — temperature above and below 0°C, profit and loss, altitude above and below sea level.

Worked Example 1.1a — Directed Number Operations

The temperature in Thredbo at 6 am was −8°C. By midday it had risen by 15°C, then fallen by 3°C during the afternoon. What was the final temperature?

1
Start: \(-8°C\)
2
Rise of 15°C: \(-8 + 15 = 7°C\)
3
Fall of 3°C: \(7 - 3 = 4°C\)

Final temperature: 4°C

Order of Operations (BODMAS)

When a calculation involves multiple operations, the order in which we perform them matters. We follow BODMAS:

BODMAS

BBracketsFirst

DDivisionThird (left to right)

AAdditionFourth (left to right)
SSubtractionFourth (left to right)
Worked Example 1.1b — Order of Operations

Evaluate: \(3 + 4 \times (2^3 - 5) \div 6\)

1
Brackets: \(2^3 - 5\). First evaluate the order inside: \(2^3 = 8\). Then \(8 - 5 = 3\).
2
Multiplication/Division (left to right): \(4 \times 3 = 12\), then \(12 \div 6 = 2\).
3
Addition: \(3 + 2 = 5\)

Answer: 5

Fractions, Decimals, and Percentages

These three forms represent the same quantities in different ways. Fluent conversion between them is essential.

Conversion Pathways
From → ToMethodExample
Fraction → DecimalDivide numerator by denominator\(\frac{3}{8} = 3 \div 8 = 0.375\)
Decimal → FractionUse place value, then simplify\(0.6 = \frac{6}{10} = \frac{3}{5}\)
Fraction → PercentageMultiply by 100\(\frac{3}{8} \times 100 = 37.5\%\)
Percentage → DecimalDivide by 100\(37.5\% = 0.375\)
Decimal → PercentageMultiply by 100\(0.375 \times 100 = 37.5\%\)
Worked Example 1.1c — Converting Between Forms

In the 2023 AFL season, a team won 14 of their 22 games. Express this as a fraction, decimal, and percentage.

1
Fraction: \(\dfrac{14}{22} = \dfrac{7}{11}\) (dividing by HCF of 2)
2
Decimal: \(14 \div 22 \approx 0.636\) (to 3 d.p.)
3
Percentage: \(0.636... \times 100 \approx 63.6\%\)

Answer: \(\dfrac{7}{11} \approx 0.636 \approx 63.6\%\)

Scientific Notation

Scientific notation (also called standard form) expresses very large or very small numbers in the form \(a \times 10^n\) where \(1 \leq a < 10\) and \(n\) is an integer.

Key Rules for Scientific Notation
  • \(a \times 10^n\) where \(1 \leq a < 10\)
  • Positive \(n\): large numbers (move decimal right to return to standard form)
  • Negative \(n\): small numbers (move decimal left to return to standard form)
  • Example: \(384{,}400{,}000 \text{ m} = 3.844 \times 10^8 \text{ m}\) (Earth to Moon)
  • Example: \(0.000{,}000{,}1 \text{ m} = 1 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}\) (diameter of a human cell)
Worked Example 1.1d — Scientific Notation

Australia's GDP in 2023 was approximately $2,222,000,000,000. Write this in scientific notation.

1
Identify the first significant digit: 2. Place the decimal after it: \(2.222\)
2
Count how many places the decimal moved from its original position: 12 places.
3
Write: \(2.222 \times 10^{12}\)

Answer: \(\$2.222 \times 10^{12}\)

Computing Connection — Data Types and Number Representation

Computers store all numbers in binary (base 2) — using only 0s and 1s. The decimal number 13 is stored as 1101 in binary (\(1 \times 2^3 + 1 \times 2^2 + 0 \times 2^1 + 1 \times 2^0\)). Scientific notation is used in computing for floating-point numbers — how computers store decimals. Understanding number representation connects directly to how data is stored and processed in Computing Technology.

1.2Financial Mathematics MA4-FIN-C-01

Understanding money is one of the most immediately practical applications of mathematics. In this section we explore how percentages are used in everyday financial decisions — from shopping to saving.

Inquiry Question

How do businesses, governments, and banks use percentages to manage money? Is a "50% off" sale always the best deal?

Percentages of a Quantity

To find a percentage of a quantity, convert the percentage to a decimal and multiply.

$$\text{Percentage of a quantity} = \frac{\text{percentage}}{100} \times \text{quantity}$$
Worked Example 1.2a — Percentage of a Quantity

A school uniform costs $85. There is a 15% student discount. Find the discount amount and the final price.

1
Discount amount: \(15\% \times \$85 = 0.15 \times 85 = \$12.75\)
2
Final price: \(\$85 - \$12.75 = \$72.25\)

Alternatively: After a 15% discount, you pay 85%: \(0.85 \times \$85 = \$72.25\)

Percentage Increase and Decrease

A percentage increase or percentage decrease expresses change as a percentage of the original value.

$$\text{Percentage change} = \frac{\text{new value} - \text{original value}}{\text{original value}} \times 100\%$$
Multiplier Method

Using a multiplier combines the original value and the change into one step:

  • Increase by 20%: multiply by \(1.20\) (you keep 100% and add 20%)
  • Decrease by 15%: multiply by \(0.85\) (you keep only 85%)
  • To reverse a 20% increase: divide by \(1.20\) (find the original)
Worked Example 1.2b — Percentage Change

The population of a regional NSW town was 12,400 in 2020. By 2023 it had grown to 13,888. Find the percentage increase.

1
Change = \(13{,}888 - 12{,}400 = 1{,}488\)
2
\(\text{Percentage increase} = \dfrac{1{,}488}{12{,}400} \times 100 = 12\%\)

Answer: 12% increase

Goods and Services Tax (GST)

In Australia, GST (Goods and Services Tax) is a 10% tax added to the price of most goods and services. Prices are shown either inclusive or exclusive of GST.

GST Calculations
  • Adding GST: Multiply price by 1.10 (or add 10%)
  • Finding GST content: Divide the GST-inclusive price by 11 (since GST is 1/11 of the inclusive price)
  • Removing GST: Divide by 1.10
Worked Example 1.2c — GST

A plumber charges $660 including GST. How much is the GST, and what was the pre-GST price?

1
GST amount: \(\$660 \div 11 = \$60\)
2
Pre-GST price: \(\$660 - \$60 = \$600\) (or \(\$660 \div 1.10 = \$600\))

GST = $60, Pre-GST price = $600

Profit and Loss

Profit occurs when the selling price is greater than the cost price. Loss occurs when the selling price is less than the cost price.

$$\text{Profit} = \text{Selling Price} - \text{Cost Price}$$ $$\text{Profit \%} = \frac{\text{Profit}}{\text{Cost Price}} \times 100$$
Worked Example 1.2d — Profit and Loss

A surf shop buys a wetsuit for $180 and sells it for $252. Calculate the profit and the percentage profit on cost price.

1
Profit: \(\$252 - \$180 = \$72\)
2
Percentage profit: \(\dfrac{72}{180} \times 100 = 40\%\)

Profit = $72, Percentage profit = 40%

Simple Interest

Simple interest is calculated on the original principal only. It does not compound (interest is not added to the principal for the next period).

$$I = Prn$$
Simple Interest Formula

Where:

  • \(I\) = interest earned (or paid)
  • \(P\) = principal (original amount)
  • \(r\) = interest rate per period (as a decimal, e.g. 6% p.a. = 0.06)
  • \(n\) = number of periods (usually years)

Total amount: \(A = P + I = P(1 + rn)\)

Worked Example 1.2e — Simple Interest

Mia deposits $4,500 in a savings account that earns simple interest at 3.5% per annum. How much interest does she earn over 4 years? What is the total amount in her account?

1
Identify: \(P = 4500\), \(r = 0.035\), \(n = 4\)
2
\(I = Prn = 4500 \times 0.035 \times 4 = \$630\)
3
Total amount: \(A = 4500 + 630 = \$5{,}130\)

Interest = $630, Total = $5,130

Computing Connection — Spreadsheets and Financial Modelling

Financial mathematics is the foundation of spreadsheet software. Functions like PMT(), FV(), and NPV() in Excel or Google Sheets use the same formulas from this section. When you write a simple interest formula as =A1*B1*C1 in a spreadsheet, you are applying \(I = Prn\) programmatically. In Computing Technology, you'll build programs that automate these kinds of calculations.

1.3Ratios and Rates MA4-RAT-C-01

A ratio compares quantities of the same type in the same units. A rate compares quantities of different types. Both are tools for comparing and communicating relationships between quantities.

Inquiry Question

Where do ratios and rates appear in the natural world, in sports, and in technology? How does understanding rates help us make better decisions?

Simplifying Ratios

A ratio is simplified by dividing all parts by their highest common factor (HCF). Both quantities must be in the same unit before simplifying.

Worked Example 1.3a — Simplifying Ratios

Simplify the ratio 45 : 60 : 75.

1
Find HCF(45, 60, 75) = 15
2
Divide each part: \(45 \div 15 = 3\), \(60 \div 15 = 4\), \(75 \div 15 = 5\)

Answer: 3 : 4 : 5

Sharing in a Given Ratio

Worked Example 1.3b — Dividing in a Ratio

Three friends — Anika, Ben, and Carlos — invest in a food truck in the ratio 3 : 2 : 5. After one year, the profit is $48,000. How much does each person receive?

1
Total parts: \(3 + 2 + 5 = 10\)
2
One part = \(\$48{,}000 \div 10 = \$4{,}800\)
3
Anika: \(3 \times \$4{,}800 = \$14{,}400\); Ben: \(2 \times \$4{,}800 = \$9{,}600\); Carlos: \(5 \times \$4{,}800 = \$24{,}000\)

Check: \(14{,}400 + 9{,}600 + 24{,}000 = 48{,}000\) ✓

Rates and Unit Rates

A unit rate expresses a rate with a denominator of 1. This makes comparison straightforward.

Worked Example 1.3c — Unit Rates and Comparison

At a supermarket: Brand A breakfast cereal costs $4.80 for 500 g. Brand B costs $7.00 for 750 g. Which is better value?

1
Brand A: \(\$4.80 \div 500 = \$0.0096\) per gram = 96¢ per 100 g
2
Brand B: \(\$7.00 \div 750 \approx \$0.00933\) per gram = 93.3¢ per 100 g

Brand B is better value (lower price per gram).

Speed, Distance, and Time

Speed is a rate: it compares distance (km) to time (h). The relationship between speed, distance, and time is one of the most important rates in applied mathematics.

$$\text{Speed} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}} \qquad \text{Distance} = \text{Speed} \times \text{Time} \qquad \text{Time} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Speed}}$$
Worked Example 1.3d — Speed, Distance, Time

The XPT train travels from Sydney to Melbourne (878 km) in 10 h 44 min. Find its average speed in km/h.

1
Convert time to hours: \(10 \text{ h } 44 \text{ min} = 10 + \frac{44}{60} = 10.\overline{7}\) h
2
\(\text{Speed} = \dfrac{878}{10.7\overline{3}} \approx 81.8 \text{ km/h}\)

Average speed ≈ 81.8 km/h

Computing Connection — Algorithms and Rates

In computing, rates are everywhere: data transfer speed (megabits per second), processor speed (MHz/GHz), frame rate (frames per second). A key concept in Computing Technology is algorithmic efficiency — measuring how quickly an algorithm solves a problem as input size grows. This is expressed as a rate of change in steps relative to input size (Big-O notation). The unit rate thinking you develop here is exactly what you'll apply when analysing algorithms.

1.4Algebraic Techniques MA4-ALG-C-01

Algebra is the language of mathematics. It allows us to express general rules and relationships using symbols (pronumerals) in place of specific numbers. This generality is what gives algebra its power — the same expression can describe infinitely many situations.

Inquiry Question

If a pattern always works for specific numbers, can we be certain it works for all numbers? How does algebra let us prove a rule is always true?

Algebraic Expressions

An algebraic expression contains one or more terms with pronumerals. Like terms have the same pronumeral(s) raised to the same power(s) and can be combined.

Vocabulary
TermMeaningExample
TermA single quantity (number, pronumeral, or product)\(3x^2\), \(-5y\), \(7\)
CoefficientThe numerical factor of a termIn \(3x^2\), the coefficient is 3
Like termsTerms with identical pronumeral part\(4x\) and \(-2x\) are like terms
ExpressionTerms combined with + or −\(3x^2 - 5y + 7\)
Worked Example 1.4a — Simplifying Expressions

Simplify: \(5x^2 + 3y - 2x^2 + 4x - y + 1\)

1
Group like terms: \((5x^2 - 2x^2) + (3y - y) + 4x + 1\)
2
Simplify each group: \(3x^2 + 2y + 4x + 1\)

Answer: \(3x^2 + 4x + 2y + 1\)

Expanding Brackets

To expand means to remove brackets by multiplying. Each term inside the bracket is multiplied by the term outside.

$$a(b + c) = ab + ac$$
Worked Example 1.4b — Expanding Brackets

Expand and simplify: \(3(2x - 5) - 2(x + 4)\)

1
Expand first bracket: \(3 \times 2x + 3 \times (-5) = 6x - 15\)
2
Expand second bracket: \(-2 \times x + (-2) \times 4 = -2x - 8\)
3
Combine: \(6x - 15 - 2x - 8 = 4x - 23\)

Answer: \(4x - 23\)

Factorising by Common Factor

Factorising is the reverse of expanding. We look for the highest common factor (HCF) of all terms and write it outside the bracket.

Worked Example 1.4c — Factorising

Factorise completely: \(12x^2y - 8xy^2\)

1
Find HCF of \(12x^2y\) and \(8xy^2\): HCF of 12 and 8 is 4; HCF of \(x^2\) and \(x\) is \(x\); HCF of \(y\) and \(y^2\) is \(y\). So HCF = \(4xy\).
2
Divide each term: \(\dfrac{12x^2y}{4xy} = 3x\) and \(\dfrac{8xy^2}{4xy} = 2y\)
3
Write factorised form: \(4xy(3x - 2y)\)

Answer: \(4xy(3x - 2y)\)

Check: expand to verify → \(4xy \times 3x - 4xy \times 2y = 12x^2y - 8xy^2\) ✓

Computing Connection — Variables and Expressions in Code

In programming, a variable plays the same role as a pronumeral in algebra — it stores a value that can change. When you write total = price * quantity in Python, you are coding an algebraic expression. Expanding and factorising expressions helps when optimising code: 4*x*y*(3*x - 2*y) requires fewer calculations than 12*x**2*y - 8*x*y**2 and is more readable — exactly what programmers aim for.

1.5Indices MA4-IND-C-01

Index notation (also called exponential notation) is a shorthand for repeated multiplication. It appears in science, computing, and finance.

$$a^n = \underbrace{a \times a \times a \times \cdots \times a}_{n \text{ factors}}$$

Here \(a\) is the base and \(n\) is the index (or exponent or power).

Index Laws
LawRuleExample
Product Law\(a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}\)\(x^3 \times x^4 = x^7\)
Quotient Law\(a^m \div a^n = a^{m-n}\)\(x^7 \div x^3 = x^4\)
Power Law\((a^m)^n = a^{mn}\)\((x^3)^4 = x^{12}\)
Power of a Product\((ab)^n = a^n b^n\)\((2x)^3 = 8x^3\)
Zero Index\(a^0 = 1 \quad (a \neq 0)\)\(7^0 = 1\)
Negative Index\(a^{-n} = \dfrac{1}{a^n}\)\(x^{-3} = \dfrac{1}{x^3}\)
Worked Example 1.5a — Applying Index Laws

Simplify: \(\dfrac{(2x^3)^2 \times x^4}{4x^5}\)

1
Power of a product: \((2x^3)^2 = 2^2 \times x^{3 \times 2} = 4x^6\)
2
Product law in numerator: \(4x^6 \times x^4 = 4x^{10}\)
3
Divide: \(\dfrac{4x^{10}}{4x^5} = x^{10-5} = x^5\)

Answer: \(x^5\)

Worked Example 1.5b — Zero and Negative Indices

Evaluate: (a) \(5^0\)    (b) \(2^{-3}\)    (c) \(3 \times 4^{-2}\)

a
\(5^0 = 1\)
b
\(2^{-3} = \dfrac{1}{2^3} = \dfrac{1}{8}\)
c
\(3 \times 4^{-2} = 3 \times \dfrac{1}{16} = \dfrac{3}{16}\)
Computing Connection — Binary, Hexadecimal, and Powers of 2

Computers count in base 2 (binary) — every unit of storage is a power of 2. 1 kilobyte = \(2^{10}\) = 1,024 bytes. 1 megabyte = \(2^{20}\) bytes. 1 gigabyte = \(2^{30}\) bytes. When you see storage advertised as 256 GB, that's \(256 \times 2^{30}\) bytes. Index laws explain why multiplying storage by 4 increases the exponent by 2. Computing Technology uses base-16 (hexadecimal) for memory addresses: each hex digit represents 4 bits (a power of 2).

1.6Equations MA4-EQU-C-01

An equation is a mathematical statement that two expressions are equal. Solving an equation means finding the value(s) of the unknown that make the equation true. We use inverse operations — doing the opposite — to isolate the pronumeral.

Inquiry Question

What kinds of problems in the real world require you to find an unknown value? How do equations give us a systematic way to solve them?

Solving Linear Equations

Golden Rule

Whatever you do to one side of an equation, you must do to the other side. This keeps the equation balanced.

Worked Example 1.6a — Two-Step Equation

Solve: \(5x - 8 = 27\)

1
Add 8 to both sides: \(5x = 35\)
2
Divide both sides by 5: \(x = 7\)

Check: \(5(7) - 8 = 35 - 8 = 27\) ✓

Worked Example 1.6b — Variables on Both Sides

Solve: \(3(2x + 1) = 4x - 7\)

1
Expand: \(6x + 3 = 4x - 7\)
2
Subtract \(4x\) from both sides: \(2x + 3 = -7\)
3
Subtract 3: \(2x = -10\)
4
Divide by 2: \(x = -5\)

Check: LHS = \(3(2(-5)+1) = 3(-9) = -27\); RHS = \(4(-5)-7 = -20-7 = -27\) ✓

Formulas and Substitution

A formula is an equation that describes a relationship between quantities. We substitute known values to find the unknown.

Worked Example 1.6c — Using and Rearranging Formulas

The formula for the perimeter of a rectangle is \(P = 2(l + w)\). A rectangle has perimeter 54 cm and width 9 cm. Find the length.

1
Substitute known values: \(54 = 2(l + 9)\)
2
Divide both sides by 2: \(27 = l + 9\)
3
Subtract 9: \(l = 18 \text{ cm}\)

Check: \(P = 2(18 + 9) = 2 \times 27 = 54\) ✓

Problem Solving with Equations

Worked Example 1.6d — Word Problem

Priya and James together have 85 trading cards. Priya has 15 more than James. How many does each person have?

1
Define variable: Let \(j\) = number of James's cards. Then Priya has \(j + 15\).
2
Write equation: \(j + (j + 15) = 85\) → \(2j + 15 = 85\)
3
Solve: \(2j = 70\), so \(j = 35\)
4
James: 35 cards; Priya: \(35 + 15 = 50\) cards

Check: \(35 + 50 = 85\) ✓

Computing Connection — Equations as Conditionals and Loops

Solving an equation is a form of search: find the value that satisfies the condition. In computing, you might write while x != solution: try_next_value(x) — a loop that searches for a solution. More directly, solving equations is the mathematical foundation of constraint solving, used in logistics software, scheduling algorithms, and artificial intelligence. When a navigation app finds the fastest route, it solves a system of equations (or inequalities) with thousands of variables.

1.7Linear Relationships MA4-LIN-C-01

A linear relationship is one where the change in one variable is proportional to the change in another. When graphed on the Cartesian plane, a linear relationship forms a straight line. Linear relationships model many real-world situations: cost vs. quantity, speed vs. time, conversion between units.

Inquiry Question

When is a relationship between two quantities exactly linear? What does the gradient tell us about how one quantity changes relative to another?

The Cartesian Plane

The Cartesian plane uses two perpendicular axes — the horizontal x-axis and the vertical y-axis — to locate any point using an ordered pair \((x, y)\).

Graphing Linear Relationships

To graph a linear equation, we create a table of values, plot the points, and draw a straight line through them.

Worked Example 1.7a — Graphing a Linear Relationship

Complete a table of values and sketch the graph of \(y = 2x - 1\) for \(x = -2\) to \(x = 3\).

1
\(x\)−2−10123
\(y = 2x-1\)−5−3−1135
2
Plot points: \((-2,-5), (-1,-3), (0,-1), (1,1), (2,3), (3,5)\)
3
Draw a straight line through all points, extending with arrows at each end.

The y-intercept is \(-1\) (where the line crosses the y-axis) and the gradient is \(2\).

Gradient

The gradient (or slope) of a line measures its steepness — how much \(y\) changes for each unit increase in \(x\).

$$m = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$
Interpreting Gradient
GradientMeaning
Positive (\(m > 0\))Line rises from left to right

Gradient-Intercept Form

The most useful form of a linear equation is the gradient-intercept form:

$$y = mx + b$$

where \(m\) is the gradient and \(b\) is the y-intercept (the value of \(y\) when \(x = 0\)).

Worked Example 1.7b — Finding Gradient and y-intercept

A phone plan charges a $25 monthly connection fee plus $0.12 per minute of calls. Write a linear equation and find the gradient and y-intercept. What do they represent?

1
Let \(x\) = minutes used, \(y\) = total cost (\$)
2
\(y = 0.12x + 25\)
3
Gradient \(m = 0.12\): The cost increases by $0.12 for each additional minute.
4
y-intercept \(b = 25\): The monthly connection fee — the cost even if you make no calls.

Context always explains gradient and intercept.

Worked Example 1.7c — Finding the Equation from Two Points

Find the equation of the line passing through \((1, 3)\) and \((4, 9)\).

1
Find gradient: \(m = \dfrac{9-3}{4-1} = \dfrac{6}{3} = 2\)
2
Substitute into \(y = mx + b\): \(3 = 2(1) + b\), so \(b = 1\)
3
Equation: \(y = 2x + 1\)

Check with second point: \(y = 2(4) + 1 = 9\) ✓

Computing Connection — Linear Models and Machine Learning

Linear regression is one of the most important algorithms in data science. It finds the best-fitting straight line through a set of data points — doing exactly what you are doing here, but for hundreds or millions of data points. The equation \(y = mx + b\) becomes a model: given an input \(x\), predict an output \(y\). In Computing Technology, when you analyse data from enterprise information systems, you use linear and non-linear models to identify trends. The gradient \(m\) tells you the rate of change — how much one variable predicts another.
