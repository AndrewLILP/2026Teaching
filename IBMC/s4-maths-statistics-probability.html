3.1Classifying Data MA4-DAT-C-01

Before you can analyse data you must understand what type of data you have. Different types of data require different representations and different methods of analysis. Misidentifying data type is one of the most common sources of misleading statistics.

Inquiry Question

Is a postcode a number? What makes something "numerical" in a statistical sense, and why does the distinction matter when we want to find an average?

The Data Classification Hierarchy
TypeDefinitionExamplesCan you average it?
Categorical — NominalNamed groups with no natural orderEye colour, favourite sport, blood type, postcodeNo — mode only
Categorical — OrdinalNamed groups with a natural orderSurvey ratings (Strongly Agree → Disagree), school grades (A, B, C…), finishing positionMedian or mode; mean is debatable
Numerical — DiscreteCounted values; only specific values possible (usually whole numbers)Number of siblings, goals scored, shoe sizeYes — mean, median, mode
Numerical — ContinuousMeasured values; any value in a range is possibleHeight, mass, temperature, time, rainfallYes — mean, median, mode
Worked Example 3.1a — Classifying Variables

Classify each variable and justify your classification:

(a) The time (in seconds) a student holds their breath    (b) The number of text messages sent in a day    (c) A student's house colour (red, blue, green, yellow)    (d) A restaurant's star rating (1–5 stars)

a
Numerical — continuous. Time is measured and can take any value (e.g. 43.7 s, 43.71 s…).
b
Numerical — discrete. Text messages are counted; you can't send 12.3 messages.
c
Categorical — nominal. The house colours are named groups with no natural order; red is not "greater than" blue.
d
Categorical — ordinal. The ratings have a natural order (5 stars is better than 1 star) but the gaps between ratings may not be equal.

Populations and Samples

A population is the entire group being studied. A sample is a subset of the population selected for study. Because studying an entire population is often impractical, we use samples — but samples must be representative (reflecting the diversity of the population) and collected without bias (systematic favouritism).

Sources of Bias in Data Collection
  • Sampling bias: The sample doesn't represent the population (e.g. surveying only Year 7 students about school canteen preferences, then reporting results for the whole school).
  • Response bias: The way a question is worded influences the answer (e.g. "Don't you agree that homework is excessive?" vs "How do you feel about the current amount of homework?").
  • Non-response bias: People who choose not to respond share characteristics (e.g. an online survey only captures people with internet access and interest in responding).
  • Observer effect: People change their behaviour when they know they're being watched or surveyed.
Computing Connection — Databases and Data Types

In Computing Technology, every column in a database table has a data type — INTEGER, FLOAT, VARCHAR (text), BOOLEAN — that mirrors the statistical classification you learn here. Storing "postcode" as an INTEGER would allow meaningless arithmetic (average postcode?). Defining the correct data type is a foundational skill in database design, one of the core topics in enterprise information systems. Data classification also determines which visualisation library functions are appropriate: a bar chart function expects categorical data; a histogram expects numerical continuous data. Mismatching these produces errors or misleading charts.

3.2Collecting and Displaying Data MA4-DAT-C-01

A good data display makes patterns immediately visible. Choosing the wrong type of graph can obscure important information or mislead the reader. In Stage 4 we extend our graphing repertoire to include frequency tables, dot plots, stem-and-leaf plots, and histograms.

Histograms

A histogram displays the frequency of data grouped into class intervals. Unlike a bar chart, there are no gaps between bars (because the data is continuous or grouped), and the horizontal axis is a numerical scale, not discrete categories.

3.3Measures of Centre MA4-DAT-C-01

A measure of centre is a single value that represents the "middle" or "typical" value of a dataset. There are three — mean, median, and mode — and each answers a slightly different question about what is "typical."

Inquiry Question

An Australian mining company has 9 workers earning $60,000 and one CEO earning $1,000,000. The mean salary is $154,000 — but is this a useful or honest summary of what a "typical" worker earns?

The Three Measures of Centre
MeasureDefinitionFormula/MethodBest used when…
Mean (\(\bar{x}\))The arithmetic average — sum divided by count\(\bar{x} = \dfrac{\sum x}{n}\)Data is roughly symmetric with no extreme outliers
MedianThe middle value when data is orderedOrder the data; find the middle value (or average of two middle values for even \(n\))Data is skewed or contains outliers
ModeThe most frequently occurring valueFind the value(s) with highest frequencyCategorical data; most popular item; may be multiple modes
Worked Example 3.3a — Mean, Median, and Mode

The daily maximum temperatures (°C) in Sydney over one week were: 24, 28, 31, 22, 35, 28, 26. Find the mean, median, and mode.

1
Mean: \(\bar{x} = \dfrac{24+28+31+22+35+28+26}{7} = \dfrac{194}{7} \approx 27.7°C\)
2
Median: Order the data: 22, 24, 26, 28, 28, 31, 35. Middle value (4th of 7) = 28°C
3
Mode: 28 appears twice — all other values once. Mode = 28°C

The mean (27.7°C), median (28°C), and mode (28°C) are all similar — the distribution is fairly symmetric. The hot day (35°C) has pulled the mean up slightly.

Worked Example 3.3b — Effect of an Outlier

The weekly sales ($) at a small café are: 3200, 3500, 3100, 3400, 3300, 3250, 12 000. Find the mean and median. Which better represents typical weekly sales?

1
Mean: \(\bar{x} = \dfrac{3200+3500+3100+3400+3300+3250+12000}{7} = \dfrac{31750}{7} \approx \$4536\)
2
Median: Order: 3100, 3200, 3250, 3300, 3400, 3500, 12000. Median = $3300
3
The $12,000 week (perhaps a special event) is an outlier that inflates the mean to $4536 — above every other week's sales. The median ($3300) better represents typical weekly revenue.
Worked Example 3.3c — Finding a Missing Value Given the Mean

The mean of seven values is 14. Six of the values are 12, 18, 9, 15, 11, and 17. Find the seventh value.

1
Sum of all seven values = mean × n = \(14 \times 7 = 98\)
2
Sum of known six values: \(12+18+9+15+11+17 = 82\)
3
Missing value: \(98 - 82 = \mathbf{16}\)
Cross-Strand Connection — Algebra and the Mean

Finding a missing value given the mean is an equation-solving problem (Chapter 1, Section 1.6). The mean formula \(\bar{x} = \dfrac{\sum x}{n}\) is rearranged to \(\sum x = \bar{x} \times n\), then the known values are subtracted. Any time you use a formula to find an unknown, you're applying algebraic thinking to a statistical context.

3.4Measures of Spread and Data Analysis MA4-DAT-C-01

Knowing the centre of a dataset is not enough to describe it fully. Two datasets can have identical means but wildly different spreads. A measure of spread quantifies how much the data varies around the centre.

Inquiry Question

Two exam classes both average 65%. Class A has scores from 60–70; Class B has scores from 20–100. Do these classes need the same teaching response? What does spread tell us that centre alone cannot?

Range

The range is the simplest measure of spread: the difference between the maximum and minimum values.

$$\text{Range} = \text{Maximum} - \text{Minimum}$$

The range is easy to calculate but is highly sensitive to outliers — a single extreme value can make the range appear very large even if most data is tightly clustered.

Interquartile Range (IQR)

The interquartile range (IQR) measures the spread of the middle 50% of data, making it resistant to outliers. To find the IQR, we divide the ordered dataset into four equal quarters using quartiles:

Finding Quartiles and IQR
  1. Order the data from smallest to largest.
  2. Median (Q2): Find the middle value of the entire dataset.
  3. Lower quartile (Q1): Find the median of the lower half of the data (values below Q2).
  4. Upper quartile (Q3): Find the median of the upper half (values above Q2).
  5. IQR = Q3 − Q1
Worked Example 3.4a — Range and IQR

Find the range, quartiles, and IQR for the dataset: 14, 22, 9, 31, 18, 25, 11, 27, 20, 16, 33, 8.

1
Order the data: 8, 9, 11, 14, 16, 18, 20, 22, 25, 27, 31, 33
2
Range: 33 − 8 = 25
3
Median (Q2): \(n = 12\) (even), middle two values are 18 and 20. \(Q2 = \dfrac{18+20}{2} = 19\)
4
Lower half: 8, 9, 11, 14, 16, 18 → \(Q1 = \dfrac{11+14}{2} = 12.5\)
5
Upper half: 20, 22, 25, 27, 31, 33 → \(Q3 = \dfrac{25+27}{2} = 26\)
6
IQR = Q3 − Q1 = 26 − 12.5 = 13.5

Range = 25, IQR = 13.5. The middle 50% of data spans 13.5 units.

Describing and Comparing Distributions

A complete statistical analysis of a dataset should describe its shape, centre, and spread — and identify any outliers.

Distribution Shapes
ShapeDescriptionImplication for measures
Symmetric (bell-shaped)Data balanced around centre; mean ≈ median ≈ modeMean is a reliable measure of centre
Positively skewed (right skew)Tail extends to the right; few high outliers pull the mean up; mean > medianMedian is preferred; mean overestimates typical value
Negatively skewed (left skew)Tail extends to the left; mean < medianMedian is preferred
BimodalTwo distinct peaks — suggests two sub-groups in the dataInvestigate whether the data should be split
Worked Example 3.4b — Comparing Two Datasets

Two classes sit the same test. Class A: mean 72, median 74, IQR 8. Class B: mean 71, median 65, IQR 28. Compare and interpret.

1
Centre: The means are almost identical (72 vs 71). However, Class B's median (65) is 6 marks below its mean (71), suggesting right-skewed data — a few high-scoring students are pulling the mean up. Class A's mean and median are close (72, 74), suggesting roughly symmetric performance.
2
Spread: Class B's IQR is 28 compared to Class A's 8. The middle 50% of Class B students' scores span 28 marks — much more variable. Class A is more consistently performing.
3
Conclusion: Class A performs more consistently around a typical score of ~73. Class B has a wider range of abilities; the median (65) better represents a typical Class B student than the mean (71).
Computing Connection — Descriptive Statistics in Data Science

Every data science library automates what you compute here. In Python: df.describe() returns count, mean, std, min, Q1, median, Q3, and max in one line. In SQL: SELECT PERCENTILE_CONT(0.25) WITHIN GROUP (ORDER BY score) returns Q1. Understanding what these numbers mean — not just how to generate them — is what distinguishes a data scientist from someone who runs code without understanding it. When algorithms like anomaly detection flag outliers in enterprise systems (fraud detection, sensor malfunction), they use IQR-based rules: values below \(Q1 - 1.5 \times IQR\) or above \(Q3 + 1.5 \times IQR\) are flagged as potential outliers.

3.5Introduction to Probability MA4-PRO-C-01

Probability is the mathematics of uncertainty. It allows us to quantify how likely an event is — from the chance of rain, to the likelihood of a particular card being drawn, to the risk of a medical test producing a false result. Every probability is a number between 0 and 1.

Inquiry Question

If you flip a fair coin 10 times and get 10 heads, is a tail "due" on the next flip? Why or why not? What does this tell us about the difference between randomness and pattern?

The Probability Scale

00.250.50.751
ImpossibleUnlikelyEven chanceLikelyCertain
Probability Language and Notation
  • An experiment is a process that produces a result (e.g. rolling a die).
  • An outcome is one possible result (e.g. rolling a 4).
  • The sample space \(S\) is the set of all possible outcomes.
  • An event is a set of one or more outcomes (e.g. "rolling an even number").
  • The probability of event \(A\) is written \(P(A)\), where \(0 \leq P(A) \leq 1\).
  • The complement of \(A\) is "not \(A\)": \(P(\bar{A}) = 1 - P(A)\).
Worked Example 3.5a — Sample Space and Basic Probability

A standard six-sided die is rolled. List the sample space and find: (a) P(rolling a 4), (b) P(rolling an even number), (c) P(rolling a number greater than 4), (d) P(not rolling a 6).

1
Sample space: \(S = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}\), so \(|S| = 6\).
a
\(P(4) = \dfrac{1}{6}\) — only one favourable outcome.
b
\(P(\text{even}) = \dfrac{3}{6} = \dfrac{1}{2}\) — favourable outcomes: {2, 4, 6}.
c
\(P(>4) = \dfrac{2}{6} = \dfrac{1}{3}\) — favourable outcomes: {5, 6}.
d
\(P(\text{not 6}) = 1 - P(6) = 1 - \dfrac{1}{6} = \dfrac{5}{6}\).

Computing Connection — Random Number Generation and Simulation

Computers use pseudo-random number generators (PRNGs) to simulate probability experiments — they're called "pseudo" because they are deterministic algorithms that produce sequences behaving as if random. In Python, random.choice(['H','T']) simulates a coin flip; random.randint(1,6) simulates a die. Monte Carlo simulation — running a probability experiment millions of times computationally — is used to estimate probabilities too complex to calculate exactly. It's used in financial risk modelling, physics simulation, and even in rendering software (ray-tracing in Pixar films uses Monte Carlo methods). The sample spaces you list by hand here become the input sets for these algorithms.

3.6Theoretical and Experimental Probability MA4-PRO-C-01

Theoretical probability is calculated from known, equally likely outcomes. Experimental probability (or relative frequency) is calculated from the results of an actual experiment. The two do not always agree — but the more trials you run, the closer experimental probability gets to theoretical probability. This is called the Law of Large Numbers.

Inquiry Question

If you toss a coin 10 times and get 7 heads, is the coin unfair? How many trials would you need before you could be confident something was wrong?

Theoretical vs Experimental Probability
TheoreticalExperimental
Formula\(P(A) = \dfrac{\text{number of favourable outcomes}}{\text{total equally likely outcomes}}\)\(P(A) \approx \dfrac{\text{frequency of event}}{\text{total number of trials}}\)
Based onLogical reasoning about the structure of the experimentActual observed results
When usedWhen all outcomes are equally likely and knownWhen the theoretical probability is unknown, or to verify theory
Gets more accurate as…— always exact (if model is correct)The number of trials increases
Worked Example 3.6a — Experimental Probability and Relative Frequency

A drawing pin is tossed 200 times. It lands "point up" 86 times and "point down" 114 times. Find the experimental probability of each outcome. Do you expect these to equal the theoretical probability?

1
\(P(\text{point up}) \approx \dfrac{86}{200} = 0.43\)   and   \(P(\text{point down}) \approx \dfrac{114}{200} = 0.57\)
2
Check: \(0.43 + 0.57 = 1.00\) ✓ (all experimental probabilities must sum to 1)
3
A drawing pin is not a fair object — there is no reason to expect the two outcomes to be equally likely. The theoretical probability is unknown; only experimental methods can estimate it. With more trials, the estimate would become more stable.
Worked Example 3.6b — Expected Frequency

A biased coin has P(heads) = 0.6. If the coin is tossed 450 times, what is the expected number of heads? Of tails?

1
Expected frequency = probability × number of trials
2
Expected heads: \(0.6 \times 450 = 270\)
3
Expected tails: \(0.4 \times 450 = 180\)   (or \(450 - 270 = 180\))

Expected: 270 heads, 180 tails — but actual results will vary. These are expected values, not guarantees.

The Gambler's Fallacy

Each trial in a probability experiment is independent — the outcome of one trial does not affect the next. If a fair coin lands on heads 10 times in a row, the probability of tails on the next flip is still exactly \(\dfrac{1}{2}\). The mistaken belief that "tails is due" is called the Gambler's Fallacy. Casino design exploits this misunderstanding deliberately.

Computing Connection — A/B Testing and Probability in Tech

A/B testing is the tech industry's application of experimental probability. When a website shows version A to 50% of visitors and version B to the rest, they are running a probability experiment. The conversion rates (click-throughs, purchases) are experimental probabilities. Data scientists then use statistics to determine whether the difference is statistically significant or due to random variation — exactly the distinction between theoretical and experimental probability you learn here. Every major tech company — Meta, Google, Atlassian — runs thousands of A/B tests simultaneously. Understanding the Law of Large Numbers tells you why you need a large enough sample before drawing conclusions.
