2.1Surface Area of Right Prisms and Cylinders MA5-ARE-C-01

The surface area of a solid is the total area of all its faces. The key strategy is always the same: identify every face, find its area, then add them all together. A net — the 2D shape that folds into the solid — makes this systematic.

Inquiry Question

A tin of paint covers 12 m² per litre. To paint the exterior walls and ceiling of a room (not the floor), how would you calculate the amount needed? What real-world complications arise that a formula doesn't capture?

Surface Area of Prisms

Surface Area Strategy for Any Prism

A prism has two identical bases (top and bottom) and a set of rectangular lateral faces connecting them. For any right prism:

$$SA = 2 \times \text{Base Area} + \text{Perimeter of base} \times \text{height}$$

This formula captures both parts: the two bases and the lateral (side) surface, which can be "unrolled" into a rectangle with width = perimeter of base and height = length of prism.

Worked Example 2.1a — Surface Area of a Triangular Prism

Find the surface area of the triangular prism shown. The triangular base has sides 6 cm, 8 cm, and 10 cm (right angle between the 6 cm and 8 cm sides). The prism is 12 cm long.

1

Area of triangular faces (×2):

$$2 \times \tfrac{1}{2} \times 6 \times 8 = 48 \text{ cm}^2$$
2

Area of rectangular faces (three, one for each side of the triangle):

$$6 \times 12 = 72 \quad 8 \times 12 = 96 \quad 10 \times 12 = 120$$
3

Total surface area:

$$SA = 48 + 72 + 96 + 120 = 336 \text{ cm}^2$$

Surface Area of a Cylinder

Surface Area of a Cylinder
$$SA = 2\pi r^2 + 2\pi r h$$

Two circular bases (\(2\pi r^2\)) + curved surface, which unrolls to a rectangle of width \(2\pi r\) and height \(h\) (\(2\pi r h\))

Worked Example 2.1b — Surface Area of a Cylinder

Find the surface area of a cylinder with radius 4.5 cm and height 10 cm. Give your answer correct to 1 decimal place.

1

Two circular ends: \(2\pi r^2 = 2\pi(4.5)^2 = 2\pi \times 20.25 = 40.5\pi\)

2

Curved surface: \(2\pi r h = 2\pi(4.5)(10) = 90\pi\)

3

\(SA = 40.5\pi + 90\pi = 130.5\pi \approx 410.0 \text{ cm}^2\)

Worked Example 2.1c — Working Backwards: Finding a Dimension

A closed cylindrical can has a surface area of 600 cm² and a radius of 5 cm. Find its height correct to 1 decimal place.

1

Write the formula: \(600 = 2\pi(5)^2 + 2\pi(5)h = 50\pi + 10\pi h\)

2

Solve for \(h\): \(600 - 50\pi = 10\pi h\) $$h = \frac{600 - 50\pi}{10\pi} = \frac{600 - 157.08}{31.416} \approx \frac{442.92}{31.416} \approx 14.1 \text{ cm}$$

2.2Volume of Composite Solids MA5-VOL-C-01

A composite solid is formed by combining two or more simpler solids — or by removing one solid from another. The volume is found by adding or subtracting the volumes of the component parts. Stage 4 covered prisms and cylinders; Stage 5 extends to composite combinations of these.

Volume Formulae — Stage 4 Review
Any right prism
\(V = A_{\text{base}} \times h\)
where \(A_{\text{base}}\) is the area of the cross-section
Cylinder
\(V = \pi r^2 h\)
\(r\) = radius, \(h\) = height
Composite
\(V_{\text{total}} = V_1 + V_2\) or \(V_1 - V_2\)
Add or subtract component volumes
Worked Example 2.2a — Volume of a Composite Solid (Addition)

A solid consists of a cylinder of radius 4 cm and height 8 cm, with a rectangular prism of dimensions 10 cm × 6 cm × 3 cm placed on top. Find the total volume.

1

Volume of cylinder: \(V_1 = \pi r^2 h = \pi(4)^2(8) = 128\pi \approx 402.1 \text{ cm}^3\)

2

Volume of rectangular prism: \(V_2 = 10 \times 6 \times 3 = 180 \text{ cm}^3\)

3

Total: \(V = 128\pi + 180 \approx 402.1 + 180 = 582.1 \text{ cm}^3\)

Worked Example 2.2b — Volume by Subtraction (Hollow Solid)

A concrete pipe (hollow cylinder) has outer radius 30 cm, inner radius 22 cm, and length 2 m. Find the volume of concrete used, in m³, correct to 3 decimal places.

1

Convert all to metres: outer radius = 0.30 m, inner radius = 0.22 m, length = 2 m.

2

Outer cylinder: \(V_{\text{outer}} = \pi(0.30)^2(2) = 0.18\pi \text{ m}^3\)

3

Inner cylinder (the hole): \(V_{\text{inner}} = \pi(0.22)^2(2) = 0.0968\pi \text{ m}^3\)

4

Volume of concrete: \(V = (0.18 - 0.0968)\pi = 0.0832\pi \approx 0.261 \text{ m}^3\)

Computing Connection — 3D Modelling and Voxels

In Chapter CT5 (Advanced Software Development), games and simulations represent 3D worlds as meshes of triangular faces (surface area) containing enclosed space (volume). Modern 3D printing software calculates the exact volume of material needed before printing — using the same composite solid decomposition you apply here. CAD (Computer-Aided Design) packages like AutoCAD use surface area calculations for rendering and materials estimation.

2.3Surface Area and Volume — Pyramids, Cones and Spheres MA5-ARE-P-01 ★ MA5-VOL-P-01 ★

These three solids — pyramid, cone, and sphere — extend the surface area and volume work into curved and pointed shapes. All three Path formulae involve the factor \(\frac{1}{3}\) in their volume formulae, because a pyramid or cone can be thought of as one-third of a prism or cylinder of the same base and height.

Surface Area Formulae — Pyramids, Cones and Spheres
Right Pyramid
\(SA = \text{Base area} + \tfrac{1}{2} \times P \times l\)
\(P\) = perimeter of base, \(l\) = slant height of a triangular face
Cone
\(SA = \pi r^2 + \pi r l\)
\(r\) = base radius, \(l\) = slant height   (Note: \(l = \sqrt{r^2 + h^2}\))
Sphere
\(SA = 4\pi r^2\)
\(r\) = radius
Volume Formulae — Pyramids, Cones and Spheres
Right Pyramid
\(V = \tfrac{1}{3} \times A_{\text{base}} \times h\)
\(h\) = perpendicular height
Cone
\(V = \tfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h\)
\(r\) = base radius, \(h\) = perpendicular height
Sphere
\(V = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3\)
\(r\) = radius
Slant Height vs Perpendicular Height

This is the most common error for cones and pyramids. The perpendicular height \(h\) goes straight down from apex to base. The slant height \(l\) goes along the face from apex to base edge. They are related by Pythagoras: \(l = \sqrt{r^2 + h^2}\) (cone) or \(l = \sqrt{(\tfrac{s}{2})^2 + h^2}\) (square pyramid, where \(s\) = side length). Use \(h\) for volume, use \(l\) for surface area.

Worked Example 2.3a — Square Pyramid: Surface Area and Volume

A square pyramid has a base side of 8 m and a perpendicular height of 6 m. Find its total surface area and volume.

1

Find the slant height using Pythagoras (from centre of base edge to apex):

$$l = \sqrt{\left(\frac{8}{2}\right)^2 + 6^2} = \sqrt{16 + 36} = \sqrt{52} = 2\sqrt{13} \approx 7.21 \text{ m}$$
2

Surface area: base \(+ 4\) triangular faces

$$SA = 8^2 + 4 \times \tfrac{1}{2} \times 8 \times 2\sqrt{13} = 64 + 32\sqrt{13} \approx 64 + 115.4 = 179.4 \text{ m}^2$$
3

Volume: \(V = \tfrac{1}{3} \times 8^2 \times 6 = \tfrac{1}{3} \times 64 \times 6 = 128 \text{ m}^3\)

Worked Example 2.3b — Cone and Sphere

(i) A cone has base radius 5 cm and perpendicular height 12 cm. Find its total surface area and volume.

(ii) A sphere has diameter 14 cm. Find its surface area and volume.

i

Slant height: \(l = \sqrt{5^2 + 12^2} = \sqrt{169} = 13 \text{ cm}\)

$$SA_{\text{cone}} = \pi(5)^2 + \pi(5)(13) = 25\pi + 65\pi = 90\pi \approx 282.7 \text{ cm}^2$$ $$V_{\text{cone}} = \tfrac{1}{3}\pi(5)^2(12) = 100\pi \approx 314.2 \text{ cm}^3$$
ii

Radius: \(r = 7\) cm

$$SA_{\text{sphere}} = 4\pi(7)^2 = 196\pi \approx 615.8 \text{ cm}^2$$ $$V_{\text{sphere}} = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi(7)^3 = \tfrac{1372\pi}{3} \approx 1436.8 \text{ cm}^3$$
Worked Example 2.3c — Composite Solid (Path)

A solid consists of a cylinder of radius 6 cm and height 10 cm, with a hemisphere (half-sphere) of radius 6 cm on top. Find the total volume and the total surface area. Give answers to 1 d.p.

1

Volume:

$$V_{\text{cylinder}} = \pi(6)^2(10) = 360\pi$$ $$V_{\text{hemisphere}} = \tfrac{1}{2} \times \tfrac{4}{3}\pi(6)^3 = \tfrac{2}{3}\pi(216) = 144\pi$$ $$V_{\text{total}} = 360\pi + 144\pi = 504\pi \approx 1583.4 \text{ cm}^3$$
2

Surface area (curved cylinder + circular base + curved hemisphere — the circle where they join is internal, not a surface):

$$SA_{\text{curved cylinder}} = 2\pi(6)(10) = 120\pi$$ $$SA_{\text{base circle}} = \pi(6)^2 = 36\pi$$ $$SA_{\text{curved hemisphere}} = \tfrac{1}{2} \times 4\pi(6)^2 = 72\pi$$ $$SA_{\text{total}} = 120\pi + 36\pi + 72\pi = 228\pi \approx 716.3 \text{ cm}^2$$

2.4Right-Angled Trigonometry MA5-TRG-C-01

Trigonometry links the angles of a right triangle to the ratios of its sides. The three ratios — sine, cosine, and tangent — are defined relative to a chosen angle, and are constant for any given angle regardless of the triangle's size. This makes them powerful tools for finding unknown sides and angles.

The Three Trigonometric Ratios
$$\sin\theta = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Hypotenuse}} \qquad \cos\theta = \frac{\text{Adjacent}}{\text{Hypotenuse}} \qquad \tan\theta = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Adjacent}}$$

To find a side: rearrange the ratio to isolate the unknown side.

To find an angle: use the inverse function: \(\theta = \sin^{-1}\!\left(\frac{O}{H}\right)\) etc. (the \(\sin^{-1}\) button on your calculator, also written \(\arcsin\)).

Exact Values — Must Know
Angle \(\theta\)\(30°\)\(45°\)\(60°\)
\(\sin\theta\)\(\dfrac{1}{2}\)\(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\)\(\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\)
\(\cos\theta\)\(\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\)\(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\)\(\dfrac{1}{2}\)
\(\tan\theta\)\(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{3}\)\(1\)\(\sqrt{3}\)
Worked Example 2.4a — Finding a Side

In triangle \(ABC\), angle \(A = 38°\), side \(AC = 14\) m (\(AC\) is adjacent to angle \(A\)), and the right angle is at \(C\). Find the length of \(BC\) (opposite to angle \(A\)) and \(AB\) (hypotenuse).

1

Find BC (opposite, given adjacent): Use TOA

$$\tan 38° = \frac{BC}{14} \implies BC = 14 \tan 38° \approx 14 \times 0.7813 \approx 10.94 \text{ m}$$
2

Find AB (hypotenuse, given adjacent): Use CAH

$$\cos 38° = \frac{14}{AB} \implies AB = \frac{14}{\cos 38°} \approx \frac{14}{0.7880} \approx 17.77 \text{ m}$$
Worked Example 2.4b — Finding an Angle

A ramp is 5.6 m long and rises 1.8 m vertically. Find the angle the ramp makes with the horizontal, correct to the nearest degree.

1

The ramp is the hypotenuse. The vertical rise is opposite the angle with the horizontal.

$$\sin\theta = \frac{1.8}{5.6} = 0.3214\ldots$$
2

\(\theta = \sin^{-1}(0.3214) \approx 19°\)

Worked Example 2.4c — Using Exact Values

Find the exact value of \(x\) if \(\tan 60° = \dfrac{x}{5}\).

1

\(\tan 60° = \sqrt{3}\), so \(\dfrac{x}{5} = \sqrt{3}\)

2

\(x = 5\sqrt{3}\) (exact answer — do not convert to decimal unless asked)

2.5Applications of Trigonometry MA5-TRG-C-02

Trigonometry is most powerful when applied to real problems. Three key contexts are angles of elevation and depression, compass bearings, and Pythagoras in three dimensions. Each requires careful diagram drawing before applying any formula.

Angles of Elevation and Depression

Key Definitions
  • Angle of elevation: measured upward from the horizontal to a line of sight toward an object above
  • Angle of depression: measured downward from the horizontal to a line of sight toward an object below
  • The angle of elevation from A to B equals the angle of depression from B to A (alternate angles, parallel horizontals)
Worked Example 2.5a — Angle of Elevation

From a point 40 m from the base of a building, the angle of elevation to the top is 62°. Find the height of the building to 1 d.p.

1

Draw a right triangle: the 40 m horizontal is adjacent to the 62° angle; the building height is opposite.

2
$$\tan 62° = \frac{h}{40} \implies h = 40\tan 62° \approx 40 \times 1.8807 \approx 75.2 \text{ m}$$
Worked Example 2.5b — Two-Step Problem with Depression

From the top of a 120 m cliff, the angles of depression of two boats at sea are 28° and 45°. Find the distance between the two boats, to the nearest metre.

1

For each boat, the cliff height (120 m) is opposite the angle of depression, and the horizontal distance to the boat is adjacent. (The angle of depression equals the angle inside the right triangle at the base of the cliff.)

2
$$d_1 = \frac{120}{\tan 28°} \approx \frac{120}{0.5317} \approx 225.7 \text{ m}$$ $$d_2 = \frac{120}{\tan 45°} = \frac{120}{1} = 120 \text{ m}$$
3

Distance between boats: \(225.7 - 120 = 105.7 \approx 106 \text{ m}\)

Compass Bearings

A true bearing is measured clockwise from north, stated as a three-digit number: N is 000°, E is 090°, S is 180°, W is 270°. Bearing problems use trigonometry to find distances and directions.

Worked Example 2.5c — Bearings

A ship sails 80 km on a bearing of 120° from Port A. How far east and how far south is it from port? Find the bearing to return to port.

1

A bearing of 120° is 30° east of due south (120° − 90° = 30° from east = 60° from south). The right triangle has: hypotenuse = 80 km, angle from north = 120°, so angle from east = 30°.

2

East component: \(80\sin 30° = 80 \times 0.5 = 40\) km east (bearing from north: \(\sin\) picks up the eastward component)

Wait — from north clockwise 120°: the angle from south is \(180°-120°=60°\). Let's work from the E-W axis: angle from east = \(120°-90°=30°\).

$$\text{East: } 80\sin(120°\!-\!90°) = 80\sin30° = 40 \text{ km}$$ $$\text{South: } 80\cos(120°\!-\!90°) = 80\cos30° = 40\sqrt{3} \approx 69.3 \text{ km}$$
3

Return bearing: opposite direction = \(120° - 180° = -60°\), i.e. \(360° - 60° = 300°\)

Return bearing is 300°

Three-Dimensional Problems

Worked Example 2.5d — Pythagoras and Trig in 3D

A rectangular room is 8 m long, 5 m wide and 3 m high. Find the length of the diagonal from a bottom corner to the opposite top corner, and the angle this diagonal makes with the floor.

1

Floor diagonal: \(d_{\text{floor}} = \sqrt{8^2 + 5^2} = \sqrt{89}\) m

2

Space diagonal: \(d = \sqrt{(\sqrt{89})^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{89 + 9} = \sqrt{98} = 7\sqrt{2} \approx 9.90\) m

3

Angle with floor: \(\tan\alpha = \dfrac{3}{\sqrt{89}} \implies \alpha = \tan^{-1}\!\left(\dfrac{3}{\sqrt{89}}\right) \approx 17.6°\)

2.6Non-Right-Angled Trigonometry — Sine and Cosine Rules MA5-TRG-P-01 ★

Right-angled trigonometry (SOH CAH TOA) only works when one angle is exactly 90°. The sine rule and cosine rule extend trigonometry to any triangle — they are the tools surveyors, navigators and architects use for irregular shapes where no right angle is guaranteed.

Sine Rule
$$\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}$$

Lower-case = side length, upper-case = opposite angle. Use when you know: two angles and one side (AAS), or two sides and a non-included angle (SSA — watch for ambiguous case).

Cosine Rule
$$c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos C \qquad \text{or equivalently} \qquad \cos C = \frac{a^2 + b^2 - c^2}{2ab}$$

Use when you know: two sides and the included angle (SAS), or all three sides and need an angle (SSS). When \(C = 90°\), \(\cos 90° = 0\) and the formula reduces to Pythagoras' theorem.

Area of a Triangle (Non-Right-Angled)
$$\text{Area} = \tfrac{1}{2}ab\sin C$$

Where \(a\) and \(b\) are two sides and \(C\) is the included angle between them. When \(C = 90°\), \(\sin 90° = 1\) and this gives \(\tfrac{1}{2}ab\), matching the standard formula.

Worked Example 2.6a — Sine Rule: Finding a Side

In triangle \(ABC\), angle \(A = 48°\), angle \(B = 62°\), and side \(b = 14\) cm. Find side \(a\).

1

Apply the sine rule: \(\dfrac{a}{\sin A} = \dfrac{b}{\sin B}\)

$$\frac{a}{\sin 48°} = \frac{14}{\sin 62°}$$
2
$$a = \frac{14\sin 48°}{\sin 62°} = \frac{14 \times 0.7431}{0.8829} \approx \frac{10.403}{0.8829} \approx 11.8 \text{ cm}$$
Worked Example 2.6b — Cosine Rule: Finding a Side

In triangle \(PQR\), \(p = 9\) cm, \(q = 12\) cm, and angle \(R = 110°\). Find side \(r\).

1

Apply cosine rule — \(r\) is opposite angle \(R\), so \(a = p\), \(b = q\), \(C = R\):

$$r^2 = p^2 + q^2 - 2pq\cos R = 9^2 + 12^2 - 2(9)(12)\cos 110°$$
2
$$r^2 = 81 + 144 - 216\cos 110° = 225 - 216(-0.3420) = 225 + 73.87 = 298.87$$ $$r = \sqrt{298.87} \approx 17.3 \text{ cm}$$
Worked Example 2.6c — Area of a Triangle

A triangular piece of land has two sides of 85 m and 60 m with an included angle of 73°. Find the area of the land.

1
$$\text{Area} = \tfrac{1}{2}ab\sin C = \tfrac{1}{2}(85)(60)\sin 73°$$
2
$$= 2550 \times 0.9563 \approx 2438.6 \text{ m}^2$$
Choosing Sine Rule or Cosine Rule
Given informationUse
Two angles + one side (AAS or ASA)Sine rule to find remaining sides
Two sides + non-included angle (SSA)Sine rule — check for ambiguous case (\(\sin^{-1}\) might give two valid angles)
Two sides + included angle (SAS)Cosine rule to find the third side
Three sides, find an angle (SSS)Cosine rule rearranged: \(\cos C = \dfrac{a^2+b^2-c^2}{2ab}\)

2.7Similar Figures and Scale Drawings MA5-GEO-C-01

Two shapes are similar if they have the same shape but different sizes — all corresponding angles are equal and all corresponding sides are in the same ratio. This ratio is called the scale factor (or similarity ratio). Similar figures appear in maps, architectural drawings, and any scaled model.

Properties of Similar Figures

If two figures are similar with scale factor \(k\), then:

  • All corresponding angles are equal
  • All corresponding sides are in ratio \(k : 1\)
  • The ratio of perimeters is \(k : 1\)
  • The ratio of areas is \(k^2 : 1\)
  • The ratio of volumes is \(k^3 : 1\)
Why \(k^2\) for area and \(k^3\) for volume?

Area involves two dimensions — if each linear dimension is multiplied by \(k\), area is multiplied by \(k \times k = k^2\). Volume involves three dimensions, so it is multiplied by \(k^3\). This is one of the most useful results in mathematics for real-world scaling problems.

Worked Example 2.7a — Finding the Scale Factor and Missing Sides

Rectangle \(ABCD\) is similar to rectangle \(PQRS\). \(AB = 12\) cm, \(BC = 8\) cm, and \(PQ = 9\) cm. Find the scale factor and the length of \(QR\).

1

Scale factor: \(k = \dfrac{PQ}{AB} = \dfrac{9}{12} = \dfrac{3}{4}\)

2

\(QR = BC \times k = 8 \times \dfrac{3}{4} = 6 \text{ cm}\)

Worked Example 2.7b — Area and Volume Scale Factors

Two similar cylinders have radii 4 cm and 10 cm. (i) Find the ratio of their surface areas. (ii) The smaller cylinder has volume 96 cm³. Find the volume of the larger cylinder.

1

Scale factor: \(k = \dfrac{10}{4} = \dfrac{5}{2}\)

2

Ratio of surface areas: \(k^2 = \left(\dfrac{5}{2}\right)^2 = \dfrac{25}{4}\)

So the larger cylinder's SA is \(\dfrac{25}{4}\) times the smaller's.

3

Volume ratio: \(k^3 = \left(\dfrac{5}{2}\right)^3 = \dfrac{125}{8}\)

Volume of larger: \(96 \times \dfrac{125}{8} = 1500 \text{ cm}^3\)

Worked Example 2.7c — Scale Drawings and Maps

On a map with scale 1 : 25 000, two towns are 8.4 cm apart. Find the actual distance in km. The map shows a park as a rectangle 3.2 cm × 2.5 cm. Find the actual area of the park in hectares (\(1 \text{ ha} = 10\,000 \text{ m}^2\)).

1

Distance: \(8.4 \text{ cm} \times 25\,000 = 210\,000 \text{ cm} = 2.1 \text{ km}\)

2

Map area: \(3.2 \times 2.5 = 8 \text{ cm}^2\)

Actual area: \(8 \times 25\,000^2 \text{ cm}^2 = 8 \times 625\,000\,000 \text{ cm}^2\)

$$= 5\,000\,000\,000 \text{ cm}^2 = 500\,000 \text{ m}^2 = 50 \text{ ha}$$
Computing Connection — Similar Figures and Scaling in Design

Every image on a computer screen is scaled using similarity ratios. When a browser resizes a photograph, it applies a scale factor to all pixel dimensions — the image's area scales by \(k^2\). In Chapter CT3 (Design Thinking), the fidelity spectrum moves from low-fidelity sketches to high-fidelity prototypes — scaling up both physical size and visual detail. In mapping software like Google Maps, the scale factor changes as you zoom, and area calculations need to account for \(k^2\) scaling to remain accurate.
