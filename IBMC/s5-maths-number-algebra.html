5.1Financial Mathematics MA5-FIN-C-01

Financial mathematics uses algebra to model money over time. The two key ideas — simple interest and compound interest — both build on percentage calculations from Stage 4, but compound interest introduces an important new structure: exponential growth.

Inquiry Question

If a bank account earns 5% interest per year, is it better to earn 5% of the original amount each year, or 5% of the current amount? How much does the difference matter over 20 years?

Simple Interest

Simple interest is calculated on the original principal only. It grows linearly — the same fixed amount is added each period.

Simple Interest Formula
$$I = Prn \qquad \text{and} \qquad A = P + I = P(1 + rn)$$

\(P\) = principal (initial amount)  ·  \(r\) = interest rate per period (as a decimal)  ·  \(n\) = number of periods  ·  \(A\) = final amount

Worked Example 5.1a — Simple Interest

Amara invests $4 500 at 3.8% p.a. simple interest for 4 years. Calculate the interest earned and the final value of the investment.

1

Identify values: \(P = 4500\), \(r = 0.038\), \(n = 4\)

2

Calculate interest: \(I = Prn = 4500 \times 0.038 \times 4 = \$684\)

3

Final amount: \(A = P + I = 4500 + 684 = \$5184\)

Compound Interest

Compound interest is calculated on the current balance — including all interest earned so far. Because interest earns interest, the balance grows exponentially rather than linearly.

Compound Interest Formula
$$A = P\!\left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}$$

\(P\) = principal  ·  \(r\) = annual interest rate (decimal)  ·  \(n\) = compounding periods per year  ·  \(t\) = time in years  ·  \(A\) = final amount

Worked Example 5.1b — Compound Interest

$8 000 is invested at 4.5% p.a. compound interest, compounded quarterly, for 6 years. Find the final amount and the total interest earned.

1

Identify values: \(P = 8000\), \(r = 0.045\), \(n = 4\) (quarterly), \(t = 6\)

2

Apply formula:

$$A = 8000\!\left(1 + \frac{0.045}{4}\right)^{4 \times 6} = 8000(1.01125)^{24}$$
3

Evaluate: \((1.01125)^{24} \approx 1.30865\)

$$A = 8000 \times 1.30865 \approx \$10{,}469.20$$
4

Interest earned: \(I = A - P = 10{,}469.20 - 8000 = \$2{,}469.20\)

Depreciation

Depreciation models the decreasing value of an asset over time. The structure mirrors compound interest, but with a subtraction instead of addition — value decreases by a fixed percentage each period.

Depreciation Formula
$$S = V_0\!\left(1 - r\right)^n$$

\(V_0\) = initial value  ·  \(r\) = depreciation rate per period (decimal)  ·  \(n\) = number of periods  ·  \(S\) = salvage (current) value

Worked Example 5.1c — Depreciation

A car is purchased for $34 000. It depreciates at 18% per year. Find its value after 3 years. After how many full years will it first be worth less than $10 000?

1

Value after 3 years: \(S = 34000(1 - 0.18)^3 = 34000(0.82)^3\)

$$S = 34000 \times 0.551368 \approx \$18{,}746.51$$
2

Find when \(S < 10\,000\): Calculate successive values.

Year \(n\)Value \(S = 34000(0.82)^n\)Above $10 000?
4\(34000(0.82)^4 \approx \$15{,}371\)Yes
5\(34000(0.82)^5 \approx \$12{,}604\)Yes
6\(34000(0.82)^6 \approx \$10{,}335\)Yes
7\(34000(0.82)^7 \approx \$8{,}475\)No — first below $10 000

5.2Expanding and Factorising MA5-ALG-C-01

Stage 4 introduced expanding single brackets and factorising by common factor. Stage 5 extends this to special products and quadratic trinomials — building the algebraic toolkit needed for solving quadratic equations in Section 5.5.

Special Products

Three Special Products to Memorise

Perfect square (sum):

$$(a + b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2$$

Perfect square (difference):

$$(a - b)^2 = a^2 - 2ab + b^2$$

Difference of two squares:

$$(a + b)(a - b) = a^2 - b^2$$

These three identities appear everywhere in mathematics — in simplifying expressions, solving equations, and proving geometric theorems. Recognising their structure is one of the most important pattern-recognition skills in algebra.

The middle term \(2ab\) in perfect squares is the most commonly missed. Always expand carefully: \((x+5)^2 = x^2 + 10x + 25\), not \(x^2 + 25\).

Worked Example 5.2a — Expanding Special Products

Expand:   (i) \((3x + 4)^2\)    (ii) \((2x - 5)^2\)    (iii) \((x + 7)(x - 7)\)    (iv) \((3a + 2b)(3a - 2b)\)

i

\((3x + 4)^2 = (3x)^2 + 2(3x)(4) + 4^2 = 9x^2 + 24x + 16\)

ii

\((2x - 5)^2 = (2x)^2 - 2(2x)(5) + 5^2 = 4x^2 - 20x + 25\)

iii

\((x + 7)(x - 7) = x^2 - 7^2 = x^2 - 49\)

iv

\((3a + 2b)(3a - 2b) = (3a)^2 - (2b)^2 = 9a^2 - 4b^2\)

Factorising — Core Techniques

Factorising is expanding in reverse. Always look for a common factor first, then apply the appropriate technique.

Worked Example 5.2b — Factorising: Difference of Two Squares and Perfect Squares

Factorise:   (i) \(16x^2 - 9\)    (ii) \(3x^2 - 48\)    (iii) \(x^2 + 12x + 36\)    (iv) \(4y^2 - 28y + 49\)

i

\(16x^2 - 9 = (4x)^2 - 3^2 = (4x + 3)(4x - 3)\)

ii

Take out common factor first: \(3x^2 - 48 = 3(x^2 - 16) = 3(x + 4)(x - 4)\)

iii

\(x^2 + 12x + 36 = x^2 + 2(x)(6) + 6^2 = (x + 6)^2\)

iv

\(4y^2 - 28y + 49 = (2y)^2 - 2(2y)(7) + 7^2 = (2y - 7)^2\)

Factorising Monic Quadratic Trinomials

A monic quadratic has the form \(x^2 + bx + c\) (leading coefficient is 1). To factorise, find two numbers that multiply to give \(c\) and add to give \(b\).

Worked Example 5.2c — Factorising Monic Quadratic Trinomials

Factorise:   (i) \(x^2 + 7x + 12\)    (ii) \(x^2 - 3x - 18\)    (iii) \(x^2 - 10x + 25\)

i

Need two numbers: multiply to \(+12\), add to \(+7\). Try: \(3 \times 4 = 12\), \(3 + 4 = 7\) ✓

\(x^2 + 7x + 12 = (x + 3)(x + 4)\)

ii

Need two numbers: multiply to \(-18\), add to \(-3\). Try: \(3 \times (-6) = -18\), \(3 + (-6) = -3\) ✓

\(x^2 - 3x - 18 = (x + 3)(x - 6)\)

iii

Recognise perfect square: \(x^2 - 10x + 25 = (x - 5)^2\)

Check: multiply \(-5 \times -5 = 25\) ✓, add \(-5 + (-5) = -10\) ✓

★ Path — Factorising Non-Monic Quadratics (MA5-ALG-P-01)

A non-monic quadratic has the form \(ax^2 + bx + c\) where \(a \neq 1\). Use the product-and-sum method (also called the cross method or splitting the middle term).

Method: Find two numbers that multiply to \(ac\) and add to \(b\). Split the middle term, then factorise by grouping.

Example: Factorise \(6x^2 + 11x + 4\).

\(ac = 6 \times 4 = 24\). Need two numbers: multiply to \(24\), add to \(11\). These are \(3\) and \(8\).

$$6x^2 + 11x + 4 = 6x^2 + 3x + 8x + 4 = 3x(2x + 1) + 4(2x + 1) = (3x + 4)(2x + 1)$$
A Common Mistake — Factorising Sums of Squares

\(a^2 + b^2\) does not factorise over the real numbers. The difference of two squares \(a^2 - b^2 = (a+b)(a-b)\) works because the middle terms cancel. The sum \(a^2 + b^2\) has no middle term to cancel, so it cannot be written as a product of two linear real factors. Always check the sign before applying the difference of two squares.

5.3Algebraic Fractions MA5-ALG-C-01 MA5-ALG-P-01 ★

Algebraic fractions follow identical rules to numerical fractions — simplify, find common denominators to add or subtract, multiply numerators and denominators directly, and flip to divide. The extra skill required is factorising, which reveals common factors that cancel.

Worked Example 5.3a — Simplifying Algebraic Fractions

Simplify:   (i) \(\dfrac{6x^2y}{9xy^2}\)     (ii) \(\dfrac{x^2 - 9}{x^2 - x - 6}\)

i

Cancel common numerical factor and common variables:

$$\frac{6x^2y}{9xy^2} = \frac{6}{9} \cdot \frac{x^2}{x} \cdot \frac{y}{y^2} = \frac{2}{3} \cdot x \cdot \frac{1}{y} = \frac{2x}{3y}$$
ii

Factorise numerator and denominator:

$$\frac{x^2 - 9}{x^2 - x - 6} = \frac{(x+3)(x-3)}{(x-3)(x+2)} = \frac{x+3}{x+2}, \quad x \neq 3$$
Worked Example 5.3b — Adding and Subtracting Algebraic Fractions

Simplify:   (i) \(\dfrac{2}{x} + \dfrac{3}{x+1}\)     (ii) \(\dfrac{4}{x-2} - \dfrac{1}{x+3}\)

i

LCD = \(x(x+1)\). Rewrite each fraction:

$$\frac{2(x+1)}{x(x+1)} + \frac{3x}{x(x+1)} = \frac{2x+2+3x}{x(x+1)} = \frac{5x+2}{x(x+1)}$$
ii

LCD = \((x-2)(x+3)\):

$$\frac{4(x+3)}{(x-2)(x+3)} - \frac{(x-2)}{(x-2)(x+3)} = \frac{4x+12 - x+2}{(x-2)(x+3)} = \frac{3x+14}{(x-2)(x+3)}$$
Worked Example 5.3c — Multiplying and Dividing Algebraic Fractions

Simplify:   (i) \(\dfrac{x^2-4}{3x} \times \dfrac{6x^2}{x+2}\)     (ii) \(\dfrac{x^2+5x+6}{x-1} \div \dfrac{x+2}{x^2-1}\)

i

Factorise, then cancel before multiplying:

$$\frac{(x+2)(x-2)}{3x} \times \frac{6x^2}{x+2} = \frac{(x+2)(x-2) \cdot 6x^2}{3x(x+2)} = \frac{6x^2(x-2)}{3x} = 2x(x-2)$$
ii

Dividing: flip the second fraction and multiply:

$$\frac{(x+2)(x+3)}{x-1} \times \frac{x^2-1}{x+2} = \frac{(x+2)(x+3)}{x-1} \times \frac{(x-1)(x+1)}{x+2} = (x+3)(x+1)$$

5.4Indices MA5-IND-C-01

Stage 4 established the index laws for positive integer powers. Stage 5 extends them to negative indices and fractional indices — which connect directly to surds and provide the algebraic foundation for logarithms in Stage 6.

Index Laws — Complete Reference (Stage 4 + 5)
LawStatementExample
Product\(a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}\)\(x^3 \times x^{-1} = x^2\)
Quotient\(a^m \div a^n = a^{m-n}\)\(x^2 \div x^5 = x^{-3}\)
Power of a power\((a^m)^n = a^{mn}\)\((x^{-2})^3 = x^{-6}\)

Worked Example 5.4a — Negative Indices

Write with positive indices only:   (i) \(x^{-4}\)    (ii) \(\dfrac{3}{x^{-2}}\)    (iii) \((2x^{-3})^2\)    (iv) \(\dfrac{x^2 y^{-3}}{x^{-1}y}\)

i

\(x^{-4} = \dfrac{1}{x^4}\)

ii

\(\dfrac{3}{x^{-2}} = 3 \times x^2 = 3x^2\)

iii

\((2x^{-3})^2 = 4x^{-6} = \dfrac{4}{x^6}\)

iv

\(\dfrac{x^2 y^{-3}}{x^{-1}y} = x^{2-(-1)} \cdot y^{-3-1} = x^3 y^{-4} = \dfrac{x^3}{y^4}\)

Worked Example 5.4b — Fractional Indices

Evaluate:   (i) \(25^{1/2}\)    (ii) \(27^{2/3}\)    (iii) \(16^{-3/4}\)    Express with fractional indices: (iv) \(\sqrt[5]{x^3}\)

i

\(25^{1/2} = \sqrt{25} = 5\)

ii

\(27^{2/3} = (\sqrt[3]{27})^2 = 3^2 = 9\)

iii

\(16^{-3/4} = \dfrac{1}{16^{3/4}} = \dfrac{1}{(\sqrt[4]{16})^3} = \dfrac{1}{2^3} = \dfrac{1}{8}\)

iv

\(\sqrt[5]{x^3} = x^{3/5}\)

★ Path — Surds and Irrational Numbers (MA5-IND-P-01)

A surd is an irrational root that cannot be simplified to a rational number: \(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}, \sqrt{5}\) are surds; \(\sqrt{4} = 2\) is not. Surds arise naturally from fractional indices.

Simplifying surds: Find the largest perfect square factor. \(\sqrt{72} = \sqrt{36 \times 2} = 6\sqrt{2}\)

Operations: Like surds can be added/subtracted. \(3\sqrt{5} + 2\sqrt{5} = 5\sqrt{5}\). Unlike surds cannot be combined: \(3\sqrt{2} + 2\sqrt{3}\) cannot be simplified.

Multiplying surds: \(\sqrt{a} \times \sqrt{b} = \sqrt{ab}\) and \(\sqrt{a} \times \sqrt{a} = a\)

Rationalising the denominator: \(\dfrac{3}{\sqrt{5}} = \dfrac{3}{\sqrt{5}} \times \dfrac{\sqrt{5}}{\sqrt{5}} = \dfrac{3\sqrt{5}}{5}\)

5.5Quadratic Equations MA5-EQU-C-01 MA5-EQU-P-01 ★

A quadratic equation has the form \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\). Unlike linear equations, quadratics typically have two solutions. The method of solution depends on the structure of the equation.

Methods for Solving Quadratic Equations
MethodWhen to useDifficulty
FactorisingWhen the quadratic factors neatly (integer solutions or simple fractions)Core
Null factor lawUsed with factorising: if \(AB = 0\) then \(A = 0\) or \(B = 0\)Core
Completing the squareAny quadratic; forms the basis for the quadratic formulaPath ★
Quadratic formulaAny quadratic; most reliable method for non-factorisable equationsPath ★
Worked Example 5.5a — Solving by Factorising (Core)

Solve:   (i) \(x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0\)    (ii) \(2x^2 + 7x - 4 = 0\)    (iii) \(3x^2 = 12x\)

i

Factorise: \((x-2)(x-3) = 0\)

Null factor: \(x - 2 = 0\) or \(x - 3 = 0\)  ⟹  \(x = 2\) or \(x = 3\)

ii

Factorise (non-monic, Path): \((2x - 1)(x + 4) = 0\)

\(x = \frac{1}{2}\) or \(x = -4\)

iii

Rearrange first — never divide both sides by \(x\) (you'd lose a solution):

\(3x^2 - 12x = 0 \implies 3x(x - 4) = 0 \implies x = 0\) or \(x = 4\)

★ Path — The Quadratic Formula (MA5-EQU-P-01)

When a quadratic does not factorise neatly, the quadratic formula always works:

$$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$$

The expression \(\Delta = b^2 - 4ac\) is called the discriminant. It determines the number and nature of solutions:

  • \(\Delta > 0\): two distinct real solutions
  • \(\Delta = 0\): one repeated real solution (the parabola touches the x-axis)
  • \(\Delta < 0\): no real solutions (the parabola doesn't cross the x-axis)

Example: Solve \(x^2 + 3x - 7 = 0\). Here \(a=1, b=3, c=-7\).

$$x = \frac{-3 \pm \sqrt{9 + 28}}{2} = \frac{-3 \pm \sqrt{37}}{2}$$

So \(x = \dfrac{-3 + \sqrt{37}}{2} \approx 1.54\) or \(x = \dfrac{-3 - \sqrt{37}}{2} \approx -4.54\)

Worked Example 5.5b — Quadratic from a Word Problem

A rectangular garden has a length 3 m more than its width. Its area is 70 m². Find the dimensions.

1

Define variable: Let width \(= x\) m, so length \(= x + 3\) m.

2

Write equation: \(x(x + 3) = 70 \implies x^2 + 3x - 70 = 0\)

3

Factorise: Need two numbers: multiply to \(-70\), add to \(3\). These are \(10\) and \(-7\).

\((x + 10)(x - 7) = 0 \implies x = -10\) or \(x = 7\)

4

Reject negative: Since width must be positive, \(x = 7\).

Width = 7 m, Length = 10 m. Check: \(7 \times 10 = 70\) ✓

5.6Simultaneous Equations MA5-EQU-C-01

Simultaneous equations are two or more equations that must all be satisfied at the same time. Their solution is the point(s) where the graphs of the equations intersect. Two methods — substitution and elimination — are used depending on the form of the equations.

Worked Example 5.6a — Substitution Method

Solve simultaneously: \(y = 2x - 1\) and \(3x + 2y = 12\)

1

Substitute \(y = 2x - 1\) into the second equation:

\(3x + 2(2x - 1) = 12 \implies 3x + 4x - 2 = 12 \implies 7x = 14 \implies x = 2\)

2

Find \(y\): \(y = 2(2) - 1 = 3\)

3

Check in both equations: \(3 = 2(2) - 1\) ✓   \(3(2) + 2(3) = 12\) ✓

Solution: \(x = 2,\ y = 3\)   (the point \((2, 3)\))

Worked Example 5.6b — Elimination Method

Solve simultaneously: \(5x + 3y = 16\) and \(2x - 3y = -2\)

1

The \(y\)-coefficients are already opposite (\(+3y\) and \(-3y\)). Add the equations:

\(7x = 14 \implies x = 2\)

2

Substitute \(x = 2\) into the first equation: \(5(2) + 3y = 16 \implies 3y = 6 \implies y = 2\)

3

Check: \(2(2) - 3(2) = 4 - 6 = -2\) ✓   Solution: \(x = 2,\ y = 2\)

Worked Example 5.6c — Elimination Requiring Scaling

Solve: \(3x + 4y = 10\) and \(5x + 6y = 15\)

1

To eliminate \(y\), multiply Eq 1 by 3 and Eq 2 by 2 (LCM of 4 and 6 is 12):

Eq 1 × 3: \(9x + 12y = 30\)

Eq 2 × 2: \(10x + 12y = 30\)

2

Subtract: \((10x + 12y) - (9x + 12y) = 30 - 30 \implies x = 0\)

3

Substitute \(x = 0\): \(4y = 10 \implies y = 2.5\)

Solution: \(x = 0,\ y = 2.5\). Check: \(5(0) + 6(2.5) = 15\) ✓

Application — Solving a Real Problem with Simultaneous Equations

A school canteen sells pies for \(\$p\) each and sausage rolls for \(\$s\) each. On Monday, 30 pies and 20 sausage rolls are sold for a total of \(\$140\). On Tuesday, 25 pies and 35 sausage rolls are sold for \(\$152.50\).

Set up a pair of simultaneous equations and solve them to find the price of each item.

5.7Linear Relationships MA5-LIN-C-01

Stage 4 introduced the gradient-intercept form \(y = mx + b\) and graphing linear equations. Stage 5 adds the formal gradient formula, midpoint formula, point-gradient form, and the relationships between parallel and perpendicular lines.

Key Formulae for Linear Relationships

Gradient between two points:

$$m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$

Midpoint of a segment:

$$M = \left(\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2},\ \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right)$$

Distance between two points ★:

$$d = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2}$$

Gradient-intercept form:

$$y = mx + b$$

Point-gradient form:

$$y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$$

General form:

$$ax + by + c = 0$$

Parallel lines: same gradient, different \(y\)-intercept. \(m_1 = m_2\)

Perpendicular lines: gradients are negative reciprocals. \(m_1 \times m_2 = -1\), so \(m_2 = -\dfrac{1}{m_1}\)

Worked Example 5.7a — Gradient, Midpoint, and Equation of a Line

Points \(A(-2, 3)\) and \(B(4, -1)\) are given. Find: (i) the gradient of \(AB\), (ii) the midpoint of \(AB\), (iii) the equation of line \(AB\).

i

\(m = \dfrac{-1 - 3}{4 - (-2)} = \dfrac{-4}{6} = -\dfrac{2}{3}\)

ii

\(M = \left(\dfrac{-2+4}{2}, \dfrac{3+(-1)}{2}\right) = \left(1,\ 1\right)\)

iii

Use point-gradient form with \(m = -\frac{2}{3}\) and point \(A(-2, 3)\):

$$y - 3 = -\tfrac{2}{3}(x - (-2)) \implies y - 3 = -\tfrac{2}{3}x - \tfrac{4}{3}$$ $$y = -\tfrac{2}{3}x + \tfrac{5}{3} \quad \text{or equivalently} \quad 2x + 3y - 5 = 0$$
Worked Example 5.7b — Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

A line \(\ell_1\) has equation \(y = 3x - 2\). Find the equation of:

(i) A line parallel to \(\ell_1\) passing through \((1, 4)\)

(ii) A line perpendicular to \(\ell_1\) passing through \((3, 1)\)

i

Parallel → same gradient: \(m = 3\). Use point-gradient form:

\(y - 4 = 3(x - 1) \implies y = 3x + 1\)

ii

Perpendicular → \(m_2 = -\dfrac{1}{3}\). Use point-gradient form with \((3, 1)\):

\(y - 1 = -\dfrac{1}{3}(x - 3) \implies y - 1 = -\dfrac{1}{3}x + 1 \implies y = -\dfrac{1}{3}x + 2\)

Worked Example 5.7c — Distance Formula (Path) and Applications

Show that the triangle with vertices \(P(1, 2)\), \(Q(5, 2)\) and \(R(3, 6)\) is isosceles, and find its area.

1

Calculate side lengths:

\(PQ = \sqrt{(5-1)^2 + (2-2)^2} = \sqrt{16} = 4\)

\(PR = \sqrt{(3-1)^2 + (6-2)^2} = \sqrt{4+16} = \sqrt{20} = 2\sqrt{5}\)

\(QR = \sqrt{(3-5)^2 + (6-2)^2} = \sqrt{4+16} = \sqrt{20} = 2\sqrt{5}\)

2

Since \(PR = QR = 2\sqrt{5}\), the triangle is isosceles.

3

Base \(PQ = 4\). Height = vertical distance from \(R\) to line \(PQ\) (which lies on \(y = 2\)): \(h = 6 - 2 = 4\).

\(\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times 4 \times 4 = 8 \text{ square units}\)
