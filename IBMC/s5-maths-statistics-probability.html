3.1Box Plots and Comparing Datasets MA5-DAT-C-01

Stage 4 introduced quartiles and the interquartile range (IQR) as measures of spread. Stage 5 uses these to construct and interpret box plots (box-and-whisker diagrams) — a powerful graphical display that simultaneously shows the centre, spread, and shape of a distribution in a compact form perfect for comparing two datasets side by side.

Inquiry Question

Two schools both report an average exam score of 72%. School A's scores range from 40% to 98%; School B's range from 65% to 82%. Which school has more consistent teaching? How does the mean fail to reveal this difference?

Five-Number Summary and Box Plots

Five-Number Summary

Every box plot is built from five values calculated from an ordered dataset:

ValueSymbolDefinition
Minimum\(x_{\min}\)Smallest value in the dataset (excluding outliers)
Lower quartile\(Q_1\)Median of the lower half of the data — 25% of values fall below this
Median\(Q_2\)Middle value — 50% of values fall below this
Upper quartile\(Q_3\)Median of the upper half — 75% of values fall below this
Maximum\(x_{\max}\)Largest value in the dataset (excluding outliers)

The interquartile range (IQR) = \(Q_3 - Q_1\) measures the spread of the middle 50% of data and is resistant to outliers.

Outlier rule: A value is an outlier if it falls more than \(1.5 \times \text{IQR}\) below \(Q_1\) or above \(Q_3\). Outliers are plotted individually as dots beyond the whiskers.

Worked Example 3.1a — Constructing a Box Plot

The times (in seconds) taken by 15 students to complete a puzzle, ordered, are:
18, 22, 25, 27, 30, 31, 34, 37, 40, 42, 45, 48, 52, 58, 89

Construct a box plot and identify any outliers.

1

Five-number summary: \(n = 15\), so median is 8th value.

\(x_{\min} = 18\),   \(Q_1 = 27\) (median of lower 7: positions 1–7),   \(Q_2 = 37\),   \(Q_3 = 48\) (median of upper 7: positions 9–15),   \(x_{\max} = 89\)

2

IQR \(= Q_3 - Q_1 = 48 - 27 = 21\)

Outlier bounds: lower \(= 27 - 1.5(21) = 27 - 31.5 = -4.5\); upper \(= 48 + 1.5(21) = 48 + 31.5 = 79.5\)

The value 89 > 79.5, so 89 is an outlier. The upper whisker extends to 58 (next largest non-outlier).

3

Box plot:

Comparing Two Datasets Using Box Plots

What to Comment on When Comparing Box Plots

Always address all three of these, with reference to specific values from the plots:

  1. Centre: Compare the medians — which group has a higher typical value?
  2. Spread: Compare IQRs and overall ranges — which group is more consistent?
  3. Shape: Is the distribution symmetric? Positively skewed (long right whisker)? Negatively skewed (long left whisker)?

Positive skew = tail stretches right = most data clustered at lower values = mean > median.
Negative skew = tail stretches left = most data clustered at higher values = mean < median.

Worked Example 3.1b — Comparing Two Box Plots

The box plots below display Year 10 test scores for two classes. Write a comparative analysis.

Comparative analysis:

  • Centre: Class B has a higher median (75%) than Class A (70%), suggesting Class B performed better overall.
  • Spread: Class B has a larger IQR (\(88 - 62 = 26\)) than Class A (\(80 - 58 = 22\)), indicating more variable performance. Class B also has a greater overall range (\(98 - 52 = 46\) vs \(95 - 48 = 47\)) — similar overall spread.
  • Shape: Class A appears roughly symmetric. Class B has a longer upper whisker, suggesting a slight positive skew — a cluster of higher achievers pulling the right tail out.

3.2Bivariate Data and Scatter Plots MA5-DAT-C-02

Bivariate data involves two variables measured on the same subject. We display it using a scatter plot — each individual becomes a point with coordinates \((x, y)\) — and analyse it to determine whether a relationship (association) exists between the variables.

Inquiry Question

Does studying more hours always lead to better exam results? Does a country's wealth always predict longer life expectancy? How do we measure the strength of such relationships — and crucially, what does it mean when two things are correlated but one does not cause the other?

Types of Correlation

Direction

Positive

As \(x\) increases, \(y\) tends to increase. Points slope upward left-to-right.

Direction

Negative

As \(x\) increases, \(y\) tends to decrease. Points slope downward left-to-right.

Direction

No correlation

No clear direction — points scattered with no pattern.

Strength

Strong

Points cluster tightly around a line. Small scatter.

Strength

Moderate

Points follow a general trend but with noticeable scatter around the line.

Strength

Weak

A very slight trend, with a lot of scatter. The pattern is difficult to see.

Line of Best Fit

A line of best fit (trend line) is drawn through a scatter plot to model a linear relationship. It is drawn by eye so that roughly equal numbers of points lie above and below it, and it passes through or near the "centre of gravity" of the data — approximately the point \((\bar{x}, \bar{y})\).

Worked Example 3.2a — Scatter Plot, Line of Best Fit, and Prediction

The table shows the number of hours eight students studied and their test score (%).

Study hours \((x)\)12345678
Test score \((y)\)4855596370747985
1

Plot the points — as hours increase, scores increase, suggesting a strong positive linear correlation.

2

Mean point: \(\bar{x} = \frac{1+2+\cdots+8}{8} = 4.5\), \(\quad \bar{y} = \frac{48+55+\cdots+85}{8} = 66.6\)

The line of best fit must pass through approximately \((4.5, 66.6)\).

3

Equation of line of best fit (drawn by eye and then read off):
Using two well-separated points on the line, e.g. \((1, 48)\) and \((8, 85)\):

$$m \approx \frac{85-48}{8-1} = \frac{37}{7} \approx 5.3$$ $$y - 48 = 5.3(x-1) \implies y = 5.3x + 42.7$$
4

Interpolation (predicting within the data range): For 5.5 hours: \(y = 5.3(5.5) + 42.7 \approx 72\%\)

Extrapolation (predicting outside the range): For 12 hours: \(y = 5.3(12) + 42.7 \approx 106\%\) — this is impossible (over 100%) and shows the danger of extrapolating too far beyond the data.

Correlation Does Not Imply Causation

A strong correlation between two variables does not mean one causes the other. Famous spurious correlations include:

  • Ice cream sales and drowning deaths are positively correlated — both are caused by hot weather (a confounding variable), not each other.
  • Countries with more televisions have longer life expectancy — both are explained by wealth.
  • Nicolas Cage film releases are correlated with swimming pool drownings — this is coincidence.

Always ask: is there a plausible mechanism by which \(x\) causes \(y\)? Could a third variable explain both? Is this just coincidence in a small sample?

Computing Connection — SQL, Python, and Bivariate Analysis

In Chapter CT4 (Analysing Data), you used SQL AVG(), COUNT() and visualised query results with Python's matplotlib. Scatter plots are generated in Python with plt.scatter(x, y) and a line of best fit using np.polyfit(x, y, 1) from NumPy. Real data scientists use Pearson's correlation coefficient \(r\) (ranging from −1 to +1) to quantify what you are judging by eye — a skill that leads directly into Stage 6 Statistics and Data Science.

3.3Two-Way Tables and Venn Diagrams MA5-PRO-C-01

Stage 4 introduced Venn diagrams and two-way tables for categorical data and basic probability. Stage 5 extends this to calculating probabilities from completed tables and diagrams, and connecting the two representations fluently.

Worked Example 3.3a — Two-Way Table and Probability

A survey of 200 students recorded whether they play a team sport and whether they have a part-time job.

Plays sportDoes not play sportTotal
Has job453075
No job8540125
Total13070200

Find the probability that a randomly selected student: (a) plays sport; (b) plays sport AND has a job; (c) plays sport OR has a job; (d) plays sport, given they have a job.

a

\(P(\text{sport}) = \dfrac{130}{200} = \dfrac{13}{20} = 0.65\)

b

\(P(\text{sport AND job}) = \dfrac{45}{200} = \dfrac{9}{40} = 0.225\)

c

\(P(\text{sport OR job}) = \dfrac{130 + 75 - 45}{200} = \dfrac{160}{200} = 0.8\)

(Add the totals for each event, then subtract the overlap to avoid double-counting.)

d

Given they have a job, we restrict to the "Has job" row (75 students). Of these, 45 play sport:

$$P(\text{sport} \mid \text{job}) = \frac{45}{75} = \frac{3}{5} = 0.6$$

This is a conditional probability — finding it from a table is a Core skill; the formal notation \(P(A \mid B)\) is developed in Section 3.6 (Path).

Worked Example 3.3b — Venn Diagram and Probability

In a class of 30 students, 18 study French (F), 14 study German (G), and 7 study both. Draw a Venn diagram and find the probability a student studies: (a) only French; (b) neither language; (c) at least one language.

1

Regions: Both: 7. Only F: \(18 - 7 = 11\). Only G: \(14 - 7 = 7\). Neither: \(30 - (11 + 7 + 7) = 5\).

2
3

(a) \(P(\text{only F}) = \dfrac{11}{30}\)    (b) \(P(\text{neither}) = \dfrac{5}{30} = \dfrac{1}{6}\)    (c) \(P(\text{at least one}) = \dfrac{11+7+7}{30} = \dfrac{25}{30} = \dfrac{5}{6}\)

3.4Multi-Stage Probability — Tree Diagrams and Tables MA5-PRO-C-01

A multi-stage experiment consists of two or more sequential steps. Tree diagrams display all outcomes systematically and make probability calculations clear. For independent events (where the outcome of one stage doesn't affect another), multiply probabilities along each branch.

Key Rules for Multi-Stage Probability
RuleStatementExample
Multiplication (AND)\(P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \times P(B)\)   (for independent events)\(P(\text{heads AND heads}) = \tfrac{1}{2} \times \tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{1}{4}\)
Addition (OR)\(P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B)\)   (for mutually exclusive events)\(P(\text{1 or 6}) = \tfrac{1}{6} + \tfrac{1}{6} = \tfrac{1}{3}\)
Complement\(P(\text{not } A) = 1 - P(A)\)\(P(\text{not 6}) = 1 - \tfrac{1}{6} = \tfrac{5}{6}\)
Without replacementDenominator decreases after each selectionSelecting 2 cards from a deck without replacing them
Worked Example 3.4a — Tree Diagram (With Replacement)

A bag contains 3 red (R) and 2 blue (B) marbles. A marble is drawn, recorded, replaced, and a second marble drawn. Find the probability of: (a) two red marbles; (b) one of each colour; (c) at least one blue marble.

1

Draw the tree. At each stage, \(P(R) = \frac{3}{5}\), \(P(B) = \frac{2}{5}\) (same each time — replacement).

2

(a) \(P(RR) = \dfrac{3}{5} \times \dfrac{3}{5} = \dfrac{9}{25}\)

(b) \(P(\text{one of each}) = P(RB) + P(BR) = \dfrac{6}{25} + \dfrac{6}{25} = \dfrac{12}{25}\)

(c) \(P(\text{at least one B}) = 1 - P(RR) = 1 - \dfrac{9}{25} = \dfrac{16}{25}\)

Worked Example 3.4b — Without Replacement

Two cards are selected from a standard deck of 52 without replacement. Find the probability that both are aces.

1

\(P(\text{ace on 1st}) = \dfrac{4}{52} = \dfrac{1}{13}\)

2

After one ace is removed: 51 cards remain, 3 are aces.

\(P(\text{ace on 2nd} \mid \text{ace on 1st}) = \dfrac{3}{51} = \dfrac{1}{17}\)

3

\(P(\text{both aces}) = \dfrac{1}{13} \times \dfrac{1}{17} = \dfrac{1}{221}\)

3.5Standard Deviation MA5-DAT-P-01 ★

The IQR measures the spread of the middle 50%. The standard deviation (\(\sigma\) or \(s\)) measures the typical distance of all data values from the mean — making it a more comprehensive measure of spread. It is the most widely used measure of variability in statistics and science.

Standard Deviation Formulae
$$\bar{x} = \frac{\sum x}{n} \qquad \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum(x - \bar{x})^2}{n}}$$

\(\bar{x}\) = mean  ·  \(n\) = number of values  ·  \(\sigma\) = population standard deviation  ·  \(\sum(x - \bar{x})^2\) = sum of squared deviations from the mean

Mean: \(\bar{x} = \dfrac{3+5+7+9+11}{5} = \dfrac{35}{5} = 7\)

Worked Example 3.5b — Using Mean and SD to Compare Datasets

Two brands of batteries were tested. Their lifetimes (hours) gave: Brand A: \(\bar{x} = 80\), \(\sigma = 4\). Brand B: \(\bar{x} = 78\), \(\sigma = 1.2\).

Which brand is better? Interpret both the mean and standard deviation.

1

Centre: Brand A has a higher mean (80 h vs 78 h) — on average, Brand A lasts longer.

2

Spread: Brand A has a much larger SD (4 h vs 1.2 h) — Brand A is far more variable. Some Brand A batteries might last 88+ hours, but others might die at 72 hours.

3

Recommendation depends on context: If you need reliability (e.g. for medical equipment, a smoke alarm), choose Brand B — its consistent 78 h is more predictable. If you can accept some risk for a chance of extra performance, Brand A might be preferred.

★ Using a Calculator for Standard Deviation

For large datasets, the by-hand method is impractical. On a scientific calculator:

  1. Enter statistics mode: MODE → STAT → 1-VAR (exact steps depend on model)
  2. Enter all data values, pressing \(=\) or DATA after each
  3. Navigate to \(\sigma x\) (population SD) or \(s x\) (sample SD)
  4. Note: use \(\sigma x\) when you have the entire population; use \(s x\) when you have a sample (divides by \(n-1\) instead of \(n\))

Always confirm your answer makes sense: the standard deviation must be between 0 (all values identical) and the range/2 (rough upper bound). A SD of 0 means every value equals the mean.

3.6Conditional Probability and Venn Diagrams MA5-PRO-P-01 ★

Conditional probability is the probability of an event occurring given that another event has already occurred. The knowledge that one event has happened changes the sample space — and therefore the probability — of another. It is written \(P(A \mid B)\), read "the probability of \(A\) given \(B\)."

Conditional Probability Formula
$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} \qquad \text{provided } P(B) \neq 0$$

Equivalently, this gives the multiplication rule for dependent events:  \(P(A \cap B) = P(B) \times P(A \mid B)\)

Worked Example 3.6a — Conditional Probability from a Venn Diagram

From the Venn diagram in Example 3.3b (French F, German G, 30 students):
Only F: 11, Both: 7, Only G: 7, Neither: 5. Find:

(a) \(P(G \mid F)\) — given they study French, what is the probability they also study German?

(b) \(P(F \mid G')\) — given they do not study German, what is the probability they study French?

a

Restrict to the 18 students who study French. Of those, 7 also study German.

$$P(G \mid F) = \frac{P(G \cap F)}{P(F)} = \frac{7/30}{18/30} = \frac{7}{18}$$
b

\(G'\) (not German) includes: only F (11) + neither (5) = 16 students.

Of these 16, the ones who study French: 11.

$$P(F \mid G') = \frac{11}{16}$$
Worked Example 3.6b — Independence and Conditional Probability

Two events \(A\) and \(B\) are independent if knowing \(B\) occurred gives no information about \(A\). Formally: \(P(A \mid B) = P(A)\), equivalently \(P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)\).

From the student survey in Example 3.3a: Is playing sport independent of having a job?

1

\(P(\text{sport}) = \frac{130}{200} = 0.65\)

\(P(\text{sport} \mid \text{job}) = \frac{45}{75} = 0.6\)

2

Since \(P(\text{sport} \mid \text{job}) = 0.6 \neq 0.65 = P(\text{sport})\), the events are not independent — having a job slightly reduces the probability of playing sport (perhaps due to time constraints).

Worked Example 3.6c — Medical Screening (Real Application)

A disease affects 1% of the population. A test is 95% accurate: it correctly identifies 95% of sick patients (true positive) and correctly clears 90% of healthy patients (true negative). A patient tests positive. What is the probability they actually have the disease?

1

Using a frequency tree with 10,000 people:

Test positiveTest negativeTotal
Has disease (1%)955100
No disease (99%)99089109900
Total1085891510,000
2
$$P(\text{disease} \mid \text{positive}) = \frac{95}{1085} \approx 0.0876 \approx 8.8\%$$

Despite the 95% accurate test, a positive result only means an ~8.8% chance of actually having the disease — because the disease is so rare. This is a profound and counterintuitive result known as the base rate fallacy, and it is critical knowledge for understanding medical screening programmes.

3.7Planning and Conducting a Statistical Inquiry MA5-DAT-P-01 ★

A statistical inquiry is the complete cycle from question to conclusion. It is the real-world application of everything in this chapter: identifying a question, collecting appropriate data, choosing displays, calculating statistics, and communicating findings with appropriate caution and critical evaluation.

Step 1

Pose a question

Define the population, variable(s), and a specific question that data can answer

Step 2

Plan & collect

Choose data source, sampling method, and determine an appropriate sample size

Step 3

Display

Choose appropriate graph type(s) for the data and the question

Step 4

Analyse

Calculate relevant statistics: mean, median, SD, IQR, quartiles, correlation

Step 5

Interpret & evaluate

Answer the original question, state limitations, and consider possible bias

Critical Evaluation — Questions to Ask About Any Statistical Claim
  • Who collected the data? Does the collector have a financial or ideological interest in a particular outcome?
  • How was the sample selected? Is it random? Is it representative of the population? How large is it?
  • What is the population? Does the sample match the population the conclusion applies to?
  • Is the graph honest? Does the y-axis start at zero? Are the scales consistent? Are the proportions accurate?
  • Correlation or causation? Is there a plausible causal mechanism, or could a third variable explain both?
  • How is the "average" defined? Is mean, median, or mode used? Which best represents this data?
  • What is not shown? Does the display hide variability, outliers, or sample size?
Activity — Stage 5 Statistical Inquiry Project

Conduct a full statistical inquiry using the five-step cycle. Choose one of the following questions or propose your own (approved by your teacher):

  1. Is there a relationship between the amount of sleep Year 10 students get and their self-reported concentration level in class?
  2. Do students at this school who participate in sport have higher or lower average screen time than those who do not?
  3. Is there a correlation between a student's travel time to school and the number of days absent per term?
  4. Do Year 9 students and Year 10 students have different distributions of reading time per week?

Required deliverables:

  • Written question and hypothesis
  • Data collection plan: method, sample size, any potential bias
  • At least two appropriate displays (one must be a box plot or scatter plot)
  • Calculation of at least three statistics (including mean or median, and IQR or SD)
  • A written conclusion that: answers the original question, acknowledges limitations, and evaluates whether the data supports the hypothesis
