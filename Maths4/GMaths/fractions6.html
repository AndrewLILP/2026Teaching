Vocabulary Pre-Teaching

Master the exact mathematical terminology required to seamlessly move between different representations of numbers.

Terminating Decimal

A decimal number that has a clean stop and contains a finite number of digits after the decimal point. Example: 34 = 0.75.

Recurring Decimal

A decimal number with one or more digits that repeat infinitely in a fixed pattern. Written with a dot or a bar over the repeating sequence. Example: 13 = 0.3̇ or 0.3̄.

Mixed Number

A number consisting of a whole number combined with a proper fraction. Example: 112.

Relative Value

The magnitude or value of a number compared to other numbers, especially visible when deciding if one value lies to the left or right of another on an axis line.

2. Navigating the Negative Direction

When deal with negative values on a number line, values change counter-intuitively:

  • For positive numbers, 1.5 is greater than 0.5 because it sits further to the right.
  • For negative numbers, -1.5 is less than -0.5 because it sits further to the left.

Think of it like a thermometer: -1.5°C is colder (lower down) than -0.5°C.

Step-Reveal Ordering Procedures

Study these structural walkthrough configurations showing comparisons across mixed format inputs.

Example 1: Ordering Mixed Formats (Ascending Order)
Arrange the following values from smallest to largest (ascending order):
65%,   58,   0.6̄,   0.62

Step 1: Convert each alternative configuration into a raw decimal format.

65% = 0.650
58 = 5 ÷ 8 = 0.625
0.6̄ = 0.666...
0.62 = 0.620

Step 2: Compare the decimals digit-by-digit, starting at the tenths place, then the hundredths, then the thousandths.

0.620 < 0.625 < 0.650 < 0.666...

Step 3: Substitute the original numbers back into the sorted chain sequence.

0.62,   58,   65%,   0.6̄
Example 2: Comparing Negative Fractions and Decimals
Insert the correct comparison symbol (<, >, or =) to make the statement true:
-115 ______ -1.25

Step 1: Convert the mixed fraction into a decimal. Keep the negative sign out front.

-115 = -(1 + 0.2) = -1.20

Step 2: Compare the two negative values. Remember, the value closer to zero on a number line is greater.

-1.20 sits to the right of -1.25 on a number line. Therefore, -1.20 is greater than -1.25.

Step 3: Write the final answer using the original numbers.

-115 > -1.25

Error Detective Room

Analyze these real-world conceptual errors. Click each card to reveal the hidden calculation bug and view the correct math process.

CASE 01

The Recurring Decimal Truncation Trap

A student was asked to compare 0.3̄ and 34%. They wrote:

"0.3̄ becomes 0.3, and 34% becomes 0.34. Since 0.3 < 0.34, 0.3̄ is smaller."
Click to reveal fix 🔄
Correct Solution Action:

Never truncate a recurring decimal! The bar means the digit 3 repeats forever.

0.3̄ = 0.3333... when written out. Since 34% = 0.3400, compare the hundredths place: 3 hundredths is smaller than 4 hundredths. While the student's final comparison symbol happened to be right, their method was incorrect because they changed the value of the recurring decimal.

CASE 02

The Negative Absolute Size Illusion

A student ordered a set of numbers from smallest to largest:

"Ordered list: -0.2,   -0.5,   0.1,   0.8"
Click to reveal fix 🔄
Correct Solution Action:

Negative numbers with larger absolute digits are actually smaller because they sit further to the left on a number line.

-0.5 is further to the left than -0.2, making it the smaller value. The correct ascending order is: -0.5,   -0.2,   0.1,   0.8.

Newman's Problem Framework Scaffolds

Break down multi-step word problems using structured steps to handle mixed numerical types.

Worded Challenge: Three students recorded how much of their homework project they completed over the weekend. Sarah completed 23 of her project, Liam completed 64%, and Maya completed 0.65 of her work. Rank the students in order from who completed the least to who completed the most.
1. Decode & Comprehend

We are given three values in different formats: a recurring fraction (23), a percentage (64%), and a decimal (0.65). We need to order them from smallest (least) to largest (most).

2. Transform & Process

Convert all values into decimals to compare them easily:

Sarah: 23 = 2 ÷ 3 = 0.666... (0.6̄)
Liam: 64% = 64 ÷ 100 = 0.640
Maya: 0.65 = 0.650

Compare the decimal values by checking their place values:

0.640 (Liam) < 0.650 (Maya) < 0.666... (Sarah)
3. Encode Representation

Write the final ranking using the students' names as requested by the problem:

Least to most completed: Liam, Maya, Sarah
