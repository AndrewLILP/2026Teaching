Act 1 · Sport
The Personal Trainer

Jaylen signs up with a personal trainer. The trainer charges a one-time sign-up fee of $20 plus $40 for each training session he attends.

When Jaylen receives his final bill, the total cost is $180.

❓ How many training sessions did Jaylen attend?
Step 1 Build the Equation

Let n = the number of sessions Jaylen attended. Use the scenario to write an equation.

Sign-up fee + (cost per session × n) = total cost
20 + 40n = 180
🗣️ In words
Write what this equation means as a sentence.
🔢 The unknown
What does n represent? What are you trying to find?
Step 2 Solve for n

Solve 20 + 40n = 180 using algebraic steps. Show your working.

Two-step plan:
Step 1 — Subtract 20 from both sides to isolate the term with n.
Step 2 — Divide both sides by 40 to find the value of n.

✅ Worked solution
1

Write the equation: 20 + 40n = 180

2

Subtract 20 from both sides: 40n = 160

3

Divide both sides by 40: n = 4

Step 3 Verify & Communicate
✅ Verify by substitution
Substitute n = 4 back in to check your answer is correct.
📝 Answer in context
Write a full sentence answering the original question.
📊 Table of values
Complete the table to show the pattern that leads to your answer.
🔄 What if…?
If Jaylen had $260, how many sessions could he book? Write the new equation.
🚀 Extension

A second trainer charges $35 per session with no sign-up fee. Write equations for both trainers' total cost. Find the number of sessions at which both trainers charge the same amount — show your working and explain your answer in words.

Act 2 · Design
The Architect's Formula

An architect calculates the cost of installing perimeter trim around rectangular rooms using the formula:

C = 2(l + w) × 12

where C is the cost in dollars, l is the room's length and w is its width (both in metres). The $12 factor is the cost per metre of trim.

❓ Part A: A room is 8 m long and 5 m wide. Find the total trim cost. Verify your answer.

❓ Part B: A second room costs $396. The room is 9 m long. Find the width.
Part A Substitute & Verify

Substitute l = 8 and w = 5 into C = 2(l + w) × 12. Show every step.

🔢 Show your substitution
Write out the values being substituted clearly.
✅ Verify your answer
How do you know your answer is correct? Explain.

2

3

Multiply left-to-right: C = 26 × 12 = $312

Part B Solve for the Unknown Width

Now C = 396 and l = 9. Substitute what you know and solve for w.

396 = 2(9 + w) × 12
Three-step plan:
Step 1 — Divide both sides by 12: you get 2(9 + w) = 33
Step 2 — Divide both sides by 2: you get 9 + w = 16.5
Step 3 — Subtract 9 from both sides to find w.
w = m
✅ Worked solution
1

Start: 396 = 2(9 + w) × 12

2

Divide both sides by 12: 33 = 2(9 + w)

3

Divide both sides by 2: 16.5 = 9 + w

4

Subtract 9: w = 7.5 m

Step 3 Communicate Your Understanding
📝 Answer in context
What does your answer mean for the architect and the room?
✅ Verify Part B
Substitute your answer back to confirm it gives C = $396.
🔄 Comparing Part A and Part B
In Part A you found C. In Part B you found w. How are these tasks different? What changed about how you used the formula?
🚀 Extension

The architect wants to design a square room where the trim cost is exactly $576. Using the same formula C = 2(l + w) × 12, find the side length. Then explain: why does a square room produce the same value for both l and w? What does this tell you about solving formulas with two unknowns?

Act 3 · Finance
Startup Growth

A tech startup's annual profit (in thousands of dollars) is modelled by the formula:

P = 5x²

where x is the number of years since the company launched and P is the profit in $thousands.

❓ Part A: In which year does the company first reach $80,000 profit? Give an exact answer.

❓ Part B: In which year does profit first reach $100,000? Give the exact value and a decimal approximation (to 2 decimal places).
Think First Why Two Solutions?

When we solve x² = k (where k > 0), there are always two possible values of x — one positive and one negative. For example:
if x² = 25, then x = 5 or x = −5 (because both 5² and (−5)² equal 25).

💭 Why only the positive answer here?
Think about what x represents in this problem. Why do we reject the negative solution?
Part A Reach $80,000 — Exact Answer

$80,000 means P = 80 in the model. Set up and solve 5x² = 80.

5x² = 80
Two-step plan:
Step 1 — Divide both sides by 5 to get x² = 16.
Step 2 — Take the square root of both sides: x = ±√16. Evaluate and choose the appropriate value.
x = years
✅ Worked solution
1

Start: 5x² = 80

2

Divide both sides by 5: x² = 16

3

Take the square root: x = ±√16 = ±4

4

Years must be positive, so: x = 4 years

Part B Reach $100,000 — Exact & Decimal

$100,000 means P = 100. Solve 5x² = 100.

5x² = 100

Exact value — choose the correct form:

Decimal approximation (2 d.p.):

x ≈ years

2

3

Exact form: x = ±√20 = ±2√5

🔄 Exact form vs decimal — which would you give an investor?

🚀 Extension

A rival startup uses the model P = 3x². In which year will both companies have the same profit? Set up and solve an equation to find x. Is there only one positive answer? Use your knowledge of quadratic equations to explain why.

DDL Competency: Communication

💬 Communication — representing understanding in multiple forms

This resource is built around DDL's Communication competency. Rather than asking students to produce a single numerical answer, every act requires them to express their mathematical thinking across four modes:

  • Symbolic/algebraic — writing and solving formal equations
  • Verbal — explaining what equations and answers mean in plain language
  • Numerical/tabular — Act 1's table of values builds the pattern beneath the algebra
  • Metacognitive/comparative — "compare the two parts" and "exact vs decimal" prompts ask students to reflect on their own choice of representation

The written communication prompts are not self-marked. They are designed for class discussion, Think-Pair-Share, peer explanation, or as exit slips. Their value lies in prompting articulation, not correctness.

NSW Syllabus Alignment

MA4-EQU-C-01
  • Act 1 — Solves linear equations up to 2 steps; models and solves word problems using equations
  • Act 2 — Verifies solutions by substitution; solves problems arising from substituting values into formulas
  • Act 3 — Reasons why there are 2 values satisfying ax² = c; gives answers in exact form and decimal approximation

Note: The quadratic content (ax² = c) appears at the Stage 4 level. If Act 3 is beyond your current cohort's readiness, use Acts 1 and 2 as a standalone sequence.

Suggested Lesson Flow (50 min)

  • Launch (5 min) — Project Act 1's scenario card only. Ask: "What do you notice? What are you wondering?" before students touch the equation.
  • Act 1 (12 min) — Individual or pairs. The equation is pre-built; you may ask students to write their own version first, then compare.
  • Act 2 (15 min) — Introduce substitution into formulas. Pause after both parts for the "compare Part A and Part B" whole-class discussion — this is the conceptual hinge of the act.
  • Act 3 (13 min) — Use the "Why Two Solutions?" block as a class discussion before students attempt the solve. The exact vs decimal reflection makes a strong closing prompt.
  • Debrief (5 min) — Return to the Communication theme: "Which mode felt most natural? Which was hardest? When would you choose a table over an equation?"

Differentiation

  • Foundation — Act 1 + Act 2 Part A only. Encourage free use of hint buttons. Focus on verbal communication prompts as the primary output.
  • Core — All three acts. Extension prompts optional.
  • Extension — All extension prompts, including the rival startup comparison in Act 3 (which surfaces the idea that 5x² = 3x² has only one positive solution: x = 0, a rich class discussion point).
  • The communication text areas have no wrong answers — they provide a safe access point for students who find symbolic work challenging but can express thinking verbally.

Student Agency Design

Students self-pace through the acts. Hint buttons reveal guidance on demand. Worked solutions appear automatically after a correct answer or after two incorrect attempts — this removes the need for teacher intervention for every stuck student, freeing you to facilitate the richer communication and discussion tasks.

The Communication task cards only appear once the core mathematical answer is correct. This structures the experience so symbolic fluency comes first, then reflection — mirroring the intended I Do / We Do / You Do flow.
