Solving Linear Equations

Expressions vs Equations

A pronumeral (like x) is a letter that stands for an unknown number.

An equation is a statement with an equals sign (=) — it says two things have the same value. Think of = as a balance scale: both sides must stay equal.

ExpressionEquation
3x + 53x + 5 = 14
2n − 72n − 7 = 3
No equals sign — just a calculationHas an = sign — states equality

The Inverse Operations Method

To solve an equation, find the value of the pronumeral that makes it true.

Strategy: undo what's been done to the pronumeral — one step at a time — doing the same thing to both sides.

Operation
+ addition
− subtraction
× multiplication
÷ division
Inverse (undo it)
→ subtract
→ add
→ divide
→ multiply

Topic 2 of 3 · Substitution & Verification

Substitution & Verification

What is Substitution?

Substitution means replacing a pronumeral with a number to calculate a value.

To verify a solution, substitute your answer back into the original equation and confirm both sides are equal.

Worked Example 4 · Verifying a Solution

Topic 3 of 3 · Quadratic Equations

Quadratic Equations

What is a Quadratic Equation?

A quadratic equation contains a pronumeral raised to the power of 2, such as x².

In Stage 4, we solve equations of the form ax² = c, where a and c are numbers.

Why Are There Two Solutions?

Consider x² = 9. We need a number that, when multiplied by itself, gives 9.

x = 3: 3 × 3 = 9 ✓
x = −3: (−3) × (−3) = 9 ✓

A positive number and its negative are mirror images — their squares are always equal. So equations of this form always have two solutions.

Understanding

Students connect the balance model to algebraic manipulation — any operation applied to one side must be applied to both.

Reasoning

Students justify why ax² = c has two solutions; verify solutions by substitution; reason about context constraints (e.g. positive-only lengths).

Problem Solving

Students model real word problems as equations; identify unknowns and translate context into algebraic statements.

Fluency

Students develop automaticity with inverse operations, multi-step procedures, and substitution into formulas.

⚠️ Common Misconceptions & How to Address Them

▼
Misconception 1 · Linear Equations
"I only need to do the operation to one side."
🔧 Reinforce the balance scale model before symbolic work. Ask: "If I remove 3 kg from the left pan, what happens to the right side?" Use physical or drawn balance diagrams until students internalise the rule.
Misconception 2 · Linear Equations
After collecting terms, students apply the inverse operation to only the term with the pronumeral, forgetting the constant (e.g. from 2x + 6 = 14, writing x = 14 ÷ 2 = 7).
🔧 Require students to write every step explicitly. Colour-code the constant term before applying inverse operations. Use structured equation templates with clearly labelled rows.
Misconception 3 · Substitution
Students treat the equals sign as a "calculate then write answer" operator rather than a statement of equality — this makes verification feel arbitrary.
🔧 Always have students evaluate LHS and RHS separately, then compare. Establish "LHS = ___ , RHS = ___ , LHS = RHS ✓" as the non-negotiable verification structure.
Misconception 4 · Quadratic Equations
"x² = 9 has only one solution: x = 3." Students routinely omit the negative root.
🔧 Ask: "What other number, when multiplied by itself, gives 9?" Use the number line mirror diagram in Topic 3 and have students verify both solutions by substitution. Explicitly work through (−3)² = 9.
Misconception 5 · Quadratic Equations
Students assume ax² = c always has two solutions, even when c < 0.
🔧 Pose x² = −4 and ask students to find a number that squares to give −4. Connect to the number line: there is no real number whose square is negative. Use this to motivate "no real solution" as a valid outcome.

🎯 Differentiation Strategies

▼

🟢 Learning Support

  • Use concrete balance scale diagrams before symbolic methods
  • Provide equation-solving templates with step labels pre-printed
  • Sequence from one-step to two-step equations deliberately
  • Allow calculators for arithmetic — keep focus on the solving process
  • Supply a reference card of inverse operations
  • Colour-code the term being eliminated and the inverse operation applied

🟡 Extension

  • Introduce equations with fractional coefficients or non-integer solutions
  • Investigate why ax² = c has no real solution when c < 0
  • Connect x² = c to the graph of y = x² — how many intersections with y = c?
  • Challenge students to write their own real-world word problems
  • Explore reverse formula problems and multi-formula systems
  • Investigate 0, 1, or 2 solutions across different equation types

How this resource supports mixed-ability classes:

  • → Tiered practice sets (Foundation / Core / Extension) — students self-select or teacher assigns
  • → Step-reveal worked examples — students control how much modelling they see before attempting
