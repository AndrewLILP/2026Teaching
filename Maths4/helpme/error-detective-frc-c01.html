Fractions: Equivalence & Comparison

🔍 Case 1 — Simplifying a Fraction
A student simplifies 3045. Find the line containing the error.
Line 1 Factors of 30:  1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 10, 15, 30
Line 2 Factors of 45:  1, 3, 5, 9, 15, 45
Line 3 Common factors: 1, 3, 5, 15  →  HCF = 5
Line 4 30 ÷ 545 ÷ 5  =  69
🎯 Error Type: Incorrect HCF Identification

The common factors of 30 and 45 are 1, 3, 5, and 15, making 15 the highest common factor — not 5. The student stopped at 5 rather than continuing to find the true largest common factor. Using HCF = 15 simplifies the fraction correctly in a single step, with no need for a second simplification.

✅ Correct:  HCF(30, 45) = 15  →  30 ÷ 1545 ÷ 15  =  23
🔍 Case 2 — Comparing Fractions
A student compares 23 and 35 to decide which is larger. Find the error.
Line 1 I need a common denominator for 3 and 5.  LCM(3, 5) = 15.
Line 2 23  =  1015   (multiplied numerator and denominator by 5)
Line 3 35  =  815   (multiplied numerator and denominator by 3)
Line 4 Comparing:  1015  vs  815  →  10 > 8
Line 5 Therefore  23  >  35
🎯 Error Type: Incorrect Equivalent Fraction — Multiplication Error

To convert 35 to fifteenths, both numerator and denominator must be multiplied by 3. The denominator was converted correctly (5 × 3 = 15), but the numerator calculation is wrong: 3 × 3 = 9, not 8. The error may stem from adding instead of multiplying. The conclusion happens to be correct, but the working is not.

✅ Correct:   35  =  915   Comparing  1015  vs  915  →  23  >  35   ✓

Fractions: Operations

🔍 Case 1 — Adding Fractions
A student adds 23 + 34. Find the line containing the error.
Line 1 LCD(3, 4) = 12
Line 2 Convert 23:  multiply top and bottom by 4  →  812
Line 3 Convert 34:  multiply top and bottom by 4  →  1212
Line 4 812  +  1212  =  2012
Line 5 2012  = 1 and  812  = 1 and  23
🎯 Error Type: Wrong Multiplier When Converting to LCD

To convert 34 to twelfths, the denominator 4 must be multiplied by 3 (not 4) to reach 12. The student applied the same multiplier (×4) to both fractions. Each fraction requires its own multiplier — whatever it takes to bring that denominator to the LCD: 23 uses ×4 (because 3 × 4 = 12); 34 uses ×3 (because 4 × 3 = 12).

✅ Correct:   34  ×  33  =  912   →   812  +  912  =  1712  = 1 and  512
🔍 Case 2 — Dividing Fractions
A student calculates 56  ÷  34. Find the error.
Line 1 Dividing by a fraction — I'll use Keep, Change, Flip.
Line 2 Flip the first fraction: 65  — Change ÷ to ×:   65  ×  34
Line 3 Multiply:   6 × 35 × 4  =  1820
Line 4 Simplify: HCF(18, 20) = 2  →  910
🎯 Error Type: Flipped the Wrong Fraction

Keep, Change, Flip means: Keep the first fraction ( 56) unchanged, Change ÷ to ×, and Flip only the second fraction ( 34 → 43). The student flipped the first fraction instead. A quick estimate confirms the error: since 56 ≈ 1 and 34 ≈ 1, the answer should be near 1. The correct answer 1 19 ≈ 1.11 is close; the student's 910 = 0.9 is not.

✅ Correct:   56  ÷  34  =  56  ×  43  =  2018  =  109  = 1 and  19

Decimals & Number Types

🔍 Case 1 — Rounding Decimals
A student rounds 4.682 to 2 decimal places. Find the line containing the error.
Line 1 I'll round 4.682 to 2 decimal places.
Line 2 First, I'll round to 1 decimal place: the 2nd decimal is 8 ≥ 5, so 4.682 → 4.7
Line 3 Now round 4.7 to 2 decimal places: 4.7 = 4.70
Line 4 Answer: 4.70
🎯 Error Type: Rounding in Stages (Cascade Error)

The student rounded to 1 decimal place first (obtaining 4.7) and then rounded that result to 2 decimal places (obtaining 4.70). This introduces a cascade error — the intermediate rounding corrupts the final answer. The correct method is to round directly to the required number of decimal places in a single step, looking only at the digit immediately after the target position.

✅ Correct:  Round 4.682 to 2 dp  →  look at the 3rd decimal place: 2 < 5  →  keep the 2nd decimal unchanged  →  4.68
🔍 Case 2 — Converting a Recurring Decimal to a Percentage
A student converts 23 to a decimal and then a percentage. Find the error.
Line 1 23  = 2 ÷ 3.  Long division: 3 into 20 is 6 times (6 × 3 = 18), remainder 2.
Line 2 Remainder 2 repeats  →  the decimal is 0.666… = 0.6̄ (recurring)
Line 3 23  = 0.6̄  — rational, recurring decimal
Line 4 As a percentage:  0.6̄ × 100 = 6.6̄%
🎯 Error Type: Incorrect Decimal-to-Percentage Conversion

Converting a decimal to a percentage requires multiplying by 100, which moves the decimal point two places to the right. The student produced 6.6̄%, which results from moving the decimal only one place (equivalent to multiplying by 10). Applying the correct operation: 0.666… × 100 = 66.666…%.

✅ Correct:  0.6̄ × 100 = 66.6̄%   (equivalently 66 and 23%)

Percentages

🔍 Case 1 — Calculating Percentage Increase
A price increased from $80 to $100. A student calculates the percentage increase. Find the error.
Line 1 Percentage increase = (new value − original value) ÷ ??? × 100
Line 2 = (100 − 80) ÷ 100 × 100  =  20100  × 100 = 20%
Line 3 The price increased by 20%.
🎯 Error Type: Dividing by the New Value Instead of the Original

Percentage change is always measured relative to the original (starting) value — it describes how much the value changed compared to where it began. The student divided the $20 change by $100 (the new price) instead of $80 (the original price). Dividing by the new price gives the profit as a percentage of the selling price, which is a different calculation and not the percentage increase.

✅ Correct:  (100 − 80) ÷ 80 × 100 = 20 ÷ 80 × 100 = 25% increase
🔍 Case 2 — Successive Percentage Changes
A $200 item is increased by 20%, then decreased by 20%. A student calculates the final price. Find the error.
Line 1 After 20% increase:  $200 × 1.20 = $240
Line 2 20% decrease:  20% of $200 = $40,  so subtract $40 from $240
Line 3 Final price:  $240 − $40 = $200
Line 4 Conclusion: The price returns to its original value.
🎯 Error Type: Applying the Second Percentage to the Wrong Base

After the 20% increase, the price is $240 — this is now the current value and the correct base for the next calculation. The 20% decrease must be taken from $240, not from the original $200. Because 20% of $240 ($48) is greater than 20% of $200 ($40), the decrease removes more than the increase added, resulting in a net loss of 4%.

✅ Correct:  20% of $240 = $48  →  $240 − $48 = $192
Or:  $200 × 1.20 × 0.80 = $200 × 0.96 = $192   (a 4% net decrease)
