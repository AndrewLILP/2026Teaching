Factorisation

Identifying factors of algebraic terms, HCF, factorising expressions, and verifying by expansion

Stage 4 — Years 7–8

📚 Vocabulary — tap a card to flip

Factorisation is the inverse of expansion — you are undoing the distributive law. The vocabulary here connects back to what you already know about factors from arithmetic.
Factor
A number or term that divides exactly into another
tap to flip ↩

Factor

DefinitionA number, pronumeral, or term that divides exactly into another with no remainder. Every term has multiple factors.
ExamplesFactors of 12: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12. Factors of 6x: 1, 2, 3, 6, x, 2x, 3x, 6x.
Non-examples5 is not a factor of 12 (12 ÷ 5 is not a whole number). y is not a factor of 6x (y does not divide into x).
Key ideaEvery term has at least two factors: 1 and itself. Factors always come in pairs.
Highest Common Factor (HCF)
The largest factor shared by all terms
tap to flip ↩

Highest Common Factor

DefinitionThe largest factor that divides exactly into every term in the expression. Both a numerical HCF and an algebraic HCF may exist.
ExamplesHCF of 6x and 9: 3. HCF of 4a² and 6a: 2a. HCF of 10xy and 15x: 5x.
Non-examples6 is a common factor of 12x and 18, but not the HCF — 6 is the HCF. (Check: is there a larger one? No.)
Key ideaFind the HCF of the coefficients, then find the common algebraic factor (lowest power of each common letter).
Factorise
Write an expression as a product of its factors
tap to flip ↩

Factorise

DefinitionTo rewrite an algebraic expression as a product: the HCF multiplied by a bracket containing the remaining factors.
ExamplesFactorise 6x + 9: → 3(2x + 3). Factorise 4a² + 6a: → 2a(2a + 3).
Non-examples3x + 4 cannot be factorised (HCF of 3 and 4 is 1 — factoring out 1 is trivial).
Key ideaFactorising is the reverse of expanding. It converts a sum into a product.
Common Factor
A factor shared by every term in the expression
tap to flip ↩

Common Factor

DefinitionA factor that divides exactly into every term in an expression. There may be several common factors — we want the highest (HCF).
ExamplesFor 12x and 8x: common factors include 1, 2, 4, x, 2x, 4x. The HCF is 4x.
Non-examples6 is a common factor of 12 and 18 but not of 12 and 10 (10 ÷ 6 is not a whole number).
Key ideaTo be a common factor, a term must divide into EVERY term. If it misses even one, it is not a common factor.
Algebraic Factor
A pronumeral or expression that is a factor
tap to flip ↩

Algebraic Factor

DefinitionA pronumeral or product of pronumerals that divides exactly into a term. The algebraic factor is the common letter part at its lowest power.
ExamplesIn 4a² and 6a: the algebraic factor is a (lowest power: a¹). In 10xy and 15x: algebraic factor is x.
Non-examplesIn 4x + 6: there is no algebraic factor common to both terms (6 has no x).
Key ideaUse the lowest power of each letter that appears in ALL terms.
Verify by Expansion
Check the answer by expanding back out
tap to flip ↩

Verify by Expansion

DefinitionAfter factorising, apply the distributive law to expand the result. If you get back the original expression, the factorisation is correct.
ExampleFactorise: 6x + 9 = 3(2x + 3). Verify: 3(2x + 3) = 6x + 9 ✓
Non-exampleIf expansion gives 6x + 6 ≠ 6x + 9, the factorisation has an error.
Key ideaVerification is required by the syllabus and takes only seconds — always do it.

A — Factors of an algebraic term

Finding all factors systematically

For the term 12ab, list factors by combining the numerical factors of 12 with each algebraic factor of ab:

Algebraic parts:   1 (no letter), a, b, ab

24 factors in total — numerical factors shown in blue, algebraic factors in green. 12ab itself is always a factor of itself.

B — Finding the HCF

Step 1: HCF of the coefficients

Find the HCF of the number parts using factor lists or prime factorisation.

HCF(6, 9) = 3  ←  for 6x + 9
HCF(4, 6) = 2  ←  for 4a² + 6a
HCF(10, 15, 5) = 5  ←  for 10xy − 15x + 5x²

Step 2: Common algebraic factor

Find letters common to ALL terms — use the lowest power of each.

6x and 9:   9 has no x  →  no alg. factor
4a² and 6a:   both have a  →  a¹ = a
10xy and 15x:   both have x  →  x
💡 Combining both steps: HCF = (numerical HCF) × (algebraic common factor). So for 4a² + 6a: HCF = 2 × a = 2a. For 10xy − 15x: HCF = 5 × x = 5x.

C — Factorisation as the inverse of expansion

FACTORISED FORM 3(2x + 3) ⇌ 6x + 9 EXPANDED FORM
← Factorise (this chapter) Expand (previous chapter) →

Both forms are equivalent — they give the same value for every value of x. Factorisation undoes expansion, and expansion verifies factorisation.

⚠️ Always extract the FULL HCF. Writing 6x + 9 = 3(2x + 3) is correct. Writing 6x + 9 = 1(6x + 9) or leaving it as is are not useful factorisations. Incomplete factorisation — e.g. 2(2a² + 3a) for 4a² + 6a — is wrong because the bracket still has a common factor (a) that was not extracted.

📌 Factorisation — four-step method

  • Step 1 — Identify the terms. Separate the expression at each + or − sign. Keep each sign with its term.
  • Step 2 — Find the HCF. Find the HCF of the coefficients (numbers), then find the common algebraic factor (lowest power of each letter that appears in ALL terms). The full HCF = both combined.
  • Step 3 — Write the factorised form. Place the HCF outside the bracket. Inside, write each term divided by the HCF. Check signs carefully.
  • Step 4 — Verify by expansion. Apply the distributive law to your answer. If you recover the original expression exactly, the factorisation is correct.

Example 1 — Listing Factors of a Term

List all factors of  12a²b
Step 1 — List all numerical factors of 12
1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12

Step 2 — Identify the algebraic parts
Letters present: a² and b   →   algebraic parts: 1, a, a², b, ab, a²b

Example 2 — Numerical HCF Factorisation

Foundation
Factorise  6x + 9   and verify your answer by expansion.
Step 1 — Find the HCF of the coefficients
Coefficients: 6 and 9   →   HCF = 3
Factors of 6: 1, 2, 3, 6. Factors of 9: 1, 3, 9. Largest in common: 3.
Step 2 — Check for a common algebraic factor
6x contains x;   9 contains no x   →   no algebraic common factor
A common algebraic factor must appear in EVERY term. Since 9 has no pronumeral, there is no algebraic factor to extract.
Step 3 — Divide each term by the HCF (3)
6x ÷ 3 = 2x  |  9 ÷ 3 = 3
These become the terms inside the bracket. The HCF goes outside.
Step 4 — Write the factorised form
6x + 9 = 3(2x + 3)
HCF outside, remaining terms inside the bracket.
Step 5 — Verify by expansion
3(2x + 3) = 3 × 2x + 3 × 3 = 6x + 9   ✓
✅ Verification: expanding 3(2x + 3) returns 6x + 9. The factorisation is correct.
Always verify by expanding your answer. If expansion gives back the original expression, you are correct.
✅ 6x + 9 = 3(2x + 3)

Example 3 — Algebraic HCF Factorisation

Core
Factorise  4a² + 6a   and verify.
Step 1 — HCF of the coefficients
HCF(4, 6) = 2
Factors of 4: 1, 2, 4. Factors of 6: 1, 2, 3, 6. Largest in common: 2.
Step 2 — Common algebraic factor
4a² has a²  |  6a has a¹   →   common factor = a¹ = a
Both terms contain a. The lowest power of a across both terms is a¹. Take the lowest power — the a² in the first term does not force a² into the HCF, because 6a only has a¹.
Step 3 — Combine: HCF = 2a
Full HCF = 2 × a = 2a
Multiply the numerical HCF (2) by the algebraic HCF (a) to get the complete HCF.
Step 4 — Divide each term by 2a
4a² ÷ 2a = 2a  |  6a ÷ 2a = 3
4 ÷ 2 = 2 and a² ÷ a = a, giving 2a. 6 ÷ 2 = 3 and a ÷ a = 1, giving 3.
Step 5 — Write and verify
4a² + 6a = 2a(2a + 3)
✅ Verify: 2a(2a + 3) = 2a × 2a + 2a × 3 = 4a² + 6a ✓
Expanding 2a(2a + 3) recovers 4a² + 6a. The factorisation is correct.
✅ 4a² + 6a = 2a(2a + 3)

Example 4 — Three-Term Factorisation

Core
Factorise  10x²y − 15xy + 5xy²   and verify.
Step 1 — HCF of coefficients 10, 15, 5
HCF(10, 15, 5) = 5
5 divides into 10 (twice), into 15 (three times), and into 5 (once). It is the largest such number.
Step 2 — Common algebraic factor across all three terms
10x²y: has x² and y  |  15xy: has x and y  |  5xy²: has x and y²
Common to all: x¹ and y¹   →   algebraic HCF = xy
x appears in all three (lowest power x¹). y appears in all three (lowest power y¹). So the algebraic HCF is xy.
Step 3 — Full HCF = 5xy. Divide each term
10x²y ÷ 5xy = 2x
−15xy ÷ 5xy = −3
5xy² ÷ 5xy = y
Divide coefficients and pronumerals separately for each term. Keep the sign of each term — the second term remains negative.
Step 4 — Write and verify
10x²y − 15xy + 5xy² = 5xy(2x − 3 + y)
✅ Verify: 5xy(2x − 3 + y) = 10x²y − 15xy + 5xy² ✓
Check by distributing 5xy to all three terms inside the bracket.
✅ 10x²y − 15xy + 5xy² = 5xy(2x − 3 + y)

Example 5 — Three-Term with Two-Variable HCF

Extension
Factorise  12a²b − 8ab² + 4ab   and verify.
Step 1 — HCF of coefficients 12, 8, 4
HCF(12, 8, 4) = 4
4 divides into 12 (three times), 8 (twice), and 4 (once). It is the largest number that divides all three.
Step 2 — Common algebraic factor
12a²b: a² and b  |  −8ab²: a and b²  |  4ab: a and b
Lowest power of a: a¹  |  Lowest power of b: b¹   →   algebraic HCF = ab
a appears in all three terms (powers: 2, 1, 1 — take the lowest: a¹). b appears in all three (powers: 1, 2, 1 — take the lowest: b¹). Algebraic HCF = ab.
Step 3 — Full HCF = 4ab. Divide each term
12a²b ÷ 4ab = 3a
−8ab² ÷ 4ab = −2b
4ab ÷ 4ab = 1
The last term 4ab ÷ 4ab = 1. This is critical: when a term equals the HCF exactly, it leaves 1 inside the bracket — not 0, not nothing. Write it as 1.
Step 4 — Write and verify
12a²b − 8ab² + 4ab = 4ab(3a − 2b + 1)
✅ Verify: 4ab × 3a = 12a²b;   4ab × (−2b) = −8ab²;   4ab × 1 = 4ab ✓
All three products match the original terms exactly. The factorisation is correct.
✅ 12a²b − 8ab² + 4ab = 4ab(3a − 2b + 1)

🔍 Error Detective — find the mistake

Each case has exactly one error. The most common factorisation errors involve not extracting the full HCF, or making a sign error inside the bracket.
🔍 Case 1 — Factorising 4a² + 6a
Task: Factorise   4a² + 6a

Click the line that contains the error:

Line 1:   HCF of coefficients 4 and 6: HCF = 2
Line 2:   No algebraic common factor needed — 2 is the HCF
Line 3:   4a² ÷ 2 = 2a²   and   6a ÷ 2 = 3a
Line 4:   4a² + 6a = 2(2a² + 3a)
Line 5:   Verify: 2(2a² + 3a) = 4a² + 6a ✓ — factorisation complete
❌ The error is on Line 2 (confirmed by Line 5)

Line 2 claims there is no algebraic common factor. This is wrong — both 4a² and 6a contain the letter a. The algebraic HCF is a¹ = a, making the full HCF = 2a, not just 2.

The verification on Line 5 passes (2(2a² + 3a) = 4a² + 6a is true), which is why this error is so easy to miss. But the factorisation 2(2a² + 3a) is incomplete — the expression inside the bracket still has a common factor of a.

  • A verification check only confirms the algebra is equivalent — it does not confirm the HCF is fully extracted.
  • Always check: does the expression inside the bracket still have a common factor? If yes, the factorisation is incomplete.
✅ Correct: HCF = 2a (numerical HCF 2, algebraic HCF a)
4a² + 6a = 2a(2a + 3)
Verify: 2a(2a + 3) = 4a² + 6a ✓

Error pattern: extracting only the numerical HCF and missing the common algebraic factor.

🔍 Case 2 — Factorising 10xy − 15x
Task: Factorise   10xy − 15x

Click the line that contains the error:

Line 1:   HCF of 10 and 15: HCF = 5
Line 2:   Common algebraic factor: x appears in both terms → algebraic HCF = x
Line 3:   Full HCF = 5x
Line 4:   10xy ÷ 5x = 2y   and   15x ÷ 5x = 3   →   answer: 5x(2y + 3)
Line 5:   Verify: 5x(2y + 3) = 10xy + 15x   ✗   (does not match original)
❌ The error is on Line 4

The working correctly identifies the HCF as 5x, but then writes 5x(2y + 3) instead of 5x(2y − 3). The second term in the original expression is −15x (negative). When divided by 5x: −15x ÷ 5x = −3, not +3.

The verification on Line 5 actually catches this error — it shows the expansion gives 10xy + 15x, which doesn't match the original 10xy − 15x. This demonstrates why verifying is so important.

  • The sign of each term inside the bracket comes from the division: keep the sign of the original term.
  • −15x ÷ 5x = −3 (negative ÷ positive = negative)
✅ Correct:
10xy ÷ 5x = 2y   and   (−15x) ÷ 5x = −3
10xy − 15x = 5x(2y − 3)
Verify: 5x(2y − 3) = 10xy − 15x ✓

Error pattern: dropping the negative sign when dividing a negative term by the HCF. Verification catches this every time.

📖 Word Problems — Newman's Framework

Each problem asks you to build an algebraic expression from a context, identify the HCF, factorise, and verify. Factorisation reveals structure that the expanded form hides.

Problem 1 — Rectangle Dimensions

1-step
A rectangle has an area of (12x + 8) cm². Factorise the area expression to find a possible pair of dimensions for the rectangle.
① READ
I need to write 12x + 8 as a _____________ (HCF × bracket). Each factor gives one _____________ of the rectangle.
② DEFINE
Terms: 12x and 8. HCF of 12 and 8 = _____. Common algebraic factor? _____________
③ FACTORISE
12x ÷ _____ = _____   and   8 ÷ _____ = _____
④ VERIFY
Expand your answer: _____(_____ + _____) = _____________   ✓ or ✗?
⑤ ANSWER
The rectangle could have dimensions _____ cm and (_____ + _____) cm.
✅ Full Solution
① Read: factorise 12x + 8 to find a product (two factors = two dimensions)
② HCF of 12 and 8 = 4. No common algebraic factor (8 has no x).
③ 12x ÷ 4 = 3x;   8 ÷ 4 = 2   →   12x + 8 = 4(3x + 2)
④ Verify: 4(3x + 2) = 12x + 8 ✓
⑤ Dimensions: width = 4 cm, length = (3x + 2) cm

Note: other valid pairs exist (e.g. width = 2, length = 6x + 4) but 4(3x + 2) uses the HCF and gives the simplest factorised form.

Problem 2 — Dividing Plants into Groups

2-step
A gardener has 15mn seedlings of one type and 10n seedlings of another. She wants to divide all the plants into identical groups — each group must have the same algebraic composition. How many groups can she make, and what does each group contain?
① READ
The total seedlings can be written as _____________ + _____________. Factorising gives the number of groups (outside) and the contents of each group (inside).
② DEFINE
m = ?   n = ?   HCF of 15mn and 10n = _____
③ FACTORISE
15mn ÷ _____ = _____   and   10n ÷ _____ = _____
④ VERIFY
Expand to check: _____(_____+_____) = _____________
⑤ ANSWER
She can make _____ groups. Each group contains _____ of the first type and _____ of the second type.
✅ Full Solution
① Total = 15mn + 10n
② HCF of 15 and 10 = 5. Algebraic HCF: n appears in both (lowest power n¹). Full HCF = 5n.
③ 15mn ÷ 5n = 3m;   10n ÷ 5n = 2   →   15mn + 10n = 5n(3m + 2)
④ Verify: 5n(3m + 2) = 15mn + 10n ✓
⑤ She can make 5n groups. Each group has 3m of the first type and 2 of the second type.

Problem 3 — Three-Term Factorisation in Context

Multi-step
A designer tiles a wall using three sections. The sections have areas 6a²b cm², 9ab cm², and 3ab² cm². Write a fully factorised expression for the total area, verify it, and find the total area when a = 2 and b = 3.
① READ
Total area = _____ + _____ + _____. I need to factorise, verify, then substitute a = _____ and b = _____.
② FIND HCF
Numerical HCF of 6, 9, 3 = _____  |  Common algebraic factor: a appears in all (lowest power: _____), b appears in all (lowest power: _____)  |  Full HCF = _____
③ DIVIDE EACH TERM
6a²b ÷ 3ab = _____  |  9ab ÷ 3ab = _____  |  3ab² ÷ 3ab = _____
④ VERIFY BY EXPANSION
3ab(_____ + _____ + _____) = _____ + _____ + _____   ✓ or ✗?
⑤ EVALUATE when a = 2, b = 3
3(___)(___)(2(___) + ___ + ___) = _____ cm²
✅ Full Solution
① Total = 6a²b + 9ab + 3ab²
② HCF(6,9,3) = 3; algebraic HCF = ab (a¹ and b¹ are lowest powers); Full HCF = 3ab
③ 6a²b ÷ 3ab = 2a;   9ab ÷ 3ab = 3;   3ab² ÷ 3ab = b
③ Factorised: 3ab(2a + 3 + b)
④ Verify: 3ab × 2a = 6a²b; 3ab × 3 = 9ab; 3ab × b = 3ab² → 6a²b + 9ab + 3ab² ✓
⑤ When a = 2, b = 3: 3(2)(3)(2×2 + 3 + 3) = 18(4 + 3 + 3) = 18 × 10 = 180 cm²

✅ Self-Marking Practice

Foundation: Identify factors, find the HCF of numbers, and factorise with a numerical HCF only.
Q1. Which list correctly shows all the factors of 8x?
First list all numerical factors of 8. Then multiply each by x to get the algebraic factors. Count them up — how many in total?
Q2. What is the HCF of 12 and 8?
List all factors of both numbers. Find the largest number that appears in both lists.
Q3. Factorise   6x + 10
Find HCF(6, 10) first. Then divide BOTH terms by that HCF. Verify by expanding your answer.
Q4. What is the HCF of 4a and 6a?
Find the HCF of the numbers (4 and 6), then identify any common letters. Combine both parts.
Core: Factorise using algebraic HCF, including two-variable and three-term expressions.
Q1. Factorise   8x² + 12x
Find HCF(8, 12) for the coefficients, then find the common algebraic factor. After factorising, check: does the bracket still have a common factor?
Q2. Factorise   5a²b − 10ab
List what is common: HCF of 5 and 10, then letters common to both terms (with lowest powers). Multiply for the full HCF.
Q3. Factorise   6m + 9n − 12
Find HCF(6, 9, 12). All three terms must be divided by it — including the constant 12. Verify by expanding your answer.
Q4. Which expansion verifies that 4(3x − 2) is the correct factorisation of 12x − 8?
Expand 4(3x − 2) using the distributive law. Multiply 4 by each term inside the bracket, including its sign.
Extension: Multi-variable HCF, three-term expressions, incomplete factorisation identification, and error analysis.
Q1. Factorise   9x²y + 6xy² − 3xy
HCF of the coefficients (9, 6, 3). Then identify letters common to all three terms at their lowest power. Note what happens when a term equals the HCF exactly.
Q2. Which factorisation is NOT correct?
Verify each option by expanding. The incorrect one will not give back the original expression when expanded.
Q3. What is the HCF of   12a²b,   8ab²,   and   4a²b²?
Three steps: (1) HCF of the coefficients 12, 8, 4. (2) Lowest power of a across all three terms. (3) Lowest power of b across all three terms. Multiply the results.
Q4. A student factorises 6x² + 4x as 2(3x² + 2x). What error did they make?
After factorising, always check: does the expression inside the bracket have any common factor remaining? If yes, the factorisation is incomplete.
