Improper Fraction
numerator ≥ denominator

Definition

A fraction where the numerator is greater than or equal to the denominator. Its value is ≥ 1. It equals one whole or more.

Examples

74 = 134,  53 = 123,  94 = 214

Non-examples

34, 12 — proper fractions (numerator < denominator, value < 1)

Key Idea

To convert: multiply whole number by denominator, add numerator. Example: 234 = (2×4)+34 = 114

Reciprocal
the flip of a fraction
tap to flip ↩
Definition

The reciprocal of a fraction is found by swapping the numerator and denominator. Any number multiplied by its reciprocal equals 1.

Examples

Reciprocal of 25 is 52.   Reciprocal of 3 is 13.   Check: 25 × 52 = 1 ✅

Non-examples

The reciprocal of 0 does not exist — you cannot have a fraction with 0 as the denominator.

Key Idea

Dividing by a fraction is the same as multiplying by its reciprocal. "Keep, Change, Flip" — keep the first fraction, change ÷ to ×, flip the second fraction.

Common Denominator
essential for adding & subtracting

Definition

A shared denominator used when adding or subtracting fractions. Ensures all fractions represent equal-sized pieces before combining.

Examples

14 + 13: LCD = 12 → 312 + 412 = 712

Non-examples

You do NOT need a common denominator when multiplying or dividing fractions — only for adding and subtracting.

Key Idea

Adding or subtracting without a common denominator is like combining apples and oranges — you need the same unit first.

Definition

An approximation calculated before or after working out an exact answer. Used to check whether an answer is reasonable.

Examples

34 × 56 ≈ 1 × 1 = 1. Exact: 1524 = 58 ≈ 0.6. Both < 1 ✅

Non-examples

An estimate is not a guess — it's a deliberate, reasoned approximation using benchmark values like 0, 12, and 1.

Key Idea

Round each fraction to the nearest half (0, 12, or 1) before estimating. Always check if your answer is in the right ballpark.

Cancelling
simplifying before multiplying
tap to flip ↩
Definition

Dividing a numerator and a denominator by a common factor before multiplying fractions. Keeps numbers smaller and avoids simplifying a large result later.

Examples

45 × 36: Cancel 4 and 6 by ÷2 → 25 × 33 = 615 = 25

Non-examples

You can only cancel a numerator with a denominator (across fractions), NOT two numerators or two denominators with each other.

Key Idea

Cancelling before multiplying is optional but efficient — the answer is the same either way, just with smaller numbers to work with.

➕➖ Adding & Subtracting Fractions

You can only add or subtract fractions directly if they share the same denominator. If they don't, find the LCD first.

SituationStrategyExample
Same denominator Add/subtract numerators; keep denominator; simplify 38 + 28 = 58
Different denominators Find LCD → convert both → add/subtract → simplify 13 + 14 → LCD=12 → 712
Mixed numbers Convert to improper fractions first, or add whole and fraction parts separately 213 + 114 → 73 + 54 = 4312 = 3712
Whole number minus fraction Rewrite whole as an equivalent fraction with same denominator 3 − 58 = 248 − 58 = 198 = 238
🚫
Never do this: 12 + 13 ≠ 25 — adding numerators AND denominators is one of the most common fraction mistakes. The denominator describes the piece size; adding denominators would change what size piece you're counting.
✖️ Multiplying Fractions

Multiplying fractions is more direct than adding — no common denominator needed. Multiply numerators together and denominators together.

Step 1Convert any mixed numbers to improper fractions
Step 2Cancel any common factors (optional but efficient)
Step 3Multiply numerators × numerators and denominators × denominators
Step 4Simplify the result if needed
📐
Example: 23 × 910
Cancel: 2 and 10 share factor 2 → 13 × 95
Cancel: 3 and 9 share factor 3 → 11 × 35 = 35
⚠️
Effect of multiplying by a fraction less than 1: The result is smaller than the original. Example: 12 of 6 = 3 (half as big). Multiplying by a proper fraction scales a quantity down.
➗ Dividing Fractions — Keep, Change, Flip

Dividing by a fraction is equivalent to multiplying by its reciprocal. The three-step "Keep, Change, Flip" method captures this.

KeepKeep the first fraction exactly as it is
ChangeChange ÷ to ×
FlipWrite the reciprocal of the second fraction (swap its numerator and denominator)
ThenMultiply as normal and simplify
📐
Example: 34 ÷ 38
Keep: 34   Change: ×   Flip: 83
= 34 × 83 = 2412 = 2
⚠️
Effect of dividing by a fraction less than 1: The result is larger than the original. Example: 6 ÷ 12 = 12. "How many halves fit in 6?" — more pieces, bigger answer.
🚫
Critical mistake: Flipping the first fraction (the dividend) instead of the second (the divisor). Always flip the fraction after the ÷ sign.

📋 Key Rules — Fraction Operations

  • Add/Subtract: always find a common denominator first — then add/subtract the numerators only. Never add the denominators.
  • Multiply: no common denominator needed — multiply top × top and bottom × bottom. Simplify before or after.
  • Divide: Keep the first fraction, Change ÷ to ×, Flip the second fraction — then multiply.
  • Mixed numbers: convert to improper fractions before multiplying or dividing.
  • Multiplying by a fraction < 1 makes the result smaller. Dividing by a fraction < 1 makes the result larger.
  • Estimate first — round fractions to 0, ½, or 1 to check your answer is in the right range.

🔍Case 1 — Adding Fractions
A student adds 23 + 34. Find the line containing the error.
Line 1 LCD(3, 4) = 12
Line 2 Convert 23: multiply top and bottom by 4 → 812
Line 3 Convert 34: multiply top and bottom by 4 → 1212
Line 4 812 + 1212 = 2012
Line 5 2012 = 1812 = 123
🎯 Error Type: Wrong Multiplier When Converting to LCD

Line 3 is wrong. To convert 34 to twelfths, we need 4 × what = 12? The answer is 3, not 4. The student used the same multiplier (4) for both fractions — but the multiplier must match what each denominator needs to reach 12.

Using multiplier 4 on 34 gives 1216 — wrong denominator. To get ?12, multiply by 33.

✅ Correct: 34 × 33 = 912 → 812 + 912 = 1712 = 1512
🔍Case 2 — Dividing Fractions
A student calculates 56 ÷ 34. Find the error.
Line 1 Dividing by a fraction — I'll use Keep, Change, Flip.
Line 2 Flip the first fraction: 65   Change ÷ to ×: 65 × 34
Line 3 Multiply: 6 × 35 × 4 = 1820
Line 4 Simplify: HCF(18, 20) = 2 → 910
🎯 Error Type: Flipped the Wrong Fraction

Line 2 is wrong. "Keep, Change, Flip" means: Keep the first fraction (56) unchanged, Change ÷ to ×, and Flip the second fraction (34 becomes 43). The student flipped the first fraction instead.

An estimate confirms the error: 56 ≈ 1 and 34 ≈ 1, so the answer should be close to 1 ÷ 1 = 1. The correct answer 119 is close to 1, while 910 is less than 1 — dividing by a number less than 1 should give a result bigger than what we started with.

✅ Correct: 56 ÷ 34 = 56 × 43 = 2018 = 109 = 119
