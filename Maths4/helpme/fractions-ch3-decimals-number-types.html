💡 Big Question: Can every number be written as a fraction — and what happens when one can't?

Rational Number

Definition

A number that can be expressed as pq where p and q are integers and q ≠ 0. All fractions, terminating decimals, recurring decimals, and whole numbers are rational.

Examples

34 = 0.75,  0.3 = 13,  −5 = −51,  62% = 62100

Non-examples

√2, π, e — these cannot be written as exact fractions; their decimal expansions never terminate or repeat.

Key Idea

Every whole number, fraction, terminating decimal, and recurring decimal is rational. The rational numbers fill most of the number line — but not all of it.

√2  π  e
Irrational Number
cannot be written as p/q
tap to flip ↩
Definition

A number that cannot be expressed as pq for any integers p and q (q ≠ 0). Its decimal expansion is non-terminating and non-repeating.

Examples

√2 ≈ 1.41421356…,  π ≈ 3.14159265…,  √3 ≈ 1.73205…,  e ≈ 2.71828…

Non-examples

√4 = 2 (rational), √9 = 3 (rational), √(1/4) = 0.5 (rational). Only surds of non-perfect-squares are irrational.

Key Idea

Irrational numbers exist on the number line — we just can't pin them down with a fraction. We use approximations (≈) to locate them.

0.375 = 38
Terminating Decimal
finite number of decimal digits
tap to flip ↩
Definition

A decimal that ends after a finite number of digits. When written as a fraction in lowest terms, its denominator has only 2 and/or 5 as prime factors.

Examples

0.5 = 12,  0.25 = 14,  0.375 = 38,  0.2 = 15

Non-examples

0.3 = 13 (denominator 3 is not 2 or 5, so it's recurring, not terminating)

Key Idea

Check the denominator (in simplest form): if its only prime factors are 2 and 5, the decimal terminates. Otherwise, it recurs.

Recurring Decimal
a repeating digit pattern
tap to flip ↩
Definition

A decimal in which one or more digits repeat infinitely. Notation: a dot above a single repeating digit, or dots above the first and last digit of the repeating block (vinculum = overline).

Examples

13 = 0.3,  23 = 0.6,  17 = 0.142857,  512 = 0.416

Non-examples

0.3333 (this is terminating — it stops after 4 digits). 0.3 has infinitely many 3s.

Key Idea

All recurring decimals are rational — they can always be converted to exact fractions. The repeating block is called the period.

3.847 ≈ 3.85
Rounding
reducing decimal digits to a set accuracy
tap to flip ↩
Definition

Expressing a number to a given number of decimal places by looking at the next digit: if it is ≥ 5, round up; if it is < 5, round down (keep).

Examples

3.847 to 2 dp → look at 3rd dp (7 ≥ 5) → round up → 3.85
3.841 to 2 dp → look at 3rd dp (1 < 5) → round down → 3.84

Non-examples

Rounding 3.847 in stages (first to 1 dp, then to 2 dp) gives wrong answers — always round directly to the required place.

Key Idea

Look at only ONE digit beyond the required place to decide. Never round a number in multiple stages.

≈
Approximation
a close but not exact value
tap to flip ↩
Definition

A value that is close to but not exactly equal to a given quantity. The symbol ≈ means "approximately equal to".

Examples

π ≈ 3.14,  √2 ≈ 1.41,  13 ≈ 0.333 (to 3 dp)

Non-examples

Exact values use = not ≈. Writing 14 ≈ 0.25 is incorrect — 0.25 is the exact value.

Key Idea

Approximations are appropriate when an exact answer is not possible (irrational numbers), when working with measurements, or when a simplified result is more practical than an exact one.

🔢 Rounding Decimals

To round a decimal to a given number of decimal places (dp), identify the digit one place beyond the required precision and apply the rounding rule.

Step 1Identify the required decimal place
Step 2Look at the digit one place further right
Step 3If that digit ≥ 5, round up the last kept digit. If < 5, keep it unchanged.
Step 4Drop all digits after the required place
📐
Round 6.4382 to each level:
To 1 dp: look at 2nd dp (3 < 5) → keep → 6.4
To 2 dp: look at 3rd dp (8 ≥ 5) → round up → 6.44
To 3 dp: look at 4th dp (2 < 5) → keep → 6.438
⚠️
When is an approximation better than an exact answer? When the exact value is irrational (e.g., √7), when a measurement can only be taken to limited precision, or when a long decimal is impractical (e.g., giving a price as $3.50, not $3.497).
🚫
Never round in stages: 6.452 rounded to 1 dp directly → look at 4 < 5 → 6.4 ✅. Rounding first to 2 dp (6.45) then to 1 dp (6.5) gives the wrong answer ❌.

🔑
Which fractions terminate? A fraction pq in simplest form terminates if and only if q has no prime factors other than 2 and 5.
Denominator 8 = 2³ → terminates ✅   Denominator 6 = 2 × 3 → recurring (has factor 3) ✅
Denominator 12 = 2² × 3 → recurring   Denominator 25 = 5² → terminates
✅
Both types are rational: Terminating decimals can be written as fractions over powers of 10. Recurring decimals can also always be converted to exact fractions using algebraic techniques.

♾️ Irrational Numbers

Irrational numbers cannot be expressed as pq. Their decimal expansions go on forever with no repeating block.

SymbolNameApproximate valueWhere encountered
πPi3.14159265…Circle circumference and area
√2Square root of 21.41421356…Diagonal of a unit square
√3Square root of 31.73205080…Height of equilateral triangle
eEuler's number2.71828182…Natural growth and logarithms
📍
Locating irrationals on the number line: Since √2 ≈ 1.414, it sits between 1 and 2, closer to 1.4 than 1.5. Since π ≈ 3.142, it sits just past 3.1, between 3 and 4. We mark their approximate position with ≈.
💡
Rational + Irrational = Irrational. For example, 1 + √2 and 2π are also irrational. But irrational × irrational can sometimes be rational: √2 × √2 = 2 (rational).
📊 Ordering Fractions, Decimals and Percentages

To compare a mixed set of FDP values (including negatives), convert everything to the same form first — decimals are usually easiest — then order.

Step 1Convert all values to decimals
Step 2For recurring decimals, use enough digits to compare accurately
Step 3Order the decimals (remembering negatives: more negative = smaller)
Step 4Rewrite the answer using the original forms
📐
Example: Order 37, 0.43, 42%, 0.4 from smallest to largest.
Convert: 37 ≈ 0.4286…,  0.43,  42% = 0.42,  0.4 = 0.4444…
Order: 0.42 < 0.4286 < 0.43 < 0.4444
Answer: 42% < 37 < 0.43 < 0.4
⚠️
Negative numbers: On a number line, −0.8 is to the left of −0.75, so −0.8 < −0.75. A more negative value is always smaller, even though its absolute value is larger.

📋 Key Facts — Number Types

  • Terminating decimal: denominator (in simplest form) has only 2s and 5s as prime factors.
  • Recurring decimal: denominator has a prime factor other than 2 or 5 — still rational.
  • All fractions and percentages are rational — they can always be written as p/q.
  • Irrational numbers have non-terminating, non-repeating decimals — they cannot equal any fraction.
  • Rounding: look at ONE digit beyond the required place; never round in stages.
  • Ordering mixed FDP: convert to decimals first for reliable comparison.

🔍Case 1 — Rounding Decimals
A student rounds 4.682 to 2 decimal places. Find the error.
Line 1 I'll round 4.682 to 2 decimal places.
Line 2 First, I'll round to 1 decimal place: the 2nd decimal is 8 ≥ 5, so the 1st decimal rounds up: 4.682 → 4.7
Line 3 Now round 4.7 to 2 decimal places: 4.7 = 4.70
Line 4 Answer: 4.70
🎯 Error Type: Rounding in Stages (Cascade Error)

Line 2 is wrong. The student rounded to 1 decimal place first (4.682 → 4.7), then rounded that result to 2 decimal places (4.7 → 4.70). This gives a wrong answer because intermediate rounding introduced an error.

The correct method: round directly to the required number of decimal places in one step. To round 4.682 to 2 dp, look only at the 3rd decimal place (2). Since 2 < 5, the 2nd decimal stays as 8.

✅ Correct: 4.682 → look at 3rd dp = 2 → 2 < 5 → keep 2nd dp unchanged → 4.68
🔍Case 2 — Converting a Recurring Decimal to a Percentage
A student converts 23 to a decimal and percentage. Find the error.
Line 1 23 = 2 ÷ 3. I'll do the long division: 3 goes into 20 six times (6 × 3 = 18), remainder 2.
Line 2 Remainder 2 repeats → the decimal is 0.666… = 0.6
Line 3 23 = 0.6 (rational, recurring decimal) ✓
Line 4 As a percentage: 0.6 × 100 = 6.6%
🎯 Error Type: Incorrect Decimal-to-Percentage Conversion

Line 4 is wrong. To convert a decimal to a percentage, multiply by 100. The student wrote 0.6 × 100 = 6.6%, but 0.666… × 100 = 66.666… = 66.6%. It appears the student moved the decimal point one place instead of two (multiplied by 10 instead of 100).

✅ Correct: 0.6 × 100 = 66.6% (or equivalently 66⅔%)

📖 Problem 3 — Extension
A designer is tiling a square floor with side length √7 metres. (a) Classify √7 as rational or irrational and explain your reasoning. (b) Estimate √7 to 2 decimal places by testing squares of values between 2 and 3. (c) The tiles are each 0.6 m wide. Approximately how many tiles fit along one side? (d) The exact area cannot be expressed as a fraction — explain why.
1
READ
A square floor has side √7 m. This problem involves _______________, _______________, and area concepts.
2
DEFINE
I need to (a) classify √7, (b) estimate √7 to 2 dp, (c) divide by _______________ to find tiles, (d) explain why √7 × √7 = 7 ≠ fraction × fraction.
3
WRITE
Test: 2.6² = ___   2.7² = ___   2.64² ≈ ___   2.65² ≈ ___
Tiles: √7 ÷ 0.6 ≈ ___ ÷ 0.6 ≈ ___
4
SOLVE
2.6² = 6.76, 2.7² = 7.29 → √7 is between 2.6 and 2.7
2.64² = 6.9696, 2.65² = 7.0225 → √7 ≈ ___ (to 2 dp)
Tiles ≈ ___ ÷ 0.6 ≈ ___
5
ANSWER
√7 is _______________ because _______________. √7 ≈ ___ m. About ___ tiles fit. The area = 7 m², which IS rational — but the side length √7 is not.
(a) Classification: √7 is irrational. Since 7 is not a perfect square (2² = 4 and 3² = 9, so no whole number squares to 7), its square root cannot be written as a fraction. Its decimal expansion is non-terminating and non-repeating.

(b) Estimating √7:
2.6² = 6.76 (too small)   2.7² = 7.29 (too big) → between 2.6 and 2.7
2.64² = 6.9696 (too small)   2.65² = 7.0225 (too big) → between 2.64 and 2.65
Since 7 is closer to 7.0225 than 6.9696, √7 ≈ 2.65 (to 2 dp)

(c) Tiles: 2.65 ÷ 0.6 ≈ 4.4 → approximately 4 tiles fit along one side (with a small gap)

(d) Area explanation: Area = √7 × √7 = 7 m². This IS rational (a whole number). Even though the side length √7 is irrational, its square happens to be rational. The side length itself cannot be expressed as an exact fraction.
