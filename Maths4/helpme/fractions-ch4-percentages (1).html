Definition

A percentage is a ratio expressed as a fraction of 100. The symbol % means "per hundred". It allows fair comparison of different-sized quantities.

Examples

75% = 75100 = 0.75   12% = 12100 = 0.12   130% = 1.3 (more than 1 whole)

Non-examples

A ratio of 3:4 is not already a percentage — it must be converted: 3÷4 × 100 = 75%.

Key Idea

To convert to a percentage: multiply by 100. To convert from a percentage: divide by 100. Percentages above 100% mean the part is larger than the whole.

Multiplier
a single factor for % change
tap to flip ↩
Definition

A single decimal number that applies a percentage increase or decrease in one step. For an increase of p%: multiply by (1 + p/100). For a decrease of p%: multiply by (1 − p/100).

Examples

15% increase → × 1.15   20% decrease → × 0.80   GST (10%) → × 1.10   100% increase → × 2.00

Non-examples

Adding p% of the value separately to the original, rather than multiplying — both methods give the same answer, but the multiplier method is one step.

Key Idea

The multiplier for a decrease is always between 0 and 1. The multiplier for an increase is always greater than 1. To reverse a percentage change, divide by the multiplier.

SP − CP = Profit
Profit & Loss
comparing selling and cost price
tap to flip ↩
Definition

Profit = Selling Price (SP) − Cost Price (CP) when SP > CP.
Loss = CP − SP when CP > SP.
Percentage profit or loss is usually expressed as a percentage of the cost price.

Examples

CP = $80, SP = $100 → Profit = $20 → Profit % = 20/80 × 100 = 25% (of cost price)

Non-examples

Profit as % of SP is different from % of CP: $20 profit on $100 SP = 20% of SP but 25% of CP = $80. Always state which you're using.

Key Idea

The syllabus requires both: profit/loss as % of cost price AND as % of selling price. These give different percentages from the same transaction.

GST
Goods and Services Tax
tap to flip ↩
Definition

The Goods and Services Tax (GST) is a 10% tax applied to most goods and services in Australia. The GST is added to the pre-tax price to give the final (inclusive) price.

Examples

Pre-GST price $240 → GST amount = $24 → GST-inclusive price = $264 (multiply by 1.10)
GST-inclusive $330 → pre-GST = $330 ÷ 1.10 = $300

Non-examples

Adding 10% of the GST-inclusive price (not pre-GST) gives the wrong GST amount — always apply the 10% to the pre-GST price.

Key Idea

To add GST: × 1.10. To remove GST from an inclusive price: ÷ 1.10 (not subtracting 10% from the inclusive price — that gives a different answer).

Unitary Method
find 1%, then scale
tap to flip ↩
Definition

A problem-solving strategy: first find the value of 1 unit (here, 1%), then multiply to find any required number of units (percentage).

Examples

Find 7% of $350:
1% of $350 = $3.50
7% = 7 × $3.50 = $24.50

Non-examples

Direct multiplication (350 × 0.07 = $24.50) gives the same answer — the unitary method is an alternative strategy, especially useful without a calculator.

Key Idea

The unitary method also works in reverse: if a certain % represents a given amount, divide to find 1%, then multiply to find 100% (the whole).

change ÷ original × 100
Percentage Change
the relative increase or decrease
tap to flip ↩
Definition

The amount of increase or decrease expressed as a percentage of the original value. Percentage change = new − originaloriginal × 100

Examples

Price: $80 → $100 → change = $20 → % change = 20/80 × 100 = 25% increase
Score: 60 → 48 → change = −12 → % change = −12/60 × 100 = −20% (20% decrease)

Non-examples

Dividing by the NEW value (not original) is a common error: 20/100 = 20% is wrong for the example above. Always divide by the original.

Key Idea

Percentage change is always relative to the starting point (original). A 25% increase from $80 and a 20% decrease from $100 have the same dollar amount ($20) but different percentage changes.

📊 Expressing One Quantity as a Percentage of Another

To find what percentage A is of B, divide A by B and multiply by 100. Always check that A and B are measured in the same units before dividing.

Step 1Ensure both quantities use the same units
Step 2Divide the part by the whole
Step 3Multiply by 100 to convert to %
📐
Example: Express 36 minutes as a percentage of 2 hours.
Convert: 2 hours = 120 minutes  (same units!)
Percentage = 36120 × 100 = 30%
🚫
Units trap: "36 out of 2" gives 1800%, not 30%. Always convert to the same unit before dividing. Hours vs minutes, dollars vs cents, kilometres vs metres — all common traps.
📈📉 Percentage Increase & Decrease — The Multiplier Method

The multiplier method applies a percentage change in a single multiplication — more efficient and less error-prone than finding the change amount separately.

ChangeMultiplierExampleCalculation
15% increase× 1.15$240 + 15%$240 × 1.15 = $276
20% decrease× 0.80$240 − 20%$240 × 0.80 = $192
100% increase× 2.00$240 doubled$240 × 2.00 = $480
10% decrease× 0.90$240 − 10%$240 × 0.90 = $216
💡
Why the multiplier works: A 15% increase means the new value is 115% of the original. 115% = 115/100 = 1.15. Multiplying by 1.15 gives "100% + 15%" in one step.
🔁
Reversing a percentage change (finding the original): If a price after a 20% increase is $240, find the original by dividing by the multiplier: $240 ÷ 1.20 = $200. Never subtract 20% from $240 — that gives $192, which is wrong.

🔢 Unitary Method & Problem-Solving Strategies

The unitary method finds the value of 1% (or 1 unit) first, then scales. It is especially useful when working without a calculator.

📐
Finding a percentage of a quantity:
"Find 7% of $630" → 1% of $630 = $6.30 → 7% = 7 × $6.30 = $44.10

Finding the whole from a percentage:
"If 12% of a quantity is $96, find the whole" → 1% = $96 ÷ 12 = $8 → 100% = 100 × $8 = $800
✅
Strategy summary:
Find P% of Q → Q × P/100 (or unitary: find 1%, scale up)
X is what % of Y? → X/Y × 100
Apply P% change → multiply by multiplier (1 ± P/100)
Find original before P% increase → new value ÷ (1 + P/100)

📋 Key Rules — Percentages

  • X as a % of Y: (X ÷ Y) × 100. Always use the same units for both values.
  • Find P% of Q: Q × P/100. Or: find 1% first (Q ÷ 100), then multiply by P.
  • % increase by P: multiply by (1 + P/100). % decrease by P: multiply by (1 − P/100).
  • Successive % changes are NOT additive: +20% then −20% gives 96% of original, not 100%.
  • GST: add 10% → × 1.10. Remove GST from inclusive price → ÷ 1.10.
  • Profit/loss %: divide by the original (usually cost price), not the new value.
  • Reverse percentage: divide by the multiplier to find the original value.

🔍Case 1 — Calculating Percentage Increase
A price increased from $80 to $100. A student calculates the percentage increase. Find the error.
Line 1 Percentage increase = new value − original value??? × 100
Line 2 = 100 − 80100 × 100 = 20100 × 100 = 20%
Line 3 The price increased by 20%.
🎯 Error Type: Dividing by the New Value Instead of the Original

Line 2 is wrong. Percentage change is always relative to the original value (the starting point). The student divided the $20 change by the new value ($100) instead of the original value ($80).

The answer 20% means "the change is 20% of the new price." But percentage increase means "the change as a percentage of what you started with."

✅ Correct: 100 − 8080 × 100 = 2080 × 100 = 25%
🔍Case 2 — Successive Percentage Changes
A $200 item is increased by 20%, then decreased by 20%. A student calculates the final price. Find the error.
Line 1 After 20% increase: $200 × 1.20 = $240
Line 2 20% decrease: 20% of $200 = $40, so subtract $40 from $240
Line 3 Final price: $240 − $40 = $200
Line 4 Conclusion: The price returns to its original value after a +20% then −20% change.
🎯 Error Type: Applying the Second Percentage to the Wrong Base

Line 2 is wrong. After the 20% increase, the new price is $240 — this becomes the new starting point. The 20% decrease must be applied to $240, not back to the original $200.

This is exactly the idea behind the Big Question! Each successive percentage applies to the current value, not the original. Because 20% of $240 is more than 20% of $200, the decrease wipes out more than the increase added.

✅ Correct: 20% decrease from $240 = $240 × 0.80 = $192 (not $200)
Overall: $200 × 1.20 × 0.80 = $200 × 0.96 = $192  → a net 4% decrease from the original.

Core — Apply the multiplier method; calculate profit/loss percentages; work with GST; find original values before a percentage change.
A $360 item is increased by 12%. Use the multiplier method to find the new price.
A 12% increase means the new price is 112% of the original. Multiplier = 1 + 12/100 = 1.12. Calculate $360 × 1.12.
A trader buys goods for $80 (cost price) and sells for $96 (selling price). What is the profit as a percentage of cost price?
Profit = SP − CP = $16. Profit % of cost = (profit ÷ cost price) × 100 = (16 ÷ 80) × 100.
A bicycle is priced at $550 (excluding GST). What is the GST-inclusive price?
GST is 10%. Adding 10% means multiplying by 1.10 (the price becomes 110% of the original).
After a 20% increase, a price is $216. What was the original price?
The multiplier for a 20% increase is 1.20. If new price = original × 1.20, then original = new price ÷ 1.20.

A price increases by 25% and then decreases by 20%. What is the net percentage change from the original?
Apply both multipliers: × 1.25 for the increase, then × 0.80 for the decrease. Multiply them: 1.25 × 0.80 = ? What does this tell you about the net change?
A trader marks up cost price by 30%, then offers a 25% discount on the marked price. The cost price is $100. What is the profit or loss as a percentage of cost price?
Marked price = $100 × 1.30. Sale price = marked price × 0.75. Compare the final sale price to the $100 cost price.
An item is sold for $150, making a profit of 25% on cost price. What was the cost price?
If profit is 25% of cost price, then SP = CP × 1.25. Rearrange: CP = SP ÷ 1.25. This is the reverse percentage method.

Unitary method: if 7% = $84, find 1% first (divide by 7), then find 100% (multiply by 100).
