Foundation Concepts
Expressions & Equations
The most important distinction in algebra. Learn it, then test yourself.
Concept What is the difference?

Expression

A mathematical phrase. It contains numbers, pronumerals, and operations — but no equals sign. You can evaluate or simplify it, but you cannot solve it.

3x + 5 2(n − 1)

Equation

A mathematical statement that two things are equal. It always contains an equals sign. You can solve it — finding the specific value that makes the statement true.

3x + 5 = 17 2n − 1 = 7
Key insight: An expression has a value that depends on the pronumeral — substitute in any number and you get a result. An equation has a solution — the specific value that makes it true. For example, in 3x + 5, if x = 4 the value is 17. In the equation 3x + 5 = 17, x = 4 is the only solution.

02
Algebraic Technique
Solving 2-Step Equations
Use inverse operations in reverse order to isolate the pronumeral. Integer and non-integer solutions.
Worked Example Solve 3x + 5 = 17

We undo what has been done to x using inverse operations — in reverse order to BEDMAS.

1
Write the equation: 3x + 5 = 17
2
Subtract 5 from both sides: 3x = 12
Subtraction undoes the +5
3
Divide both sides by 3: x = 4
Division undoes the ×3
4
Verify by substitution: 3(4) + 5 = 12 + 5 = 17 ✓
Always check your answer makes the equation true
The two steps: (1) Add or subtract to isolate the term containing the pronumeral. (2) Multiply or divide to isolate the pronumeral itself.

Extended Technique
Pronumerals on Both Sides
When the unknown appears on both sides, collect it on one side first — then solve as usual.
Worked Example Solve 5x + 3 = 3x + 11
New strategy: Subtract the smaller pronumeral term from both sides to collect all x terms on one side. Then solve the resulting 2-step equation.
1
Write the equation: 5x + 3 = 3x + 11
2
Subtract 3x from both sides: 2x + 3 = 11
All x terms are now on the left
3
Subtract 3 from both sides: 2x = 8
4
Divide both sides by 2: x = 4
5
Verify both sides: 5(4) + 3 = 23 and 3(4) + 11 = 23 ✓
Substitute into both expressions — they must match

📋 Teacher Guide — DDL Design Notes

▼

DDL Competency: Critical Thinking

🔎 Critical Thinking — reasoning, evidence-based decisions, problem-solving

Critical Thinking is embedded in the architecture of this resource in three ways:

  • Section 1 — Distinguish and evaluate: The classifier is not a pattern-matching task. Items like 7 = 3m − 2 (pronumeral on the right) and 5k = 6 − 2k (a teaser for Section 3) require students to reason about structure, not form. Students who look only for the equals sign will still get all 8, but item 4 challenges students who expect the unknown to appear on the left.
  • Sections 2–3 — Justify your technique: The visible tier switcher invites self-assessment. A high-value teacher move is asking students to defend their tier choice to a partner before they start: "Why did you choose Core?"
  • Section 4 — Structured reasoning under uncertainty: The 4-step template positions problem-solving as sequential evidence-based decision-making. The verification step is non-negotiable — students must evaluate whether their numerical answer is consistent with the original context, which is the core of mathematical reasoning.

NSW Syllabus Alignment

MA4-EQU-C-01
  • Section 1 — Represent unknown quantities using pronumerals; describe number sentences as equations; distinguish between and compare expressions and equations
  • Section 2 — Solve linear equations with integer and non-integer solutions using algebraic techniques (up to 2 steps)
  • Section 3 — Solve linear equations with pronumerals on both sides (up to 2 steps)
  • Section 4 — Model and solve word problems using equations of up to 2 steps

Suggested Lesson Flow (60 min)

  • Section 1 (15 min) — Display the concept cards on screen as a whole class launch. Run the classifier as a Think-Pair-Share: students tag individually, then discuss disagreements with a partner before you reveal answers. Item 8 is the conversation pivot: "We'll solve this kind of equation in Section 3."
  • Section 2 (15 min) — Open the worked example together, then release students to self-select a tier. Circulate to check non-integer reasoning (Extension tier) — many students will struggle to express 9/4 as 2.25.
  • Section 3 (15 min) — Teach the "collect pronumeral terms first" strategy explicitly before releasing. Foundation tier isolates the new skill cleanly. The Extension bracket problems work well as a partner task.
  • Section 4 (15 min) — Model the 4-step template with Problem 1 as a class, narrating aloud. Problems 2 and 3 are ideal partner tasks. The Layla/Jordan problem (Problem 3) frequently surfaces the question "does it matter which side?" — an excellent debrief discussion.

Differentiation

📘 Foundation Sections 1–2 (integer solutions only) + Word Problem 1. Use hint buttons freely.
📗 Core All sections. Negative solutions in Section 2. Both-sides collection in Section 3. Word Problems 1–2.
📙 Extension Non-integer solutions, bracket expansion, Word Problem 3 including the extension discussion question.

Pedagogical Notes

  • Classifier item 4 — 7 = 3m − 2 is deliberate. It checks whether students understand that equations are symmetric — the unknown can appear on either side. Worth naming this if students get it wrong.
  • Section 3 verification step — Ensure students substitute into both expressions, not just the left side. This is a key habit to establish early.
  • Non-integer solutions — A common error is students writing x = 9/4 and stopping. Prompt: "Your calculator gives a decimal — what is it? How could you verify it?" This bridges fraction ↔ decimal fluency.
  • Word Problem 2 — The two-part check (x first, then cost) models the habit of not stopping when you find x. In real-world problems, x is often a stepping stone, not the final answer.
