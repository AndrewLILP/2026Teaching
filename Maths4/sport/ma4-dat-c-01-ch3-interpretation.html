Interpreting Data
Reading values · Drawing conclusions · Making predictions · Identifying misleading graphs

Can You Trust What You See?

Reading a graph is a critical skill — but so is questioning one. The same data can be displayed honestly or manipulated to deceive. Sports journalists, coaches and marketers do both. This chapter teaches you to read graphs fluently and to spot when they're lying.

Three Headlines. Three Questions.

Each headline came with a graph. Each graph told a partial or distorted story. As you work through this chapter, you'll learn exactly what's wrong with each one.

🏉 NRL · Attendance
"Panthers' crowd is more than TRIPLE the Storm's — the gap is growing!"
The bar chart looks dramatic. But the y-axis starts at 15,000 — not zero. The actual difference is less than 20%.
🦅 AFL · Team Form
"Our team is unstoppable — four straight months of improvement!"
The line graph starts at Round 9. The full 12-month picture shows eight inconsistent months before that run began.
🏊 Swimming · Survey
"87% of Australians prefer swimming to any other sport."
The pie chart doesn't mention: the survey was an online poll on a swimming club's own website — not a representative sample.

📊 Reading a graph means extracting specific values accurately — the highest/lowest bars, exact coordinates, differences between groups, and totals. Always check the scale before reading any value.

Sydney: 2+4+6+3 = 15 goals total.

Differences per quarter:

The biggest lead was 3 goals by Hawthorn in Q4.

🔎 Drawing conclusions means identifying patterns and features within a graph — not just reading individual values. Key features to find: range, mode, clusters, gaps and outliers.
📏 Range = max − min
🔵 Cluster = dense group of values
🏆 Mode = most frequent value
🔴 Outlier = value far from the rest
⬜ Gap = interval with no values
Source: Hypothetical data | NRL 2024

📏 Range

Max (95m) − Min (55m) = 40 metres. A large range shows high variability — this player's output was quite inconsistent.

🏆 Mode

68 metres appears 3 times (tallest stack, shown in green). The mode is the most typical game performance.

🔵 Cluster

Most values fall between 58–75 metres (10 of 12 games). This is where the player's typical game-day performance lies.

🔴 Outlier + ⬜ Gap

95 metres is an outlier — it sits far above the cluster. The gap from 83–94m means no game landed in that range, making 95m even more isolated.

📈 Making predictions means using the trend or pattern in existing data to estimate future values. A prediction should be reasonable — close to the trend — not a guess at an extreme value.
Source: Hypothetical data | NSW Swimming 2024
📍 Toggle the prediction above. Notice it shows as a dashed line with open circles — a visual convention meaning "this is estimated, not measured." Predictions should be clearly distinguished from actual data.

Rules for Making Reasonable Predictions

  • ✅ Follow the trend — if times are decreasing, predict a slight further decrease
  • ✅ Allow for setbacks — not every race will be a personal best
  • ✅ Stay within a reasonable range — don't predict a value far outside existing data
  • ❌ Don't predict an exact value — say "approximately 55s" not "exactly 55.13s"
  • ❌ Don't extend too far — the further ahead you predict, the less reliable it is
  • ❌ Don't use outliers as the basis for prediction — use the overall pattern
⚠️ A graph can display accurate data but still mislead the reader through design choices — axis manipulation, selective time windows, biased data sources, or distorted visual proportions. Each type below is common in real sports media.
Type 1
Truncated Y-Axis
Starting the y-axis above zero makes small differences look dramatically large. The bars appear to show a huge gap, when the actual proportional difference is minor. This is the most common misleading graph technique in sports media.

❌ As Published — "Panthers crowd is TRIPLE the Storm's!"

✅ Honest Version — y-axis from zero

💡 The actual data: Storm 16,250 · Roosters 17,840 · Broncos 18,920 · Panthers 19,300. The largest gap is 3,050 — about 19% more than the smallest. Not "triple". The truncated axis makes a 19% difference look like a 300% difference.
Type 2
Cherry-Picked Time Window
Showing only the most favourable section of a time period hides earlier poor performance. The graph may be entirely accurate — but by selecting the window, the presenter controls the impression it creates.

❌ Club announcement: "We're on fire — wins improving every month!"

✅ Full 12 months — January to December

💡 Always ask: What time period is this graph showing? A team that won 8 games in December looks great — until you see they won only 1 in April. The trend may be real, but the full context changes the story.
Type 3
Biased (Non-Representative) Sample
A graph can only be trusted if the data came from a sample that represents the broader population. When the sample is self-selected or drawn from a narrow group, the graph's conclusion doesn't apply to everyone.
📢 Claim: "87% of Australians prefer swimming to any other sport"
Source: Online poll run on SwimAustralia.com (n = 150 respondents)

❌ What the pie chart looks like

What's wrong?

  • ❌ Sample drawn from a swimming website — inherently biased toward swimmers
  • ❌ Self-selected — only people who care about swimming completed the poll
  • ❌ n = 150 is too small to represent 26 million Australians
  • ❌ No mention of how "prefer" was defined
  • ✅ The graph itself is fine — it's the data collection that's flawed
Type 4
Unequal Class Intervals in Histograms
In a frequency histogram, the area of each bar represents frequency — not just the height. When bars have different widths, a wide-but-short bar can appear visually larger than a narrow-but-tall bar, even if its frequency is lower.
Source: Hypothetical data | NRL Sports Science 2024
⚠️ Bar 4 (30–34 m/s, freq=10) looks visually larger than Bar 3 (28–30 m/s, freq=15) even though it has fewer players. This is because the bar is twice as wide — its area is misleadingly large. In a correct histogram, all class intervals must be equal width.

A frequency histogram shows cricket batters' scores at a club competition. One bar (60–80 runs) is twice as wide as the other bars but the same height. A club statistician claims: "The 60–80 range has the same number of batters as the 40–50 range." Is he right?

Key Vocabulary

Outlier
A value that sits well apart from the main cluster of data — unusually high or low.
e.g. 64s when others cluster at 52–58s
Cluster
A dense group of data values — where most of the data falls.
e.g. NRL metres clustering in the 60s–80s
Trend
A consistent direction of change shown in a line graph — upward, downward, or plateau.
e.g. swimmer's times decreasing over a season
Prediction
An estimate of a future value based on an existing trend or pattern. Should be reasonable and close to the trend.
e.g. "likely around 55s next race"
Truncated axis
A y-axis that starts above zero, making small differences appear visually large.
e.g. crowd chart starting at 15,000
Biased sample
A sample that is not representative of the broader population — drawn from a narrow or self-selected group.
e.g. surveying swim fans about favourite sport
