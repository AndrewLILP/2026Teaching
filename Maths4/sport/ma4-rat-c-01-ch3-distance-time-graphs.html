📊 What can a graph tell us that words can't?

A distance–time graph turns a journey into a visual shape. Different slopes tell different stories:

↗ Positive slope

Moving away from start — the steeper, the faster

→ Zero slope (flat)

Not moving — stopped / at rest

↘ Negative slope

Returning toward the starting point

💭 A flat line on a distance–time graph means "not moving". But what would a vertical line mean? Is that physically possible?

📘 Key Concept: DISTANCE–TIME GRAPH

What it shows

How far an object travels over time. Distance on the y-axis, time on the x-axis. Each straight-line segment = constant speed.

Reading slope

Steep = fast. Gentle = slow. Flat = stopped. Negative slope = returning to start.

Calculating speed

Speed = change in distancechange in time = slope of the segment. Units: m/s or km/h.

Common mistakes

  • Confusing a negative slope with "going backwards in time"
  • Forgetting the unit in the speed answer
  • Reading distance off the wrong axis
Worked Example 1 Calculating speed from a segment — swimming relay
🏊 Swimming

Swimming Training: 200 m Pool Return

A swimmer travels 200 m to the far wall, rests 15 seconds, then swims back.

Calculate the swimmer's speed for the outward leg (t = 0 to 30 s).

1
Identify change in distance: 200 m − 0 m = 200 m
2
Identify change in time: 30 s − 0 s = 30 s
3
Calculate speed: Speed = 200 ÷ 30 = 6.67 m/s (to 2 d.p.)
✅ Speed on outward leg ≈ 6.67 m/s
Return leg: The swimmer covers 200 m in 45 s (t=45 to t=90). Speed = 200 ÷ 45 ≈ 4.44 m/s. The negative slope shows they're returning — but speed is still positive.
Worked Example 2 Multi-segment analysis — NRL player GPS tracking
🏉 NRL

NRL Player GPS: Movement in a 60-second play

Using the graph, calculate the player's speed in each moving segment and identify the stopped phase.

A
Segment A (t=0–10s, d=0→50m): Speed = 50 ÷ 10 = 5 m/s (sprinting)
B
Segment B (t=10–20s, d=50m flat): Horizontal line → player is stopped (in a tackle/breakdown)
C
Segment C (t=20–35s, d=50→80m): Change in d = 30 m, change in t = 15 s → Speed = 30 ÷ 15 = 2 m/s (jogging into position)
D
Segment D (t=35–60s, d=80→0m): Change in d = 80 m, change in t = 25 s → Speed = 80 ÷ 25 = 3.2 m/s — the negative slope means he's returning to his own end.
✅ Fastest segment: A (5 m/s) · Stopped: Segment B · Returning: Segment D
🔍 Error Detective — interpreting slope direction

A student was asked to describe a D–T graph segment with a negative slope. Find the error:

Graph shows: distance goes from 80 m to 0 m over 20 seconds. Student's answer: "The negative slope means the athlete slowed down and is now moving in negative time."
Two errors: (1) "Negative time" is not a thing — time always moves forward on a D–T graph. (2) A negative slope means the athlete is returning toward the starting point, not slowing down. Slowing down would show a positive but less steep slope. The correct description: "The athlete is travelling back toward the starting point at a speed of 80 ÷ 20 = 4 m/s."

Netball player's training session

A netball centre begins at the baseline. She sprints 30 m in 5 seconds to the attacking third. She rests for 10 seconds. She walks 10 m further in 10 seconds. She sprints the 40 m back to the baseline in 8 seconds.

💭 What is her speed in each moving segment? Which segment has the steepest slope?

Segment 1 (0–5s, 0→30m): Speed = 30÷5 = 6 m/s (steepest — fastest)
2
Segment 2 (5–15s, 30m flat): Stopped — horizontal line
3
Segment 3 (15–25s, 30→40m): Speed = 10÷10 = 1 m/s (gentle positive slope)
4
Segment 4 (25–33s, 40→0m): Speed = 40÷8 = 5 m/s (steep negative slope — returning fast)
