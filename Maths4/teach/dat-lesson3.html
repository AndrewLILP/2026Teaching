Interpreting Data

Trends, outliers, predictions, misleading graphs, and drawing justified conclusions

📚 Key Vocabulary

Click or tap each card to flip it and see the full definition, examples, and non-examples.

Trend
Tap to flip →
A general pattern or direction in data over time — increasing, decreasing, or remaining steady
ExamplesTemperature rising across summer months; sales increasing each quarter
Non-ExamplesA single data point; random fluctuation with no clear direction
Key IdeaTrends describe the overall direction, ignoring short-term ups and downs
Outlier
Tap to flip →
A data value that is significantly different from the rest of the dataset
ExamplesA score of 98 when most students scored 55–70; a temperature of 45°C in a dataset of 20–25°C values
Non-ExamplesA value at the high or low end of a normal spread; the maximum value in a symmetric dataset
Key IdeaOutliers may indicate errors, unusual events, or genuinely extreme cases
Prediction
Tap to flip →
An estimate of a future (or unknown) value based on patterns observed in the data
ExamplesPredicting next month's sales based on a rising trend; estimating a student's score from a pattern
Non-ExamplesStating a fact already shown in the graph; guessing without reference to data
Key IdeaPredictions must be justified with reference to patterns in the data
Misrepresentation
Tap to flip →
When a graph is designed or presented in a way that gives a false or misleading impression of the data
ExamplesTruncated y-axis; inconsistent scale intervals; 3D effects distorting proportions
Non-ExamplesA graph with a clear title and accurate scale; a sector graph with correct proportions
Key IdeaMisrepresentation can be intentional (persuasion) or accidental (poor design)
Conclusion
Tap to flip →
A statement summarising what the data shows, supported by specific evidence from the graph
Examples"Sales increased by 40% from January to March, suggesting strong early-year demand."
Non-Examples"The graph looks interesting." (too vague, no evidence)
Key IdeaAlways link your conclusion to specific data values or features visible in the graph
Scale
Tap to flip →
The set of evenly spaced values on an axis — the scale determines how data is visually represented
ExamplesY-axis: 0, 10, 20, 30 (even intervals); x-axis: Jan, Feb, Mar (time sequence)
Non-ExamplesUnequal intervals like 0, 5, 20, 50 on an axis; starting at a non-zero value without indicating it
Key IdeaAn inappropriate scale is one of the most common ways graphs mislead readers

What to look for when interpreting a graph

📈
Overall trendIs the data going up, going down, or staying roughly the same? Describe the general direction over the full time period.
⬆️⬇️
Maximum and minimumIdentify the highest and lowest values. When did they occur? What do they mean in context?
💥
OutliersAre there values that stand out as unusually high or low? Consider whether they represent an error or a genuine event.
🔄
Clusters and gapsWhere do data points bunch together? Where are there gaps? What does this tell us about the distribution?
🔮
PredictionBased on the trend, what might happen next? State your prediction and the pattern that supports it.
⚠️
Misleading featuresDoes the scale start at zero? Are intervals even? Does the graph type suit the data? Could the visual design exaggerate or hide patterns?

⚠️ Common ways graphs mislead

🔪 Truncated Y-axis
The y-axis starts above zero, making small differences look dramatic. A 5% increase can look like a 500% increase when the axis starts at 95%.
📏 Inconsistent scale
Intervals on the axis are unequal — e.g. jumping from 0→10→20→100. This distorts the visual distance between values.
🍒 Cherry-picked data
Only a favourable portion of the time period is shown, hiding a broader declining trend or inconvenient data points.
🎲 3D effects
Three-dimensional sector graphs or bar charts distort proportions — slices at the "front" appear larger than they really are.
🏷️ Missing labels or title
Without axis labels, units, or a title, the reader cannot correctly interpret what the graph shows or how large the values are.
📊 Wrong graph type
Using a graph type that doesn't suit the data — e.g. a line graph for unrelated categories — implies a relationship that doesn't exist.

Example 1 — Reading values from a histogram Foundation
A frequency histogram shows the heights (cm) of 30 students. The bars have frequencies: 140–149: 3, 150–159: 7, 160–169: 12, 170–179: 6, 180–189: 2. What is the modal class? How many students are shorter than 160 cm?
1
Identify the modal class
The modal class is the interval with the highest frequency: 160–169 cm (frequency = 12)
The modal class is the tallest bar — the interval containing the most data values.
2
Count students shorter than 160 cm
Intervals below 160 cm: 140–149 (3) + 150–159 (7) = 10 students
Add the frequencies of all bars to the left of 160 cm.
✓
State both answers clearly
Modal class = 160–169 cm. 10 students are shorter than 160 cm.
Always verify: 3 + 7 + 12 + 6 + 2 = 30 ✓ (matches the total of 30 students).
Example 2 — Identify a trend in a line graph Foundation
A line graph shows the average daily temperature in a city across 12 months. Values (°C): Jan 22, Feb 23, Mar 21, Apr 18, May 14, Jun 11, Jul 10, Aug 11, Sep 14, Oct 17, Nov 20, Dec 22. Describe the trend and identify the minimum.
1
Describe the overall pattern
The temperature decreases from January to July, then increases from July to December.
This forms a U-shaped (concave) pattern — typical of Southern Hemisphere seasonal temperature data.
2
Identify the minimum
Minimum = 10°C in July
Read the lowest point on the line graph — it falls at July with a value of 10°C.
3
Write a justified conclusion
Temperatures show a decreasing trend Jan–Jul (summer to winter), reaching a minimum of 10°C in July, then rising through spring back toward summer highs of approximately 22–23°C.
Example 3 — Making a prediction from a trend Core
A line graph shows a shop's weekly sales ($): Week 1: $400, Week 2: $550, Week 3: $700, Week 4: $850. If the trend continues, predict sales in Week 5 and Week 6.
1
Identify the pattern
Each week, sales increase by approximately $150.
Calculate the difference between consecutive weeks: 550−400=150, 700−550=150, 850−700=150. The increase is constant.
2
Apply the pattern to predict
Week 5: $850 + $150 = $1,000
Week 6: $1,000 + $150 = $1,150
Extend the pattern by adding $150 for each additional week.
3
State the prediction with a caveat
Predicted Week 5: $1,000; Week 6: $1,150, assuming the linear trend of +$150 per week continues. In practice, the trend may change.
Predictions are estimates based on the observed pattern — always note that real data may not follow the trend perfectly.
Example 4 — Spotting a misleading sector graph Core
A company publishes a sector graph showing 4 product categories. Category A appears to take up about half the circle, but the label says 30%. Categories B, C, D show 25%, 25%, 20% respectively. Identify the misleading technique and explain its effect.
1
Check if percentages add to 100%
30 + 25 + 25 + 20 = 100% ✓
The percentages are correct — so the distortion is in the visual representation, not the numbers.
2
Identify the likely cause
A 3D perspective effect tilts the graph — sectors at the "front" of the tilted circle appear larger than their true angle suggests.
Category A is placed at the front of the 3D sector graph, making its 30% slice look like ~50%.
3
State the effect and correction
The 3D effect makes Category A appear more dominant than it is (30% looks like ~50%). Fix: use a flat 2D sector graph where angles accurately represent proportions.
Example 5 — Drawing a conclusion from a dot plot Extension
A dot plot shows the number of books read by 15 students: most dots cluster at 2 and 3, with one dot at 12. Write a detailed conclusion identifying the outlier, cluster, and what the data suggests about reading habits.
1
Identify the cluster
Most students read 2–3 books — the dots cluster at 2 and 3 on the number line.
Clustering tells us where the most common values are.
2
Identify and interpret the outlier
The value of 12 is an outlier — it is significantly higher than the rest of the data (next highest is likely 4–5).
An outlier is a value far from the main cluster. It may represent an exceptionally avid reader, or possibly a data recording error.
3
Draw a justified conclusion
Most of the 15 students (the cluster at 2–3 books) read only a few books during the period. One student read 12 books — an outlier that skews the average higher. The median of 2–3 better represents a "typical" student than the mean, which would be inflated by the outlier.
A strong conclusion addresses the cluster, the outlier, and considers how each affects statistical measures.

Case 1 — Conclusion about a line graph

A student reads a line graph showing rainfall over 5 years. Find the unjustified conclusion.
1
The rainfall graph shows values for 2019, 2020, 2021, 2022, and 2023.
2
Rainfall increased from 520 mm in 2019 to 680 mm in 2023 — an overall upward trend.
3
Rainfall will continue increasing every year forever into the future. ← click if wrong
4
Based on the trend, rainfall in 2024 may be higher than 680 mm, though this cannot be guaranteed.
❌ The error is on Line 3.

A 5-year upward trend does not mean rainfall will increase forever. Trends observed in a dataset can change — rainfall is affected by climate cycles, weather patterns, and other variables. Line 3 overgeneralises the trend far beyond what the data justifies.

✅ Correct approach: Line 4 is correct — "may be higher" and "cannot be guaranteed" appropriately acknowledge uncertainty. Predictions should be specific (e.g. one or two steps ahead) and qualified.

Common error: Extending short-term trends indefinitely. Always qualify predictions with "if the trend continues" or similar language.

🕵️

Case 2 — Conclusion about a sector graph

A student interprets a sector graph showing transport methods. Find the unsupported conclusion.
1
The sector graph shows 40% of students travel by bus, 30% by car, 20% on foot, and 10% by other means.
2
Bus is the most common travel method — it represents the largest sector (40%).
3
Therefore, bus is also the most popular method at other schools in NSW. ← click if wrong
4
This data was collected from one school — conclusions apply only to that school's students.
❌ The error is on Line 3.

A sector graph of one school's data cannot be used to draw conclusions about all NSW schools. This is an overgeneralisation — the sample is not representative of all schools, which may differ in location, access to transport, and demographics.

✅ Correct approach: Line 4 is correct — conclusions should be limited to the population the data was collected from. To generalise to other schools, data from a representative sample of multiple schools would be needed.

Common error: Generalising a conclusion beyond the scope of the data. Always identify who the data was collected from and limit conclusions accordingly.

📖 Word Problems — Newman's Framework

Problem 1 — Interpret a graph and draw a conclusion
A dot plot shows the number of hours Year 7 students spend on social media per day. Most values cluster at 2 and 3 hours, with one value at 9 hours. The range is 8 hours.
① Read
The graph shows _______ for _______. The type of graph is _______.
② Define
The cluster is at _______. There is an outlier at _______. The range is _______.
③ Write
The outlier means _______. The range of _______ is affected because _______.
④ Solve
A typical student spends about _______ hours on social media because _______.
⑤ Answer
Conclusion: _______. The outlier _______ the mean, which means _______ better represents the typical student.
Full Solution:

The dot plot shows a cluster at 2–3 hours — most students spend 2 or 3 hours daily on social media. The value of 9 hours is an outlier; it is much higher than the rest of the data. The outlier inflates the mean (pulling it above 3 hours), meaning the median (likely 2–3 hours) better represents the typical student. Conclusion: most Year 7 students spend 2–3 hours per day on social media; one unusually high value inflates the mean, so the median is the better measure of centre for this dataset.

Problem 2 — Justify a prediction from a line graph
A line graph shows a school's enrolment over 4 years: 2021: 820, 2022: 855, 2023: 890, 2024: 925. The principal wants to know how many students to plan for in 2025.
① Read
The graph shows _______ from 2021 to 2024. The purpose is to _______.
② Define
The change each year is: 2021→2022: _______, 2022→2023: _______, 2023→2024: _______.
③ Write
The trend is _______ by approximately _______ students each year.
④ Solve
Predicted 2025 enrolment: _______ + _______ = _______.
⑤ Answer
If the trend continues, the school can expect approximately _______ students in 2025. This prediction assumes _______.
Full Solution:

Annual increases: 855−820=35, 890−855=35, 925−890=35. The enrolment increases by exactly 35 students per year — a consistent linear trend. Predicted 2025 enrolment: 925 + 35 = 960 students. This prediction assumes the trend continues at the same rate, which may be affected by factors such as new housing developments, school policy changes, or demographic shifts.

Problem 3 — Evaluate whether a graph could mislead (Extension)
A health-food company publishes a bar graph showing its product's sales grew from 10,000 units to 10,800 units over one year. The y-axis starts at 9,500. They claim: "Sales have skyrocketed — growth of over 50%!" Evaluate this claim using the data.
① Read
The claim is that sales grew by over _______%. The actual sales figures are: start _______, end _______.
② Define
Actual growth = _______ units. Percentage increase = (_______ ÷ _______) × 100 = _______%.
③ Write
The y-axis starts at _______ instead of _______. This makes the bar for 10,800 appear _______ times taller than the bar for 10,000.
④ Solve
The visual impression suggests approximately _______% growth. The actual growth is _______%. The difference is caused by _______.
⑤ Answer
The company's claim of "over 50% growth" is _______. The actual growth was _______%. The graph is misleading because _______.
Full Solution:

Actual growth: 10,800 − 10,000 = 800 units. Percentage increase: (800 ÷ 10,000) × 100 = 8%. The claim of "over 50% growth" is false and misleading. The y-axis starts at 9,500 — a truncated axis. The bar for 10,800 rises from 9,500 to 10,800 (height of 1,300 units on the axis), while the bar for 10,000 rises from 9,500 to 10,000 (height of 500 units). Visually, 1,300 ÷ 500 = 2.6× — so the graph looks like the bar more than doubled. In reality, sales grew by only 8%. The company's claim is inaccurate and the graph uses a truncated y-axis to manufacture a false visual impression of dramatic growth.
