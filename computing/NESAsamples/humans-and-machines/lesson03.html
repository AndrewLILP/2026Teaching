How this lesson fits

The five-system shortlist below is fixed deliberately: every option has decision logic simple enough to flowchart cleanly in Lesson 6, so a student who picks well here won't hit a wall later. The DART activity is analysis-style text marking — students see a fully worked example (an elevator safety brake, chosen because it isn't on their own shortlist and so can't be mistaken for their answer) before marking their own found source. This is the first lesson where students do independent research rather than whole-class discussion, so CT5-DPM-01 (iterative processes) enters the outcome mix alongside CT5-EVL-01.

Choose your system

Pick one of these five. Each has a clear, learnable decision logic — you'll turn that logic into a flowchart in Lesson 6, so a system you understand well now will save you time later.

Teacher tip

If two students want the same system, let them — they'll end up with different milestones and different timelines anyway, since Lesson 7's tool choice will differ. Discourage swapping later; the flowchart in Lesson 6 gets much harder to redo from scratch.

What counts as a milestone?

Not every fact you find is a milestone. A genuine milestone answers at least one of these questions — a detail usually answers none of them.

A milestone test

Ask of every fact you find

  1. What actually changed because of this — a law, a design, an industry?
  2. Could someone suddenly do something they couldn't do before?
  3. Did an event, law or standard cause this, or get caused by it?
  4. Would leaving this out make the story confusing to someone reading your timeline?

If a fact answers "no" to all four, it's probably a detail — interesting, maybe worth a footnote, but not a timeline entry.

DART: mark the milestones

Directed Activities Related to Text — marking

Worked example: the elevator safety brake

Read the paragraph below. Sentences highlighted yellow pass the milestone test. Sentences highlighted pink are true and interesting, but don't.

In 1852, engineer Elisha Otis developed a device that could stop an elevator car from falling if its hoisting rope snapped. The device used a spring-loaded mechanism that gripped the guide rails the instant tension in the rope was lost. Otis demonstrated it publicly in New York in 1854, cutting the rope himself while standing on the platform to prove it worked. Before this invention, elevators were considered too dangerous for carrying people and were mainly used to move freight. Otis's company later became one of the world's largest elevator manufacturers. Safety brakes based on similar principles are still required in passenger lifts today. Early elevator cars were often decorated with ornate ironwork, a style popular at the time.

Notice what the pink sentences have in common: they're all true, and a person could reasonably include them — but none of them changes the "before / after" story of the elevator. That's the test.

Now try it yourself

Find one source about your own chosen system (a museum page, a manufacturer's history page, an encyclopedia article — avoid forums and opinion pieces). In your notebook, copy out 4–6 sentences and mark each one milestone or detail using the same test above.

Start your milestone log

Every milestone you keep from the marking activity goes into this log. You'll add to it over the next few lessons — Lesson 10 turns these rows directly into your timeline entries, so write them as full sentences now, not shorthand you'll have to decode later.
