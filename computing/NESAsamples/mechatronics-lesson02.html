Identifying & Defining

The IPO model

Computing systems of every kind — not just mechatronic ones — can be described using three stages: Input, Process and Output. In a mechatronic system, our sensor–actuator–effector chain from Lesson 1 simply is the IPO model, wearing different labels.

Input

A smoke sensor detects particles in the air

→
Process

The control system checks: has the particle level crossed the threshold?

→
Output

The alarm effector sounds

Mapping the two models together Sensor → Input · Actuator + Effector → Output · the decision in between (the bit we couldn't see last lesson) is the Process — the program or control logic running inside the system.

Teacher tip

Process is the stage students most often skip when describing a system, because it's invisible — there's no physical part to point at. Push students to name what the system is checking or deciding, not just what it senses and does.

Identifying & Defining

Worked examples

Trace the IPO model through two more familiar systems.

Automatic garage door Input: a remote-control signal or a sensor detects the car. Process: the control system checks whether the signal matches a valid code, and whether the path is clear. Output: the motor (actuator) drives the door (effector) up or down.
Sensor light Input: a motion sensor detects movement in its range. Process: the control system checks whether it's dark enough for the light to be needed. Output: the relay (actuator) switches the globe (effector) on for a set time.

Activity: Order the chain

We do — paper sequencing

Put the stages in the right order

Your teacher will give you (or display) six shuffled cards describing one mechatronic system, out of order. In your notebook, write the numbers 1 to 6 next to each card to rebuild the correct Input → Process → Output sequence, then label which stage each card belongs to.

Once you've ordered your own system, swap with a partner and check each other's sequencing — can you explain why each card belongs where it does?
