A Brief History of Mechatronics

Mechatronics didn't begin with computers. Today you'll trace four eras of automation — from clockwork automata to today's connected, AI-assisted machines — then use a DART sequencing activity to test whether you can tell the story in the right order without being told.

CT5-EVL-01 CT5-THI-01

50 minutes · Identifying & Defining

How this lesson fits

The four-era framework below isn't in the NESA sample unit itself — it's a scaffold for making "the evolution of computing technology" (CT5-EVL-01) concrete before Lesson 3 asks students to research one system's milestones in depth. The DART activity here is sequencing: students are given the four eras out of order and must reconstruct the timeline using only what the lesson has told them, before it's confirmed. Treat the expand-cards as the source text and the sequencing task as a closed-book check — encourage students to attempt the order from memory first.

Four eras of automation

Expand each card to read about that era. Don't memorise dates — focus on what changed between each one.

Long before electronics, inventors built machines that looked like they could think. Clockwork automata performed pre-set sequences of movement using nothing but gears and springs. The Jacquard loom used punched cards to automatically weave complex patterns — an early example of a machine "reading" stored instructions. James Watt's steam governor used a mechanical feedback loop to keep an engine running at a constant speed with no person adjusting it.

Examples Clockwork automata · The Jacquard loom · Watt's steam governor

The factory assembly line broke production into repeatable, automatable steps. Relay logic — electrical switches wired to trigger each other — let machines make simple decisions without a person present. Feedback control matured into servo mechanisms and PID control, technology refined during WWII for aiming guns and autopilots, then repurposed for peacetime industry.

Examples Assembly lines · Relay logic control panels · Servo & PID-controlled systems

Unimate, installed on a General Motors line in 1961, is widely considered the first industrial robot — a programmable arm that didn't need to be rebuilt for each new task. The microprocessor put a full computer on one chip, small and cheap enough to embed inside machinery. Programmable Logic Controllers (PLCs) and Computer Numerical Control (CNC) let factories reprogram equipment in software instead of rewiring it by hand.

Examples Unimate, the first industrial robot · Microprocessors · PLCs & CNC machining

Cheap sensors, wireless networking and machine learning combined to let systems sense their environment and decide, not just execute a fixed sequence. Autonomous vehicles interpret live road conditions. Collaborative robots ("cobots") work safely alongside people on factory floors. The Internet of Things connects everyday devices so they can share data and act on it without a person in the loop.

Examples Autonomous vehicles · Collaborative robots (cobots) · Internet of Things (IoT) devices

Put them back in order

The four eras below are shuffled. Working from memory — not by scrolling back up — write down the order you think they actually happened in, then check your order in the reveal below.

  1. Digital & Computer-Controlled Systems
  2. Smart, Connected & Autonomous Systems
  3. Early Automata & Mechanical Ingenuity
  4. Industrial Automation & Control
  1. Early Automata & Mechanical Ingenuity (pre-1900s)
  2. Industrial Automation & Control (early–mid 1900s)
  3. Digital & Computer-Controlled Systems (1950s–1990s)
  4. Smart, Connected & Autonomous Systems (2000s–now)

If you got this right from memory, you've already spotted the pattern the next two lessons build on: each era doesn't replace the last one's machines, it adds a new way for them to sense, decide or connect.

Looking ahead

In Lesson 3 you'll pick one real system — an automatic door, a smoke detector, ABS, a robotic vacuum, or a set of traffic lights — and dig into its specific milestones. This four-era map is the backdrop; Lesson 3 is the close-up. Lesson 4 then comes back to this same map and asks a different question: not what changed each era, but how people felt about it at the time.
