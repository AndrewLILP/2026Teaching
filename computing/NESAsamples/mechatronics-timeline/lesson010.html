Have Humans and Machines Become ONE?

Over the next thirteen lessons, you'll investigate how mechatronic and automated systems have evolved — and build your own interactive historical timeline to make the case for or against one big claim. Today is about the hook, the plan, and your first brainstorm.

Identifying & Defining

The provocation

Before anything else, sit with this statement.

The statement "Humans and machines have become ONE."

Vote with your gut first — agree, disagree, or not sure — before anyone explains anything. Then talk it through as a class:

  • What's the first example that comes to mind when you hear that statement?
  • Is there a difference between a machine helping a human and a machine becoming part of a human?
  • Has this always felt true, or is it a recent feeling?

Hold onto your answer. You'll be asked the same question again in Lesson 13 — after you've actually researched the history of mechatronic and automated systems — to see whether your view has shifted.

Identifying & Defining

What we're building

Across this unit, you'll research the evolution of mechatronic and automated systems and turn that research into an interactive historical timeline. The tool you use to build it is entirely your choice — we'll compare options properly in Lesson 7, but here's the shortlist so you can start thinking:

Your tool, your call Interactive website · Virtual reality walk-through · Interactive presentation · Video / infomercial · Digital storytelling app · Comic strip

Your finished timeline needs to work for a range of people — not just you — so functionality, accessibility, usability and aesthetics all count. In Lesson 13, your class will use these timelines to debate the provocation statement properly, backed by evidence instead of gut feeling.

Teacher tip

Resist locking in a tool today. Lesson 7 is where students formally compare options against the brief — today is just so the shortlist isn't a surprise later.

Identifying & Defining

Mechatronic and automated, quickly

A few terms you'll lean on for the rest of the unit:

Word bank

Mechatronic system

A system where an electrical/computing component controls and regulates a mechanical one — combining engineering disciplines into one smart device.

Automated system

A system that uses sensors and programming to initiate an action on its own, without a person triggering it directly.

Sensor

A component that detects a condition in the environment — light, motion, weight, temperature — and turns it into data the system can act on.

Actuator

A component that carries out a physical action in response to a decision — opening, closing, moving, braking.

Mechatronic and automated systems show up everywhere once you start looking for them:

Where they turn up Health care — exoskeletons, surgical robots, smart wheelchairs · Residential — robotic vacuums, smart homes · Transport — autonomous driving, anti-lock braking · Cultural — VR story-sharing, real-time tourism apps · Manufacturing — robotic arms · Exploration — Mars rovers
Identifying & Defining

Brainstorm: automation through history

In teams, build a mind map or mood board of mechatronic and automated systems throughout history — and how they've changed. This is the raw material your whole unit will build on, so cast a wide net.

Choose your task

Foundation · D–E

Spot five

List five mechatronic or automated systems you already know about, and sort them into "older" and "newer."

Core · C

Map the eras

Build a mind map of at least eight systems, grouped into rough eras (pre-1900s, 20th century, 21st century).

Extension · A–B

Connect the thread

Build a mood board that follows one theme (e.g. transport, healthcare) across eras, annotating how the underlying human need stayed the same while the technology changed.

Teacher tip

Paper, sticky notes and a wall are just as valid here as a digital mind-mapping tool — non-digital brainstorming is genuinely fine for this task, not a fallback.

Checkpoint

Three quick questions before you go.

1. What best describes an automated system?
2. Which of these is the clearest example of automation, rather than human control?
3. What happens with your interactive timeline at the end of the unit?
