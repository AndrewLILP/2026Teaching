Identifying & Defining

Four eras of mechatronics

"Mechatronics" as a field is newer than you'd think — but the idea behind it, a machine that senses and reacts on its own, goes back centuries. Click each era to read more.

Long before electricity, inventors built self-operating mechanical devices — water clocks, mechanical birds, puppet figures driven by gears and weights. These "automata" followed a fixed sequence of moves, with no sensing or decision-making involved. They impressed people precisely because a machine appearing to "decide" to move felt almost magical.

The Industrial Revolution scaled mechanisation up from single novelty devices to entire factories. Steam engines, mechanised looms and early feedback devices (like the centrifugal governor, which automatically regulated an engine's speed) introduced something new: a machine that could correct its own behaviour, without a person adjusting it each time.

This is the era that gives the field its name. In 1969, a Japanese engineer at Yaskawa Electric combined "mechanical" and "electronics" to coin the word mechatronics — a system where an electronic control layer governs a mechanical one. The same era saw the first industrial robot arm (Unimate, installed on a car production line in 1961) and the space race push sensor and control technology forward fast.

Cheap microprocessors and ever-smaller sensors moved mechatronic systems out of factories and into everyday life — anti-lock brakes, robotic vacuums, smart home devices, autonomous vehicles. The systems in this era don't just follow a fixed program; many adjust their behaviour using data gathered in real time, which is the thread connecting this era back to today's provocation statement.

Researching & Planning

Milestone task

Take the examples from your Lesson 1 brainstorm and sort them properly against the four eras.

Choose your task

Sort and sequence

Extension · A–B

Find the gap

Sort your examples, then research and add one milestone NOT already on your map. Explain what enterprise need, innovation or limitation of its time it responded to.

Teacher tip

If a team's brainstorm leaned heavily on one era (modern automation is the easiest to think of), this is the moment to nudge them back toward Eras 1–2 — the unit needs spread across history, not just recent examples.

Checkpoint

Three quick questions before you go.

1. Which era gave the field of mechatronics its actual name?
2. What made the centrifugal governor (Era 2) different from an automaton (Era 1)?
3. From Lesson 1's vocabulary, what's the key difference between an automaton and an automated system?
