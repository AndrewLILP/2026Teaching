Researching & Planning

Worked example: the automatic door

Here's how to research milestones properly, using a system you won't be choosing yourself today — the automatic door — as the model.

1st century AD — Heron of Alexandria's temple doors Heat from a fire built up pressure in a hidden chamber, pumping water through a system of containers that pulled the temple doors open on ropes and pulleys. Need it responded to: a "miracle" effect for worshippers — and proof that automatic motion was possible at all, even with zero electronics. This is an automaton, not an automated system — it follows one fixed physical reaction, with no sensor and no decision being made.
1931 — Stanley Works' photoelectric door Installed at Wilcox's Pier Restaurant in Connecticut, this door opened when a photoelectric "magic eye" detected someone approaching. Need it responded to: waitstaff carrying loaded trays had no free hands to open doors.
1954 — Dee Horton & Lew Hewitt's mat-actuated sliding door A pressure mat under the floor triggered the door, commercialised from 1960 through Horton Automatics. Need it responded to: photoelectric doors were costly and finicky; a mat sensor was cheaper and more reliable for busy entrances.
1970s — Motion and infrared sensors Doors started reacting to movement rather than a fixed mat or beam. Need it responded to: growing demand for buildings to be genuinely accessible to people using wheelchairs or carrying things, not just "hands-free for staff."

Notice the pattern in every entry: a year or era, what actually changed, and the specific need or limitation that change responded to. That last part is the part people skip — and it's the part that matters most.

Researching & Planning

Choose your case-study system

Pick one of the four systems below. You'll keep researching, flowcharting and evaluating this same system through to Lesson 6 — so pick one that genuinely interests your team.

Smoke detector

1930s accidental discovery → 1970s affordable home models → today's networked, app-connected alarms.

Anti-lock braking (ABS)

1930s patent → 1978 first digital production system → today's stability-control integration.

Robotic vacuum

1996 first prototype → 2002 first mass-market success → today's AI room-mapping models.

Traffic light

Several inventors in the 1910s–20s claimed similar designs — genuinely contested history, which is itself worth digging into.

Teacher tip

The traffic light's contested credit is a deliberate inclusion — it's a real example of how "who invented this" doesn't always have a clean answer, which feeds directly into Lesson 4's look at perception and whose story gets told.

Checkpoint

Three quick questions before you go.

1. Why is "who invented the first electric traffic light" genuinely disputed by historians?
2. What specific need did Stanley Works' 1931 photoelectric automatic door respond to?
3. In this lesson's annotation template, what actually makes something count as a "milestone"?
