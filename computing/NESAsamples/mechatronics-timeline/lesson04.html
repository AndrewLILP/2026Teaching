Perceptions through history

Click each card to read more.

Early automata were treated as marvels — a moving figure or self-opening door felt close to magic. But alongside the wonder sat a much older unease, found across many cultures' myths, about artificial figures that imitate life a little too well. That mix of "amazing" and "unsettling" toward anything that mimics human or animal behaviour has never fully gone away.

Between 1811 and 1816, English textile workers known as Luddites smashed power looms and knitting frames in organised protests. It's tempting to read this as "people who hated machines" — but most accounts agree their real target was how mechanisation was being used: to cut wages and replace skilled workers without any say in the matter. The machine wasn't the whole problem; who controlled it, and who it left behind, was.

The word "robot" itself comes from a 1920 Czech play, R.U.R., by Karel Čapek — built from the Czech word for forced labour. The word was born already tangled up with a threat narrative. Since then, popular media has told two very different ongoing stories: Western films like Frankenstein and The Terminator often frame robots as a danger to control, while Japanese manga and anime — Astro Boy, Doraemon — have long featured robots as loyal helpers and friends.

It's tempting to conclude "Western culture fears robots, Japanese culture loves them" — and you'll see that claim everywhere online. But when researchers actually survey people directly, that simple split mostly doesn't hold up; differences in media storytelling are real, but a whole culture feeling one fixed way is closer to a stereotype than a finding. Worth remembering before accepting any tidy cultural generalisation at face value — including this one.

Researching & Planning

Your system: then and now

We do — modelled with the automatic door

Then: early automatic doors made some building owners uneasy — a door "deciding" to open on its own felt odd, and some worried it made buildings easier to break into. Now: automatic doors are invisible, expected infrastructure — most people don't consciously register one at all, and for many wheelchair users they're a baseline accessibility expectation rather than a novelty. The shift: better, cheaper sensors and decades of accessibility advocacy moved automatic doors from "odd convenience" to "basic expectation."

Now apply the same thinking to your own case-study system.

Choose your task

Foundation · D–E

Then and now

Write one sentence on how your system was seen when it was new, and one sentence on how it's seen today.

Core · C

What shifted, and why

Describe how perception of your system has shifted, and identify at least one specific reason for the shift (cheaper technology, new laws, marketing, a generational change).

Extension · A–B

Whose perspective?

Identify two different groups of people who might see (or have seen) your system differently — for example, workers vs. customers, older vs. younger users, or people with disability vs. the general public — and explain why their perspectives differ.

Teacher tip

If a team is stuck on "whose perspective," prompt with: who benefited immediately, who lost something (a job, a routine, control), and who wasn't asked at all?

Checkpoint

Three quick questions before you go.

1. Where does the word "robot" actually come from?
2. According to today's lesson, what were the Luddites actually protesting?
3. According to actual research (not just the popular online claim), what's true about cultural attitudes to robots?
