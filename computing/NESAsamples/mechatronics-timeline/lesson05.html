What Automation Costs, What It Gives

Every automated system trades something for something else. Today you'll learn four lenses for spotting that trade — job losses, safety, liability, and access and participation — then apply all four to your own case-study system.

Identifying & Defining

Recap

Lesson 4 looked at how people felt about automation, and whose story gets told. Today is about something more concrete: what automation actually does — to jobs, to safety, to who's responsible when something goes wrong, and to who gets to participate in everyday life.

Identifying & Defining

Four lenses of impact

Each card below uses a different shortlist system to model one lens clearly. Click each one to read it.

Before electric signals, busy intersections were often directed by a police officer on point duty, manually waving traffic through. Automatic lights didn't eliminate traffic policing, but they removed much of the need for an officer at every junction full-time — freeing police resources for other duties. That's the standard trade-off behind "job losses due to automation": a task shrinks or disappears, and resources shift elsewhere — but the people whose job it was don't always benefit from where those resources end up.

Before ABS, hard braking on a slippery road could lock the wheels completely, taking away a driver's ability to steer around an obstacle. The 1978 Mercedes-Benz/Bosch system used a sensor on each wheel to detect when it was about to lock, then rapidly released and reapplied brake pressure to stop that happening — letting the driver keep steering control even under emergency braking. This is automation doing exactly what the "safety" lens looks for: removing a specific risk a human alone couldn't react to fast enough to manage.

If a smoke detector fails to sound during a real fire, who's responsible? It isn't simple — it could be the manufacturer (a faulty sensor), the installer (wrong placement), the building owner (a battery never replaced), or even the safety standard itself, if it wasn't designed to catch that type of fire. Automated systems shift responsibility around in ways that weren't an issue when a human was directly doing the watching — which is exactly why liability is one of the impacts NESA asks you to consider.

For someone with limited mobility, chronic pain, or a condition that makes pushing a vacuum difficult, a robotic vacuum can mean keeping a clean, healthy home without needing to ask for help. This is the upside the "access and participation" lens points to — automation opening up independence that wasn't available before. It's worth noticing this is the most straightforwardly positive of the four lenses; the other three usually involve a genuine trade-off rather than a clean win.

Teacher tip

If a team insists their system has "no downsides," push on liability specifically — every automated system fails sometimes, and "who's responsible when it does" almost always surfaces something worth discussing.

Checkpoint

Three quick questions before you go.

1. ABS preventing wheel lock-up during hard braking is the clearest example of which impact?
2. Deciding whether a faulty sensor, bad installation, or a dead battery caused a smoke detector to fail is an example of which impact?
3. Which best describes a "trade-off" in the context of today's lesson?
