Choosing Our Timeline Tool

Back in Lesson 1, six tool options went on the table and you were asked not to lock one in yet. Today's the day — but first, you'll evaluate an existing piece of interactive media properly, the same way you'd want someone to evaluate yours.

Comparing the six options

Same six options from Lesson 1, now with enough detail to actually decide between them.

ToolGood fit if…You'll needWatch out for
Interactive website Your team is comfortable with HTML/CSS/JS, or a no-code site builder A computer, a browser, optionally a site builder A way to share or host the finished version for the class
VR walk-through You want full immersion and have some 3D or Unity experience A VR-capable tool (e.g. Unity, or a browser-based VR builder) and ideally a headset or simulator VR hardware may be limited — check what's actually available before committing
Interactive presentation You want clickable navigation without coding Presentation software that supports hyperlinks or triggers (e.g. Slides, PowerPoint) Easy to default to a plain slideshow — build in real clickable navigation, not just "next slide"
Video / infomercial Your story works best told start to finish Video editing software or an app, plus a script Video is the least naturally "interactive" option — plan deliberate pause points or chapter markers
Digital storytelling app You want a branching, choose-your-path narrative A branching storytelling tool or app (e.g. Twine) Branching logic gets complex fast — plan the branches on paper before building
Comic strip Visual storytelling is your team's strength Drawing tools, or a comic-maker app Static panels need a deliberate interactive layer — clickable panels or hotspots, not just images

Teacher tip

If your school's already built Unity skills this year through the Games & Simulations focus area, a simple Unity walk-through scene is a genuinely strong option for VR — flag this to students who've done that work already.

Researching & Planning

Evaluate an existing example

Before you choose, find one existing example of interactive media — a museum's online timeline, a branching story, an explainer video, a webcomic, anything that resembles a tool option above — and evaluate it against the same four criteria your own timeline will be judged on.

We do — the evaluation lens

Functionality: does every interactive element actually work as expected? Accessibility: could someone using a screen reader, or with limited mobility, actually use this? Usability: is it obvious what to do next, without instructions? Aesthetics: does the visual design support the content, or distract from it?

Write one sentence against each of the four criteria for your chosen example, then answer: could this example be modified or reused for a completely different topic? What would need to change?

Researching & Planning

Choose your tool

Now make the call, weighing your team's skills, the requirements from Lesson 6, and what's actually available to you.

Choose your task

Foundation · D–E

Pick and state why

Choose a tool from the table and write one sentence on why it fits your team.

Core · C

Compare two, then decide

Compare your top two options against your Lesson 6 requirements, then choose one and explain what tipped the decision.

Extension · A–B

Justify against a real constraint

Make your choice, then explain how you'd adapt your plan if your first-choice tool turned out to be unavailable (no VR headset, no licence, no access) — what's your backup, and why?

Checkpoint

Three quick questions before you go.

1. Which of today's "watch out for" notes is most likely to be a real hardware availability constraint, rather than a design choice?
2. Your team's story has many points where the audience needs to choose what happens next. Which tool from today's table fits best?
3. What does today's lesson ask you to do before finalising your tool choice?

Notebook entry

Project notebook — Researching & Planning

  • Record your chosen tool and your top two reasons for choosing it. Sentence starter: "We're building our timeline as a... because..."
  • Record the existing example you evaluated, and the most useful thing you learned from looking at it. Sentence starter: "Looking at [example] taught us..."
