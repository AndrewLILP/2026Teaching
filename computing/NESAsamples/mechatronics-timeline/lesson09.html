Planning the Timeline: Scale and Structure

Before you build anything digital, draw it on paper to scale. Today is real ratio maths — converting a span of years into centimetres — plus turning that drawing into an actual build plan for your chosen tool.

Recap

You've got milestones (Lesson 3), perception shifts (Lesson 4), impact (Lesson 5), a flowchart and requirements (Lesson 6), a tool (Lesson 7), and UI/UX decisions (Lesson 8). Today turns all of that into an actual physical drawing, before you touch your chosen tool.

Researching & Planning

Worked example: scaling the door's timeline

The automatic door's milestones run from 1931 to today (2026) — a 95-year span. Say you have a 30 cm paper strip to draw it on.

We do — calculating the scale

Step 1 — find the range: 2026 − 1931 = 95 years.
Step 2 — divide by available length: 95 years ÷ 30 cm ≈ 3.2 years per cm.
Step 3 — round to a clean number: 1 cm = 5 years is easier to measure and calculate with than 3.2.
Step 4 — check the total length: 95 years ÷ 5 years/cm = 19 cm. That fits comfortably on the 30 cm strip, with room left for labels.

Now place each milestone using (milestone year − 1931) ÷ 5 to get its distance in centimetres from the start of the strip:

MilestoneYearCalculationPosition on strip
Stanley's photoelectric door1931(1931 − 1931) ÷ 50 cm
Horton & Hewitt's mat-actuated door1954(1954 − 1931) ÷ 54.6 cm
Motion and infrared sensors1975(1975 − 1931) ÷ 58.8 cm
Today2026(2026 − 1931) ÷ 519 cm

But Lesson 3's worked example also included Heron of Alexandria's automatic temple doors, from the 1st century AD — roughly 1925 years before today. Plug that into the same scale and it sits about 385 cm to the left of 1931. That's not a drawing problem, that's a different drawing entirely.

The actual decision When one milestone is wildly older than the rest, don't stretch your scale to fit it — that crushes every other milestone into an unreadable sliver. Instead, draw your main strip scaled to the milestones that are reasonably close together, and show the outlier as a separate "historical echo" callout box off to the side, clearly labelled as not-to-scale.

From scale drawing to structure plan

Your scale drawing isn't the final deliverable — it's the map you'll build the digital version from. What that translates to depends on your Lesson 7 tool choice.

ToolYour structure plan should list…
Interactive websitePages or sections, in order, with what content sits on each
VR walk-throughStops or waypoints along the path, in order
Interactive presentationSlides, in order, with what's on each
Video / infomercialA shot or scene list, in order, with rough timing for each
Digital storytelling appDecision nodes, in order or as a branching map
Comic stripPanels, in order, with what's drawn in each

Your scale and structure

Choose your task

Foundation · D–E

Plot on a given scale

Using a scale of 1 cm = 5 years on a 30 cm strip, calculate and mark the position of three of your own milestones.

Core · C

Calculate your own scale

Calculate your own scale from your milestone range and available strip length, draw the full scale drawing, and write a structure plan matching your tool from the table above.

Extension · A–B

Handle the outlier

Do the Core task. If you have a milestone that doesn't fit your linear scale, decide how you'll show it and justify your choice. If your milestones are evenly spread, explain why a single linear scale works well for your system specifically.

Teacher tip

Paper, ruler, pencil — no app needed for this one. The scale drawing genuinely works better as a physical object teams can point at and argue over.

Checkpoint

Three quick questions before you go.

1. A system's milestones span 80 years, and you have a 16 cm strip. What's an appropriate scale?
2. One of your milestones is 1,000 years older than all the others. What does today's lesson recommend?
3. What is the actual purpose of today's scale drawing?
