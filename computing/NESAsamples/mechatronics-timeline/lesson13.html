Recap: the whole unit

You've researched milestones, traced shifting perceptions, weighed up impact, flowcharted a real decision, specified requirements, chosen and justified a tool, designed for accessibility, calculated a scale, built content and interactivity, and tested against your own criteria. Today is where all of that actually gets used for something — an argument.

Testing & Evaluating

Presentations

Each team gets a lightning round — about two minutes — to show their finished interactive timeline to the class. Cover three things only: your case-study system, the tool you built it in, and the one entry you're proudest of.

Teacher tip

Keep a visible timer running for the lightning round — two minutes per team across a full class adds up fast, and the debate needs its own proper block of time afterward.

Testing & Evaluating

The debate

Back to the statement from Lesson 1: "Humans and machines have become ONE." Vote again — agree, disagree, or unsure — but this time, every position needs at least one specific piece of evidence from your own case-study research.

We do — an evidence-based position, modelled with the automatic door

"Looking at the automatic door, I'd say humans and machines have only partly become one — the door still just reacts to a fixed sensor input; it doesn't combine with a person the way the statement suggests. But the speed at which we stopped consciously noticing it suggests our expectations have quietly merged with what machines do invisibly around us." Notice this isn't a flat yes or no — it's a position that weighs evidence on both sides before landing somewhere specific.

Choose your task

Foundation · D–E

One piece of evidence

State your position — agree, disagree, or unsure — with one specific piece of evidence from your research.

Core · C

Two pieces, two lessons

State your position with two specific pieces of evidence, drawn from two different lessons (for example, a milestone and an impact lens).

Extension · A–B

Weigh the counterargument

State a nuanced position using evidence from at least three different lessons, explicitly naming and responding to the strongest argument against your own view.

Careers and the future

NESA's own guiding question for this unit asks: what is the significance of mechatronic and automated systems today, and in the future? One honest answer is that this field isn't finished — every era you researched thought its own technology was the end point, and every era was wrong. Whatever you think "ONE" means right now will likely look different again in twenty years.

This field also needs people. A few of the careers connected to what you've researched and built this unit:

Mechatronics Engineer Robotics Engineer UX/UI Designer Automation Technician Accessibility Consultant Human-Robot Interaction Researcher Embedded Systems Developer Industrial Designer

Checkpoint

Three quick questions before you go.

1. What does today's debate require that Lesson 1's gut-reaction vote didn't?
2. According to today's lesson, what's true about every era's belief that its technology was close to "finished"?
3. What's the actual point of revisiting Lesson 1's gut-reaction question today?

Notebook entry

Project notebook — Testing & Evaluating

  • Compare today's position to your Lesson 1 gut reaction. Did it change, and why or why not? Sentence starter: "Back in Lesson 1 I said... Now I think... because..."
  • Record the single piece of evidence from this whole unit that affected your thinking the most, and which lesson it came from. Sentence starter: "The evidence that stuck with me most was... from Lesson..."
  • Record one career from today's list that genuinely interests you, and why. Sentence starter: "[Career] interests me because..."
