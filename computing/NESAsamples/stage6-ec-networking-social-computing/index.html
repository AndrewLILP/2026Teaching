NESA Sample Unit · Enterprise Computing 11–12 · Networking Systems and Social Computing (Year 11)

Networking Systems and Social Computing

Across fourteen lessons, you'll trace one arc: from the human and social side of networking — how start-ups build culture, how social platforms model relationships as graphs — into the genuinely technical side of how networks are built, secured and optimised, finishing with a hands-on project designing your own connected home network. The unit follows NESA's own six-part structure (A–F) rather than a generic phase model, because the parts themselves already tell the social-to-technical story.

14 lessons · 8–10 weeks (approx. 34–40 hours) · Parts A–F

How this unit fits

Networking Systems and Social Computing is the Year 11 Enterprise Computing counterpart to Practical Programming in Context on the Software Engineering side — both establish foundational thinking before each course's Year 12 major project begins. NESA's own sample unit explicitly allows teachers to "adjust or swap out any part to suit the school context," and this build preserves that six-part (A–F) structure on the site rather than reflowing it into the Identify/Research/Produce/Test phase tags used elsewhere, since the real content doesn't map cleanly onto that model — Parts A–B are social and conceptual, Parts C–E get progressively technical, and Part F is a hands-on build project. Establishing a documentation habit here (the notebook entries in every lesson) matters beyond this unit: the Year 12 Enterprise Computing Project runs concurrently across three terms rather than as a standalone block, and students who are already used to logging their thinking will find that transition far easier.

Your journey through this unit

Each Part below is NESA's own division of the unit, not a reflow into a generic model — Parts A–B build the social and conceptual groundwork, Parts C–E get progressively more technical, and Part F is the unit's hands-on build project.

Part A · Social computing is networking · 8–10 hrs Part B · Start-ups and their tech · 5 hrs Part C · Network connectivity · 8–10 hrs Part D · IoT and its protocols · 2 hrs Part E · Where does the data go and how is it secured? · 3–5 hrs Part F · Building a network · 8 hrs
Part A · Social computing is networking

Social computing is networking

The human and social side of networking, first. How social platforms, apps and start-up culture all turn out to be running on the same underlying idea: networks of connected people and things.

Lesson 1 — What Is Social Computing? Disruptive Tech in Everyday Life

Human-centric computing, disruptive technology, and why "everyday computing" is really a network of decisions, devices and platforms.

Lesson 2 — Graph and Network Theory: Modelling Social Networks

Nodes, edges and labelled relationships — how social network analysis represents people, categories and connections as a graph.

Lesson 3 — Start-Up Culture: What Makes a Start-Up Succeed?

Investigating a real start-up's culture, social media strategy and the operational technology that supports its growth.

Part B · Start-ups and their tech

Start-ups and their tech

From people to devices: the same start-ups from Part A, examined now for the IoT and IoMe hardware and software underneath them.

Lesson 4 — The Internet of Things and the Internet of Me

What IoT and IoMe actually mean for a start-up's employees, clients and customers — and the regulation, compliance and cybersecurity that come with them.

Lesson 5 — Mapping a Start-Up's Tech: the IoT/IoMe Infographic

Categorising a start-up's devices as consumer, enterprise or industrial, and building the network map you'll keep referring back to later in the unit.

Part C · Network connectivity

Network connectivity

The technical core of the unit: how enterprise networks are actually built, what gets in the way of data moving across them, and the graph-theory concepts used to optimise them.

Lesson 6 — Network Architecture and Infrastructure

How devices connect to form a network, and the architecture that holds an enterprise's infrastructure together.

Lesson 7 — Transmission Media and What Gets in the Way

Distance, topography, physical and environmental barriers — the real-world factors that interfere with data flowing across a network.

Lesson 8 — Graph Theory for Network Optimisation: Adjacency, Centrality, Connectedness

Applying named graph-theory concepts — adjacency, centrality, connectedness and weighted graphs — to the design of optimised computer networks.

Part D · IoT and its protocols

IoT and its protocols

A short, sharp part: how IoT devices actually talk to each other, and what machine learning adds on top.

Lesson 9 — IoT Protocols, Interoperability and Machine Learning

Device interoperability, SCADA, and how machine learning interfacing with IoT improves outcomes from manufacturing to farm irrigation.

Part E · Where does the data go and how is it secured?

Where does the data go and how is it secured?

Reconnecting with the Part A start-ups: where their data is stored, how cloud services compare, how their networks are actually drawn, and how access to them is secured.

Lesson 10 — Data Storage, Workflows and Cloud Computing

Digital workflow benefits and limitations, enterprise data storage requirements, and comparing IaaS, SaaS and PaaS cloud models.

Lesson 11 — Network Diagrams and Securing Access

Building a proper network diagram with NESA's own device symbols, then peer-evaluating diagrams using a second, lighter pass at graph theory.

Part F · Building a network

Building a network

Everything from Parts A–E lands here: a project to design, build or simulate a working home network.

Lesson 12 — Designing Your Home Network

Designing and modelling a network of interconnected devices — an internet connection, a cabled console, a wireless device and an IoT device.

Lesson 13 — Configuring Devices and Cybersecurity

Naming and updating devices, configuring security protocols, and protecting an intelligent home network with firewalls, VPNs and backup planning.

Lesson 14 — Optimising, Evaluating and Presenting Your Network

Improving bandwidth, updating drivers and firmware, and presenting your finished network design to its intended audience.

Tools for this unit

Parts B and E call for an infographic/diagramming tool — any locally installed or online option works, since the brief deliberately doesn't mandate one. Part C and Part F lean on a network-diagramming tool that supports the device symbols NESA uses in its own course specifications (cables, routers, hubs, switches, NICs, named server types). A mind-mapping tool is useful for Part A's social-network activities. None of these need to be the same tool across the unit — pick whatever your school already has access to.
