What Is Social Computing? Disruptive Tech in Everyday Life

Computing systems are everywhere in daily life now — so ordinary that it's easy to stop noticing them. Today's lesson is about noticing them again, on purpose, and starting to see something specific underneath all of it: every one of these systems is really a network, whether the connections in it are social, technical, or both at once.

The hook

Think about the last hour before you arrived here today. A messaging app pinged you. A streaming service recommended something based on what you watched last week. Maybe a smart speaker turned a light on. None of those things happened in isolation — each one only works because it's connected to something else: a server, a friend's device, a recommendation algorithm trained on millions of other people's choices.

The question for today If you had to explain to someone from twenty years ago why your phone "knows" what you might want to watch next, what would you actually point to as the cause? Is it the phone? The app? Or something about the connections between you, the app, and everyone else using it?

That last framing — connections, not just devices — is the real subject of this unit. Enterprise Computing calls this human-centric computing: systems designed around how people actually behave, connect and influence each other, rather than just around what a single device can technically do. Over the next two lessons, you'll see this play out twice: once as social network theory (Lesson 2), and once as the technology choices a real start-up makes because of it (Lesson 3).

Disruptive technology

A disruptive technology is one that changes the way people or enterprises operate so significantly that it displaces the previous way of doing things — not just an incremental improvement, but a genuine shift. Ride-share apps disrupted taxis. Streaming disrupted video rental. Cloud storage disrupted the external hard drive sitting in a desk drawer.

Three places disruption shows up Effects on the individual (how a person's daily habits change) · innovations in infrastructure, hardware and software (what gets built to support the new way of doing things) · trends and technology buzzwords (the language that springs up around the shift — "the cloud," "the gig economy," "AI-powered")

Buzzwords are worth pausing on specifically. They're easy to dismiss as marketing noise, but a buzzword catching on is itself a signal — it usually means enough people are talking about a real underlying shift that the language needed a shortcut. Part of thinking critically about disruptive technology is being able to separate the genuine shift from the noise being made about it.

Teacher tip

If students struggle to name a disruptive technology, ask what they used to do five years ago that they don't do anymore — the answer is almost always sitting right there.

Investigate a disruptive technology

Before choosing your own example, look closely at one worked case first.

Worked example

Buy-now-pay-later apps

A few years ago, splitting a purchase into instalments meant a formal credit application through a bank. Buy-now-pay-later apps disrupted that by embedding instalment payments directly into the checkout process of ordinary online stores — no separate application, approval in seconds, no visible interest rate on the surface.

  1. Effect on the individual: purchasing decisions get made faster, with the size of the total commitment less visible at the moment of deciding.
  2. Infrastructure, hardware and software: the technology relies on instant approval algorithms, APIs that plug into thousands of separate retailers' checkout software, and risk-modelling systems running behind the scenes.
  3. Trends and buzzwords: "BNPL" became a shorthand fast enough that it's now regulated by name in several countries — a sign the shift was real, not just marketing.
