Graph and Network Theory: Modelling Social Networks

Lesson 1 ended with a question: are the connections themselves the real subject, rather than the devices? Today you get a formal way to answer that. Graph and network theory gives you a precise language — nodes, edges, degree, paths — for describing exactly how something or someone connects to everything else, and it's the same language social network analysis uses to model relationships at scale.

Part A · Social computing is networking

The hook

Every social network you've ever used — no matter how the app actually looks — is built from exactly two kinds of thing underneath. Something is connected, and something connects it. A graph captures precisely that, and nothing more: things (call them nodes) and the connections between them (call them edges). It sounds almost too simple to be useful — but this is the same structure underneath friend lists, follower counts, recommendation engines, and the "people you may know" suggestion that always seems to know more than it should.

The question for today If a social media app wanted to suggest a new friend to you, what information about your existing connections would it actually need to look at to make a sensible guess?

Worked example: Maya's network

Maya runs social media for a start-up. Here's a small slice of her own network, drawn as a graph — her direct friends, plus the categories of content she follows.

Worked example

Reading the diagram

Count Maya's edges directly off the diagram: Jordan, Priya, Tech Roundup, Founders Forum, Startup Weekly — five connecting lines touch her node, so her degree is 5. That's not an estimate; it's a count you can re-do yourself by tracing each line back to her circle.

Now check the rest of the diagram for consistency. Jordan's degree is 2 (Maya, Tech Roundup). Priya's degree is 2 (Maya, Founders Forum). Tech Roundup's degree is 2 (Maya, Jordan). Founders Forum's degree is 2 (Maya, Priya). Startup Weekly's degree is 1 (Maya only). Add all six degrees together: 5 + 2 + 2 + 2 + 2 + 1 = 14. There are 7 edges in the diagram, and 14 is exactly double that — which is always true for any graph, since every single edge is counted once at each end. If your total degree count is ever an odd number, or doesn't come to exactly double the edge count, you've miscounted somewhere — this is a genuine check, not just a curiosity.

Your turn: degree and paths

We do this one together

What is the shortest path from Startup Weekly to Tech Roundup? Trace it: Startup Weekly only connects to Maya, and Maya connects directly to Tech Roundup. That's Startup Weekly → Maya → Tech Roundup — two edges. Could you get there in one edge instead? No — there's no line drawn directly between Startup Weekly and Tech Roundup, so two edges really is the shortest path, not just the first one you happened to spot.

Now apply the same thinking to a network of your own.

Teacher tip

Encourage pencil-and-paper sketches here rather than a polished digital diagram — the labelling and degree-counting is the skill, not the drawing tool.

Foundation · D–E

Sketch and count

Sketch a small social network with yourself as the focal node and 3 connections. Label each edge, then state your own degree.

Core · C

Sketch, count and verify

Sketch a network with yourself as focal node, 4–5 connections, and at least one connection between two of your contacts (not through you). State every node's degree, then check that the degrees add to double the number of edges.

Extension · A–B

Sketch, count and find the longest shortest path

Build on the Core task, then identify which two nodes in your network are "furthest apart" — the pair whose shortest path uses the most edges — and justify why no shorter path exists between them.

Checkpoint

Three quick questions, using Maya's network from the worked example.

1. Which best describes a node (vertex) in a graph?
2. Based on the diagram, what is the degree of the Founders Forum node?
3. What is the shortest path from Startup Weekly to Tech Roundup in the diagram?
