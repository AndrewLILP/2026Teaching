The hook

Two start-ups can sell almost the same product and have wildly different outcomes. One grows a loyal customer base that recommends it to friends unprompted; the other spends heavily on advertising and still struggles. The difference is rarely the product alone — it's usually something harder to see: the start-up's culture, and how deliberately it builds its network of customers rather than just buying attention.

The question for today If you had zero advertising budget, but customers who already loved what you made, what would you do to grow? And if you had a large advertising budget but no customers yet, what would you do instead? Are these actually two different strategies, or the same one at two different stages?

Case study: Hatchly

Case study

Hatchly — connecting local makers with buyers nearby

Hatchly is a three-person start-up running a mobile marketplace app that connects amateur local makers — woodworkers, bakers, small-batch crafters — with buyers in their own suburb. The founders work remote-first: a weekly video "show and tell" call replaces a physical office, decisions are made flat rather than through management layers, and most day-to-day coordination happens asynchronously through a shared messaging app and project board.

Hatchly's growth happened in three distinct phases. In month one, with no customers yet, the founders bought targeted social ads to reach local makers directly — paid media. Once a small base of makers joined, the founders began posting build-in-public updates on Hatchly's own blog and official social accounts, showing real makers and real sales — owned media. By month six, something changed: buyers who'd had a good experience started tagging friends and posting their own finds without being asked — earned media — and that's when Hatchly's growth accelerated past what the ad budget alone had ever produced.

Discuss: Of Hatchly's three growth phases — paid, owned and earned media — which do you think contributed most to its long-term success, and why might that phase matter more than the others even though it happened last?

Teacher tip

Push students past "earned media is free so it's best" — the real point is that earned media is usually only possible once paid and owned media have already built enough of a base for word-of-mouth to spread through.

Foundation · D–E

Name a culture trait and a media example

Core · C

Identify culture traits and classify media examples

Choose a start-up. Identify at least two characteristics of its culture, and classify at least two real promotional examples it has used into paid, owned or earned media.

Extension · A–B

Argue which growth phase mattered most

Complete the Core task, then argue — using your own researched evidence, not assumption — which media type contributed most to your chosen start-up's success, addressing why the others alone wouldn't have been enough.

Checkpoint

Three quick questions, using the Hatchly case study.

1. Which best defines "start-up culture," as used in this lesson?
2. Hatchly's month-one targeted social ads are an example of which type of media?
3. A Hatchly buyer tagging friends and posting about a maker they found, with no payment from Hatchly involved, is an example of which type of media?
