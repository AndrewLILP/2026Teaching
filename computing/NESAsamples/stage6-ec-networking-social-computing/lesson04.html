The hook

Three people run Hatchly. No office, no IT department, no dedicated server room. And yet behind that small team sits a surprising amount of connected technology — sensors tracking stock at a market stall, a smart lock on a shared packing space, wearables tracking a founder's sleep after a late night shipping orders. None of that is "the app." It's the infrastructure the app and the people around it depend on.

The question for today Some of Hatchly's connected devices exist to run the business. Others exist just to make a founder's personal life easier or healthier. Are those really the same kind of "smart device," or is something fundamentally different about them?

Servers, storage and endpoints

Every organisation's IT infrastructure — no matter how small — is built from the same three networking components:

The three components, every time Servers (always-on computers that provide a service or data to other devices on the network) · storage (where the data actually, physically lives) · end-point devices (the laptops, phones and tablets people actually use to access everything)

Where those servers and storage physically sit is the on-premises-versus-cloud decision every enterprise has to make — including a three-person start-up with no IT department at all.

On-premises computingCloud computing
Who owns the hardware?The enterprise itselfA third-party provider
Who maintains it?The enterprise's own staff (or contractors)The cloud provider
Typical cost patternLarge upfront purchase, then ongoing maintenanceOngoing subscription, scales with use
Realistic for Hatchly?No physical office or IT staff to maintain itYes — exactly what three remote founders actually use

That last row matters: Hatchly's entire infrastructure — file storage, the marketplace app's backend, the messaging and project-board tools from Lesson 3 — runs on cloud computing, precisely because none of the founders want to own, house or maintain physical servers themselves.

Regulation, compliance and cybersecurity

IoT devices have a reputation problem: many ship with weak default security — an unchanged default password is the classic example — because the manufacturer prioritised getting the device to market over locking it down. Regulators have started responding directly: several countries now require minimum security standards before a connected device can legally be sold at all.

Compliance means following the specific rules that apply to your enterprise's situation — a smart device collecting health data faces different compliance requirements than one tracking warehouse temperature, even if the underlying hardware looks similar.

Teacher tip

Ask students to check the default password situation on any smart device in their own home — most have never changed it, which makes the regulation point concrete rather than abstract.
