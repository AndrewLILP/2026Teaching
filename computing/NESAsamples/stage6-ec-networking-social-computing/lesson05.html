Worked example

The cold-chain sensor and the smartwatch

From Lesson 4's worked pair: a cold-chain temperature sensor monitoring a maker's delivery van, and a founder's smartwatch tracking sleep during a busy shipping week.

  1. Cold-chain sensor: this is specialised logistics-monitoring hardware, built specifically for tracking temperature-sensitive shipments at a professional level — not something sold in an ordinary electronics aisle. That makes it Industrial, regardless of the fact that a tiny three-person start-up is the one using it.
  2. Smartwatch: this is exactly the kind of device anyone could buy off the shelf for personal use — the fact that a Hatchly founder happens to be wearing one doesn't change what the hardware fundamentally is. That makes it Consumer, even though it's contributing useful information to how the founder manages a demanding work schedule.

Notice the pattern: category is about what the hardware is, not who is using it or why. A device doesn't become "Enterprise" just because a business happens to be the one using it.

    Teacher tip

    Some devices genuinely sit on the boundary between categories — that's expected. A reasonable justification matters more than landing on the "official" answer.

    Foundation · D–E

    Categorise your list

    Sort all 10 devices from your Lesson 4 scaffold into consumer, enterprise or industrial.

    Core · C

    Categorise and justify

    Sort all 10 devices, and for any device that was hard to decide on, write one sentence justifying your choice using the "what the hardware is, not who's using it" test.

    Extension · A–B

    Categorise, justify and map

    Complete the Core task, then sketch your own simplified network map — Hatchly as the focal node, at least 4 of your devices as connected nodes, each edge labelled with its category.

    What your infographic should answer

    A finished infographic isn't just a grouped list and a diagram — a good one answers questions a real reader would have:

    Four questions to address How did Hatchly likely select its hardware, based on the features each device offers? · How did Hatchly likely select its software, based on the features it offers? · How does any of this technology connect to the Internet of Things? · How does each device use data storage and backup, and how does it satisfy regulation, compliance and cybersecurity from Lesson 4?

    You don't need a separate essay for each question — a sentence or two per category, woven into your infographic's captions, is enough to show you've thought about all four.

    Checkpoint

    Three quick questions before you log your infographic progress today.

    1. What actually determines whether a device is consumer, enterprise or industrial?
    2. A founder's smartwatch, used to track sleep during a busy week, falls into which category?
    3. According to this lesson, what's the main problem with handing someone a raw, ungrouped list of 10 devices?
