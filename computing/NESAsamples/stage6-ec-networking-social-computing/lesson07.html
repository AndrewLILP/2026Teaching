The hook

Every line in Lesson 6's diagrams represents a real, physical thing carrying real data — a cable, or a stretch of radio waves through the air. And every one of those physical things has limits: a maximum distance, a vulnerability to something getting in the way, a point where it just stops working reliably. Today's lesson is about both halves of that problem — what the media actually are, and what defeats them.

The question for today Think of a place near you with notoriously bad phone signal — a basement, a lift, a specific street corner. What do you think is actually causing that, physically? Is it distance, or something blocking the signal, or something else entirely?

Improving data flow

Knowing what causes interference is only half the job — the syllabus also wants you to know what enterprises actually do about it.

Worked example

Hatchly's packing space has weak Wi-Fi

Founders keep losing connection while uploading product photos from the packing space. Working through the interference factors: it's not distance (the router is in the next room) and not environmental (no unusual weather or device congestion) — it's a physical barrier, specifically the packing space's thick brick wall sitting directly between the router and the founders' usual desk.

  1. Antenna size, location and power: moving the router itself closer to the packing desk — or adding a second access point on the desk's side of the wall — directly addresses the actual cause.
  2. Traffic filtering: prioritising the order-confirmation messages over large photo uploads means customers are never kept waiting just because someone's uploading photos at the same time.
  3. Flow scheduling: batching large photo uploads to run overnight, when no one else needs the connection, avoids competing with daytime order traffic entirely.
  4. Traffic monitoring: watching data use over a week would have shown the connection consistently struggling at the same desk, pointing straight at the wall as the cause before anyone needed to guess.

Only the first technique actually fixes Hatchly's specific problem — the other three are genuinely useful in other situations, but moving the access point is the one that matches a physical-barrier cause.

Teacher tip

Push students to match the technique to the actual cause, not just list all four — a flow-scheduling fix won't help a physical-barrier problem, and that mismatch is the real test of understanding.
