Part D · IoT and its protocols

Protocols in practice

Different IoT situations call for different protocols — no single one works best everywhere. The right choice depends on what the device needs most: range, power efficiency, speed, or reliability.

Protocol Best suited to Hatchly example
MQTT Sensor data on low-bandwidth or intermittent connections Cold-chain sensor publishing temperature readings to the cloud server when signal allows
HTTP/REST Device-to-web communication where bandwidth is reliable Hatchly's app fetching listing data from the cloud backend
Bluetooth LE Short-range, very low power — wearables and proximity devices A founder's smartwatch syncing activity data to their phone at close range
Zigbee Short-range mesh networking for building automation Smart lock and door sensor at the packing space communicating without Wi-Fi

The interoperability problem emerges here: Hatchly's cold-chain sensor might speak MQTT, its smart lock might speak Zigbee, and its founder's phone speaks HTTP. Getting them to share data requires either a common standard that all three support, or a gateway — a bridging device that translates between protocols, sitting between incompatible systems the way a translator sits between two people who share no common language.

Teacher tip

The gateway/translator analogy lands well here — ask students whether they think the translator (gateway) could become a single point of failure, then connect this back to Lesson 6's discussion of star topology risks.

Machine learning and IoT

IoT devices generate streams of sensor readings — temperature every 30 seconds, GPS position every minute, power consumption every hour. A human can't watch all of that in real time. Machine learning can: a model trained on what "normal" looks like will raise an alert when readings deviate from that pattern, often before a human would notice anything was wrong.

Case study

Two industries, same principle

Farm irrigation: A SCADA system monitors soil moisture sensors across a large property. A machine learning model trained on historical moisture, temperature and weather data decides when to trigger irrigation — not on a fixed schedule, but precisely when and where the soil actually needs it. The result is measurably less water used for the same crop yield.

Manufacturing quality control: A sensor array on a production line feeds readings into an ML model trained on thousands of examples of good and faulty products. The model flags anomalous readings in real time, stopping a faulty batch before it ships rather than after a customer complains.

Connect: In both examples, what is the sensor doing, what is the ML model doing, and what would happen to the outcome if either component were removed — sensors without ML, or ML without sensors?

Hatchly's version is smaller in scale but identical in principle: the cold-chain sensor generates readings, an ML model trained on historical delivery data detects an anomalous temperature climb (perhaps a van door left open) and alerts the founder's phone before the goods are damaged. The sensor provides the data; the model provides the judgement that raw data alone can't.
