The hook

You've spent eleven lessons learning how Hatchly's network works. Now the project shifts: design a home network for someone you'll invent. Not a classroom exercise in labelling symbols, but a real design that someone could actually hand to a technician and say "build this." A design that accounts for where devices will physically be, how they'll get connectivity, and what happens if the router goes down.

The brief for Part F Design, configure and optimise a home network that includes: an internet connection, a cabled console, a wireless device and an IoT device. Your finished design will be documented as a network diagram (using the device symbols from Lesson 11), configured in Lesson 13, and optimised and evaluated in Lesson 14.

Part F · Building a network

The four required components

NESA's Part F brief specifies exactly what a complete home network design must contain. These aren't suggestions — all four must appear in your final diagram and your Lesson 13 configuration.

Required component What it means Example choices
Internet connection The household's link from the ISP to the home network — the entry point for all external data NBN fibre-to-the-premises, fibre-to-the-node over copper, cable broadband, fixed wireless
Cabled console A device physically wired via Ethernet — chosen for reliable low-latency connection where wireless interference would be unacceptable Gaming console (PS5, Xbox), smart TV, desktop PC, NAS storage device
Wireless device A device connecting via Wi-Fi — mobile or in a location where running a cable is impractical Smartphone, tablet, laptop, wireless printer
IoT device A networked device with a specific physical-world function, often with limited interface — connects via Wi-Fi, Zigbee, Bluetooth LE or similar Smart thermostat, security camera, smart doorbell, smart speaker, smart lock

What every home network starts with

Before adding your four required components, every home network has the same core infrastructure sitting between the ISP and everything else. This core is not optional — it's the foundation your design builds on top of.

The switch is labelled "if needed" because many modern routers include built-in switching ports — a small household might connect everything directly to the router without a separate switch. Once you have more than four wired devices, or devices in different rooms, a dedicated switch becomes necessary.

Worked design: the Mendez household

Worked example

Starting from requirements, not devices

The Mendez household: two parents working from home, two school-age children, and an elderly grandparent who lives with them. Before choosing a single device, the designer captures requirements:

  1. Users and devices: 5 people, typically 8–12 connected devices simultaneously including phones, laptops, a TV, and a tablet.
  2. Coverage: a two-storey house — the router in the living room on the ground floor must reach the upstairs bedrooms reliably.
  3. Low-latency priority: one parent is a video editor who uploads large files; a child plays online games. The cabled console (gaming PC) needs wired Ethernet to avoid lag.
  4. IoT requirement: the grandparent has a medical alert pendant that connects to a hub — this is the IoT device, and it needs continuous connectivity, so the router must never have its power switched off by the smart powerstrip the family considered buying.
  5. Security: the working-from-home parents access employer systems — a guest Wi-Fi network, separate from the main LAN, is required for the children's and grandparent's devices.

Only after capturing these five requirements does the designer choose topology (star, from the router), devices (WAP on the upper floor to address coverage, switch for the wired devices, separate guest SSID for the IoT/children's devices), and connection type (NBN FTTP for sufficient upload speed). Requirements first — devices second.

Teacher tip

Encourage students to write their own requirements in the same numbered format before starting their diagram — a one-paragraph "device dump" is far harder to design from than five clear, testable requirements.

Part F · Building a network

Your network design

Design a home network for a household of your own invention. Your design must include all four required components and be documented as a network diagram using NESA's device symbols from Lesson 11. You'll use this exact design in Lessons 13 and 14.

Foundation · D–E

Design and diagram

Invent a household with at least two users. Write 2–3 requirements, then draw a network diagram that includes all four required components, correctly labelled with NESA device symbols.

Core · C

Requirements-first design

Write 4–5 numbered requirements for your household, then produce a diagram that demonstrably satisfies each one. Annotate the diagram with a note explaining how each requirement is met.

Extension · A–B

Justify and evaluate

Complete the Core task, then evaluate the topology of your design using the graph theory concepts from Lesson 11 — identify your most central device, check for single points of disconnection, and argue whether your design has an acceptable level of resilience for the household's stated needs.

Design checklist — before you finalise your diagram

Does every device connect to the network via the correct medium (Ethernet for the cabled console, Wi-Fi or short-range wireless for the IoT device)? Is the modem-router present and connected to the internet? Does your topology match the Lesson 6 topology that best fits your scenario? Are all device boxes labelled with NESA device-type names, not just brand names?

Checkpoint

Three quick questions before you record your design choices today.

1. What is the primary role of the modem in a home network?
2. Why would a gaming console in a home network specifically benefit from a cabled (Ethernet) connection rather than Wi-Fi?
3. Deciding where in the house to place the router and how to run Ethernet cables between floors is part of which type of design?
