Optimising, Evaluating and Presenting Your Network

You've designed a network and configured its security. The final question is whether it actually works as specified — and if it doesn't, what to change. Today you'll walk through a structured optimisation and evaluation process, then prepare a presentation that explains your design from requirements through to final performance. This is the last lesson in the unit, and the work in it draws on everything from Part A through to Part F.

The hook

The Mendez household in Lesson 12 had five requirements. After setup and configuration in Lesson 13, requirement 3 — low-latency wired connection for the gaming PC — was working well. But requirement 2, coverage across both floors, was failing: the children's bedroom router signal was barely one bar, and video calls kept dropping. The design was technically complete. But it wasn't finished until it met every requirement it set out to meet.

The question for today A network that's been designed, configured and secured but still has a performance problem isn't a finished network — it's a first version. What's the difference between a fix that solves a symptom and a fix that addresses the underlying cause?

Optimising the network

Worked example

The Mendez coverage problem — symptoms vs cause

Requirement 2 failed: weak signal upstairs, video calls dropping. Before changing anything, the Mendez family identified the cause rather than just the symptom.

  1. Symptom: one bar of signal in the children's bedroom, calls dropping during peak evening hours.
  2. Diagnosis: the router is on the ground floor in the living room. A speed test run at the bedroom shows 4 Mbps download; the same test at the router shows 95 Mbps. The interference factor (from Lesson 7) is distance plus one concrete floor — a physical barrier, not an environmental one.
  3. Fix matched to cause: adding a wireless access point on the upper floor, connected by a single Ethernet cable running through the wall cavity, resolves the physical-barrier problem directly. A Wi-Fi extender was also considered but rejected — extenders halve effective bandwidth each hop; a wired WAP avoids this.
  4. Secondary fix: QoS was configured on the router to prioritise the parents' video-call traffic between 9 am and 5 pm. This didn't fix the coverage problem, but it did resolve the drop-out during calls — two separate issues, two separate fixes.

After these changes, re-running the speed test at the bedroom showed 78 Mbps — within acceptable range of the ISP's stated speed. Requirement 2 was now met.

Optimisation technique What it addresses When to use it
Firmware update (router/WAP) Security vulnerabilities, bugs, Wi-Fi performance improvements Before deployment and whenever a new update is released
Driver update (device NIC) Device-side connection instability, speed limitations with newer Wi-Fi standards When a specific device underperforms even close to the router
QoS configuration Congestion during peak use — multiple devices competing for bandwidth When high-priority applications suffer during periods of heavy household use
Wired WAP or Ethernet switch Physical barriers and distance reducing wireless signal When speed tests show large drop-off between router and end device
Schedule large transfers Background downloads competing with real-time traffic When overnight or off-peak scheduling is practical (backups, system updates)

Teacher tip

The distinction between firmware update (router) and driver update (end device NIC) is easy to blur — firmware updates the hardware's own embedded software; a driver update is an OS-level change on the device using the hardware. Both matter; they fix different things.

Evaluating against requirements

A design is only as good as its requirements were specific. "Good coverage" is not a testable requirement. "All devices reach at least 25 Mbps anywhere in the house" is.

We do this one together

Take Mendez requirement 1: "5 people, typically 8–12 connected devices simultaneously." The evaluation test for this is: connect 12 devices simultaneously and run a speed test on each. If all 12 receive at least their minimum usable bandwidth for their function (a video call needs approximately 4 Mbps; a background download can use whatever's left), the requirement is met. If some devices time out or receive zero bandwidth, the switch port count or router's DHCP lease limit needs checking — specific, testable, fixable.

Now apply the same logic to each of your own Lesson 12 requirements. For each one: what is the specific test? What is the pass condition? What would you do if it fails?

Structuring the presentation

A network presentation to an informed audience covers the whole lifecycle, not just the diagram. The five sections below are the minimum expected structure — your audience should be able to understand the design, reproduce it, and know whether it succeeded.

Five-section presentation structure 1. Household scenario and requirements — who lives there, what they need, the 4–5 numbered requirements from Lesson 12. 2. Network diagram — the logical design using NESA device symbols, annotated with connection types. 3. Configuration and security — SSID assignments, segmentation rationale, password policy, firewall and VPN decisions. 4. Optimisation — any changes made after initial setup and why; firmware/driver updates applied. 5. Evaluation — each original requirement tested, the result, and any remaining gaps with proposed next steps.

Your final submission

This is the culminating task for the unit. It draws on the design from Lesson 12, the configuration from Lesson 13, and today's optimisation and evaluation work. Your notebook entries from all three Part F lessons are your source material.

Foundation · D–E

Design, diagram and evaluate

Submit your network diagram with all four required components labelled, your requirements list, and a basic evaluation: for each requirement, state whether your design meets it and one thing you would change if you were to redo it.

Core · C

Full five-section presentation

Produce a presentation covering all five sections, with each requirement evaluated against a specific, stated test condition. Include at least one optimisation technique applied to your design and the reasoning behind it.

Extension · A–B

Critically evaluate and reflect

Complete the Core task, then add a sixth section: a genuine critical evaluation of your design's weaknesses — requirements it doesn't fully meet, security considerations it leaves unresolved, or resilience limitations identified by graph-theory analysis (from Lesson 11). Propose specific, costed or effort-estimated changes and argue which is the highest priority.
