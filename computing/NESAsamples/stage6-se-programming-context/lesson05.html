Decomposition and Pattern Recognition

Back in Lesson 1, you were asked whether you could explain how you solve Sudoku precisely enough for someone else to follow, step by step, with no skipped thoughts. Today you'll actually do it — by breaking the problem into smaller pieces, and noticing which of those pieces you end up doing over and over again.

Researching & Designing

No skipped thoughts

Try saying out loud how you solve a Sudoku square. You'll probably say something like "I just look at what's missing." That sentence has a hidden skipped thought in it: how do you know what's missing? What, exactly, are you checking, and in what order? A computer can't run on "I just look" — every single step has to be made explicit.

The question for today Two skills make "I just look" turn into something a computer could actually run: breaking the problem into smaller pieces (decomposition), and noticing which of those pieces repeat (pattern recognition).
Researching & Designing

Decomposition

Decomposition means breaking a big, hard-to-tackle problem into smaller problems that are each much easier to solve on their own.

"Solve the Sudoku puzzle" broken down Instead of one giant instruction, the problem splits into smaller, manageable pieces: find an empty square → try a value in it → check whether that value is allowed → place it (or try a different value) → move on

None of those five smaller pieces is hard on its own. The difficulty in Sudoku was never any single step — it was trying to hold the whole puzzle in your head at once. Decomposition is what makes that unnecessary.

Researching & Designing

Pattern recognition

Pattern recognition means noticing when one of your smaller pieces isn't actually unique — it's the same piece of work showing up again and again, just with different numbers plugged in.

The pattern hiding in our breakdown "Check whether that value is allowed" happens for every single square, for every single value you try — dozens or even hundreds of times in one solve. It's exactly the same check every time: does this value already exist in this row, this column, or this 3×3 box?

Once you've spotted a pattern like that, it's a strong signal that piece deserves to become its own reusable building block — something you'll act on directly when you start writing functions in Lesson 10.

Sequence, selection, iteration

Every algorithm, however complicated, is built from just three basic kinds of step. Tap each card to reveal what it means.

These three names will matter a lot more in Lesson 7, where you'll use selection and iteration explicitly to scan the grid. For now, it's enough to be able to spot each one inside your own decomposition — which is exactly what the next task asks you to do.

Researching & Designing

Decomposing our solver

Let's decompose "solve the puzzle" together, in full

  1. Find the next empty square. If there isn't one, the puzzle is already solved.
  2. Try placing a value in it, starting at 1.
  3. Check whether that value is already used in its row, its column, or its 3×3 box.
  4. If it's allowed, place it and move on to the next empty square.
  5. If it isn't allowed, try the next value instead.
  6. If no value from 1–9 works in this square, undo the last placement and go back to try a different value there. (This last step is called backtracking — you'll meet it properly in Lessons 8 and 9.)

Step 3 is the pattern we identified above — it repeats, unchanged, no matter which square or value you're checking. Steps 1, 2, 4 and 6 each happen too, but they're more tied to a specific moment in the solve rather than being one identical, reusable check.

Foundation · D–E

Spot the pattern

Looking at the six-step breakdown above, identify which step is the clearest example of a repeating pattern, and explain in one or two sentences why it counts as one.

Core · C

Decompose a new problem

Choose a different everyday task — alphabetising a shelf of books, or assembling a jigsaw puzzle — and break it down into a similar list of smaller steps. Underline whichever step(s) repeat as a pattern.

Extension · A–B

Plan your functions

Look back at the six-step Sudoku breakdown and decide which steps would naturally become their own separate function later in the build, versus which ones are one-off and wouldn't be reused. Justify each call.
