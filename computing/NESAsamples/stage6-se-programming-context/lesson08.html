Divide and Conquer, and Backtracking: The Idea

Back in Lesson 1, you noticed a moment in your own Sudoku-solving where a number you placed turned out to be wrong, and you had to erase it and try again. Today that moment gets a name — backtracking — and you'll meet it through a problem that has nothing to do with Sudoku at all, alongside a second strategy, divide and conquer, that the syllabus pairs with it.

Researching & Designing

Two new strategies

Decomposition told you how to break a problem into pieces. Loops and branching told you how to repeat and decide. Today's two strategies are about something different: how you actually search for a solution when you don't know in advance which choice will work. Neither idea will touch a Sudoku grid today — that's deliberate. Both are easier to trust once you've seen them somewhere simpler first.

Researching & Designing

Divide and conquer

Divide and conquer means splitting a problem into smaller subproblems of the same kind, solving each one, then combining the results. A classic example is searching a sorted list: instead of checking every item one by one, you can repeatedly cut the list in half.

Let's trace a binary search together

Sorted list: [2, 5, 8, 12, 16, 23, 38, 45, 56, 72, 91] (indices 0–10). Target: find 45.

Steplow, highmid indexValue at midDecision
10, 1052345 > 23 → search the right half
26, 1085645 < 56 → search the left half
36, 763845 > 38 → search the right half
47, 7745Found it!

Eleven items, found in just four steps — because each step throws away half of what's left, instead of checking items one at a time.

Backtracking

Backtracking means trying a choice, and — if it eventually leads to a dead end — undoing that choice and trying a different one instead. Picture a small maze with two junctions:

The maze Junction 1 offers Left or Right. Left leads to Junction 2; Right is a dead end.
Junction 2 offers Left or Right. Left is a dead end; Right leads to the Finish.

Let's trace a path through it together

StepPositionChoice triedResult
1Junction 1Try LeftLeads to Junction 2 — continue
2Junction 2Try LeftDead end — backtrack
3Junction 2Try RightLeads to the Finish — success!

Notice that the dead end at Step 2 didn't end the search — it just meant undoing that one choice and trying the next option at the same junction, not starting over from Junction 1. That's the entire idea of backtracking in one sentence: undo the smallest possible amount, then try again.

Now trace a maze of your own.

Choose your task

Foundation · D–E

Trace a given maze

Junction 1 offers Up or Down — Up is a dead end, Down leads to Junction 2. Junction 2 has only one path, leading straight to the Finish. Trace the path through it in a table like the one above.

Core · C

Design and trace your own

Design your own small maze with at least two junctions and at least one dead end, then trace a full path through it from start to finish, including any backtracking step.

Extension · A–B

Add a three-way junction

Design a maze where at least one junction has three options, not two, and trace a path through it. Explain what information the algorithm would need to keep track of at that junction so it doesn't try the same dead-end option twice.

Teacher tip

The Extension task is deliberately fishing for the idea of "remembering which options have already been tried" — that's exactly the bookkeeping the real Sudoku solver will need in Lesson 9.

Comparing the two

AspectDivide and conquerBacktracking
What it doesSplits a problem into independent subproblems, solves each, combines resultsTries a choice, and undoes it if it leads to a dead end
SubproblemsIndependent of each otherEach choice depends on the choices made so far
Example todayBinary searchMaze navigation
Role in our solverNot used directly by the Sudoku solverExactly how the solver will decide what to place — next lesson

Both strategies are named together in the syllabus because they're both genuine algorithm design strategies worth knowing — but only one of them is about to become the engine of your Sudoku solver. Lesson 9 takes today's maze idea and points it directly at the grid.
