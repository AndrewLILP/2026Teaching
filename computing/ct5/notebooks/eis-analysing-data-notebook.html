Your Project
Notebook

This notebook documents how you turned a real-world question into an evidence-based answer. It captures your data decisions — what you collected, how you cleaned and queried it, what your visualisations revealed, and what you concluded.

Suggested pathway The Evaluation Consultant

One strong direction for this project: build a system that collects, stores, and visualises project evaluation data from student work across Year 7–12 — including Year 10 major projects. Students and teachers bring their projects to you. You gather ratings, rubric scores, and qualitative feedback, then use your system to find patterns: what makes a strong project? where do students consistently lose marks? what do high-scoring projects have in common?

If you follow this path, you become a data consultant for your school — your findings have genuine value for the students who come after you, and for Year 11/12 where data analysis sits at the core of Enterprise Computing. The prompts in this notebook work for both this pathway and an open dataset investigation — use whichever fits your project.

Identifying & Defining
Question & Purpose
Data analysis without a clear question is just data shuffling. Your question shapes every decision that follows — what data you need, how you clean it, what visualisations you choose, and what counts as a finding.
Consultant pathway

Your question might be: "What patterns exist in project evaluation scores across Year 7–10, and what characteristics distinguish high-achieving projects from low-achieving ones?" Or more specific: "Do students who document their design process more thoroughly achieve higher evaluation scores?" A focused, answerable question is always stronger than a broad one.

❓ The research question
All
  • 01State your research question clearly. It should be specific enough to answer with data, and open enough to yield interesting findings — not just a yes/no.
  • 02Break it into sub-questions. What smaller questions, if answered, would together answer your main question?
  • 03Who are the users of your analysis? Who will act on your findings? How will they use them? (e.g. a teacher adjusting their feedback approach; a student deciding how to allocate time; a school identifying curriculum gaps.)
  • 04What decisions could your analysis inform? A good research question is connected to a real decision someone needs to make.
⬆ To reach Grade B–A: your question should be genuinely investigative — it should be possible to be surprised by the answer. "How many students completed the project?" is descriptive. "Is there a relationship between project planning thoroughness and final score?" is analytical.

Identifying & Defining
Data Sources & Collection
You can only answer questions your data can support. Before collecting anything, understand what data you need, where it comes from, and what form it takes.
Consultant pathway

Your data comes from students and teachers. You might collect: rubric scores per criterion, overall grades, teacher comments, self-assessment ratings, time spent per D&P phase, project type (game / app / mechatronics / data), and year level. Each of these is a field in your database — plan them before collecting a single response.

📦 Data identification
All
  • 01What data do you need to answer your research question? List every field/variable, its data type (numerical, categorical, text, date), and what it represents.
  • 02Where will this data come from? (A public dataset, a survey you design, existing records, an API, manual observation?) For each source, note how reliable and complete it is likely to be.
  • 03What data is not available to you? Every dataset has gaps. Acknowledging them shapes how confident you can be in your conclusions.
  • 04What is the minimum dataset that would still let you answer your research question? This is your MVP — what you need before you can produce any meaningful analysis.
⬆ To reach Grade B–A: justify your field choices. "I'm collecting overall grade" is weaker than "I'm collecting scores per rubric criterion because overall grade alone doesn't show where students lose marks."

Identifying & Defining
Ethics, Privacy & Bias
Data about people carries responsibilities. Every decision about what data to collect, how to store it, and how to present findings has an ethical dimension — and the syllabus requires you to address it directly.
🔐 Privacy, security & data ownership
All
  • 01Does your data contain personally identifiable information (PII)? Name, student ID, year level, and scores can all identify individuals. How will you protect this — anonymisation, aggregation, access controls?
  • 02Who owns the data you are collecting? If you're collecting scores from student projects, do those students have the right to know their data is being used? Do they have the right to opt out?
  • 03Where will the data be stored? Who can access it? What happens to it at the end of the project?
  • 04What would a data breach mean for the people in your dataset? What steps have you taken to minimise the risk?
⬆ To reach Grade B–A: data ethics is not a checkbox — it's a design decision. Show that your privacy protections are built into your system from the start, not added as an afterthought. "I will anonymise all data before analysis" should appear in your schema, not just in your ethics section.
write your response in your notebook document
⚖️ Bias in data & visualisation
All
  • 01Where could bias enter your dataset? Consider: who chooses to respond to your survey (self-selection bias), whether some groups are systematically underrepresented, whether your scoring criteria disadvantage certain types of projects.
  • 02How could your visualisation choices create a misleading impression? (e.g. a truncated y-axis exaggerates differences; cherry-picking the date range of a trend; choosing a chart type that hides outliers.)
  • 03What is the data trail for your analysis? If someone wanted to verify your findings, what would they need to access? How have you documented your process so it could be audited?
⬆ To reach Grade A: the best data analysts are sceptical of their own findings. Proactively identifying where your visualisations could mislead — and showing how you've designed against it — is Grade A systems thinking applied to data.

🔎 Query planning
All
  • 01For each sub-question in your investigation, plan the database query or filter you will use to extract the relevant data. What fields will you select? What conditions will you filter on? What aggregation (count, average, sum) will you apply?
  • 02Write pseudocode or a plain-language description of each key query before implementing it. (e.g. "Select all projects where year_level = 10 AND project_type = 'game', group by criterion, calculate average score per criterion.")
  • 03What output does each query produce? What format will the results be in, and how will they feed into your visualisations?
Example query pseudocode
// Sub-question: Which criterion do Year 10 students
// score lowest on average?
SELECT criterion, AVG(score / max_score * 100) AS avg_pct
FROM criterion_scores
JOIN projects ON criterion_scores.project_id = projects.project_id
WHERE projects.year_level = 10
GROUP BY criterion
ORDER BY avg_pct ASC

// Expected output: table of criterion names + avg %
// → feeds into a horizontal bar chart
