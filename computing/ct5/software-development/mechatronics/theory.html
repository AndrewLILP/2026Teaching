Building Mechatronic & Automated Systems

Mechatronics is where mechanical engineering, electronics, and programming converge. In this focus area you'll explore how robots and automated systems are designed, built, and evaluated — from identifying what a system must do through to programming, testing, and reflecting on its social and ethical impact.

📋 Teaching advice

Students apply computing skill domains to build and program a mechatronic and/or automated system using a range of sensors, actuators and end effectors. Projects explore robotics, controllers and object-oriented programming. This focus area can be combined with Analysing Data (using sensors to collect and plot data) or Modelling Networks & Social Connections (building a remotely controlled robot).

Key vocabulary

These terms appear throughout the focus area. Make sure you can explain each one in your own words before moving to the activities below.

📖 Word bank

Mechatronic system
A system that integrates mechanical components, electronics, sensors, actuators and software to perform automated tasks.
Sensor
A device that detects and measures physical input from the environment — distance, light, temperature, touch — and converts it to a signal a controller can read.
Actuator
A device that converts a signal from the controller into physical movement or action — motors, servos, solenoids, and pistons are all actuators.
End effector / manipulator
The part of a robot that directly interacts with the environment — a gripper, claw, drill, or paint nozzle.
Microcontroller
A small, self-contained computing unit — processor, memory, and I/O pins in one chip — used to control mechatronic systems. Examples: VEX EXP brain, Arduino.
Co-processor
A secondary processor that handles specific tasks (e.g. vision processing, wireless communication) so the main controller can focus on logic.
Functional requirement
What the system must do — a specific behaviour or feature (e.g. "the arm must lift objects weighing up to 500g").
Non-functional requirement
How well the system must do it — qualities like speed, accuracy, noise level, battery life, or manufacturing cost.
Algorithm
A precise, step-by-step set of instructions to solve a problem or control a process. In mechatronics, algorithms typically involve reading sensor data, making decisions, and triggering actuators.
Autonomous control
A system operating under its own programming without ongoing human input — the system detects, decides, and acts by itself.
Iterative design
A cyclical development approach: design → build → test → improve. You don't build it perfectly the first time — you build, evaluate, and refine.
Desk checking
Manually tracing through pseudocode step-by-step to validate algorithm logic before coding it — identifying logical errors before they become bugs in hardware.
Identifying & Defining

Identifying & Defining

Before building any system, developers must understand what it needs to do. This phase is about exploring what mechatronic systems are, how they operate, and defining exactly what your system will need to achieve.

A mechatronic system integrates multiple disciplines to automate or enhance physical tasks:

  • Mechanics — the physical structure, joints, gears, and materials
  • Electronics — the circuits, power supplies, and signal pathways
  • Computing — the microcontroller, sensors, and the program that ties it all together

Examples range from simple (an automatic sliding door) to complex (a robotic surgical arm or a self-driving vehicle). What they share is a tight integration between hardware and software.

Think about it: A washing machine, a drone, a factory assembly line, a prosthetic arm, and a home thermostat are all mechatronic systems. What sensors, actuators, and programs does each one use?

Mechatronic and automated systems have evolved dramatically in response to people's needs and opportunities:

  • Industrial automation (1960s–80s): Hydraulic robot arms replaced dangerous repetitive factory work.
  • Embedded systems (1990s): Microcontrollers became cheap enough to include in consumer products — cars, appliances, medical devices.
  • Sensor revolution (2000s–10s): MEMS sensors (accelerometers, gyroscopes) enabled smartphones, drones, and wearables.
  • AI and autonomy (2020s): Machine learning enables systems to adapt to their environment — self-driving vehicles, agricultural robots, surgical assistants.
Syllabus link (CT5-EVL-01): How has enterprise and innovation driven the development of mechatronic systems? Who funded the research? What problems did they solve? Who benefited — and who was displaced?

  • Inputs: Sensor readings — distance, colour, speed, angle, force, temperature.
  • Storage: Variables in the microcontroller's memory; sometimes logged to a file or transmitted to a cloud database.
  • Processes: The algorithm — comparisons, calculations, control logic that translates sensor data into decisions.

Key skill: Before designing your system, trace the signal path for an existing automated device. For a VEX robot: what are the inputs? What does the brain decide? What do the motors do in response?

Before selecting hardware, you specify what your system must do (functional) and how well it must do it (non-functional). Requirements come from talking to stakeholders and analysing the problem.

  • Functional requirements: "The robot must detect and avoid obstacles at distances under 30cm." "The arm must pick up objects from position A and place them at position B."
  • Non-functional requirements: Accuracy, response time, battery life, noise level, cost of manufacture, safety under failure, ease of maintenance.
NESA examples (non-functional): Battery disposal and environmental impact · Improved wellbeing as a result of implementation (e.g. a prosthetic assists daily living) · Ease of manufacturing the system · Safety and fail-safe behaviour

Requirements define your test cases — once built, you'll check each requirement against observed behaviour.

Mechatronic systems affect society at scale. Designers must consider the perspectives of diverse groups, including Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples, people with disability, different ages and genders, and culturally and linguistically diverse communities.

NESA examples: Companion robots for aged care and social support · Job loss due to factory automation · The impact of surveillance drones on privacy · Asimov's Laws of Robotics as a framework for thinking about AI ethics · Military robots and autonomous weapons systems · Safety and liability when an automated system causes harm · Permissions and protocols for capturing images of Aboriginal Peoples via robotic or drone systems
Reflection prompt: If your system automates a task a human currently performs, who benefits? Who loses work or income? Who decides when the robot takes over?
Researching & Planning

Researching & Planning

This phase is about investigating existing systems, selecting hardware components, representing algorithms, and planning how you'll build and manage your project. Good planning in mechatronics saves significant time once hardware is involved — debugging physical systems is much harder than debugging code alone.

Professional engineers never build the first idea they have. Generating multiple design alternatives and evaluating them against requirements is a core engineering skill.

  • Describe how mechatronic systems have evolved in response to real-world needs
  • Explore design principles and issues relevant to your specific system (motion, stability, power, safety)
  • Generate at least two alternative designs and evaluate them against your functional requirements
  • Select a preferred design and justify your choice — why does it best meet the requirements?
Design thinking tip: Use a simple decision matrix — list your requirements as columns, your design options as rows, and score each combination. Objective criteria make design decisions defensible and easier to explain to stakeholders.

Component selection is a design decision — every choice has a trade-off between cost, capability, power consumption, and ease of programming.

Component type Role Examples Selection factors
Microcontroller The "brain" — runs your program VEX EXP Brain, Arduino, Raspberry Pi Pico I/O pins, processing power, programming language
Co-processor Offloads specialist tasks VEX Vision Sensor, wireless modules What task needs offloading? Communication protocol?
Sensor Reads physical environment Ultrasonic (distance), line tracker, IMU, colour, bumper Range, accuracy, response time, analog or digital
Actuator Creates physical movement DC motor, servo motor, stepper motor, solenoid Speed, torque, precision, power draw

Justify your choices: "I selected the ultrasonic sensor because it provides accurate distance measurement at the 5–200cm range required by our obstacle avoidance algorithm, and its digital output integrates simply with the VEX EXP smart port." That's a justified component selection.

Even simple mechatronic systems collect data. Before building, specify what data your system collects, who owns it, and how it is protected.

Example — a robot collecting GPS data:
  • Data collected: Smartphone/robot GPS coordinates and timestamps
  • Ownership: Users and vendors — consider cultural considerations, Indigenous Cultural and Intellectual Property (ICIP)
  • Who has access: Could location data be onsold to a third party? Could law enforcement request it?
  • Protection: Password protection, encryption, or legal data use agreements
  • Identify what data your system will collect and interpret
  • Apply privacy principles — collect only what's necessary, protect it, and be transparent
  • Apply cybersecurity principles — what happens if your system is hacked or spoofed?

Before writing a single line of code, represent your algorithm in NSW pseudocode. This separates logical thinking from syntax concerns and lets you find errors cheaply — before hardware is involved.

  • Select appropriate data types for sensor readings and control variables
  • Represent algorithms using NSW pseudocode and/or flowcharts
  • Design or modify existing algorithms for your specific system
  • Validate algorithms with desk checking before implementation
Desk checking example: If your obstacle avoidance algorithm says "IF distance < 30 THEN reverse", desk check by asking: what if distance = 29? What if distance = 30? What if the sensor returns 0? What if it returns a negative value? Catching these edge cases in pseudocode costs nothing — finding them after the robot has driven into a wall costs time and possibly hardware.

Mechatronic projects are inherently iterative — you cannot build everything at once, and physical components introduce constraints that change plans. Effective project management keeps a complex build on track.

  • Break the system into manageable subsystems (drive, sensing, grabbing, displaying)
  • Use an iterative approach — build and test one subsystem before integrating the next
  • Document your project development throughout — what you planned, what you built, and what changed
  • Use specialist terminology correctly when documenting and communicating your work
Sprint structure: Sprint 0 — familiarise yourself with the hardware and environment by completing a guided challenge. Sprint 1+ — design, build, and refine your system to meet a real brief. Each sprint ends with a test and evaluation.
Producing & Implementing

Producing & Implementing

This phase is where the design becomes real. Students investigate a problem, build and program their system, and apply modular programming techniques in an object-oriented language to create maintainable, testable code.

Before building, investigate the problem deeply — including interviewing stakeholders to understand their actual needs.

  • Investigate a real-world problem or need that can be solved by a mechatronic and/or automated system
  • Break the problem down into manageable sub-problems
  • Interview stakeholders to identify their needs, priorities and constraints
  • Evaluate existing solutions to the problem or related problems — what already exists? What does it do well? Where does it fall short?
Analysis before creation: Tracing and annotating an existing system before designing your own is one of the most powerful learning strategies in engineering. You'll understand design choices better once you've had to make them yourself.

Building a mechatronic system means integrating hardware and software — and adapting your plan when reality doesn't match theory.

  • Interpret and modify existing programs (code) for mechatronic and/or automated systems
  • Design and implement modular programs using functions for mechatronic and/or automated systems
  • Build the system using the preferred design and code in an object-oriented programming language
  • Apply selected algorithms and data structures appropriate to the system's needs
  • Create a record of project development demonstrating iterative design and evaluation
Modular thinking: Write a separate function for each distinct behaviour — driveForward(), readDistance(), avoidObstacle(). Functions are easier to test individually and easier to reuse across sprints.

Choosing the right data type matters more in embedded systems than in most other programming contexts — memory is limited, and incorrect types cause subtle bugs.

  • Represent data and code to facilitate computation — select appropriate data types, understand their limitations
  • Structure code systematically — avoid magic numbers, use named constants for thresholds
  • Use integer types for sensor counts and loop counters; floating-point for calculated values like speed or angle
Example: int distanceCM = sensor.distance(); — stores the ultrasonic reading as a whole number (cm).
const int STOP_DISTANCE = 30; — a named constant makes the threshold readable and easy to change.
Testing & Evaluating

Testing & Evaluating

Testing a mechatronic system means verifying both the software logic and the physical behaviour. A program that passes desk checking may still fail in the real world — sensors are noisy, motors slip, and environments are unpredictable.

  • Test cases: Use predetermined functional and non-functional requirements as the basis for each test. Define: input condition → expected behaviour → actual behaviour → pass/fail.
  • Tracing: Validate algorithms and programs through tracing — step through the logic manually, tracking variable values at each step. Critical before deploying to hardware.
  • Algorithm validation: Use selected data to check algorithms by testing. Include boundary values (e.g. distance = 0, distance = 30, distance = 31).
  • Peer evaluation: Evaluate your own project and that of peers using the same predetermined requirements — a peer may catch failures you've stopped seeing.
  • Debug a range of errors: Validate programs using test cases; learn to distinguish sensor error, logic error, and hardware fault.

Testing always reveals gaps between design intent and actual performance. Evaluation is the foundation of the next iteration.

  • Propose software modifications to increase the effectiveness of your system
  • Propose hardware modifications — alternative sensors, structural improvements, power supply changes
  • Evaluate social, ethical and cybersecurity considerations that emerged during the project
Professional engineering mindset: A system that "kind of works" is not a finished system. Document what works, what doesn't, what you'd change, and why — this reflection is evidence of engineering thinking, not failure.

Analysis activity — examine before you build

Before designing your own system, you'll analyse an existing one. Understanding how a real mechatronic system works gives you far better design instincts than starting from scratch.

🔍 Analysis activity

Annotate a real mechatronic system

Choose a real-world mechatronic or automated system (e.g. a robotic vacuum cleaner, a self-checkout machine, a drone, an automatic car park boom gate, or your class VEX robot). Work through the following steps in your project notebook:

  1. System identification: Is this system autonomous, semi-autonomous, or human-controlled? What evidence supports your answer?
  2. Component mapping: Identify the microcontroller (or equivalent), at least two sensors, at least one actuator, and the end effector/manipulator (if any). Draw and label the physical layout.
  3. Signal path trace: Describe how a sensor reading travels through the system to produce a physical output. What data is collected? What decision is made? What movement results?
  4. Algorithm sketch: Write a simplified NSW pseudocode algorithm that could describe one key behaviour of the system (e.g. obstacle detection and avoidance).
  5. Social and ethical scan: Who benefits from this system? Are there groups who are disadvantaged or excluded? What happens if the system fails? Who is liable?

Social & ethical considerations

Automated systems can dramatically improve people's lives — or cause significant harm. Engineers who build these systems carry legal and ethical responsibility for what they create.

Automation and employment

Robots and automated systems replace repetitive or dangerous tasks — but also displace workers. Who bears the cost of that transition? How do companies, governments and communities respond?

Privacy and surveillance

Systems with cameras, microphones, or GPS continuously collect data. Who has access? Can data be used to track or monitor people without their knowledge or consent?

Safety and liability

When an autonomous system causes an accident, who is legally responsible — the programmer, the company, or the user? Real cases from self-driving vehicles and surgical robots have shaped how courts answer this.

Environmental impact

Batteries, motors, and electronics require manufacturing, use energy, and generate waste. Responsible design considers the full lifecycle — from sourcing materials to safe disposal.

Inclusion and accessibility

Mechatronic systems can improve independence for people with disability — prosthetics, mobility aids, voice interfaces. But they must be designed for the full range of users, not just the average person.

Cultural and community rights

Robots and drones operating in or near Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities raise questions about permissions, protocols, and cultural rights to land, image and data. ICIP applies to automated data collection.

Tiered tasks

Choose the task that matches your current level — or challenge yourself with the next tier up.

Foundation

System annotation

Choose a provided image of a real mechatronic system (e.g. a robotic arm or drone). Label the microcontroller, at least two sensors, and at least one actuator. Write one sentence describing what each component does and how it connects to the rest of the system.

Core

Requirements and algorithm sketch

Choose a simple automated task (e.g. a robot that sorts objects by colour). Write three functional requirements and two non-functional requirements. Sketch a NSW pseudocode algorithm for one key behaviour and desk check it with two test cases.

Extension

Design critique and proposal

Select a real automated system (self-checkout, warehouse robot, or agricultural drone). Critically evaluate its design against functional requirements you reverse-engineer, identify one social or ethical concern, and propose a specific modification — with hardware and software changes — that would address it. Justify your proposal.
