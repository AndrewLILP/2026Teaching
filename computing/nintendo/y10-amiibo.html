Amiibo Redesign

Back in Year 7, you designed a simple figurine in TinkerCAD. Now you're revisiting that same character with three years of extra skill and a real commercial reference point: Nintendo's own Amiibo line. Redesign it, print it, photograph it properly, and list it as a genuine product in the e-Store you just built. This lesson closes the loop between physical design and digital commerce.

Designing

Your Brief

Studio context: "Merchandise is a real product line, not an afterthought — Amiibo generate serious revenue alongside the games themselves. I want your Year 7 character redesigned to a standard that could sit on a real shelf: printable without excessive support material, photographed like a product listing, and actually for sale in your own e-Store."

Find your original Year 7 TinkerCAD file before starting. You're not starting from nothing — you're revising a real design decision you made three years ago with the knowledge you have now.

Designing

Analyse Real Amiibo Design

Before redesigning, look at photos of real Amiibo figures (search online, or examine one in person if you have access to one). Note the conventions: a circular base of a consistent diameter across the entire product line, a dynamic character pose that reads clearly from any angle, and a level of detail deliberately simplified for manufacturing at scale — not the same as a hand-painted collectible sculpture.

Constraints are part of the design

A standard base diameter isn't a limitation imposed on the designer — it's a deliberate choice that lets every figure in the product line sit on the same NFC-reading base and display consistently together on a shelf. Good product design often means designing within a constraint, not around it.

Designing

Redesign in TinkerCAD

Revise your Year 7 figurine with three real constraints in mind: a minimum wall thickness so thin sections don't snap during or after printing, minimal overhangs so the model needs little or no support material, and a standard circular base matching your class's agreed diameter.

Tiered Redesign Task

Foundation · Grades D–E

Base and thickness fixed

Add a standard circular base to your original design and correct any wall sections thinner than the class minimum. Pose stays as originally designed.

Core · Grade C

Pose refined for printability

Revise the pose itself to reduce unsupported overhangs — angling limbs, thickening thin protrusions — while keeping the character clearly recognisable.

Extension · Grades A–B

Genuine product-line thinking

Design as if this is the first of a series — a consistent basing style, silhouette, or detail language that a second and third figure in the same "line" could visibly share.

Producing & Implementing

Print & Photograph

Export your model and slice it, checking the estimated print time and support material before committing to a print. Once printed, photograph it the way a real product listing would — even lighting, a plain background, and a consistent angle. This isn't decoration: it's the same "aesthetic and minimalist design" heuristic from your e-Store audit, applied to a physical object instead of a screen.

Designing

Enterprise, Innovation & Automation

Studio Discussion

Amiibo exist because merchandise is a genuine business line alongside the games themselves — a company innovates not just by building better games, but by finding new ways to turn a character players already love into a product. 3D printing itself is a form of automation: the same digital file can be manufactured repeatedly without a human resculpting each copy by hand.

Answer in your notebook:

  1. Beyond selling more copies, what does a physical collectible line give a company that a purely digital product can't?
  2. Your TinkerCAD file is the "automated" part of this process — the same file could print your figure hundreds of times identically. What would be lost, and what would be gained, if every figure were instead hand-sculpted individually?

Producing & Implementing

List in Your e-Store

Add your finished Amiibo as a new entry in your products.json file from the e-Store Build lesson — a new id, a name, a price, a category, and your product photograph as the image. Reload your e-Store and confirm it appears in the catalogue, can be filtered and sorted correctly alongside the rest of your listings, and can be added to the cart like any other product.

This is the real test

If your new entry breaks the grid, doesn't match your existing category filters, or looks visually inconsistent next to your other listings, that's a genuine finding about your data structure or your filter logic — not just a cosmetic issue to shrug off.

Studio Checkpoint

Before this counts as complete, all of the following must be true:

  • Redesigned model meets your class's wall thickness, overhang, and base-diameter standards.
  • Model is printed (or slicer-verified as printable within the lesson's constraints).
  • Product photograph meets the same aesthetic standard as your e-Store audit findings.
  • Enterprise, Innovation & Automation discussion answers are recorded in your notebook.
  • The finished product is listed in your e-Store and functions correctly alongside your existing catalogue.
