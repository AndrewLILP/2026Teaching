e-Store: Design

Before a single line of HTML exists, your Nintendo-branded e-Store needs to earn its UX. This lesson has you audit real e-commerce sites against a set of UX heuristics, then translate what you find into low-fidelity wireframes and a click-through Figma prototype — validated by someone who has never seen it before. Sprint 2's build phase assumes this design work is done, not improvised on the fly.

Researching & Planning

Your Brief

Studio context: "Every good e-commerce experience looks obvious in hindsight and took real design work to get there. Before you build, I want evidence you've studied what actually works — audit real stores, then wireframe and test your own layout on a real person. Nobody builds a checkout flow from a blank page and hopes for the best."

This lesson produces the design artifacts Sprint 2 (e-Store: Build) will implement directly. Treat your wireframes and prototype as the specification you're handing to your future self — vague or missing detail here becomes a guessing game later.

Researching & Planning

Audit Three Stores

In your own browser, open three real e-commerce sites — the Nintendo eShop, plus two others of your choice (a general retailer and a different games storefront work well). For each heuristic, find one concrete example — good or bad — from each site. Record your findings in a table like the one below in your notebook.

HeuristicWhat to Look ForYour Example
Visibility of System Status Does the site confirm actions clearly? Is the cart count always visible? Record here
Consistency & Standards Do product cards, buttons, and navigation stay visually consistent across pages? Record here
Recognition Rather Than Recall Are filters, search, and cart always visible, or does the user need to remember where things are? Record here
Error Prevention Does checkout guard against missing details? Can a cart item be removed by accident easily? Record here
Aesthetic & Minimalist Design Does the product page focus attention on what matters, or is it cluttered with competing information? Record here
Bad examples count too

A frustrating checkout flow or a hidden cart icon is just as useful a finding as a good one — you're building a list of things to imitate and a list of things to deliberately avoid in your own e-Store.

Designing

Wireframe in Figma

Build low-fidelity wireframes for four key screens — greyscale boxes and labels only, no colours, fonts, or final artwork yet. Wireframes are cheap to change; a finished-looking design tempts everyone (including you) to stop questioning the layout.

Home / Browse

  • Navigation and search
  • Product grid or list
  • Cart icon with item count

Product Detail

  • Product image placeholder
  • Title, price, description
  • "Add to Cart" action

Cart

  • List of added items
  • Quantity adjustment and remove
  • Running total and checkout action

Checkout Confirmation

  • Order summary
  • Clear confirmation message
  • Way to return to browsing

Tiered Wireframe Task

Foundation · Grades D–E

All four screens, basic layout

Wireframe all four screens with clearly labelled sections. Each screen matches at least one heuristic from your audit.

Core · Grade C

Consistent system, annotated

All four screens use a consistent layout system (spacing, navigation position). Annotate at least two design decisions with the heuristic they address.

Extension · Grades A–B

Edge cases considered

Wireframes also cover at least one edge case — an empty cart, an out-of-stock product, or a validation error at checkout — each handled deliberately, not left undesigned.

Testing & Evaluating

Prototype & Walkthrough

Use Figma's Prototype mode to link your four screens into a click-through flow: Home → Product Detail → Cart → Checkout Confirmation. Then hand it to a peer who hasn't seen it, and ask them to complete one task — "buy this specific item" — while you watch silently.

Why silently?

If you explain your design as they use it, you're testing your explanation, not your design. Watching without commentary reveals exactly where a real user would hesitate, misclick, or get lost — the things you can't see yourself, because you already know how your own design is supposed to work.

Record at least two issues your peer encountered and the specific wireframe change you'd make in response. If they completed the task with zero hesitation, that's a genuine result too — note what specifically worked.

Design Checkpoint

Before Sprint 2 (e-Store: Build) begins, all of the following must exist:

  • A completed heuristic audit table covering all five heuristics across three real stores.
  • Low-fidelity wireframes for all four key screens.
  • A linked Figma click-through prototype connecting all four screens.
  • Written notes from a peer walkthrough, including at least two specific findings and your planned response to each.

e-Store: Design — Notebook Entry

This lesson crosses three D&P phases — Researching & Planning, Designing, and Testing & Evaluating. Your notebook entry needs evidence of all three.

Your notebook entry must include:

  1. Audit table: Your completed heuristic audit across all three stores.
  2. Wireframe summary: One sentence per screen — what layout decision you made and which heuristic it addresses.
  3. Walkthrough findings: At least two specific issues your peer encountered, and the exact change you're making to each wireframe in response.
  4. Design reflection: What surprised you most about watching someone else use your design? Was it something you'd never have noticed on your own?

Your full audit table and Figma link belong in your project notebook (physical or digital) before Sprint 2 begins. Building without a tested wireframe means guessing at layout while also writing code — don't stack those two problems together.
