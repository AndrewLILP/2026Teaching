Vehicle Physics & Track Collision

Your GDD pseudocode from Lessons 1–2 described how your kart should accelerate and steer. This sprint makes that real: force-based acceleration, speed-gated steering, a simplified drift mechanic, and a track whose walls scrape or bounce a kart rather than stopping it dead. Nothing here is decorative yet — this is the physical feel everything else in Kart World Racing depends on.

Producing & Implementing

Sprint Steps

Work through these in order. Each step builds on the last.

1 Read Input & Apply Acceleration Produce

Add a Rigidbody to your kart. In FixedUpdate(), read forward/reverse input and apply it as a force along transform.forward — the direction the kart is currently facing, not a fixed world direction.

  • Store accelForce as a serialized field so you can tune how punchy the acceleration feels without editing code.
  • Test reverse separately from forward — a negative input value should push the kart backward along the same forward axis, not spin it around.
2 Speed-Gated Steering Produce

Read left/right input and rotate the kart around its Y-axis — but only when the kart is actually moving. Predict your pseudocode first, then compare against the annotated example below.

Pseudocode 
BEGIN FixedUpdate()
  forwardInput = ReadForwardInput()
  turnInput = ReadTurnInput()
  rb.AddForce(transform.forward * forwardInput * accelForce)
  IF rb.velocity.magnitude > turnThreshold THEN
    Rotate(0, turnInput * turnSpeed, 0)
  END IF
END
C# 
private void FixedUpdate()
{
    float forwardInput = Input.GetAxis("Vertical");
    float turnInput    = Input.GetAxis("Horizontal");

    rb.AddForce(transform.forward * forwardInput * accelForce);

    if (rb.velocity.magnitude > turnThreshold)
    {
        transform.Rotate(0f, turnInput * turnSpeed * Time.fixedDeltaTime, 0f);
    }
}
Why gate steering behind speed?

A stationary kart can't turn its wheels against no motion the way a moving one can. Without the turnThreshold check, a stopped kart would spin in place like a top — technically working code, but physically unconvincing. This is a case where the "correct" algorithm and the "convincing" algorithm aren't automatically the same thing.

3 Traction & Drift Produce

Real tyres resist sliding sideways — without simulating that, your kart will slip like it's on ice. Convert the Rigidbody's velocity into the kart's own local space, reduce the sideways component to simulate grip, then convert back. Holding a drift button reduces the grip on purpose, letting the kart slide.

Pseudocode 
BEGIN ApplyTraction()
  localVelocity = ToLocalSpace(rb.velocity)
  IF DriftHeld() THEN
    localVelocity.sideways = localVelocity.sideways * driftGrip
  ELSE
    localVelocity.sideways = localVelocity.sideways * normalGrip
  END IF
  rb.velocity = ToWorldSpace(localVelocity)
END
C# 
private void ApplyTraction()
{
    Vector3 localVel = transform.InverseTransformDirection(rb.velocity);
    float grip = Input.GetButton("Drift") ? driftGrip : normalGrip;
    localVel.x *= grip;
    rb.velocity = transform.TransformDirection(localVel);
}

InverseTransformDirection asks "from the kart's own point of view, which way is this vector pointing?" — its local x is sideways, regardless of which way the kart is facing in the world. That's what makes reducing only the sideways component possible; doing this directly in world-space velocity wouldn't know which direction counts as "sideways" for a rotated kart.

4 Track Boundary Collision Produce

Build a simple track from wall colliders (not triggers — the kart needs to physically stop against them). Create a Physics Material with low friction and moderate bounciness, and apply it to both the kart's Collider and the wall Colliders, so a hit scrapes and deflects the kart rather than freezing it in place.

  • Test at both low and high speed — a Physics Material tuned for a gentle bump can behave very differently at race speed.

Sprint Checkpoint

Before you move to Sprint 2, all of the following must be true:

  • Kart accelerates forward and reverses smoothly using force, not direct position changes.
  • Steering only takes effect once the kart is moving above turnThreshold.
  • Holding the drift button visibly reduces sideways grip, and releasing it restores normal handling.
  • Track walls use a Physics Material — collisions deflect or scrape rather than stopping the kart dead.
  • High-speed wall hits don't launch the kart into the air or clip through geometry.
  • All five test cases above have recorded actual results.

Sprint 1 — Notebook Entry

Sprint 1 crosses two D&P phases — Producing & Implementing and Testing & Evaluating. Your notebook entry needs evidence of both.

Your sprint notebook entry must include:

  1. Sprint log: For each of the four steps, one sentence — what you implemented and whether your pseudocode prediction matched the real behaviour.
  2. Test table: The completed test table from the Testing section, with your actual results recorded.
  3. Bug report: Describe at least one thing that didn't work first try — especially around traction or wall collisions. What was wrong, and what did you change?
  4. Design reflection: Drive a few laps of your test track. Does the handling feel like a kart, or does it feel too slippery, too stiff, or too floaty? What's one tuning change (accelForce, turnThreshold, driftGrip) you'd try next?

Your completed test table must be in your project notebook (physical or digital) before you move to Sprint 2. Untested handling doesn't go to production.
