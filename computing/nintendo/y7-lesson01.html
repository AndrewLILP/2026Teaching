Nintendo Timeline

Nintendo has been reinventing itself for over 130 years. Each era introduced a new computing technology or design idea that changed what games could be. Key milestones are marked ★.

●
1889
🃏

Playing Cards

Fusajiro Yamauchi founds Nintendo. Handcrafted Hanafuda cards — zero technology.

●
1977
📺

Color TV-Game

First hardware — games hardwired in circuits, not software cartridges.

★
1981
🦍

Donkey Kong

"Jumpman" debuts — Mario's first appearance. Miyamoto designs levels as obstacle courses.

●
1983
🎮

Famicom / NES

Programmable cartridges — games become software products for the first time.

★
1985
🍄

Super Mario Bros

World 1-1 becomes the most analysed level in game history — an invisible tutorial through play.

●
1989
🟩

Game Boy

Monochrome screen sacrificed colour for 30+ hours of battery. Constraint became innovation.

★
1996
🌟

Super Mario 64

3D movement invented from scratch. No tutorial — the castle courtyard is a learning playground.

●
2006
🕹️

Wii

Motion controls redefine input. Nintendo chases a new audience, not better graphics.

●
2017
🔄

Nintendo Switch

Home console and handheld in one. The hybrid form factor becomes their best-selling hardware ever.

Identifying & Defining

Enterprise & Innovation in Computing

Two ideas run through all of Computing Technology: enterprise and innovation. Nintendo's history is one of the clearest real-world examples of both.

🏢 Enterprise

A business or organisation that takes initiative to create value — often by identifying a problem in the market and solving it with technology.

Nintendo example: Nintendo pivoted from playing cards → toys → electronic games → hybrid consoles. Each pivot was an enterprise decision driven by a changing market.

💡 Innovation

A new or improved idea, method, or product that creates value. Innovation often means a clever solution within constraints — not just the most powerful option.

Nintendo example: The Game Boy used a monochrome screen. Battery life beat colour. The constraint became the innovation.

Automation

Automation means using technology to complete tasks without continuous human input. In game development, automation appears at every level:

  • Build pipelines — automated scripts compile, test, and package a game for release
  • Level generation — algorithms place tiles and obstacles without a designer touching each one
  • Enemy AI — the Goomba's patrol pattern is a simple automated decision: move left, hit wall, reverse
  • Physics engines — gravity and collision detection are automated calculations running every frame

Super Mario Bros World 1-1 (1985) teaches every mechanic without a single word of instruction. Before you design your own level, you need to explain how it does this.
