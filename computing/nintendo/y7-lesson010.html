How Mario Began

Before you build a game, you need to understand what made Nintendo's games great. In this lesson you will trace Nintendo's journey from a playing-card company to the world's most influential game studio — and begin analysing the design decisions that made Mario a global icon.

Your Brief

Studio context: You've just joined Nintendo AU as a junior developer. Before you write a single line of code, your studio lead wants to know that you understand where Nintendo came from — and why that history matters for the games you'll build.

By the end of this lesson you will be able to explain Nintendo's key historical milestones, define enterprise, innovation, and automation in a computing context, and identify the design decisions that made Super Mario Bros World 1-1 the most studied game level ever created.

D&P Phase — Identifying & Defining: This lesson sits in the first phase of the Design and Production Framework. You are building the context and knowledge you need before you can design or build anything. Good developers research before they code.

Enterprise & Innovation in Computing

Two ideas come up constantly in Computing Technology: enterprise and innovation. Nintendo's history is one of the clearest real-world examples of both — and understanding them now will help you explain design decisions in your own projects.

🏢 Enterprise

A business or organisation that takes initiative to create value — often by identifying a problem in the market and solving it with technology.

Nintendo example: Nintendo pivoted from playing cards → toys → electronic games → hybrid consoles. Each pivot was an enterprise decision driven by a changing market.

💡 Innovation

A new or improved idea, method, or product that creates value. In computing, innovation often means finding a clever solution within constraints — not just the most powerful option.

Nintendo example: The Game Boy used a monochrome screen — inferior to competitors. But it lasted 30+ hours on batteries. The constraint became the innovation.

Automation

Automation means using technology to complete tasks without continuous human input. In game development, automation appears at every level:

  • Build pipelines — automated scripts compile, test, and package a game for release
  • Level generation — algorithms create level layouts without a designer placing each tile by hand
  • Enemy AI — the Goomba's patrol pattern is a simple automated decision system: move left, detect wall or edge, reverse direction
  • Physics engines — gravity and collision detection are automated calculations that run every frame

Think: When Nintendo released the Wii with motion controls in 2006, was that enterprise, innovation, automation — or all three? Choose a position and write one paragraph justifying your answer in your project notebook.

Analyse a Classic — World 1-1

Super Mario Bros World 1-1 (1985) is considered the most perfectly designed game level ever made. It teaches every mechanic the player needs — without a single word of instructions. Before you design your own level, you need to be able to explain how it does this.

The Analysis Framework

Use the table below as a guide. The third column is where your own observations go — record these in your project notebook as you watch a playthrough or play the level yourself.

Design element What World 1-1 does Your observation / notes
First screen Mario appears on the left edge. Open space stretches to the right — the player moves right instinctively. No arrow, no instruction, just open space. Record your observations in your project notebook →
First obstacle A Goomba walks in from the right — but only after a short delay, giving the player time to experiment with controls first. Dying on the very first enemy would feel unfair. Record your observations in your project notebook →
First reward The ? block floats above and slightly ahead — players who jump discover coins and a power-up. Curiosity is the mechanic, not instruction. Record your observations in your project notebook →
Player feedback Sound effect + visual change (Mario grows) when hitting a block. The game immediately confirms the player's action. No action goes unacknowledged. Record your observations in your project notebook →
Fail condition Touch a Goomba → shrink (if Super Mario) or lose a life. The cost of failure is proportionate and consistent — it never feels arbitrary. Record your observations in your project notebook →
Win condition Reach the flagpole on the right. The pole is visible from a distance. The player always knows the goal — no confusion about what they're working toward. Record your observations in your project notebook →

Vocabulary

Tap each card to reveal the definition. You will use these terms throughout the unit.

Enterprise

A business or organisation that takes initiative to create value, often by identifying and solving a problem in the market using technology.

Innovation

A new or improved idea, method, or product that creates value. In computing, innovation often means a clever solution within constraints — not just the most powerful option.

Automation

Using technology to complete tasks without continuous human input. In games: AI behaviour, build pipelines, physics engines, and procedural generation are all automation.

Game mechanic

A rule or system that defines how a player interacts with a game. Jumping, collecting, building, and shooting are all mechanics. Good mechanics are learnable and consistent.

Player feedback

The signals a game sends to confirm a player's actions — sound effects, score changes, animations, and screen shake are all feedback. No action should go unacknowledged.

Iterative design

Repeating a design-test-improve cycle, with each loop producing a better version. Nintendo's level designers played World 1-1 thousands of times before release.

D&P Framework

The four-phase Design and Production process: Identifying & Defining → Researching & Planning → Producing & Implementing → Testing & Evaluating.

Computing domain

One of four areas of knowledge in CT: Technical Knowledge & Skills, Social Awareness, Project Management, and Thinking Skills. All four appear in every unit.
