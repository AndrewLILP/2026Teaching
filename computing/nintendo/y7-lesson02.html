Researching & Planning

What is Prototyping?

A prototype is an early, rough version of a product used to test ideas before committing to a final design. In game development, prototyping almost always happens on paper first — it's faster, cheaper, and easier to change than code.

Why paper first?

Nintendo's game designers famously sketch levels and mechanics on paper before any code is written. Shigeru Miyamoto (creator of Mario) still draws his level ideas by hand. A paper prototype lets you test whether your idea is fun before spending hours programming it.

Paper prototyping lives in the Researching & Planning phase of the D&P Framework — but it immediately feeds into Producing & Implementing. Your pseudocode (written in this lesson) becomes the direct blueprint for your MakeCode Arcade code in Lessons 3–6.

Researching & Planning

Game Design Elements

Every platformer — from World 1-1 to your own game — is built from the same core design elements. You need to define all six for your prototype before you start building.

🎮
Mechanics
The rules of interaction — what can the player do? Jump, run, collect, shoot? Each mechanic must be learnable.
📋
Rules
The constraints on the player — what are they not allowed to do? What happens if they break a rule?
📢
Player Feedback
How does the game confirm actions? Sound, animation, score change, screen flash — every action needs a response.
💀
Fail Condition
When and how does the player lose? The cost of failure must be clear, consistent, and proportionate.
🏆
Win Condition
How does the player win the level? The goal should always be visible or clearly communicated.
🗺️
Level Structure
Where do obstacles, rewards, and the goal appear? Level layout teaches mechanics without words.

Researching & Planning

Prototype Activity

Use the prompts below to plan your platformer. Complete this on paper in your project notebook — draw your level layout, then fill in each design element. Rough sketches are fine. The goal is clarity of ideas, not artistic quality.

Your Platformer — Design Plan

Game Title & Theme

What is your game called? What is the world or setting? (e.g. jungle, space, underwater, city). Record in your notebook.

Player Character

Who or what does the player control? Sketch a rough design. What makes your character unique? Record in your notebook.

Mechanics (at least 2)

List your player's actions: e.g. run left/right, jump, collect items, avoid enemies. Record in your notebook.

Rules & Constraints

What limits the player? e.g. gravity, screen boundaries, lives/health, time limit. Record in your notebook.

Player Feedback

How will the game respond to the player? List at least two: score counter, sound effect, animation, health bar. Record in your notebook.

Fail & Win Conditions

When does the player lose (fail condition)? When do they complete the level (win condition)? Record in your notebook.

Level Sketch

Draw a rough bird's-eye or side-view sketch of your level. Mark where the player starts, where obstacles appear, where rewards are, and where the goal is. Record in your notebook.

✎ Tip: compare your prototype with a partner. Can they understand your game just from reading your design plan? If not, clarify before moving to pseudocode.

Producing & Implementing

Pseudocode — Planning Before Code

Pseudocode is a way of writing the logic of a program in plain English, using a structured set of keywords. In NSW Computing Technology, we use specific keywords so your pseudocode is consistent and unambiguous — your teacher and classmates can read it even if they use a different programming language.

NSW Structured English Keywords

Core Pseudocode Keywords

BEGIN / END
Mark the start and finish of a program or procedure.
INPUT / OUTPUT
Receive data from the user or display data to the screen.
IF / THEN / ELSE / ENDIF
Make a decision based on a condition — run one block of code or another.
WHILE / ENDWHILE
Repeat a block of code while a condition is TRUE.
FOR / NEXT
Repeat a block of code a set number of times.
SET / ←
Assign a value to a variable. e.g. SET score ← 0

Pseudocode for Player Movement

Here is how the core movement loop of a platformer looks in NSW structured English. This is what you will translate into MakeCode Arcade blocks in Lesson 3.

Basic Movement BEGIN movePlayer WHILE game is running IF LEFT key pressed THEN player.x ← player.x - speed ENDIF IF RIGHT key pressed THEN player.x ← player.x + speed ENDIF IF UP key pressed THEN player.velocityY ← -jumpForce ENDIF ENDWHILE END movePlayer
Gravity & Collision BEGIN applyPhysics WHILE game is running // Apply gravity every frame player.velocityY ← player.velocityY + gravity // Move player vertically player.y ← player.y + player.velocityY IF player overlaps ground THEN player.y ← groundLevel player.velocityY ← 0 ENDIF ENDWHILE END applyPhysics

Pseudocode Task

Write Your Own Movement Pseudocode

Using the examples above as a guide, write pseudocode for your game's player movement in your project notebook. Include:

  1. Left and right movement
  2. A jump mechanic
  3. What happens when the player hits an obstacle (your fail condition)
  4. What happens when the player reaches the goal (your win condition)

Use NSW keywords: BEGIN, END, WHILE, ENDWHILE, IF, THEN, ENDIF, SET, ←

Pseudocode vs Code Pseudocode is not run by a computer — it's written for humans. The goal is to think through your logic clearly before you have to worry about exact syntax. In Lesson 3 you will translate your pseudocode directly into MakeCode Arcade blocks.

Notebook Prompt

Your notebook entry for this lesson should include your completed paper prototype and your pseudocode. Write your reflection below — it saves automatically.

Respond to at least one prompt. Aim for a full paragraph each:

  1. Describe one design decision you made in your paper prototype. Why did you make that choice? What were you trying to achieve for the player?
