Identifying & Defining

The Bridge Moment

Here is the most important thing you'll learn in this lesson: every block in MakeCode Arcade is JavaScript. The block view and the JavaScript view are two ways of looking at exactly the same code. You've been writing JavaScript since Lesson 3 — you just had a visual interface generating it.

⚡ The Bridge

Switch to JavaScript View Right Now

Open your MakeCode Arcade project. Click the JavaScript toggle at the top of the editor. Look at what's there.

Everything you built across Sprints 1, 2, and 3 is visible as JavaScript code. Every block became a function call, a variable declaration, or an event handler. You didn't write this code by hand — but you made every decision that produced it: the sprite kinds, the speed values, the overlap events, the game.over() calls.

Now you're going to write the next lines directly — without using a block — and they'll become part of that same program.

The difference between "blocks programmer" and "JavaScript programmer" is smaller than it looks. The concepts — variables, functions, events, conditions, loops — are identical. The syntax is what changes. In Lessons 5 and 6 of the MakeCode Arcade sequence, you were already reading JavaScript. In Sprint 4, you write it.

Your Full Game as JavaScript

Here is what four sprints of block-based development produce in JavaScript. Your actual code will differ in the images, positions, and values you chose — but the structure will match this. Find each section in your own JS view and annotate your notebook copy with what each line does.

// ── SPRINT 4 ADDITIONS ★ ─────────────────────
game.splash("YOUR GAME TITLE", "by Your Name")  // ★ title screen

enemy.x = 120

Producing & Implementing

Sprint 4 — Four Steps

These steps are completed in JavaScript view — not the blocks view. Switch to JS before starting Step 1 and stay there. You can switch back to blocks view to verify your code is correct, but write the new lines directly in JS.

1
Write Your First JavaScript Line
Your First JS

In JavaScript view, scroll to the very top of your code. Click to position your cursor before the first line. Type this — replacing the text with your actual game name and your name:

What you type 
game.splash("GAME TITLE", "by Your Name")
What it does 
// Pauses the game before
// the loop starts and shows
// a two-line splash screen.
// Press A to dismiss it.
// Runs once, at the very start.

Switch back to Blocks view. You should see a new block has appeared — game splash [GAME TITLE] [by Your Name]. MakeCode went the other direction: your JavaScript became a block. This works both ways.

Play your game. The splash screen should appear before the player can move. Press A to dismiss it and start playing.

★ This is the moment

You just wrote JavaScript. Not a block, not a drag-and-drop — a typed line of code. The same skill that produces this splash screen is used to write every JavaScript program on every website, game, and app in the world. The syntax gets more complex; the concept doesn't change.

2
Add Background Music
Audio

In JavaScript view, on the line after your game.splash() line, add:

What you type 
music.play(
  music.melodyPlayable(
    music.powerUp
  ),
  music.PlaybackMode.InBackground
)
What it does 
// Plays the "powerUp" melody
// in the background while
// the game runs.
//
// Try other melodies:
// music.melodyPlayable(
//   music.jumpUp
//   music.mystery
//   music.dadadadum
// )

Try at least two different melody options from the comments. Choose the one that best fits your game's theme — a tense level deserves different music than a cheerful one. Record your choice and reason in your project notebook.

The key argument is InBackground — this means the music plays continuously without pausing the game loop. UntilDone (used for collection sounds) pauses execution until the sound finishes.

3
Modify an Existing Value Directly
Iteration

Find your Sprint 3 enemy patrol code in JavaScript view. The line enemy.vx = 60 sets the patrol speed. Based on your Sprint 3 test results — was the enemy too fast, too slow, or just right?

Edit the number directly in JavaScript view. Don't use a block — click the number and change it. This is the kind of rapid iteration that makes JavaScript view more efficient than block view for fine-tuning values.

Before (your Sprint 3 value) 
enemy.vx = 60  // original
After (your adjusted value) 
enemy.vx = ??  // your decision

Do the same for any other values that felt wrong during testing — jump height (player.vy = -150), gravity (scene.setGravity(500)), or movement speed (controller.moveSprite(player, 100, 0)). Each adjustment is an iteration — a deliberate design decision backed by evidence from testing.

Record every change in your sprint log: what value you changed, what it was before, what it is now, and why.

4
Polish Your Level Design
Design

Open your tilemap editor. Based on your Sprint 3 peer feedback and your own playtest notes, make at least two deliberate improvements to the level layout. This is not random change — it's design iteration informed by evaluation.

Consider these questions, and record your answers in your notebook before making changes:

  • Is the difficulty curve appropriate? Does it start accessible and get harder, or does it feel uneven?
  • Are your collectibles placed where they reward exploration — or are they too easy to ignore?
  • Does the player encounter the enemy at a moment of tension — or does the patrol feel random?
  • Can the player see the goal from a distance — or is it hidden? Should it be?
  • Is the level too short (players win without challenge) or too long (players give up)?
Design iteration vs random change Changing your level because "it didn't feel right" is not enough — you need to be able to articulate what felt wrong and why the specific change you made should fix it. That reasoning goes in your notebook. "I moved the first enemy further from the start because players were losing a life before they'd had a chance to learn the controls" is a design decision. "I changed things a bit" is not.

Testing & Evaluating

Peer Playtest — Structured Feedback

Before your game is submitted, it must be playtested by someone who didn't build it. A player who has never seen your game will find problems you can't see anymore — you know your level too well.

For the Player (tester):

  1. Play the game through once without any instructions from the developer. Notice when you feel confused.
  2. Try to win and try to lose. Did both outcomes trigger correctly?
  3. After playing, answer these in writing: What was the most fun moment? What was the most frustrating moment? Was anything unclear about how to play?

For the Developer (you):

  1. Do not explain anything while the tester plays. Watch what they do. Note every moment they pause, miss, or look confused.
  2. After the playtest, read their written feedback. Identify one specific, actionable change you could make based on it.
  3. Record the feedback and your response in your project notebook. If you disagree with the feedback, explain why — that's a valid design decision too.
