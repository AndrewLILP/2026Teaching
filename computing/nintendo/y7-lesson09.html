TinkerCAD Amiibo Design

Your game has a character — now give them a physical form. In this lesson you'll study what makes Nintendo's Amiibo figurines distinctive, design your own character on paper, then start building a 3D model in TinkerCAD. In Lesson 10 you'll prepare it for 3D printing. In World 3 (Year 10), it ships in your e-store.

Researching & Planning

Your Brief

Studio context: The hardware and physical product team has one requirement: design an Amiibo-style figurine of your game's character. It must be original — not a copy of an existing Nintendo character. It must be 3D-printable without support structures where possible. And it must be recognisable as your character from a metre away.

Amiibo figurines are physical products — they have to survive being picked up, knocked over, and handled by players. That constraint shapes every design decision. Before you open TinkerCAD, you'll analyse existing Amiibo to understand how their designers solved this problem — then you'll sketch your own solution on paper.

Paper before software — always Experienced 3D designers sketch on paper first, even when they've used 3D software for years. A paper sketch takes two minutes to produce and two seconds to throw away. A TinkerCAD model takes much longer. Working out your ideas on paper means you don't waste that modelling time on ideas that don't work.
Identifying & Defining

Analyse Amiibo Design Language

Nintendo's Amiibo range has a consistent design language — a set of visual conventions that make any Amiibo immediately recognisable as part of the product family, even without branding. Before designing your own, you need to understand those conventions.

Analysis Task

Deconstruct Three Amiibo

Search "Nintendo Amiibo figures" on the Nintendo website or an approved image search. Choose three different Amiibo from different game series (not all from the same franchise). For each one, record the following in your project notebook:

  1. Which game character is it? What is their distinctive visual feature (the thing that makes them immediately recognisable)?
  2. What pose does the figure use — standing, action, dynamic, neutral? How does the pose relate to the character's personality or role?
  3. Where is the figure's centre of gravity? How does the base prevent it from tipping over?
  4. How much detail is included — is the figure realistic, stylised, or simplified? What did the designer leave out?

Then use your observations to complete this analysis table in your notebook:

Three-View Character Sheet

Professional character designers draw their character from three angles before building anything in 3D: front, side, and top. This three-view (or orthographic) drawing is the definitive reference that guides the whole modelling process.

Draw all three views in your project notebook — not on this page. Each view should be at least 6cm tall. Include:

  • The round base (draw it in all three views)
  • The character's body, head, and any distinctive features
  • Proportions — use a ruler to keep the views consistent with each other
  • Labels for any unusual features or design decisions

Your three-view drawing does not need to be beautiful — it needs to be clear enough that another person could build the model from it.

Design Constraints

Your Figurine Must Meet These Requirements

  • Height: 60–80mm total (base + figure) — scaled for the Bambu P2S build plate
  • Base: flat cylinder, minimum 30mm diameter, minimum 3mm thick
  • Wall thickness: no geometry thinner than 2mm anywhere
  • Overhang: avoid horizontal extensions over 10mm without support from below
  • Connection: the figure must connect to the base with a contact area wider than 10mm

Check your character sheet against these requirements before opening TinkerCAD. If something in your design violates a constraint, fix it on paper — not in the 3D model.

Producing & Implementing

TinkerCAD — The Interface

Go to tinkercad.com and sign in with your school account. Create a new design and name it [Your Name] Amiibo v1. The version number matters — you'll refine this design in World 3.

Six operations you need to know

📦
Add Shape
Drag any shape from the right-hand panel onto the workplane. Box, Cylinder, Sphere, Cone, Roof — start here.
↔️
Resize
Click a shape, then drag the white handles. Hold Shift to scale proportionally. Type a value for precise dimensions.
✋
Move
Click and drag on the workplane to move in X/Y. Use the black cone handle to move in Z (up/down). Arrow keys also work.
🔄
Rotate
Click the curved arrows around a shape to rotate it. Hold Shift for 45° increments. Type exact angles for precision.
🔗
Group
Select multiple shapes (Ctrl+A or drag to select), then press Ctrl+G. Groups behave as one object — move and resize together.
⭕
Hole / Cut
Set a shape to "Hole" in its properties, overlap it with a solid shape, then Group. The hole shape is subtracted — creating cuts, tunnels, or recesses.

Keyboard shortcuts that save time

Ctrl+Z — undo (use freely) · Ctrl+D — duplicate · Ctrl+G — group · Ctrl+Shift+G — ungroup · F — fit view to selected · R — toggle ruler · Home — reset camera view

Producing & Implementing

Modelling Steps — Build in Order

Always build from the base up. The base is your foundation — everything else connects to it. If you build details before the base is correct, you'll spend more time repositioning than designing.

Keep your three-view character sheet visible while you work. Every decision should refer back to it.

1
The Base
Foundation

Add a Cylinder from the shapes panel. Resize it to your chosen base diameter (minimum 30mm) and a height of 4mm. This is the flat platform the character stands on.

  • Set diameter: click the shape, type your dimension in the white width/depth boxes
  • Set height to 4mm — type it directly in the height box
  • Make sure the base is sitting on the workplane (Z position = 0)

Your base should look exactly like the base view on your character sheet. If it doesn't, adjust now.

Why a flat base? FDM (fused deposition modelling) 3D printers build objects layer by layer from the bottom up. The very first layer must be flat against the print bed — any curved or angled base will warp, fail to adhere, or print with gaps. The flat cylinder base ensures a perfect first layer every time.
2
The Body
Main Form

Add your character's main body shape. This is usually a combination of a Box, Cylinder, or Sphere — simplified from the real character. Refer to your front-view sketch for proportions.

  • Position the body so its bottom surface sits on top of the base (Z = 4mm if your base is 4mm tall)
  • Use the Z-axis handle (black cone) to lift shapes precisely
  • The body width should be narrower than the base diameter
  • For a humanoid character: a slightly tapered cylinder or rounded box works well for a torso

Keep the body simple at this stage — you'll add details after the basic structure is right. A rough block that captures the overall height and width is better than a detailed shape in the wrong position.

3
The Head
Character Feature

Add the head — typically a Sphere or rounded Box, positioned above the body. This is where your character's most recognisable feature usually lives, so proportions matter here more than anywhere else.

  • Amiibo heads are often slightly larger than "realistic" — this makes them more readable and easier to print
  • Position using the Z handle: head bottom = body top height
  • If your character has a hat, helmet, or distinctive headpiece — add it as a separate shape now and position it on top of the head
  • Use Duplicate (Ctrl+D) if you need a mirrored element — e.g. ears on both sides

Save your work — TinkerCAD autosaves, but also press Ctrl+S manually. Name the save [Your Name] Amiibo v1 — Base+Body+Head.

4
One Distinctive Feature
Character Identity

Add one feature that makes your character immediately recognisable. This could be a weapon, accessory, clothing element, or physical trait — whatever makes someone look at the figure and say "that's [character name]."

From your Amiibo analysis: what was each figure's most distinctive feature? Apply the same thinking to your character.

  • Keep it bold and simple — fine details don't survive 3D printing at this scale
  • Any feature extending horizontally from the body should be connected at the widest possible point
  • Avoid features that are thinner than 2mm in any dimension

If this lesson is ending, stop here and save as v1 — in progress. You'll complete the model and prepare it for printing in Lesson 10.

Notebook Prompt

Your notebook entry for Lesson 9 is your evidence of the Researching & Planning and Producing & Implementing phases for the physical product. Respond to all four prompts:

  1. Amiibo analysis summary: From the three Amiibo you analysed, what is the single most important design convention you'll apply to your own figure? Why did you choose that one over the others?
  2. Constraint check: Review your character sheet against the five design constraints (height, base, wall thickness, overhang, connection). Did any constraint require you to change your original design? Describe what you changed and why the constraint exists.
  3. Modelling log: Describe each shape you've added to your TinkerCAD model so far — what shape type, approximate dimensions, and what part of your character it represents.
  4. Design decision: What is your character's most distinctive feature — the one thing that makes them immediately recognisable? How did you represent it in 3D? Would a player of your MakeCode Arcade game recognise this figure as the player character?
