3D Print Preparation

Your TinkerCAD model needs one more thing before it can be printed: it needs to pass the print-readiness checklist. In this lesson you'll analyse common 3D printing failures, apply printability rules to your model, finalise the design, and export it as an STL file ready for slicing. Then World 1 is complete.

Producing & Implementing

Your Brief

Studio context: The physical product team needs the STL file by end of lesson — that's the universal 3D printing format. Before you export, your model must pass a printability check. A model that fails on the print bed wastes filament, time, and money. The team needs to know it will print first time.

This lesson has two tracks running in parallel. The analysis track teaches you why prints fail and how to prevent it. The production track is you in TinkerCAD, applying what you learn as you go. Don't read everything first and then open TinkerCAD — read each section, apply it to your model, then move to the next.

Identifying & Defining

Analyse First — What Goes Wrong and Why

Understanding how prints fail helps you design models that succeed. These are the four most common FDM print failures and their design causes.

🫠
Warping
Cause: base lifts off the print bed during printing
Fix: ensure base is flat and wide enough. Do not design concave or angled bottoms.
🌉
Overhang collapse
Cause: geometry extends outward at too shallow an angle, leaving layers unsupported in mid-air
Fix: keep overhangs under 45° from vertical, or add support material in the slicer.
💀
Thin wall failure
Cause: a wall or feature is thinner than the printer's nozzle width — it either collapses or doesn't print at all
Fix: ensure no geometry is thinner than 1.5mm (2mm recommended for figurines).
🔻
Unsupported spire
Cause: a tall, thin vertical feature (sword, antenna, spike) has insufficient base to prevent vibration-induced failure
Fix: widen the base of any tall thin feature, or angle it against another surface.

Analysis Task

Check Your Own Model for Risks

Open your TinkerCAD model from Lesson 9. In your notebook, answer:

  1. Does your model have any geometry extending horizontally from the body (arms, weapons, accessories)? Measure the overhang angle — is it steeper than 45° from vertical?
  2. Are there any features thinner than 2mm? Use the ruler tool (R) in TinkerCAD to measure.
  3. Does your character have any tall, thin elements (antennae, spikes, hair spires)? How wide is the base of each one?
  4. Is your base completely flat on the bottom? Rotate the view to check there's nothing protruding below the workplane.
Producing & Implementing

Printability Rules — Apply These Now

The 45° Overhang Rule

FDM printers can print overhanging geometry if the angle from vertical is 45° or steeper — each layer has enough support from the layer below. Beyond 45°, layers have insufficient support and begin to sag or fail.

Overhang Angle — 45° Rule

Minimum Wall Thickness

The Bambu P2S uses a 0.4mm nozzle by default. Any wall thinner than the nozzle simply won't print — the slicer ignores it. For structural integrity, the practical minimum is 1.5mm, and 2mm is recommended for figurines that will be handled.

Use TinkerCAD's ruler tool (press R) to measure any feature you're unsure about. Click two points on your model — TinkerCAD shows the distance in millimetres.

Applying the Rules to Your Model

1
Fix Any Overhang Issues
Redesign

For any horizontal extension that exceeds 45° from vertical, choose one:

  • Angle it — tilt the arm/accessory downward so the angle from vertical is 45° or less
  • Thicken it — make horizontal elements taller (thicker in Z) so they have more cross-section to support themselves
  • Connect it — add a support column from the arm down to the base or body (thin connecting rod, 2mm wide)
  • Remove it — if the feature isn't essential to the character's identity, simplify the design

After each change, view your model from the side. Ask: if gravity were pulling this feature straight down, would the layers below support it?

2
Check All Wall Thicknesses
Measure

Use the ruler tool (R) on every feature in your model:

  • Arms and legs — measure the thinnest cross-section
  • Fingers or claws (if any) — each must be at least 1.5mm wide
  • Accessories — weapons, hats, clothing details
  • The connection between figure and base — must be at least 10mm wide

For anything under 2mm: select the shape and scale it up in the thin dimension only. Hold Shift to scale proportionally, or drag only the relevant handle.

3
Add Final Details
Polish

With printability issues fixed, add any remaining details that distinguish your character. Keep the 2mm rule in mind for everything you add.

Consider:

  • Eyes or facial features — use a small Cylinder as a Hole to cut recesses, or a small Sphere for raised eyes
  • Clothing or armour detail — low-relief (1–2mm) raised patterns work well at this scale
  • A name or initials on the base — use the Text shape in TinkerCAD, set depth to 1mm

Producing & Implementing

Finalise & Export as STL

Group Everything

Before exporting, select all shapes in your design (Ctrl+A) and group them (Ctrl+G). This merges all geometry into a single solid object — essential for a clean STL export. A non-grouped model can export as multiple separate meshes that some slicers can't handle.

Export to STL

  1. Click Export in the top-right of TinkerCAD
  2. Select .STL (not OBJ or SVG)
  3. TinkerCAD will download a file named after your design
  4. Rename the file: [YourName]_Amiibo_v1.stl
  5. Submit the STL file to your teacher along with your three-view character sheet
What is STL? STL (Standard Tessellation Language, also called Standard Triangle Language) is the universal file format for 3D printing. It describes a 3D surface as a mesh of triangles. Every 3D printer slicer — including Bambu Studio — can open STL files. The slicer converts the STL into G-code: the specific machine instructions for the printer's nozzle and bed movements.
🔎 Evaluation — Before You Export

Rotate your model slowly in all directions in TinkerCAD. Ask yourself:

  1. Is the base flat and in contact with the workplane?
  2. Does the silhouette clearly read as my character?
  3. Are there any obviously thin or fragile features?
  4. Does the figure connect solidly to the base with no gaps?
  5. Would I recognise this as my MakeCode Arcade character?

If you answer "no" to any question — fix it before exporting. An exported and submitted STL that fails the print-readiness checklist will be returned for revision.

Testing & Evaluating

Print Readiness Checklist

This is the formal evaluation step before submission. Complete this checklist in your project notebook — tick each item only when you have confirmed it in TinkerCAD.

✓ Print Readiness — Complete in your notebook
Base is flat. The bottom surface of the cylinder base is exactly on the workplane — nothing protrudes below Z = 0.
Base diameter ≥ 30mm. Measured with the ruler tool — the base is wide enough to provide stability.
Total height 60–80mm. The complete model from base bottom to highest point is within the specified range.
No wall thinner than 1.5mm. All features measured with the ruler tool — minimum 1.5mm, recommended 2mm.
Overhangs ≤ 45° from vertical. Any horizontal extensions are angled, connected, or thickened to meet the 45° rule.
Figure-to-base connection ≥ 10mm wide. The contact area between the figure and the base is solid and wide.
All shapes grouped. Ctrl+A → Ctrl+G performed — model exports as a single solid mesh.
STL exported and renamed. File saved as [YourName]_Amiibo_v1.stl and ready to submit.
Character is recognisable. A classmate who has played your MakeCode Arcade game can identify the figure as your player character without being told.

Notebook Prompt — World 1 Reflection

This is the final notebook entry for World 1 — the entire Year 7 semester. It's also the opening entry for your World 3 (Year 10) work, because what you write here will be the baseline you compare against in three years.

Your notebook entry must include:

  1. Print readiness evaluation: For each item in the print readiness checklist, write one sentence describing what you found and what (if anything) you had to change to pass it.
  2. Design retrospective: Compare your final TinkerCAD model to your original three-view character sheet. What changed during modelling? What did the print constraints force you to simplify? What are you most satisfied with?
  3. World 1 complete — what you built: In your own words, list every product you've completed across the ten lessons — game, controller, Amiibo. For each one, write one sentence about what computing skill it required that you didn't have at the start of Year 7.
  4. Letter to Year 10 yourself: Write a short paragraph addressed to the version of you who will open this notebook in Year 10 to redesign the Amiibo and list it in the e-store. What do you want them to know about this character? What would you change about the v1 design if you could start again?
