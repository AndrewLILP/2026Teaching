Project Notebook

Your project notebook is not a polished report — it is evidence of thinking. It shows your studio lead what decisions you made, why you made them, and what you changed when things didn't work. This page is your notebook headquarters: a digital snapshot of saved reflections, a guide to what goes in the physical notebook each lesson, and the submission checklist for when it's time to hand it in.

What is the Project Notebook?

Nintendo designers keep detailed design notebooks — not because they're required to, but because working out ideas on paper is faster than working them out in code or 3D software. Your project notebook does the same job.

The notebook is evidence of the Design and Production process. It shows that you didn't just produce a product — you identified a problem, researched and planned a solution, built and improved it iteratively, and evaluated the result against your original design. That process is what Computing Technology assesses, not just the final product.

Physical vs Digital

Your project notebook has two parts:

  • Physical notebook — handwritten or printed entries, sketches, diagrams, analysis tables, sprint checklists, and test results. This is the primary evidence artefact. It must exist on paper or as a saved PDF/document.
  • Digital entries — the reflection boxes in each lesson page save automatically to this browser. They are your working notes — a starting point, not a substitute for the physical notebook. The Digital Snapshot below shows you what's been saved.
What the notebook is NOT It is not a polished essay. It is not a step-by-step recount of what you did. It does not need correct spelling and grammar to be excellent evidence. What makes a notebook excellent is specificity — recording exactly what decision you made, exactly what happened when you tested it, and exactly what you changed as a result.

What Quality Looks Like

Notebook quality isn't about length or neatness. It's about specificity and evidence of thinking across all four D&P phases. Compare these examples and locate your own work honestly.

Developing
Entries describe what was done, not why. Sparse detail. Checklist items ticked but not supported by written evidence. Reflections are one or two sentences. Test tables have expected results copied from the lesson page.
"I added an enemy. It worked. I did the sprint checkpoint."
Achieving
Entries record decisions and results. Sprint logs describe what happened at each step. Test tables have actual observed results. Reflections explain one or two key choices with reasons. Analysis tasks completed in full.
"I set enemy vx to 60 — it felt too fast so I changed it to 40. The test table shows lives decreased correctly on contact but the win condition triggered too early because I'd counted the goal as a collectible."
Extending
Entries connect decisions across lessons. The D&P framework is visible in the notebook structure — analysis before design, testing informing iteration. Bugs are documented with causes, fixes, and regression checks. Retrospectives show genuine critical reflection.
"In L2 I planned a speed of 100 (vx). By L5 playtesting showed the enemy at 60 outran the player on narrow platforms, which wasn't fair given the 45° tilt threshold set in L8. Reducing to 40 fixed the fairness issue and aligned with the controller's natural dead zone range."
