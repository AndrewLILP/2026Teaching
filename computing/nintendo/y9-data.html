Investigating & Researching

Why Data-Driven Design

Your UX audit two lessons ago was valuable, but it had one weakness: you already knew how your own game was supposed to work. A first-time player doesn't have that advantage — and their confusion is exactly the information a designer needs. This is the difference between opinion ("I think the enemy is fair") and evidence ("3 of 4 playtesters died to the enemy on their first attempt, and two said afterward they didn't see it coming").

Two kinds of playtesting data Quantitative data is numeric — a rating out of 5, a time in seconds, a number of deaths. It's easy to average and compare. Qualitative data is descriptive — what a player said, what you observed them do. It's harder to summarise numerically but often explains why the quantitative data looks the way it does. Good playtesting collects both.
Investigating & Researching

Design Your Feedback Survey

A badly worded question produces useless data, no matter how many people answer it. Compare these two ways of asking about the same thing:

Weak Question

"Did you like the game?"

Yes/no gives almost no useful information. It doesn't say what worked, what didn't, or how strongly they felt either way.

Strong Question

"On a scale of 1 (not at all clear) to 5 (completely clear), how clear was it that you had been hit by the enemy?"

This is a Likert scale question — it captures degree, not just a binary answer, and can be averaged across every tester.

Your survey needs a mix of question types. Include at least:

  • Three Likert scale questions (1–5), covering movement feel, hit feedback clarity, and overall difficulty.
  • One open-ended question, such as "Describe the moment in the game that felt most confusing."
  • One closed multiple-choice question with mutually exclusive options, such as which of your two levels felt harder.

While each tester plays, observe silently — don't hint, don't explain controls, don't react. Note where they hesitate, where they die repeatedly, and anything they say out loud unprompted. This is qualitative data you won't get from the survey alone.

Visualise Your Findings

A table of raw numbers is hard to read at a glance. Once you've collected responses from every playtester, average each Likert question and chart the results — a bar chart makes it immediately obvious which areas are strong and which need work.

This example makes one problem immediately visible: hit feedback scored far lower than everything else. That single low bar is worth more than a page of "it felt fine" opinions — it tells you exactly where to focus Step 5.

Build your own version of this chart from your real data, using Chart.js, a spreadsheet, or graph paper if needed. Whatever tool you use, make sure the lowest and highest scores are easy to spot at a glance.

From Data to Design Decision

Data on its own doesn't fix anything — it only points at a problem. The final step is turning a specific finding into a specific, justified change.

Worked Example — We Do

Data finding

Average rating for "clear when hit by enemy" was 2.1/5. Two testers said in the open-ended question that they only realised they'd been hit after checking the Lives label.

→
Design decision

Add a brief screen shake and a single colour-tint flash on the Player sprite when hit, alongside the existing Lives label update — a one-time tint change, not rapid strobing, per the accessibility guidance from the UX lesson.

Notice the decision cites a specific number and specific tester comments — not "I think it needs more feedback." It also respects a constraint from an earlier lesson (no strobing effects) rather than solving one problem by creating another.

Task: pick your lowest-scoring finding and write your own data-to-decision pair in the same format. Then actually implement the change in your Godot project.

Investigating & Researching

Handling Data Safely & Ethically

Your playtesters are real people, most of them classmates. The data they give you needs the same care as the high score file from Sprint 3 — deliberate decisions about what's collected, how it's stored, and who can see it.

🔒 Safe, Secure & Ethical Data Use
  • Anonymise responses. Label testers "Tester 1," "Tester 2," and so on in your notebook rather than using full names.
  • Be upfront. Tell each playtester what you're collecting and why before they start — a survey and some observation notes, used only for your assessment task.
  • Avoid leading questions. A question like "Don't you think the coins felt great?" pushes testers toward a particular answer, producing biased, less trustworthy data — a data-integrity problem as much as an ethical one.
  • Store it appropriately. Keep raw responses in your project notebook or a teacher-approved shared document, not posted publicly where testers didn't agree to have their comments shared.

Anonymising your data isn't just good ethics — testers who know their name won't be attached to a comment are often more willing to give you honest, critical feedback, which is exactly what makes the data useful in the first place.
