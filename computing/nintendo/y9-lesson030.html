Game Design Document

Every professional pitch starts with a document, not a build. Today you turn what you found in the starter project into a Game Design Document (GDD) for your own platformer — including structured English pseudocode for your core loop. Sprint 1 begins next lesson, and it starts from this document, not from a blank script.

Identifying & Defining

Your Brief

Studio memo: "No pitch reaches Nintendo AU without a Game Design Document. I don't need art or code yet — I need to know what your game is: what the player does, moment to moment, and why it's fun. Write your core loop in structured English before you touch GDScript. If you can't describe it in pseudocode, you don't understand it well enough to build it yet."

You have one lesson to produce a complete GDD. It won't be perfect — it doesn't need to be. It needs to be specific enough that Sprint 1 can start from a plan instead of a blank editor. You'll revise it as you build, which is normal; that's what the iterative D&P framework expects.

Identifying & Defining

From Analysis to Design

Your Lessons 1–2 notebook entry isn't just evidence you did the task — it's raw material for today. Every design decision you write in your GDD should trace back to something you noticed in the starter project, either as something you're deliberately reusing or something you're deliberately changing.

Worked Example — We Do

What you analysed

The starter project used Area2D for the Coin, and queue_free() to remove it on pickup, with score handled in a separate script.

→
What you design

"Collectibles will use Area2D with overlap detection, matching the starter project's pattern. I'll extend it with a small pop animation on pickup, which the starter project didn't have."

Notice the design decision does two things: it keeps a proven pattern (the node type, the detection method) and it makes one deliberate, original addition (the animation). That's the shape every entry in your GDD should take.

Identifying & Defining

GDD Structure — Six Sections

Your GDD needs these six sections. Keep each one short — a paragraph or a short list is enough. Depth comes later, through iteration.

1

Concept & Pitch

One sentence describing your game, written the way you'd pitch it to Nintendo AU in person. What makes it different from the starter project?

2

Core Loop

The moment-to-moment cycle the player repeats — move, avoid, collect, survive. This is what Sprint 1–3 actually builds, and it's the only section requiring pseudocode today.

3

Player Mechanics

Every action the player can take — move, jump, and any mechanic beyond what the starter project had. List them; you don't need to design them all today.

4

Level Structure

How many levels, roughly how long each one is, and what changes from one level to the next (difficulty, layout, new hazards).

5

Win & Fail Conditions

Exactly what causes a level to end in victory, and exactly what causes the player to fail. Both need to be testable — a condition you can't test isn't finished design.

6

Node/Scene Plan

A first-draft scene tree for your own game, using what you learned from the starter project's node types (CharacterBody2D, Area2D, and so on).

Identifying & Defining

Pseudocode Your Core Loop

Of the six GDD sections, the core loop is the only one written as formal structured English pseudocode — because it's the one section that becomes code directly, in Sprint 1. Here's a generic platformer core loop to show the shape yours should take:

WHILE level is active IF player presses movement key THEN move player horizontally IF player presses jump AND player is on ground THEN apply upward velocity apply gravity every frame IF player overlaps collectible THEN increase score, remove collectible IF player overlaps enemy THEN decrease lives IF lives = 0 THEN trigger fail condition IF player reaches level goal THEN trigger win condition END WHILE

Design Task

Write Your Own Core Loop

Rewrite the pseudocode above for your own game concept. It must be different from the generic version above in at least one meaningful way — a mechanic the starter project didn't have, a different fail condition, an extra rule. Keep every line testable: if you can't describe how you'd check a line works, rewrite it until you can.
