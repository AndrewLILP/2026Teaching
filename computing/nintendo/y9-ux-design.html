Investigating & Researching

From Store to Game — a Complete User Journey

A player's experience of a game doesn't start when the level loads — it starts the moment they open a storefront looking for something to play. A well-designed journey carries the player smoothly through several distinct stages, each with its own UX job to do:

A typical storefront-to-gameplay journey Browse and discover (store listings, screenshots, ratings) → decide and acquire (price, download, install progress clearly shown) → first launch (title screen, any onboarding or tutorial prompts) → core gameplay (the actual moment-to-moment loop) → pause, menus, and exit (can the player always tell how to get back to what they were doing?). Every stage either builds a player's confidence that the system is trustworthy and predictable, or erodes it.

Analysis Task

Trace a Real Storefront Journey

Using a digital game storefront you have access to (Nintendo eShop, Steam, or similar), answer these questions in your notebook:

  1. How does the store communicate that a purchase or download is in progress, rather than leaving you guessing whether anything happened?
  2. What happens between tapping "Play" and actually being in control of the game? Is there a loading screen, a title screen, a tutorial prompt — and does it feel too long, too short, or about right?
  3. Find one piece of visual language (an icon, a colour, a button style) that means the same thing everywhere in the store and inside the game itself. Why might a designer deliberately keep that consistent?
  4. Find one moment where the interface could confuse a first-time user. What would you change?
Identifying & Defining

UX Heuristics — A Shared Vocabulary for Judging Design

"I don't like it" isn't useful design feedback — it doesn't tell anyone what to fix. Professional designers use usability heuristics: named principles that turn a vague feeling into a specific, actionable observation. Here are five you'll use today, adapted from Jakob Nielsen's widely used set.

1

Visibility of System Status

The system should always show the player what's happening, in a reasonable time, through clear feedback — a score that updates instantly, a loading bar that actually moves.

2

Consistency & Standards

The same action should look and behave the same way everywhere. If one button style means "confirm" on one screen, it shouldn't mean "cancel" on another.

3

Error Prevention

Good design prevents problems before they happen, rather than showing a good error message after. A button that's impossible to misclick beats a clear warning about a misclick.

4

Recognition Over Recall

Players shouldn't have to remember information from one screen to use the next. Controls, objects, and options should be visible, not memorised.

5

Aesthetic & Minimalist Design

Every extra element on screen competes for attention with the ones that matter. Interfaces should contain only relevant, needed information.

Identifying & Defining

Audit Your Own Game

Now turn all five heuristics on your own build from Sprints 1–3. Play through it as if you'd never seen it before — from the moment it launches to reaching the Win screen.

Analysis Task

Heuristic-by-Heuristic Audit

  1. Visibility of System Status: when you collect a coin or get hit by the enemy, does the Score or Lives label update immediately and clearly? Is there any delay or ambiguity?
  2. Consistency & Standards: do your Win and Lose screens use the same button style, font, and layout logic? If a player only saw one of the two, would the other feel familiar or completely different?
  3. Error Prevention: is there any point where a player could get stuck — falling into a gap with no way back, or an enemy blocking the only path forward?
  4. Recognition Over Recall: does anything in your game require the player to remember an instruction from earlier with no on-screen reminder (like which key jumps)?
  5. Aesthetic & Minimalist Design: is there anything on screen right now that doesn't help the player understand or play the game?

Rate each heuristic honestly. A perfect score across all five after one sprint's worth of polish would be surprising — the point of an audit is finding real, specific problems, not confirming everything is fine.

Producing & Implementing

Redesign One Screen in Figma

Pick your single worst-scoring screen from the audit — likely the Win or Lose screen, since they're the newest and least polished. Before redesigning it in full colour, sketch a wireframe: a low-fidelity layout showing structure and content, without final art, fonts, or colour choices.

Fidelity is a deliberate choice, not a shortcut A low-fidelity wireframe (boxes, labels, rough placement) is fast to change and invites feedback on layout and flow before anyone is emotionally attached to a colour scheme. A high-fidelity mockup (final fonts, colours, spacing) communicates the finished feel but is slower to revise. Professional UX designers move deliberately from low to high fidelity — jumping straight to polish risks polishing the wrong layout.

Worked Example — We Do

Audit finding

The Lose screen's "Try Again" button looks completely different from the Win screen's "Next" button — different size, different position, different colour. This breaks Consistency & Standards.

→
Redesign decision

Both screens now use an identical button component — same size, same position, same shape — with only the label text and colour differing (green for "Next", amber for "Try Again") to signal outcome without breaking the shared pattern.

In Figma, create a low-fidelity wireframe of your redesigned screen first. Once you're confident in the layout, produce one high-fidelity version showing your actual intended colours, fonts, and spacing.

Identifying & Defining

Accessible & Ethical Design

Good UX isn't only about looking polished — it's about making sure the widest possible range of players can actually use what you've built. This is where UX design and safe, ethical practice meet directly.

Colour contrast. Text needs enough contrast against its background to be readable, including for players with low vision or colour vision deficiency. Compare these two examples:

🔒 Safe, Secure & Ethical Design

Go back to your Enemy's STUNNED state from Sprint 2. If your visual feedback for being hit involves rapid flashing or strobing, that's more than a style choice — rapid, high-contrast flashing can trigger seizures in players with photosensitive epilepsy. A single colour-tint flash or a brief shake is a safer way to signal the same information without that risk.

This is the same principle from Sprint 3's high-score validation, applied to visual design instead of data: before you ship a design decision, ask who it might affect and how, not just whether it looks good on your own screen.

Task: check your Score and Lives label colours against their background using a contrast checker, and check your enemy's hit-feedback effect for rapid flashing. Record at least two concrete accessibility changes you're making as a result.
