Key Vocabulary

Click a card to flip it and reveal the definition.

Gradient
Definition: How steep a line is — the amount y changes for every 1 unit increase in x. Positive means increasing, negative means decreasing.
Example: In y = 3x + 2, the gradient is 3.
Non-example: The 2 is not the gradient — that's the intercept.
Intercept
Definition: The point where a line crosses the y-axis — the value of y when x = 0.
Example: In y = 3x + 2, the intercept is 2, so the line crosses at (0, 2).
Non-example: The 3 is not the intercept — that's the gradient.
Parallel Lines
Definition: Lines with the exact same gradient. They never meet, no matter how far they're extended.
Example: y = 2x + 1 and y = 2x − 3 are parallel (both gradient 2).
Non-example: y = 2x + 1 and y = −2x + 1 are not parallel — different gradients, even though they share an intercept.
Increasing / Decreasing
Definition: A line is increasing if y gets bigger as x increases (positive gradient), and decreasing if y gets smaller as x increases (negative gradient).
Example: y = 4x + 1 increases; y = −4x + 1 decreases.
Non-example: A horizontal line (gradient 0) is neither increasing nor decreasing.

Comparing Three Lines on the Same Axes

y = x + 1 y = x − 2 y = −2x + 1
Step 1: Graph y = x + 1 (gradient 1, crosses the y-axis at 1) — blue.
Step 2: Graph y = x − 2 (same gradient 1, but crosses at −2) — green. Notice it's parallel to the first line — same steepness, never meets it.
Step 3: Graph y = −2x + 1 (a different, steeper, decreasing gradient) — red. It crosses both other lines.
Step 4: Compare: lines 1 & 2 are parallel (equal gradient, different intercept); line 3 is neither parallel to them nor increasing like they are.
