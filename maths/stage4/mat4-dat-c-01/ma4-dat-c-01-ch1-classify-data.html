Chapter 1 — Classifying Data

MA4-DAT-C-01 · MAO-WM-01 · Stage 4 Mathematics

The Canteen Survey

A school is planning a new canteen menu. They survey every student and record four things about each person:

  • Their favourite food
  • The number of items they buy per week
  • Their height in centimetres
  • Their preferred meal type — Snack, Lunch, or Dinner

Before we learn any definitions, think about these questions:

  1. Which of these pieces of information are numbers? Which are labels or categories?
  2. Of the ones that are numbers — can you count them exactly, or do they exist on a continuous scale like a ruler?
  3. Does "preferred meal type" behave like a number at all? What's different about it compared to height?

Why does this matter?

The type of data you collect determines which graphs you can use and what questions you can answer. Choosing the wrong graph for your data type is one of the most common mistakes in statistics — even by professional journalists.

By the end of this chapter you'll have a precise vocabulary to describe any data you encounter.

I Do — Explicit Teaching

Key Vocabulary

Click each card to flip it and see the definition, example, and non-example.

VariableTap to flip

Any characteristic, number, or quantity that can be measured or counted.

✓ Height, eye colour, test score

✗ A fixed constant like π

Numerical VariableTap to flip

A variable whose values are meaningful numbers — arithmetic operations make sense.

✓ Height (180 cm), number of siblings (3)

✗ Phone number — adding phone numbers is meaningless

DiscreteTap to flip

A numerical variable that can only take specific, countable values — no values in between.

✓ Number of students — you can't have 2.7 students

✗ Height — 170.2 cm is a valid value

ContinuousTap to flip

A numerical variable that can take any value within a range — measured, not counted.

✓ Temperature (23.47°C), time, weight

✗ Number of goals — you can't score 1.5 goals

Categorical VariableTap to flip

A variable whose values are names, labels, or groups — not meaningful numbers.

✓ Eye colour, favourite sport, suburb

✗ Temperature — arithmetic is meaningful

NominalTap to flip

A categorical variable with no natural order between categories.

✓ Eye colour — blue is not "greater" than green

✗ T-shirt size (S < M < L — there is an order)

OrdinalTap to flip

A categorical variable with a natural order, but gaps between categories aren't equal or measurable.

✓ Survey rating: Disagree / Neutral / Agree

✗ Temperature — gaps between degrees are equal and measurable

A dataset describes participants in a fun run. Classify each variable and explain why.

Variables: finishing time (min and sec), gender identity, age group (Under 18 / 18–40 / 40+), number of fun runs completed, suburb of residence

Step 1 — Numerical or categorical?

Numerical: finishing time, number of fun runs completed

Categorical: gender identity, age group, suburb of residence

Step 2 — Classify numerical variables

Finishing time → measured precisely (e.g. 43 min 17.4 sec) → Continuous
Time can take any value; it's measured not counted.

Number of fun runs completed → counted in whole numbers → Discrete
You can't complete 3.5 fun runs.

Step 3 — Classify categorical variables

Gender identity → categories with no meaningful order → Nominal

Age group → categories with a natural order (Under 18 < 18–40 < 40+) → Ordinal

Suburb of residence → no natural order between suburbs → Nominal

Claim: "A star rating out of 5 (★★★☆☆) is a numerical discrete variable — it uses whole numbers from 1 to 5."

Evaluate this claim. Is a 5-star rating discrete, ordinal, or does it depend on context?

Step 1 — Apply the numerical test

Does arithmetic make sense? Is 4 stars "twice as good" as 2 stars? Averages of ratings are used in practice (e.g. Google Maps), so they appear numerical.

Step 2 — Apply the ordinal test

The gaps between star levels are not necessarily equal. The difference in quality between 1★ and 2★ may not equal the difference between 4★ and 5★. Order is meaningful but gaps are not — the defining feature of ordinal data.

Step 3 — Evaluate the claim

The claim is contestable. In strict statistical terms, star ratings are Categorical Ordinal — the values have order but the intervals are not guaranteed to be equal. In practice, analysts often treat them as numerical discrete for convenience. The correct answer depends on the assumptions your analysis makes about equal-interval spacing.

The key question to ask every time

Step 1: Is the data a number or a label/category?

Step 2: If it's a number — can you count it exactly (discrete) or does it exist on a continuous scale (continuous)?

Error Detective 🔍

A student classified variables from a survey. One step contains a genuine error — find it.

Survey variables: number of pets, favourite colour, distance to school, year group
✓ Correction: Year group (Year 7, 8, 9) is Categorical Ordinal, not numerical discrete. Although year groups are written as numbers, they are labels that have a natural order — Year 8 is not "twice" Year 4, and you can't meaningfully average year groups. The numbers are identifiers, not quantities.
