I Do — Graph Conventions

What Every Graph Must Include

  • 📌Title — tells the reader what the graph shows (who, what, when)
  • 📌Axis labels — each axis named with what it shows and the unit (e.g. "Frequency", "Score (points)")
  • 📌Scale — consistent spacing on axes; starts at 0 unless there's a clearly marked break
  • 📌Source — where the data came from (e.g. "Source: ABS Time Use Survey 2020")
  • ⚠️Key/legend — required when colours or patterns represent different groups
  • ⚠️Stem-and-leaf key — e.g. "3 | 7 means 37" — required for every stem-and-leaf plot
I Do — Worked Examples

Constructing Graphs Step by Step

A class voted on their favourite sport: Football – 8, Basketball – 5, Swimming – 4, Tennis – 3. Construct a column graph.

Step 1 — Set up axes

Horizontal axis (x): categories — Football, Basketball, Swimming, Tennis
Vertical axis (y): Frequency — scale 0 to 8, mark every 2

Step 2 — Draw the bars

Each bar sits above its category. Bars have equal width and gaps between them (unlike a histogram). Heights: Football = 8, Basketball = 5, Swimming = 4, Tennis = 3.

Step 3 — Add all required conventions
  • ✓ Title: "Favourite Sports of Year 7 Students"
  • ✓ x-axis label: "Sport"
  • ✓ y-axis label: "Number of students"
  • ✓ Scale: 0, 2, 4, 6, 8 (consistent intervals)
  • ✓ Source: "Source: Year 7 class survey, 2026"

A school council survey: 40 students — Walk 16, Bus 12, Car 8, Bike 4. Construct both a divided bar graph and a sector graph. Calculate each sector angle.

Step 1 — Calculate proportions

Total = 40  |  Walk: 16/40 = 40%  |  Bus: 12/40 = 30%  |  Car: 8/40 = 20%  |  Bike: 4/40 = 10%

Step 2 — Calculate sector angles (proportion × 360°)

Walk: 0.40 × 360° = 144°

Bus: 0.30 × 360° = 108°

Car: 0.20 × 360° = 72°

Bike: 0.10 × 360° = 36°

Check: 144 + 108 + 72 + 36 = 360° ✓

Step 3 — Divided bar graph (total length = 100 units)

Walk: 40 units | Bus: 30 units | Car: 20 units | Bike: 10 units

Walk 40%
Bus 30%
Car 20%
Bike

Both graphs require: Title, Source, Key/Legend. The sector graph also requires a protractor for accurate angle measurement.

Quick Check — Graph Features

Look back at the 8 graph examples in the Learn tab. Answer these questions from memory:

1. Which graph type has bars that touch? Why?

2. What is always required on a stem-and-leaf plot that isn't needed on a column graph?

3. What does the formula angle = proportion × 360° calculate?

4. What does "each symbol = 2 students" mean on a pictogram?

Show answers

1. Frequency histogram — bars touch because the data is continuous (class intervals are adjacent with no gaps between them).

2. A key (e.g. "3|7 means 37") — without it, the reader can't decode the stem-leaf notation.

3. The angle of each sector in a sector graph — how many degrees of the full circle (360°) each category takes up.

4. Each symbol represents 2 students, so you multiply the number of symbols by 2 to get the frequency for that category.

Error Detective 🔍

A student constructs a stem-and-leaf plot from: 23, 31, 28, 35, 22, 19, 34, 27. Find the one error.

✓ Correction: Leaves must always be written in ascending order, not in the order they appeared in the dataset. The correct leaves for stem 3 are: 1, 4, 5 (representing 31, 34, 35). This is a core convention — ascending order is what allows you to read the median and identify the distribution shape.
