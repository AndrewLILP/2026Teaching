The challenge of this chapter

There are seven main graph types in the NSW syllabus for Stage 4. Each has specific construction rules — titles, labels, scales, and conventions that make the graph readable and honest.

By the end of this chapter you'll be able to construct any of them correctly.

Graph Conventions — What Makes a Graph Complete?

Below are the conventions every graph must include. Rate your confidence with each one — have you seen it before?

  • 📌Title — tells the reader what the graph is about (who, what, when)
  • 📌Axis labels — each axis named with what it shows and the unit (e.g. "Frequency", "Score (points)")
  • 📌Scale — consistent spacing on axes; starts at 0 unless there's a good reason not to
  • 📌Source — where the data came from (e.g. "Source: ABS Time Use Survey 2020")
  • ⚠️Key/legend — required when colours or patterns represent different groups
  • ⚠️Stem-and-leaf key — e.g. "3 | 7 means 37" — required for every stem-and-leaf plot

Graph Types — Key Vocabulary

Click each card to flip it.

Frequency HistogramTap to flip

A bar graph for continuous data — bars touch, showing data grouped into equal class intervals.

✓ Use for: heights, times, test scores in ranges

✗ Not for: categories (use column graph instead)

Stem-and-Leaf PlotTap to flip

Displays individual data values using a stem (leading digit) and leaf (trailing digit). Shows shape and retains original values.

✓ Key required: "3 | 7 means 37"

✗ Leaves must be in ascending order

Dot PlotTap to flip

Each data value shown as a dot above a number line. Good for small datasets — shows all individual values.

✓ Use for: small discrete datasets, comparing groups

✗ Not practical for large datasets

Divided Bar GraphTap to flip

A single bar divided into sections, each proportional to its category's frequency. Shows part-to-whole relationships.

✓ Use for: comparing parts of a whole across groups

✗ Hard to compare non-adjacent sections

Sector GraphTap to flip

A circle divided into sectors, each proportional to its category. Angle = (frequency ÷ total) × 360°.

✓ Use for: parts of a whole (when categories sum to 100%)

✗ Hard to compare similar-sized sectors

Line GraphTap to flip

Points plotted and connected by line segments, showing change over time or continuous progression.

✓ Use for: temperature over a week, stock prices

✗ Not for unordered categories

Worked Examples

A class voted on their favourite sport. Results: Football – 8, Basketball – 5, Swimming – 4, Tennis – 3. Construct a column graph.

Step 1 — Set up axes

Horizontal axis (x): Categories — Football, Basketball, Swimming, Tennis
Vertical axis (y): Frequency (number of students) — scale 0 to 8, mark every 2

Step 2 — Draw the bars

Each bar sits above its category. Bars have equal width and gaps between them (unlike a histogram). Heights: Football = 8, Basketball = 5, Swimming = 4, Tennis = 3.

Step 3 — Add all required conventions
  • ✓ Title: "Favourite Sports of Year 7 Students"
  • ✓ x-axis label: "Sport"
  • ✓ y-axis label: "Number of students"
  • ✓ Scale: 0, 2, 4, 6, 8 (consistent intervals)
  • ✓ Source: "Source: Year 7 class survey, 2026"

A school council survey asks 40 students how they travel to school: Walk – 16, Bus – 12, Car – 8, Bike – 4. Construct both a divided bar graph and a sector graph. Calculate the angle for each sector.

Step 1 — Calculate proportions

Total = 16 + 12 + 8 + 4 = 40 students

Walk: 16/40 = 0.40 = 40%  |  Bus: 12/40 = 0.30 = 30%

Car: 8/40 = 0.20 = 20%  |  Bike: 4/40 = 0.10 = 10%

Step 2 — Calculate sector angles

Angle = proportion × 360°

Walk: 0.40 × 360° = 144°

Bus: 0.30 × 360° = 108°

Car: 0.20 × 360° = 72°

Bike: 0.10 × 360° = 36°

Check: 144 + 108 + 72 + 36 = 360° ✓

Step 3 — Divided bar graph (total length = 100 units)

Walk: 40 units | Bus: 30 units | Car: 20 units | Bike: 10 units

Walk 40%
Bus 30%
Car 20%
Bike

Both graphs require: Title, Source, Key/Legend. Sector graph also requires a protractor for accurate angle measurement.

Error Detective 🔍

A student constructs a stem-and-leaf plot from the dataset: 23, 31, 28, 35, 22, 19, 34, 27. Find the one error in their working.

✓ Correction: Leaves must always be written in ascending order, not in the order they appeared in the dataset. The correct leaves for stem 3 are: 1, 4, 5 (representing 31, 34, 35). This is a convention of stem-and-leaf plots — the ascending order is what allows you to read the median and identify the distribution shape.
