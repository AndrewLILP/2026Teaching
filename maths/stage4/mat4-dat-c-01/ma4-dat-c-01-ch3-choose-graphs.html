Same Data, Different Story?

Two students receive the same dataset about monthly rainfall in Sydney across one year. One displays it as a column graph, the other as a line graph.

Both graphs are technically correct. Both have proper titles, labels, and scales.

Before we learn the rules, think about these questions:

  1. Are the two graphs equally good for this data, or is one more appropriate?
  2. The line graph implies a continuous trend — does rainfall really flow continuously between months, or are the monthly totals separate amounts?
  3. Could the choice of graph change what story the data seems to tell?

Why graph choice matters

A graph is a communication tool. The "best" graph for a dataset is the one that most honestly represents what the data actually shows and makes the right comparisons easy to see.

Choosing the wrong graph type isn't just a technicality — it can mislead readers, even unintentionally.

I Do — The Framework

The Graph Choice Framework

Ask these questions in order to select the right graph type:

1. Parts of a whole?

Do categories add up to a meaningful total (100%)? Use:

Sector graph Divided bar graph

2. Change over time?

Is the x-axis a continuous time variable? Use:

Line graph Frequency polygon

3. Comparing categories?

Independent groups, no time component? Use:

Column graph Dot plot Pictogram

4. Distribution shape?

Want to see spread, clusters, or shape? Use:

Stem-and-leaf Histogram Dot plot
I Do — Key Vocabulary

Key Terms

Parts of a WholeTap to flip

Data where each category is a portion of a total — all categories together equal 100%.

✓ Budget split, transport methods, election vote shares

✗ Monthly temperatures — they don't add to a meaningful total

TrendTap to flip

A general direction or pattern in data over time — increasing, decreasing, or stable.

✓ Line graphs reveal trends — the slope shows direction of change

✗ Column graphs for time data separate each period and obscure trends

DistributionTap to flip

How data values are spread across the range — shows where most values cluster and how spread out the data is.

✓ Histograms and stem-and-leaf plots reveal distribution shape

✗ Sector graphs hide individual values and distribution shape

Justifying Graph ChoiceTap to flip

Explaining why one graph type is more appropriate than another using data-specific reasons.

✓ "A line graph is better here because the data shows change over time."

✗ "I chose a line graph because it looks good." — not a justification

I Do — Worked Examples

Choosing and Justifying Graph Types

Match three scenarios to the correct graph type: sector graph, line graph, column graph.

  1. A student's test scores, week by week over a term
  2. Which sport was most popular at the school carnival
  3. What proportion of a day is spent on different activities
Step 1 — Identify what each dataset is about

Scenario 1: change over time → time is on the x-axis

Scenario 2: comparing separate categories → sports are independent groups

Scenario 3: parts of a whole → all activities together = 24 hours = 100%

Step 2 — Apply the framework

Scenario 1 → Line graph — change over time; connected points show the trend

Scenario 2 → Column graph — comparing independent categories; each sport gets its own bar

Scenario 3 → Sector graph — parts of a whole; each activity is a slice of the 24-hour day

Step 3 — Justify each in one sentence

"A line graph is appropriate because time is a continuous variable and we want to see the trend in scores."

"A column graph is appropriate because sports are independent categories with no time order or part-whole relationship."

"A sector graph is appropriate because all activities together make up exactly one day — a part-whole relationship."

Monthly average temperature in Sydney across 12 months. A student displays this as a column graph. Justify why a line graph is more appropriate.

Step 1 — Identify the nature of the x-axis variable

The x-axis is time — months in order. Time is a continuous variable; there is a natural, ordered progression from January to December. The months are not independent categories.

Step 2 — Identify the purpose

The reader wants to see how temperature changes across the year — a trend. A connected line makes the falling and rising pattern immediately visible. A column graph separates each month into an isolated bar, making trends harder to read.

Step 3 — Write the justification

"A line graph is more appropriate because the x-axis is a continuous time variable and the purpose is to show how temperature changes over the year. The connected line clearly reveals the cooling trend through autumn and winter and the warming through spring and summer. A column graph would treat each month as a separate category, obscuring this continuous relationship."

A researcher wants to compare travel methods (Walk/Bus/Car/Bike) across two school years (2024 and 2025). Evaluate whether a divided bar graph or side-by-side column graph better serves their purpose — and under what conditions the answer might change.

Step 1 — What each graph type shows well

Divided bar graph: Excellent for part-to-whole relationships within each year. Hard to compare specific categories (e.g. Bus 2024 vs Bus 2025) when segments aren't aligned.

Side-by-side column graph: Excellent for comparing specific categories across years. Harder to see overall proportional composition.

Step 2 — Identify the primary purpose

Key question: is the researcher most interested in (a) how the overall mix changed, or (b) how each specific method changed?

If (a) → divided bar graph  |  If (b) → side-by-side column graph

Step 3 — Conditional answer

For overall composition → divided bar graph. For comparing specific modes → side-by-side column graph.

Condition where the answer changes: If one category is very small (e.g. Bike = 2%), a divided bar segment is nearly invisible. Here the column graph is fairer — it gives small categories a visible bar height.

Error Detective 🔍

A student justifies their graph choice for a dataset showing time spent on different activities in a day. Find the one error.

Dataset: Sleep 8 hrs · School 6 hrs · Sport 1.5 hrs · Screen 3.5 hrs · Other 5 hrs (total = 24 hours)
✓ Correction: When data represents parts of a whole, a sector graph or divided bar graph is more appropriate than a column graph. A column graph does show category sizes, but does not communicate the part-whole relationship — the reader cannot see that the activities together make up exactly one day. The student correctly eliminated the line graph in Step 3 but picked the wrong alternative in Step 4.
