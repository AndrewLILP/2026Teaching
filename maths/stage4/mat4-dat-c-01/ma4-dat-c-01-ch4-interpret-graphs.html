I Do — Key Vocabulary

Key Terms

PatternTap to flip

A regular, repeating structure in data — a trend, cycle, or cluster the graph makes visible.

✓ "Scores cluster between 12 and 16" is a pattern

✗ "The highest score is 18" is a reading, not a pattern

ConclusionTap to flip

A statement drawn from the data that goes beyond simply reading values — an interpretation supported by evidence.

✓ "Most students scored above 12 out of 20" — uses the pattern

✗ "The most common score was 12" — just a reading

PredictionTap to flip

Using a pattern in the data to estimate future or unobserved values — always stated with appropriate uncertainty.

✓ "Based on the trend, spending is likely to exceed $90k in Year 5"

✗ "Spending will definitely be $95k" — overstated certainty

Misleading GraphTap to flip

A graph that gives the reader a false impression through design choices — even when the data values are accurate.

✓ Truncated axis, 3D icons, inconsistent scales, missing labels

✗ A misleading graph ≠ wrong data — the data can be accurate but the design deceptive

I Do — Interpretation Framework

How to Interpret a Graph — Four Steps

Step 1 — Read: What does the graph show? What are the axes? What is the scale? What time period or group?

Step 2 — Identify features: Maximum? Minimum? Mode? Median? Where do values cluster?

Step 3 — Identify patterns: Trend (increasing/decreasing)? A cluster? An outlier? Symmetric or skewed shape?

Step 4 — Conclude and predict: What does the data tell us? What might happen next? What questions does it raise?

⚠️ Critical reading: Always check the scale, the axis origin, and whether icon sizes are proportional. Ask: "Does the visual impression match the actual numbers?"
I Do — Worked Examples

Reading and Interpreting — Step by Step

A dot plot shows class test scores: 8, 9, 11, 11, 12, 12, 12, 13, 14, 14, 15, 15, 16, 17, 18. Identify the mode, range, and describe the shape.

Step 1 — Read the graph

Values range from 8 to 18. The score 12 has three dots above it — more than any other score.

Step 2 — Identify the mode

Mode = the value with the most dots = 12 (appears 3 times)

Step 3 — Calculate the range

Range = maximum − minimum = 18 − 8 = 10

Step 4 — Describe the shape

"The distribution is roughly symmetric, with most scores clustered between 11 and 15. There is one low outlier at 8 which pulls the left tail slightly lower. Most students scored above 10 out of 20."

Council spending on parks: Year 1 = $200,000, Year 2 = $220,000. The y-axis starts at $195,000. The bars appear to show Year 2 is more than three times Year 1. Identify the technique, calculate the actual percentage increase, and explain the distortion.

Step 1 — Identify the misleading technique

Truncated y-axis — the y-axis starts at $195,000 instead of $0.

Visible bar for Year 1 = $200k − $195k = $5k  |  Visible bar for Year 2 = $220k − $195k = $25k

Visual ratio: $25k ÷ $5k = 5:1 — the bar appears 5 times taller.

Step 2 — Calculate the actual percentage increase

Increase = $220,000 − $200,000 = $20,000

Percentage increase = (20,000 ÷ 200,000) × 100 = 10%

The graph implies a ~400% visual increase for what is actually a 10% increase.

Step 3 — Explain for two audiences

For a mathematician: "The y-axis truncation amplifies a 10% increase into a visual ratio of approximately 5:1. The labelled values are accurate but the bar heights are visually deceptive."

For a general reader: "The graph starts at $195,000 instead of zero, so the bars only show the top sliver of each value. A small real difference looks enormous because you're not seeing the full bars."

Spotting Misleading Techniques

Two common techniques used to mislead readers. For each, identify what has been done and why it distorts the data.

Technique 1 — Truncated y-axis. A bar graph shows sales of $198,000 and $202,000. The y-axis starts at $195,000. The second bar appears about 4 times taller than the first.

Starting above zero removes baseline context. The actual difference is $4,000 — about 2%. The visual impression of "4 times taller" vastly exaggerates this. An honest graph's y-axis should start at 0, or clearly mark the truncation with a break symbol (//).

Technique 2 — 3D icons scaled in all dimensions. An icon for $18k is 1cm tall; the $74k icon is 4cm tall AND 4cm wide AND 4cm deep.

When scaled in all three dimensions, visual volume grows by the cube of the scale factor. 4× taller looks 4³ = 64× larger to the eye. The actual ratio is $74k ÷ $18k ≈ 4. The graph makes it look like 64 times the cost, not 4 times.

Error Detective 🔍

A student finds the median of a stem-and-leaf plot with 9 values. Find the one error.

1
2 5 8
2
1 4 7
3
0 3 6

Key: 1|2 means 12

✓ Correction: For an odd number of values, the median is the single middle value — not the average of two values. With 9 values, the median is at position (9 + 1) ÷ 2 = 5th value = 24. The student applied the even-number rule to an odd dataset. This is one of the most common median misconceptions.

Newman's Word Problem

A journalist publishes a graph showing that a council's spending on parks increased from $200,000 to $220,000 over two years. The y-axis starts at $195,000. The bars appear to show the second year's spending is more than three times the first year's.

Identify the technique used to create a misleading impression. Calculate the actual percentage increase in spending. Explain how a reader unfamiliar with graph conventions might misinterpret this graph.

📖 Read — What does the graph claim to show?

What do you notice about the y-axis? What values are shown?

💡 Comprehend — What is the actual difference?

Scaffold frame

"The actual increase is $___, from $___ to $___."

🔄 Transform — What formula gives percentage increase?

Scaffold frame

Percentage increase = (increase ÷ original) × 100

⚙️ Process — Calculate and describe the distortion

Scaffold frame

"The actual increase is ___%. The graph makes it look like ___."

✅ Encode — Model answer

Technique: Truncated y-axis — starting at $195,000 removes the baseline and exaggerates the visual difference.

Actual percentage increase: (220,000 − 200,000) ÷ 200,000 × 100 = 10%

Visual distortion: Visible bars are $5,000 (Yr 1) and $25,000 (Yr 2) — a 5:1 ratio. But the actual ratio is 1.1:1.

Scaffold frame

"Mathematically, the spending increased by 10%. A general reader might think spending more than tripled because the bar appears 5 times taller — created by starting the y-axis well above zero."
