Same Data, Different Story?

Two students receive the same dataset about monthly rainfall in Sydney (mm per month across one year). One displays it as a column graph, the other as a line graph.

Both graphs are technically correct. Both use proper titles, labels, and scales.

Before we learn the rules, think about these questions:

  1. Are the two graphs equally good for this data, or is one more appropriate?
  2. The line graph implies a continuous trend — does rainfall really flow continuously between months, or are the monthly totals separate amounts?
  3. Could the choice of graph change what story the data seems to tell? Could it lead a reader to a different conclusion?

Why graph choice matters

A graph is a communication tool. The "best" graph for a dataset is the one that most honestly represents what the data actually shows — and makes the right comparisons easy to see.

Choosing the wrong graph type isn't just a technicality. It can mislead readers — even unintentionally.

The Graph Choice Framework

Ask these three questions in order:

1. Parts of a whole?

Do categories add up to a meaningful total (100%)? Use:

Sector graph Divided bar graph

2. Change over time?

Is the x-axis a continuous time variable? Use:

Line graph Frequency polygon

3. Comparing categories?

Independent groups without a time component? Use:

Column graph Dot plot Pictogram

4. Distribution shape?

Want to see spread, clusters, or shape of data? Use:

Stem-and-leaf Histogram Dot plot

Key Vocabulary

Parts of a WholeTap to flip

Data where each category is a portion of a total — all categories together equal 100%.

✓ Budget split, transport methods, election vote shares

✗ Monthly temperatures (they don't add to a meaningful total)

TrendTap to flip

A general direction or pattern in data over time — increasing, decreasing, or stable.

✓ Line graphs reveal trends — the slope shows direction of change

✗ Column graphs for time data obscure trends by separating each period

DistributionTap to flip

How data values are spread across the range — shows where most values cluster and how spread out the data is.

✓ Histograms and stem-and-leaf plots reveal distribution shape

✗ Sector graphs hide individual values and distribution shape

Justifying Graph ChoiceTap to flip

Explaining why one graph type is more appropriate than another using data-specific reasons.

✓ "A line graph is better here because the data shows change over time and a trend is visible."

✗ "I chose a line graph because it looks good." (Not a justification)

Scenario 1 → Line graph — change over time; connected points show the trend

Step 3 — Justify each choice in one sentence

Line graph: "Time is a continuous variable and we want to see the trend in scores."

Column graph: "Sports are independent categories with no time order or part-whole relationship."

Sector graph: "All activities together make up exactly one day — this is a part-whole relationship."

A dataset shows the monthly average temperature in Sydney across 12 months. A student displays this as a column graph. Justify why a line graph is more appropriate.

MonthJanFebMarAprMayJun
Temp (°C)262624221917
Step 1 — Identify the nature of the x-axis variable

The x-axis is time (months in order). Time is a continuous variable — there is a natural, ordered progression from January to December. The months are not independent categories.

Step 2 — Identify the purpose: what does the reader need to see?

The reader wants to see how temperature changes across the year — a trend. A connected line makes the trend (cooling through winter, warming through summer) immediately visible.

A column graph separates each month into an isolated bar — this makes comparing adjacent months harder and hides the continuous nature of temperature change.

Step 3 — Write the justification

"A line graph is more appropriate because the x-axis is a continuous time variable (months in sequence), and the purpose is to show how temperature changes over the year. The connected line clearly reveals the trend — falling through autumn and winter, rising through spring and summer. A column graph would treat each month as a separate category, obscuring the continuous relationship between time and temperature."

A researcher wants to compare the breakdown of travel methods (Walk/Bus/Car/Bike) across two school years (2024 and 2025). Evaluate whether a divided bar graph or side-by-side column graph better serves their purpose. Under what conditions might that answer change?

Step 1 — Identify what each graph type shows well

Divided bar graph: Excellent for part-to-whole relationships within each year. The reader can immediately see how the proportions shift. Hard to compare individual categories (e.g. Bus 2024 vs Bus 2025) when segments are not aligned.

Side-by-side column graph: Excellent for comparing specific categories across years (e.g. how Bus usage changed). Harder to perceive the overall proportional composition of each year.

Step 2 — Identify the researcher's primary purpose

The researcher wants to compare across two time periods. The key question: are they most interested in (a) how the overall mix of methods changed, or (b) how each specific method changed?

If (a) → divided bar graph is better
If (b) → side-by-side column graph is better

Step 3 — Evaluate and state the conditional answer

If the purpose is to show the overall composition (what proportion uses each method each year), the divided bar graph is better — it makes the part-whole structure of each year immediately visible.

If the purpose is to compare specific modes (how walking grew, how car use fell), the side-by-side column graph is better — adjacent bars for the same category make direct comparison easy.

Condition where the answer changes: If one category is very small (e.g. Bike = 2%), a divided bar segment is nearly invisible. Here the column graph is fairer — it gives the small category a visible bar height.

✅ Encode — Model answer

Why line graph is wrong: A line graph is designed for data showing change over time — it implies a continuous trend between data points. Transport categories (walk, bus, car, bike, other) are not time points and have no sequential relationship. Connecting them with a line implies a trend that doesn't exist.

Better choice: Sector graph or divided bar graph. Both are designed for part-to-whole data. Total = 45 + 30 + 25 + 15 + 5 = 120 students.

Key feature: The graph must include a key/legend mapping each colour/section to a transport category, so the reader knows which slice or segment represents which method.
